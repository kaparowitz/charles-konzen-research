Parish Search Coverage — German Confirmation Registers

What has been searched for Charles's birth in the Hessian parishes, and what has not. The June 2026 widened sweep: any boy named Carl, born 1833–1841, surname open — read straight off the confirmation registers.

A conclusion is only as strong as the searches behind it. This page is the at-a-glance ledger for the 2026 confirmation-register sweep across the Kirchenkreis Hofgeismar — one row per parish, with the register searched, the birth window covered, the method, and the result. It is the companion to the longer-running negative-results audit (which logs the earlier Konze-focused baptism searches) and feeds the GPS test of reasonably exhaustive research.

What this sweep was looking for

The earlier searches (logged in the negative-results audit) filtered on the surname — they looked for a Konze-family child, and outside the core parishes they narrowed to roughly January–February 1835. A negative-results re-audit exposed two blind spots in that approach, so this sweep deliberately widened all four criteria — given name as the anchor, surname open, the full 1833–1841 window, and both confessions — as set out in the search-terms card above.

Why confirmations, not baptisms

Confirmation registers are a fast filter: each class lists every ~13–15-year-old by name, birth date, and father. A child born 1833–1841 surfaces in the 1847–1855 confirmation classes regardless of which parish baptised him or what his surname was — so one class-list does the work of scanning years of scattered baptisms. This sweep reads the confirmation books; the baptism registers remain the deeper Tier described on Open Questions.

Coverage — all 40 parishes searched

Every parish examined for Charles — 40 in all — grouped by the kind of search done: the Konze-pool cluster, the confirmation-register sweep, the wider 1835 baptism-window sweep, the Weser / Lippoldsberg ring (incl. the Waldensian colony of Gottstreu), the cross-Weser Hannover neighbours (Wahmbeck, Bodenfelde, Bursfelde), the Catholic Westphalia border, and the distant toponym / family-trail tests. The full per-parish detail — register searched, classes read, birth window, and result — lives on each parish’s own research log, beside its Archion source citations, all reachable from the Hessian parish map. To avoid maintaining the same row twice, this page keeps only the at-a-glance status and links straight through to each log.

Method & reliability — read this before trusting a row

The registers were read in Archion's high-resolution viewer. Two limits shape how far the by-eye reads can be trusted:

• The given name is reliable; the exact birth year is not. Distinguishing Carl from Johann or Conrad in Kurrent script is dependable, so “is there a boy named Carl in this class?” is a sound result. Reading the year numerals is not — a calibration slip of several years was caught and corrected mid-search at Sielen. Exact birth dates here should be confirmed against the AI transcription or by a Kurrent reader.

• Surnames in Kurrent warrant a second eye. Given names and dates read more cleanly than surnames; any surname above is a best reading, not a certified transcription.

For these reasons the precise page-maps (register, image, class) are kept in the project's working log and are best handed to Peter Schräder (Kurrent specialist) or read on the September archive trip, rather than treated as final from the by-eye pass alone.

Illegitimate births — the cross-cutting sweep

The coverage table above filters on the surname pool (Konze / Kontze / Gans…). But that filter has a blind spot: an illegitimate child was usually entered under the mother’s surname, so a boy who later anglicised his name to “Gannsen” could be hiding in a parish this page marks “eliminated — no pool surname.” This section pulls every illegitimate male birth in the 1833–1841 window back out of the per-parish logs and lines them up on their own, regardless of surname — the net cast as wide as “any illegitimate boy, any forename.” It also flags the parishes where the baptisms were not read entry-by-entry across the window, because that is where an unrecorded illegitimate birth could still be.

What this sweep changes — and what it doesn’t

The illegitimacy lens does not overturn the negative result: of 13 illegitimate boys logged across the window, none carries a Konze / *n·s·n surname, and the four born in the Jan–Feb 1835 core (Wehmann, Albore, Thielemann, “Johann Carl” Schröder) are each eliminated on surname, territory, legitimisation, or a US forward-trace. The Heisebeck Wehmann boy remains the closest single profile to Charles.

What it does change is the read of “eliminated”: a parish cleared because it held no pool surname has not been cleared of an illegitimate boy entered under a mother’s name unless its window baptisms were read entry-by-entry. The 19 parishes in the gap table are where that read is still owed — the priority list for the September trip and for Schräder’s Kurrent eye.
