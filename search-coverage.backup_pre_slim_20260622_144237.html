Parish Search Coverage — German Confirmation Registers

What has been searched for Charles's birth in the Hessian parishes, and what has not. The June 2026 widened sweep: any boy named Carl, born 1833–1841, surname open — read straight off the confirmation registers.

A conclusion is only as strong as the searches behind it. This page is the at-a-glance ledger for the 2026 confirmation-register sweep across the Kirchenkreis Hofgeismar — one row per parish, with the register searched, the birth window covered, the method, and the result. It is the companion to the longer-running negative-results audit (which logs the earlier Konze-focused baptism searches) and feeds the GPS test of reasonably exhaustive research.

What this sweep was looking for

The earlier searches (logged in the negative-results audit) filtered on the surname — they looked for a Konze-family child, and outside the core parishes they narrowed to roughly January–February 1835. A negative-results re-audit exposed two blind spots in that approach, so this sweep deliberately widened all four criteria:

Given name is the anchor

Any Carl / Karl (the German form of Charles) — not a surname. Charles named a son Charles, and put “Charles” in a grandson's name, so the forename is the thread.

Surname open

Any surname, especially endings that could be heard as *n·s·n (Gansen, Ganssen, Gonsen, Gonson, Hansen, Jansen, Konzen…), legitimate or illegitimate.

Full window

Birth 1833–1841 (core 1834–1838). The U.S. records scatter Charles's birth across these years; 26 Jan 1835 is the working anchor, not the boundary. See Birth date across the records.

Both confessions

Lutheran and Reformed registers wherever a parish kept both — Kurhessen was split between the two.

Why confirmations, not baptisms

Confirmation registers are a fast filter: each class lists every ~13–15-year-old by name, birth date, and father. A child born 1833–1841 surfaces in the 1847–1855 confirmation classes regardless of which parish baptised him or what his surname was — so one class-list does the work of scanning years of scattered baptisms. This sweep reads the confirmation books; the baptism registers remain the deeper Tier described on Open Questions.

The boys named Carl that turned up

The forename Carl is common in these villages — which is the point. The given name does not narrow the field; the surname is the discriminator, and none of the Carls found carries a surname resembling Charles's American names (Gannsen / Gonson / Konzen) or an *n-s-n ending:

  1. Carl Wilhelm Wilmes — Hümme, b. 12 March 1834 non-matching surname

    Read clearly off the “Geburts Jahr und Tag” column of the 1849 class. Father David Wilmes, a day-labourer; mother Margarethe Elisabeth née Tille. Squarely in the window, but the surname is Wilmes.

  2. Carl Julius Bringemann — Helmarshausen, b. 1836 non-matching surname

    From the 1849 class; father Johannes Bringemann, a farmer. In the window, but Bringemann is a local family with several confirmands in the same book.

  3. Carl Wilhelm Tiltner — Gudensberg, b. 1836 non-matching surname

    The single Carl in Gudensberg's 1850 class (boys 1–20, read in full); father Daniel, a master mason. In the window, but the surname reads Tiltner — nothing like an *n-s-n ending. This was the most probative class to read for a boy born 26 Jan 1835, and it yields no Konze-type Carl.

  4. Carl Schäfer — Gudensberg, b. 1835 non-matching surname

    The one primary Carl in Gudensberg's 1849 class (entry 9 of ~33 boys); born 1835, the canonical year. Surname Schäfer — again not an *n-s-n ending. Two further classes (1850, 1851) carried only middle-name Carls (Friedrich Carl Hildebrand, Philipp Carl Junge).

  5. “Carl Ludwig” — Sielen, mid-1840s class unverified

    A boy named Carl Ludwig in a Sielen class; the surname read tentatively as Dößler and the birth-year is not verified (see the method note below). Recorded so it can be re-checked, not relied on.

Method & reliability — read this before trusting a row

The registers were read in Archion's high-resolution viewer. Two limits shape how far the by-eye reads can be trusted:

• The given name is reliable; the exact birth year is not. Distinguishing Carl from Johann or Conrad in Kurrent script is dependable, so “is there a boy named Carl in this class?” is a sound result. Reading the year numerals is not — a calibration slip of several years was caught and corrected mid-search at Sielen. Exact birth dates here should be confirmed against the AI transcription or by a Kurrent reader.

• Surnames in Kurrent warrant a second eye. Given names and dates read more cleanly than surnames; any surname above is a best reading, not a certified transcription.

For these reasons the precise page-maps (register, image, class) are kept in the project's working log and are best handed to Peter Schräder (Kurrent specialist) or read on the September archive trip, rather than treated as final from the by-eye pass alone.

What the sweep means so far

Across the tabular confirmation books actually read — Helmarshausen, Hümme, Sielen, and now Gudensberg, all covering the class where a boy born in the mid-1830s would be confirmed — the result is consistent: the name Carl appears, but never on a Konze-type or *n-s-n surname, and Sielen's 1849 class had no Carl at all. Gudensberg matters because it was the strongest of James Gunnis's three “Deissen” candidates and the only one with an online confirmation book for the window; its 1850 class (the exact cohort for a 26 Jan 1835 birth) was read boy-by-boy and produced one Carl on the wrong surname. That is a meaningful negative across all four parishes. It points away from these already-digitised parishes and back toward the registers that are not online, plus the Dissen/Grifte baptism scans — the natural work for an on-site visit.

What remains — the gaps this sweep does not close

Queued, awaiting transcription

Deisel (218836) and Trendelburg (220102) core classes — the two highest-priority books — are queued for AI transcription and need a return visit once processed.

Tabular classes still to read

Gudensberg 1848–49 & 1851–55 (244437); Helmarshausen 1847 & 1850–55; Sielen 1847–48 & 1850–52; Hümme 1850–51. All located; eye-readable in the viewer.

Tier-3 baptism scans

Dissen (253403) and Grifte (253444) have no confirmation book for the window; their baptisms are online instead. The core 1834–1835 window is read — no male Carl, and Grifte records no January-1835 birth at all. Remaining: Dissen 1834 + both parishes 1836–1841.

Prose / mixed volumes

Schöneberg's 1847–55 confirmations sit inside a mixed prose Kirchenbuch (220009); better read on-site than scanned by name.

Not digitised — the on-site Tier

Heisebeck (pre-1845), Liebenau (1835), and Schöneberg (pre-1822) are not on Archion — core targets for the archive trip. (The Tier-3 “Deissen” parishes Dissen, Grifte & Gudensberg turned out to be on Archion — now mapped and partly read above.) See Open Questions.
