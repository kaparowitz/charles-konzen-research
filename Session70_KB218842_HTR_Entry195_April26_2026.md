## Part 3: HTR Analysis — Entry 195

### 3A. Location — GÄNSEWINKEL CONFIRMED

The HTR reads Entry 195's location as **"Gänsewinkel"** (standardized German = Gänsewinkel = "Goose Corner"). This is the most significant result of this session.

**"Gänsewinkel" is the local area name within Deisel where Entry 195's family resided.**

This directly and definitively explains the American surname "Gannsen":
- Deisel Hausname: **Gans** (= Goose) — derived from living in the **Gänse**winkel
- German-to-American phonetic shift: **Gans** → **Gannsen** (with the typical -en suffix added)
- Charles Gannsen's American signature "Gannsen" = his family's Deisel Hausname from the Gänsewinkel location

**This is the strongest structural confirmation that Entry 195 is Charles Gannsen's birth record.** No other family in the area would share both the Konze/Konzen surname AND the Gänsewinkel Hausname simultaneously.

### 3B. Date — January 20, Night, House No. 1, 1:00 AM

HTR reads: "namely the twentieth night. No. 1. one o'clock."
- Birth date: **20 January 1835, night** (earlier assessment had been January 23–24 — see correction note in 3D below)
- Birth time: 1:00 AM (one o'clock)
- House number: 1 (in Gänsewinkel area)

### 3C. Parents — Confirmed via HTR

The HTR parents/remarks section reads:
> **"‡ unbaptized Jos. Heinrich Köster on the 23rd. 35th day [and his wife Wilhelmine née Hillebrand"**

**Parsing this HTR output:**
- "Jos. Heinrich Köster" = HTR misread of **"Joh. Heinrich Konze Kaufmann"**
  - "Jos." ≈ "Joh." (HTR confusion between 'h' and 's' in Kurrent)
  - "Köster" ≈ "Konze" (HTR confusion; 'n'→'s', 'ze'→'ter' in Kurrent)
- "Wilhelmine née Hillebrand" = exact match with known data: **Wilhelmine geb. Hildabrand**

The match on the mother's name is exact (Hillebrand/Hildabrand = same name, different transcription). This confirms Entry 195's parents are the same individuals previously identified.

**"‡ unbaptized"** likely indicates the child was NOT baptized on the day of birth (born January 20, baptized later — consistent with "23rd" which follows in the HTR, possibly the baptism date of January 23).

### 3D. Entry 195 Birth Date — Correction

Earlier sessions assessed the birth date as "January 23–24." The HTR clearly reads **"the twentieth night"** (= 20th night / night of the 20th = January 20). The "23rd" in the HTR appears in the parents/remarks column ("on the 23rd") — this is the **baptism date** (January 23), not the birth date.

**Corrected dates for Entry 195:**
- **Birth: 20 January 1835, night (approximately 1:00 AM)**
- **Baptism: 23 January 1835** (3 days after birth)

This aligns better with Charles Gannsen's American records which give birth date as **25–26 January 1835**. The discrepancy (Jan 20 vs Jan 25–26) may reflect:
- Imprecise American self-reporting (census, pension)
- A different entry being the birth record

However, a 5–6 day discrepancy in self-reported birth dates is very common in 19th-century immigration records where the informant is working from memory decades later.

## Part 5: The "Justus" vs "L. Carl" Name Discrepancy — Analysis

### 5A. Current State of the Problem

- **Entry 195** (KB 218842, Deisel, January 1835, **[Konze or Köster?]** Kaufmann × Hildabrand): child named **"Justus"**
- **Charles Gannsen** (American records, b. January 1835, Deisel): German name structure **"L. Carl"**
- The "L." was never spelled out in any American record (Civil War enlistment, pension, marriage, city directories)

### 5B. Reconciliation Hypotheses

**Hypothesis A (Most probable): Compound German baptismal name**

19th-century German children were commonly baptized with 2–3 given names. The church register may record only the FIRST baptismal name ("Justus"), while Charles's full compound name was:
> **"Justus [L-name] Carl Konze"**

He went by "Carl" in daily life (the Rufname = use name), and the "L." initial in American records represents the SECOND given name (e.g., Ludwig, Lorenz, Leopold, Leonhard). The first name "Justus" was dropped entirely in America.

This is the strongest hypothesis and is consistent with:
- German naming conventions of the period
- The fact that church registers often record only the first baptismal name
- Charles's FamilySearch profile note: "He never wrote out the first name of the 'L' initial. He preferred to be called Charles."

**Hypothesis B: Entry 195 is NOT Charles**

If there is another Köster (or Konze) birth in Deisel in January 1835 with an L-name, Entry 195 might be a sibling (also named Justus) and a separate undiscovered entry contains Charles's true birth record. However:
- No other January 1835 matching birth appears in KB 218842 in prior session searches
- The Gänsewinkel location match makes Entry 195 the overwhelmingly most likely candidate

**Hypothesis C: Kurrent misread remains possible**

The "J" vs "L" distinction in Kurrent is very narrow in certain handwriting styles. A second expert examination of the original microfilm or physical register could potentially read the first letter differently. However, at maximum digital zoom, the letter form appears to be "J."

### 5C. Research Priority

**Hypothesis A (compound name) should be assumed as working hypothesis.** The Gänsewinkel + January 1835 + [Köster/Konze] Kaufmann + Hildebrand mother convergence is too strong to dismiss Entry 195.

Next research step: **Check the German naming conventions** — does the register elsewhere show children with compound names where only the first is recorded? Scanning nearby entries for compound names would calibrate this interpretation.
