# Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson (LB5X-RYC) — Life Sketch

> Drafted 12 June 2026 for the FamilySearch Brief Life History field. Grounded in the sources attached
> to the profile and the project page children/friedrich-wilhelm.html. Replaces the earlier short
> history (all of its facts retained: the "George" Find a Grave name, the Old St. Marcus cemetery-to-park history).

---

Friederich Wilhelm Gonnson was born 20 June 1866 in St. Louis, Missouri, the firstborn child of Charles Johnson Sr. — a Hessian-born immigrant and recently discharged Civil War artillery sergeant — and Auguste Kreichelt, born in Clausthal, Hanover. He arrived in the first summer of his parents' marriage, three days after Prussia declared war on his father's homeland of Hesse-Kassel.

He was baptized 2 September 1866 at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church in St. Louis (register pp. 164–165, no. 5201), recorded by Pastor Georg Wendelin Wall — the same pastor who had married his parents that January. His sponsors were Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann, for whom he was named; Wilhelmine Ebeling Leue, a senior German Lutheran neighbor from the family's South 7th Street years; and both maternal grandparents, Georg and Henriette Kreichelt. His name also happens to be that of Hesse-Kassel's elector, Friedrich Wilhelm I, deposed by Prussia that same summer — a suggestive echo of his father's origins, though the documented namesake is the godfather. The clerk wrote the family surname "Gonnson," the same spelling as his parents' marriage record and the first entry in the seven-baptism surname trail that later helped trace the family to Hesse.

Friederich Wilhelm died 26 October 1867 in St. Louis, sixteen months old, and was buried 31 October 1867 in the family plot at Old St. Marcus Cemetery; the Find a Grave index records him as "George Gansen." The cemetery was later converted into a city park, and because the family graves were not under perpetual care they were not relocated to the New St. Marcus Cemetery — the markers have since been removed. He never knew his six younger siblings: the firstborn son whose short life opened the family's American story.
