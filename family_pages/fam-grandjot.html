What this rewrites
  • Peter's "Massachusetts" attribution is fully corroborated — the destination is recorded on the original 1854 NY Customs Passenger List, not just on a later note. Peter wrote in his correspondence "destination Massachusetts," and the actual ship's manifest says exactly that.
  • "Johannes" → "Johan" (German), and Catherine Elisabeth Grandjot was recorded under her middle name "Elisabeth" — that's why Massachusetts vital-record searches under "John Herwig" + "Catherine" returned nothing. The family's US records are likely under Elisabeth Herwig, not Catherine.
  • 4 of 6 living children boarded, matching Peter's prior count. The two unaccounted-for at emigration are: Carl Louis Herwig · PCXT-X3M (b. 1830, eldest surviving — stayed in Germany or emigrated separately, possibly the 1857 cluster) and either Maria Magdalena or Caroline Phillipine (whichever isn't "Agnes" on the manifest).
  • "Sonter Johnny" is a clerk's transcription of an unfamiliar ship name — likely Sönder Jylland ("South Jutland," a Danish-Schleswig packet that ran Bremen → NYC under various names in the 1850s) or a similar Bremen-flagged vessel mis-spelled by an English-speaking customs clerk. Worth checking the original manifest image.
Why the Mass vital-record searches earlier returned zero
  • The earlier "John Herwig" + Mass search missed the family because the head of household was indexed as Johan (German spelling kept) rather than John, and the wife was indexed as Elisabeth not Catherine. A re-search on those exact spellings against the 1855 Mass state census + 1860 federal census is the obvious next move and should now resolve where in Mass they settled.
  • The Brooklyn 1870 Carl Herwig (b. 1829 Hesse) is therefore most likely a different family, not Carl Louis Herwig — though Carl Louis remains a possible 1857 emigrant who landed independently.

Net answer to "where did they go": The 1854 manifest declared Massachusetts as destination, but the family did not actually settle there. The 1855 NY State Census already places eldest daughter Jeanette in NYC Ward 13 — six months after arrival. By 1860 the family was in NYC Ward 7 (Lower East Side) with explicit "Hesse-Kassel" origin recorded. By 1885 they had moved across the Hudson to Jersey City, Hudson County, New Jersey, where they remained at least until 1915 (Jeanette age 81 still in Jersey City). The surname Anglicized from Herwig to Herig within months of arrival, which is why every prior search under the original German spelling missed them.

What this rewrites — partial reversal of the May 2 finding
  • The May 2 conclusion that "the family did not actually settle in Massachusetts" needs a nuance. The parents and the four 1854-manifested children indeed pushed on to NYC within months of arrival. But the eldest surviving son, Carl Louis Herwig, evidently disembarked or stopped in Plymouth, Mass. long enough to marry there 6 Oct 1855 — only ~10 months after the family arrived in NYC. Plymouth was therefore not a fictitious "declared" destination; at least one Herwig actually went there.
  • Carl Louis was not on the Sonter Johnny. The manifest "Carl Herwig 1842" is Carl Peter Herwig (b. 1841, age ~13). Carl Louis must have emigrated separately. Two prior NY arrivals are now plausible candidates for his crossing:
    • Carl Herrig · age 20 · b. abt 1831 · Bremen → NY 23 Sep 1851 · ship Young · place of origin "Germany"
    • Luis Harney [Luis Harweg] · age 22 · b. abt 1830 · Bremen → NY 5 Apr 1852 · ship Ocean · destination written as "St Luis"
    Neither is decisive on its own. The 1851 Carl Herrig age and birth year are off by one but very close; the 1852 Luis Harweg birth year is exact (1830) but the manifest destination "St Luis" is more likely St. Louis MO than Plymouth MA. A pull of the original manifest images is needed to disambiguate.
  • The Hanover-born "Louis Herwig" of 1860 St. Louis is a name-twin, not Carl Louis. The Find a Grave Index records Louis J Herwig (b. 1829 Hanover, d. 26 Aug 1870, Bethany Section, St. Peter's Cemetery, Pagedale, St. Louis Co. MO; memorial 87945136). His 1860 St. Louis Ward 9 census household (Louis 30 confectioner, wife Virginia 28, daughter Caroline, infant son Francis) is from Hanover, not Hesse-Kassel — and is unrelated to the Trendelburg Herwigs.

Per 3 x emmigration 1857, she may be one of the unaccounted-for 1857 emigrants.
Updated next steps
  1. ★ Pull the 6 Oct 1855 Plymouth marriage record image directly from collection 2495 record 14571973 (or the Plymouth Town Clerk's marriage register vol. for 1855) — should give: groom's age in days/months, occupation, town of last residence, and bride's birthplace. The original may also list witnesses or officiating minister, opening doors to Plymouth-area network research.
  2. Search 1860 / 1865 / 1870 Massachusetts state & federal censuses for "Lewis Harwig" or "Lewes Herweg" + "Catherine" in Plymouth and surrounding Plymouth County towns (Carver, Kingston, Duxbury, Marshfield). Also expand to 1855 Mass. State Census which we now know they were in 7 weeks after arrival (the 1855 census date was 1 June 1855 — they would have been counted).
  3. Search Plymouth-area death/cemetery records for Lewis/Louis/Lewes Herweg/Harwig/Herwig — the FS profile records him as "Deceased" without a date, and a Plymouth burial would close the loop. Try Find a Grave Plymouth County, Massachusetts deaths 1856–1915.
  4. Identify Christian Sharda (Catherine Sharda's father) in Plymouth records — his presence in Plymouth pre-1855 may explain why Carl Louis stopped there. The surname "Sharda" is uncommon and could itself be an Anglicization (German Schardau, Schardt) — checking 1850 Plymouth federal census for a Sharda/Schardt household would establish the bride's family.
  5. Pull the original 1851 "Carl Herrig" and 1852 "Luis Harney" passenger manifest images — the indexed birth-place "Germany" or "Citizens" may be more specific on the original (often the page header lists a province or town of last residence), and the destination column on the 1852 record should clarify whether "St Luis" was actually St. Louis MO or a misreading.
  6. Re-search Johannes Grandjot under additional Anglicizations — try "John Grant," "John Grandt," "Hannes Grandjot," and the wife's first name as a search axis once we identify her (the FS profile lists no spouse for Johannes Grandjot · PCXP-H6X yet; Peter's chart could supply her name for a targeted lookup).

Net of the May 3 missing-emigrants pass: Carl Louis Herwig (PCXT-X3M) is now documented in the United States — 6 Oct 1855 marriage to Catherine Sharda in Plymouth, Massachusetts, with his father correctly identified as Johannes ("John") Herwig. This is the first concrete US trace of Carl Louis after his 1830 Trendelburg baptism, and it partially restores Peter's "Massachusetts" attribution: the parents' declared destination was real for at least one son. Brother Johannes Grandjot's 1855 arrival and sister Caroline Phillipine's whereabouts remain undocumented in Ancestry's indexed collections; both probably require Bremen-side passenger-list research or German-American newspaper / church-record databases not yet checked.
