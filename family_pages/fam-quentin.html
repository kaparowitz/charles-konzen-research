Final verdict — Quentins eliminated as Charles candidates on four independent grounds

After ten passes of US-side research (30 April – 1 May 2026), the Quentin file is closed for the Charles Gannsen identification project. The Friedrichsfeld 1853 Quentin emigrants are now richly documented in Cincinnati, but none of them is Charles. The grounds:

  1. Profession mismatch. LAGIS records Georg Karl Quentin as Bäcker / Konditor. Pass 7 nailed him to the 1855-56 Cincinnati directory entry "Quentin Geo. bakery, 345 W. Row." Pass 10 added Hermann Quentin's 1855 Wine-Street bakery lease and 1859-63 mortgage portfolio. Charles Gannsen was not a baker in any record.
  2. Geographic mismatch. Every Friedrichsfeld 1853 Quentin trail ends in Cincinnati (Georg, Auguste, Hermann's lending business through 1863). Adolph Quentin from Eschwege went to Milwaukee. Sophie Auguste, Elise Auguste, Friedrich the Sontra mayor, and Carl Quentin the Bückeburg-Milwaukee senator stayed in Germany or went to Wisconsin. Charles was in St. Louis from at least Dec 1862 onward. No Quentin trail crosses through St. Louis at the right time.
  3. Surname implausibility. "Quentin" → "Gannsen" / "Konsen" is not a plausible phonetic Anglicization in either direction. The Q-cluster does not become G-cluster, and "-tin" does not become "-sen." Peter's phonetic constraint (Charles answered an English-speaking clerk in German) does not bridge these.
  4. Family-network test. If Georg Karl Quentin had become Charles Gannsen by relocating to St. Louis post-1855, we would expect some documented intersection — Hermann's lending portfolio, Eliza's later trail, Adolph in Milwaukee, the Stammtafel. There is none. Charles's St. Louis life shows zero Cincinnati / baker / Quentin connections.

For Charles's identity: file closed. For Peter's broader Quentin family reconstruction: the Pass 1-10 documentation below records what we found about the four Friedrichsfeld 1853 emigrants in Cincinnati — useful for his stammtafel, not for Charles. The full follow-up note is at Quentin_FollowUp_May1_2026.html; the manual-browser action sheet for any further Peter-side work is at Quentin_Action_Sheet_May1_2026.html.

Carl Quentin's stated purpose (Vorwort, p. 1)

"Ein Beamter, der plötzlich aus ſeinem Berufe geriſſen und genöthigt wird, für ſich ſelbſt zu ſorgen … So erging es auch mir. Was ich ſah, erlebte und lernte in dem freien Lande jenſeits des Meeres, glaubte ich nicht mir allein erworben, glaubte ich aufzeichnen zu müſſen für meine Landsleute im fernen, theuern Vaterlande."

"An official, suddenly torn from his profession and forced to care for himself … so it happened to me. What I saw, experienced, and learned in the free land across the sea, I did not believe I had acquired for myself alone — I believed I had to record it for my countrymen in the distant, dear Fatherland."

The book is explicitly addressed to "meine Landsleute" — German-speaking readers generally. No region-specific framing. A Friedrichsfeld emigrant reading it would have gotten the same recommendations as a Bavarian, Saxon, or Lippe-born one.

Carl Quentin on Germans in Wisconsin (excerpt from "die Deutſchen in Wisconſin")

"Sehr erfreulich iſt die Wahrnehmung, daß die Deutſchen auch in dieſem Geſchäftsbetriebe im Allgemeinen den Ruf der größeren Ehrlichkeit ſich erworben haben, daher ſie in New-York, Philadelphia, New-Orleans … in der Regel leichter und für größere Beträge Credit ſinden, als manche der eingebornen Amerikaner."

"It is very pleasing to observe that the Germans in this business sector have generally earned a reputation for greater honesty, and therefore find in New York, Philadelphia, New Orleans … more easily, and for larger sums, credit than many native-born Americans."

Exactly the kind of explicit positive-recommendation passage that would have encouraged a 1853 Hesse-Cassel emigrant household — especially one with a working-class trade like baker / confectioner — to choose Milwaukee or another Wisconsin city.

Pass 5 verdict

No direct kinship or named connection between Carl Quentin and the four 1853 Friedrichsfeld emigrants exists in the book. The 1851 publication date and explicit address to "meine Landsleute" mean the book was in circulation in Hesse-Cassel reading-rooms by 1851/1852 — exactly when the 1853 Friedrichsfeld emigrants were planning their departure.
