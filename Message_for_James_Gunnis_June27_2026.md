Hi Jim,

I ran the Dassen/Fulda lead all the way to the end — thank you for it, it was a good one to chase.

**The entry is real, and you read it right.** On Archion (Fulda Stadt ev. Taufen 1830–1863,
Bild 852, Seite 64, entry 298): *Karl Friedrich*, legitimate first son, born the 26th (Feb)
1838 on the Petersgasse, baptized 3 March 1838. Father **Michael Gastmann**, Feldwebel in the
1st Company of the 2nd Infantry Regiment; mother Anna Catharina. The surname's initial is
clearly a **G** — "Gastmann," not the "Hartmann" the machine text guessed. Nice catch.

I don't think he's our Charles, though, for reasons that stack up:
- Surname **Gastmann** isn't in the Konze/Gansen (*n·s·n) family I keep landing on.
- It's **Kreis Fulda**, not the "Kreis Cassel" Charles gave his pastor in 1866.
- Born **1838**, a year off the best U.S. record (26 Jan 1835).
- And the timing rules him out anyway: Charles was already here by Dec 1862 (Battery B) and
  married in St. Louis in 1866.

I kept going to be thorough. Michael was a career Feldwebel, so I traced the unit — the
Kurhessen **2nd Infantry Regiment** ("Landgraf Wilhelm" from 1845), which rotated through the
garrison towns, with the 1st Company in Fulda in 1838. A soldier's family moved on with the
regiment, which fits the Fulda confirmations — I read the 1852 and 1853 classes and there's
**no Gastmann**. FamilySearch and Ancestry show no marriage or death for him either. The one
near-name hit is a **"Carl Gestmann," farmer, b. ~1837 "Hessen," who landed in New York
(Brooklyn) on the *Atalanta*, 18 May 1867** — but the manifest gives only "Hessen" (no town),
he's travelling alone with no kin, and nothing ties him to Fulda, so I'd call that unproven.

I've written it all up as a Fulda research log (images + permalinks) here if you want it:
https://charles-research.netlify.app/germany/fulda-research-log.html

**Where I'm taking it next, on Charles himself:** since he was likely a young man dodging the
draft, I'm leaning on the Kurhessen **military/conscription records**. I've a request in with
the Staatsarchiv Marburg, and just worked out the right fonds: the *local* recruitment rolls
(Best. 331) don't survive for Kreis Hofgeismar in the 1850s, so the real target is **Best. 12,
the Kurhessisches Kriegsministerium (1813–1867)** — the army's own rolls — plus the Kreisamt
Hofgeismar levy files (Best. 180). If anything in your experience suggests a better angle on
Kurhessen conscription/desertion records, I'd love your thoughts.

Really appreciate you stretching the place-name like this — exactly the kind of lateral
thinking that'll crack it. Keep them coming.
