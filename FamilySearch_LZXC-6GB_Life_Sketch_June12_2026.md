Charles Johnson Sr. was born 26 January 1835 in the Electorate of Hesse (Kurhessen), Germany — a date he gave consistently throughout his life and which is preserved in the St. Marcus burial register. His exact birthplace and original name are the subject of ongoing research. His 1866 marriage record names his home village as "Deisse," best read as Deisel near Trendelburg in Kreis Hofgeismar; his Civil War enlistment says "Freundburg," likely Trendelburg itself. No German baptism record has yet been found. The working conclusion is that he was born into a Konzen (Konze/Kontze) family of that area: his son's 1875 St. Marcus baptism was recorded as "Erwin Carl Konzen," and his daughter Auguste's 1890 death record reads "geb. Konzen." He signed himself "L. Charles" but never wrote out the first name — perhaps Ludwig — and the initial remains a clue for locating his German records. See the notes on the Collaborate tab for the full origin analysis.

About 1859, at around twenty-four, he emigrated to America (per the 1900 census; no passenger record has been found), most likely sailing from Bremen to New Orleans and continuing by steamboat up the Mississippi to St. Louis, where he would spend the rest of his life.

On 2 December 1862 he enlisted at St. Louis as "L. Charles Gannsen" in Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery, under Captain John J. Sutter — the same battery in which his future brother-in-law August Kreichelt served. Over three years' service he rose from private to corporal to sergeant (he was reduced to private in March 1865) and was discharged 20 December 1865. The war marked him for life: a hernia suffered at Jefferson City in 1864, hearing loss from the guns, and rheumatism from severe exposure at Rolla left him unable to do heavy manual labor. He first applied for a pension in January 1890 and was granted $12 a month, his name appearing among the approved pensioners in the St. Louis Post-Dispatch of 4 June 1893.

Eighteen days after his discharge, on 7 January 1866, he married Auguste Kreichelt at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church, the register recording him as "Carl Gonnson, aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen." He became a U.S. citizen on 21 October 1868 in St. Louis (the record misindexed as "Gausen"). St. Marcus remained the family's church: all seven children were baptized there, the clerks spelling the surname a different way almost every visit — Gonnson, Gonson, Jansen, Konsen, Konzen — phonetic captures of his Hessian pronunciation that eventually settled, in public records, into "Johnson." Those very spellings later became the key evidence trail to the family's German origins.

His children were Friederich Wilhelm (1866–1867), Maria Josephine (1868–1890), Katherine Carolina (1870–1949), Auguste (1872–1890), Louise (1873–1947), Erwin Carl — known as Charles Johnson Jr. (1875–1950) — and Bertha (1880–1959). He knew heavy losses: his firstborn died in infancy, two grown daughters died in 1890, and his wife Auguste died in December 1896. He supported the family as a cotton-press worker (1880 census) and from 1887 to 1897 as a watchman, his homes traced through the pension file and city directories from South 7th Street to the DeKalb Street years and finally to Sidney Street, where he lived next door to his son Charles Jr., his close companion to the end.

Charles died of stomach cancer on 16 December 1903 at his Sidney Street home, aged 68, and was buried 19 December in the family plot at Old St. Marcus Cemetery beside Auguste. The cemetery grounds were later converted to a city park and the grave markers lost. Through his four surviving children — and through the surname trail he unknowingly left across the records of St. Louis — his story continues to be recovered.
