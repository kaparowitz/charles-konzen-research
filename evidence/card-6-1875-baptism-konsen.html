Card 6 · Konsen attestation · linguistic time capsule

1875 St. Marcus baptism · Erwin Carl Konsen

27 August 1875 · Pastor Julius Hoffmann

The strongest direct surname link between Charles in St. Louis and the Konze line in Hesse: Pastor Hoffmann recorded his sixth child as Erwin Carl Konsen, preserving the German -en form. The 1890 daughter’s death record reads Konsen a second time, fifteen years later — establishing two-record persistence by two different scribes.

Pastor Julius Hoffmann, Braschler’s successor at St. Marcus from May 1875, recorded Charles’s sixth child’s baptism on 27 August 1875: “Erwin Carl Konsen · Geburt: 27. Aug. 1875 · Taufe: 3. Oct. 1875 · Eltern: Carl Konsen u. d. ihm Ehefrau Auguste geb. Kreichelt · Pathen: Erwin Sproul, der Vater des Kindes.”

Hoffmann recorded the surname here as KONSEN — preserving the German-Hessian form 16 years after Charles emigrated. The closest German precedent is Kontzen / Contzen: only the soft t in the tz cluster collapses to s; the terminal -en is identical. Near-direct match.

The Hessian -en form recurs. Fifteen years later, on the 1890 death record of Erwin Carl’s sister Auguste, the maiden-name attestation reads “Auguste Somers geb Konzen” — the full Hessian z-form, by a different scribe (reading confirmed in the 17 May 2026 re-examination). Two-record persistence by two different scribes — Konsen 1875 with an s, Konzen 1890 with a z — is strong evidence that the German-Hessian -en form was Charles’s own self-identification; the family kept the Hessian K-initial / -en form in continuous use across at least 15 years.

Erwin Carl went by “Charles Johnson Jr.” in adulthood, but the baptism preserves both his original given name and the parental surname in its German form — a linguistic time-capsule of the form Charles brought to America in 1859.
