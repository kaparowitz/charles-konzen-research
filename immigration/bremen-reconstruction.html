Every surviving reconstruction (ISTG U.S.-side transcriptions, Zimmerman & Wolfert print volume, Die MAUS / public-juling.de, Deutsche Auswanderer-Datenbank, the 2026 Zenodo CHUP dataset) and every direct U.S.-arrival manifest series has been queried for Charles. The strongest near-miss by demographic-and-origin alignment is Carl Klippert m23 druggist Hessen-Cassel → New Jersey on Bark Agen and Heinrich 15 Dec 1856 (3-of-4 demographic criteria match Charles, but Klippert -ert fails the suffix filter and married status eliminates). The geographically closest finding is the Schlitzberger family from Trentelburg → Baltimore on Anna 12 Jun 1854 — Trentelburg = Trendelburg, Charles's actual ancestral village area.

Forward research vector is off the bremen page: (a) Antwerp / Le Havre / Rotterdam departure indexes 1853–1862; (b) alternative-given-name + alternative-surname compound hypothesis (Charles sailing as Friedrich/Heinrich/Wilhelm/Johannes under a surname distortion outside the 10 tracked variants); (c) German-side parish reconstruction (Hessen-Kassel parish records for the Trendelburg-area villages — Deisel, Hofgeismar, Helmarshausen — to identify whether any Konze descendant born ~1830–1835 actually emigrated to North America at all in this window). ★ Methodological refinement (Jed, 7 May 2026): the K/C/G + -en/-on/-in filter

The ten surname variants the project tracks for Charles — Konze · Konzen · Konsen · Kontze · Gannsen · Gonnson · Gansen · Gonsen · Gunnsen · Hansen — all share an -en, -on, or -in suffix. This terminal -en/-on/-in is the structural feature most likely to survive scribal distortion across U.S. clerks: the project's own historical record of Charles's surname through U.S. censuses, military records, and city directories preserves this ending in every variant tracked.

The image-inspection methodology scans specifically for surnames matching the suffix filter in three initial-letter clusters: K-cluster, C-cluster, and G-cluster. The C-cluster is included because German "K" is routinely rendered as "C" by Anglophone clerks — "Konze" can plausibly surface on a U.S. manifest as "Conze" / "Conzen" / "Cansen" / "Contze" / "Connsen" depending on the clerk. Even in cases where the Bremen captain wrote "K" in German on the manifest header, U.S. customs collectors occasionally re-rendered K-initial passenger surnames as C-initial when transcribing or filing. Applying the filter to all three clusters rather than just K and G is therefore the more conservative pass — it adds essentially no false positives (C-initial surnames are uncommon on a typical Bremen passenger list) while protecting against the most plausible single-letter clerk distortion of Charles's surname at the point of arrival.

The K/C/G + -en/-on/-in filter eliminates the bulk of the K- and C-cluster present on each manifest (Klee, Kutt, Krüger, Kaufmann, Klosa, Koppel, Krauss, Klattich, Keller, Buschke, Büchner, Kirsching, Krunkel, Kemtner, Krug, Kretzer, Kampe, Koch, Kind, Krug, Ceras, etc., none of which end in -en/-on/-in), and correctly surfaces the closest near-misses (Knemken on Elise & Mathilde, Konker, Kayman, Kilian, Kolveie on Athena) for closer handwriting verification. None of those near-misses survives Kurrent-level inspection as a Konze candidate.

The prior 9 ISTG-ship inspections (which used the K/G-only filter) were re-validated at the index level on 7 May 2026 evening — see the C-cluster index-level re-pass log entry below. Aggregate result: 23 C-cluster surnames found across ~2,200 passengers, none ending in -en/-on/-in. Two single-male 1835/1837-born C-cluster passengers (Coock on Orpheus 9 Jan 1860, Cohn on Bark Nord Amerika 14 Sep 1858) are demographic near-misses for Charles but their surnames fail the suffix filter. The methodology gap is now closed. Going forward, the full K/C/G + -en/-on/-in filter applies to all new image inspections.
