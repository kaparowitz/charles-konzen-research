Charles Research / Immigration Journey
Chapter Two · The Atlantic Crossing

The Crossing

Per the 1900 census, Charles immigrated in 1859. Per the 1866 marriage record, he was already in St. Louis "since seven years." The trans-Atlantic crossing itself remains undocumented — the manifest his name lives on still unfound.

Year of immigrationca. 1859
Likely routeBremen → New York → St. Louis
StatusManifest unrecovered

This is the section landing for the immigration investigation. The full narrative — adjacent emigrations, surname-variant searches, Bremen-side reconstructions — is on the home-page Immigration Journey tab; this page surfaces the deep-dives and the still-missing manifest.

★ Open · 1859 manifest unrecovered

The crossing is documented at both ends but not in the middle

Both the U.S. census and Charles's own marriage register give 1859 as the arrival year. He was 24, almost certainly travelling alone, almost certainly without an Auswanderer-Beleg. NYC, New Orleans, and Baltimore arrivals 1858–1860 have been searched against the full surname pool — the five Hesse-attested forms (Konze, Kontze, Kontzen, Contze, Konzen) plus the nine American renderings later recorded for the family in St. Louis (Gonnson, Gansen, Gonsen, Konsen, Gonson, Jansen, Konzen, Gannsen, Conson) plus the related American mishearings (Jansen, Johnson) — without surfacing a candidate. The Bremen-side originals are largely lost (1875 fire); the Hamburg-side searches return nothing.
