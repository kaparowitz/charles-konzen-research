Frederick Wilhelm Pillmann

Baptism sponsor at TWO Gannsen baptisms (1866 firstborn & 1870 Katherine Carolina) · husband of Fredericka Albrecht (Catharina's sister) · St. Louis butcher · German-born immigrant
SPONSOR · 1866 + 1870 Brother-in-law to Carl Kreichelt Born Germany · 1846 FamilySearch · L17N-8Q6

★ At a Glance

✓ ID Confirmed
SPONSOR PROFILE · IDENTIFIED FS L17N-8Q6

A St. Louis butcher and German immigrant who sponsored two of Charles and Auguste's children at baptism, and later married into the Albrecht family.

Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann (b. 1846 Germany) was a St. Louis butcher and the only person to sponsor TWO Gannsen baptisms across the seven-baptism series — the 1866 firstborn (where he was unmarried, age 20) and the 1870 Katherine Carolina baptism (where he had just married into the Albrecht family seven weeks earlier). His marriage to Fredericka Albrecht (5 April 1870) — sister to Catharina Albrecht who married Carl Kreichelt nine months later — made him brother-in-law to Carl and tied the Pillmann-Albrecht-Kreichelt families into a single marriage chain.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: The 1866 sponsorship is exceptional: at age 20, unmarried and not yet kin to Auguste, Pillmann was clearly already part of the wider Albrecht / Kreichelt social circle — meaning the Charles & Auguste social world in 1865-1866 already included the man who would later marry Catharina's sister. The first child Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson shares both given names with Pillmann — a suggestive parallel, though "Friedrich Wilhelm" was a very common German given-name pairing of the period, so a direct naming-after cannot be asserted without further documentation.

Quick facts & surname correction

★ Surname correction · "Billmann" → Pillmann

Until May 2026 the 1866 and 1870 Gannsen baptism sponsor lists rendered his surname as "Billmann" on this site (and on the corresponding children page). FamilySearch tree person L17N-8Q6 establishes the correct surname as Pillmann. The St. Marcus parish clerk's "B"/"P" is a known German-script ambiguity (Bavarian-style cursive lowercase initial B and P are nearly identical). The corrected reading is now retroactively applied to all sponsor lists on this site.

Connection to Charles Gannsen & Auguste Kreichelt

★ Sponsor at TWO Gannsen baptisms — uniquely so

Friedrich Pillmann is the only person other than the Kreichelts themselves who appears as sponsor at more than one Gannsen baptism. He stood at:

  • 1866 · Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson (the firstborn, who died as an infant) — at this time he was unmarried and unconnected by formal kinship. His role here was likely "family friend of the Kreichelts" or possibly already a cousin / in-law via the Albrecht family on a path that has not yet been documented.
  • 1870 · Friedrike Catharina Caroline Amalia Gansen (Katherine Carolina) — this time he was formally kin: he had married Fredericka Albrecht on 5 April 1870, just seven weeks earlier, making him brother-in-law-to-be to Carl Kreichelt (Auguste's brother). The Pillmann ⟷ Albrecht ⟷ Kreichelt marriage chain was just then knitting into existence.

The 1866 baptism is the more interesting of the two. At that point neither he nor anyone else in the Albrecht family had married into the Kreichelts. The fact that Friedrich was nonetheless invited to stand at the firstborn's baptism is the strongest piece of evidence that the Kreichelts and the Pillmanns / Albrechts were already a single tightly-bonded social network in 1866, even before the formal marriages of 1870 and 1871 created kinship ties on paper.

The Kassel illegal-emigration "Pillmann" lead — Peter Schräder's Hessian database

Per Peter Schräder's database of Hessian emigrants (December 2025 correspondence), there is exactly one Billmann/Pillmann recorded as having emigrated illegally from Kassel to avoid serving in the Kurfürst's army during the mid-19th century. Whether that record refers specifically to this Friedrich Pillmann's family — i.e. to William Pillmann, Friedrich's father — is not yet established, but the timing is consistent: Friedrich was born 1846 in Germany and the family arrived in St. Louis by 1847 or 1849, the family-of-origin emigration window roughly aligning with the Hessian-emigrant database.

Why this matters for Charles. Jed's working hypothesis (18 December 2025) is that Charles Gannsen himself emigrated illegally from Kurhessen for the same reason — to avoid conscription into the Kurfürst's army. If the same illegal-emigration pattern shows up in the Pillmann family that the Gannsens chose to be deeply connected with sacramentally, this is circumstantial evidence that Charles's social-circle preference in St. Louis ran toward fellow illegal-emigrant Hessians. The Pillmann lead remains active as of May 2026.

📂 Full research log — Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann marriage chain, broader-investigation significance

The Kreichelt ⟷ Albrecht ⟷ Pillmann marriage chain

Friedrich Pillmann is one node of a three-marriage chain that ties the Albrecht, Pillmann, and Kreichelt families into a single St. Louis Hannoverian-American kin network in 1870–1872:

5 April 1870Friedrich Pillmann × Fredericka Albrecht · St. Louis, Missouri
22 May 1870Katherine Carolina Gannsen baptized at St. Marcus, sponsors all from the Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann-Mincke network
5 March 1871Carl Kreichelt × Katherine "Catharina" Albrecht · St. Louis, Missouri — making Friedrich brother-in-law to Carl

After his 1870 marriage Friedrich and Fredericka had seven children (1871–1885+) and remained settled in St. Louis as butchers and tradespeople through the 1910 census. He outlived Fredericka by two years, dying at 71 in late 1917.

Significance to the broader investigation

  1. Surname correction is now documentary. The 1866 and 1870 St. Marcus parish clerk wrote what looks like "Billmann" — but the FamilySearch tree person, attached marriage records, attached census records, and his death certificate all use Pillmann. Both children pages have been updated. This is one of several "B/P" corrections needed in mid-19th-c. St. Marcus baptism records and is unrelated to the surname-drift on Charles's own surname (Gonnson / Konsen / Jansen).
  2. Two-baptism sponsorship is unique. Only Friedrich Pillmann, among the dozens of named sponsors across the seven Gannsen baptisms, appears at more than one of them. This signals a closer-than-usual social bond — closer even than the Kreichelt brothers, who each appeared at only one baptism each.
  3. The Hessian illegal-emigration parallel is the live thread. Friedrich is the only sponsor whose family's German origin shows the same "illegal emigration to avoid Kurfürst's conscription" pattern that Jed believes underlies Charles's own concealment of birthplace and surname. This makes Friedrich's own German-side family history the most actionable next research lead among the sponsors.
