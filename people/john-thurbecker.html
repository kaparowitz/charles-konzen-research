John P. Thurbecker

Another co-witness on Charles's 1891 Declaration for Invalid Pension — likely a Westphalian/Lower-Saxon St. Louis German neighbor with about a 20-year acquaintance with Charles.

John P. Thurbecker (b. likely 1830s-1840s Germany — Westphalia / Lower Saxony per Münster–Osnabrück surname cluster · likely son of Lewis Thurbecker enumerated 1860 St. Louis County) co-witnessed Charles's signature on the 9 February 1891 Declaration for Invalid Pension alongside John Hebbeler. The surname spelling is uncertain — the pension-file clerical hand could read "Thurbecker," "Thurbocker," or "Thurbocher," and the surname clusters with the German Durrbeck / Durbeck variants common across St. Louis records. Per the pension viewer's editorial note, his acquaintance with Charles ran approximately 20 years.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Thurbecker is the second of Charles's two formal-Declaration witnesses (the other being John Hebbeler). Their addresses on neighboring north-side blocks (638 + 916) suggest a tight-knit German-immigrant community where Charles was a known and identifiable resident. The 1860 Lewis Thurbecker household at St. Louis County is the only Missouri Thurbecker entry surfaced so far in indexed records — placing the family in St. Louis a year before the war and consistent with the Westphalian emigration pattern that also produced the Hebbeler, Niemeier, and Adam Herweck branches of Charles's circle.

Why he matters

Thurbecker, as co-witness with John Hebbeler, helps establish Charles's identity for the formal 9 February 1891 Declaration. The two witnesses' addresses (638 and 916 on neighboring north-side blocks) suggest a tight-knit German-immigrant community where Charles was a known resident.

★ Independent record verification (3 May 2026)

1860 Missouri State Census, St. Louis County — Thurbecker family enumerated

FS Full-Text Search located a Thurbecker entry in the 1858-1860 Missouri State Census of St. Louis County (E.F. Dietz, Printer, taken in the year of our Lord 1860):

  • Lewis Thurbecker · head of household, enumerated alongside neighbors Frederick Cristian, Joseph Schriner, Hugh McNutter, Peter Sheck, etc.
  • Household column counts indicate 4-6 family members
  • Census Image 332 of 448

Lewis Thurbecker is most likely John P. Thurbecker's father — a generation older, established in St. Louis County by 1860, and head of an immigrant German household. John P. would have been a young adult or teen in 1860, growing up in this household, and inherited the family's St. Louis civic standing by 1891.

The 1860 census places the Thurbecker family at St. Louis County in 1860, on the same page as Frederick Cristian and Joseph Schriner — a German-Lutheran neighborhood cluster in the early-immigrant phase.

Surname disambiguation

The surname "Thurbecker" presents transcription challenges. The cluster of similar entries returned across FS searches includes:

  • Thurbecker (Lewis, 1860 St. Louis Co.) — the only confirmed Missouri match
  • Durrbeck (John R. Durrbeck, multiple WWI service records, St. Louis 1916-1939) — possibly later-generation kin
  • Deterbeck (John Deterbeck, 1883 St. Louis directory)
  • Aterbecker (John Aterbecker, Missouri marriage record, married Celene Welenke)

The German clerical hand commonly shifts Th- → D- in American transcription (cf. Theodor → Deodor), making Durrbeck/Thurbecker likely the same underlying surname. The 1891 pension affidavit's "Thurbecker" reading is preserved here as the canonical spelling.

Probable region of origin

The "Thurbecker" / "Durrbeck" / "Durbeck" surname cluster is associated with Westphalia and Lower Saxony — particularly the Münster / Osnabrück region, where compound -beck (German for "stream/brook") surnames are common. The 1860 St. Louis enumeration alongside other Westphalian-style names supports this placement.
