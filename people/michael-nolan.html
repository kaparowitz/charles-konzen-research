Michael Nolan

1st Mo. Light Artillery veteran · Co-worker · Pension witness · Knew Charles since 1867
1ST MO LIGHT ARTILLERY · CO. F · APRIL 1861-JUNE 1864 Pension witness · October 1891 "Worked with him"

★ At a Glance

✓ ID Confirmed
CO-WORKER WITNESS · 1ST MO LIGHT ARTILLERY VETERAN

An Irish-born co-worker of Charles — the only pension witness whose testimony explicitly states that he "worked with" Charles.

Michael Nolan (b. ~1841-1845 — likely Ireland · wife Ellen Nolan · FS K8SY-TZB) is the only one of Charles's civilian-witness affidavits whose testimony explicitly says he "worked with" Charles. His General Affidavit attests acquaintance since 1867 and confirms Charles "lost considerable time" on the job from rheumatism — a key element of the pension-disability claim. ★ Major May 2026 finding: the 1890 Census of Union Veterans and Widows of the Civil War records Nolan as Pvt., Co. F, 1st Reg't Missouri Light Artillery — a separate regiment from Charles's 2nd MO Light Artillery, but a near-sister organization raised at the same Jefferson Barracks in April 1861, within days of Lincoln's call for 75,000 volunteers.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Nolan is the project's first documented Irish-Catholic in Charles's circle — a counter-balance to the otherwise heavily German-Lutheran composition of the pension witnesses. His role as a workplace co-worker (rather than a comrade-in-arms or a parish neighbor) shows that Charles's social network reached beyond the St. Marcus / Battery B / DeKalb-Street German Lutheran sphere into the broader St. Louis labor force. His own Civil War service in the 1st MO Light Artillery means four of the pension file's witnesses (Derr, Meyer, Nolan, and Stoll — the last in Battery L of the 2nd MO LA) were artillery veterans of the same Jefferson Barracks regimental cluster — far from coincidence.

What he swore to

General Affidavit · October 18[?]

That affiant has been well acquainted with Charles Gannson since 1867, and that he was often that time complaining of rheumatism, which he has continued to suffer of[f] and on ever since, and has lost considerable time on account of same. Affiant also knows that the said Gannson has been during the whole time suffering from a rupture. [He] further says that said Gannson's hearing was somewhat impaired at the time of first acquaintance & has grown worse year by year, so when speaking to him, [one] would have to speak quite loud, the same being worse in damp or [chilly weather]. Affiant makes this statement from personal knowledge, which was derived from working with him as he/intimate acquaintance.

Source: Pension File Page 18 (verso jurat Page 19). Notary: Fred[?] [W?] Fritz Jr. Filed by Fred. W. Fout, U.S. Claim Agent.

Why he matters

Nolan is the only one of the civilian-witness affidavits whose testimony explicitly says he "worked with" Charles. This makes him a uniquely valuable workplace observer — confirming that Charles's rheumatism caused him to "lose considerable time" on the job (a key element of the 1890 pension act's "unable to earn a support by manual labor" standard).

The 1867 acquaintance date corresponds to Charles's railroad-conductor period (per the 1871 Edwards' St. Louis Directory: "Johnson, Charles, conductor, St. Louis R.R., r. 718 Gate"). Nolan may have been a fellow railroad worker.

★ Newly identified (3 May 2026): Nolan was himself a Civil War veteran of the same arm of service as Charles. The 1890 Census of Union Veterans records him as Private, Company F, 1st Regiment Missouri Light Artillery, enlisted April 1861 (one of the very earliest Missouri Union enlistments) and discharged 13 June 1864 after a full 3-year term. This recasts his role in Charles's pension narrative: Nolan is not merely a workplace witness but a fellow Missouri Light Artillery veteran giving civilian-period testimony for a regimental neighbor.

★ Independent record verification (3 May 2026)

1890 Census of Union Veterans and Widows of the Civil War — St. Louis, Missouri

FS index entry: Michael Nolan, 1890 Veterans Census, St. Louis (K8SY-TZB). The original schedule (image 140 of 1,128 in the St. Louis / St. Charles / Jefferson 1890 Veterans roll) records:

  • Name: Nolan, Michael (line 22, household 52/77)
  • Rank: Private
  • Company: F
  • Regiment: 1 Mo. L.A. (1st Missouri Light Artillery)
  • Date of enlistment: Apr 1861
  • Date of discharge: 13 Jun 1864
  • Length of service: 3 years
  • Spouse: Ellen Nolan
  • Place of residence: St. Louis, Missouri

April 1861 — significance of the enlistment date

Nolan enlisted in April 1861 — within days of the firing on Fort Sumter (12-14 April 1861) and Lincoln's call for 75,000 volunteers (15 April 1861). The 1st Missouri Light Artillery was raised at Jefferson Barracks/St. Louis primarily by Capt. Frank P. Blair Jr. and Capt. Nathaniel Lyon's Home Guard organization in the contested early weeks of the war when Missouri's loyalty was actively in question. Nolan was thus part of the founding Missouri Union artillery cohort. Charles, by contrast, enlisted in 2nd MO Light Artillery in 1862 — a later regiment raised after the loyalist position in Missouri had been militarily secured.

Pension Index — multiple Michael Nolans

The General Index to Pension Files (1861-1934) returned 13 Michael Nolan entries linked to Missouri, with filings spanning 1866 → 1907. Without access to the full pension cards, the specific entry for Charles's witness cannot be uniquely identified yet. The earliest Missouri filings (1866, 1872, 1879) are most consistent with our 1st MO L.A. veteran filing his own pension; the 1902-1907 widow's pensions are consistent with Ellen Nolan filing after Michael's death.

Probable origin — Ireland (or Irish-American)

"Michael Nolan" with wife "Ellen" is a near-paradigmatic Irish Catholic name pair. Nolan is a County Carlow / Kilkenny / Wexford surname; St. Louis received tens of thousands of Irish Famine-era and post-Famine immigrants, particularly in the Kerry Patch and waterfront wards. The April 1861 enlistment fits the well-documented pattern of unmarried young Irish immigrants enlisting at the war's outbreak. Note: the Civil War-era Missouri Light Artillery regiments were ethnically mixed in a way the volunteer infantry regiments were not — Nolan's Irish-Catholic background, sitting alongside Charles Gannsen's Hessian-Lutheran background, is itself characteristic of Light Artillery regimental composition.
