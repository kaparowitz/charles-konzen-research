Katherine "Catharina" Albrecht

Baptism sponsor for Katherine Carolina (1870) · fiancée & later wife of Carl Kreichelt (Auguste's brother) · St. Louis-born daughter of Martin Albrecht and Catharine Wilhelmine Lieber

Charles's sister-in-law by marriage — the fiancée (and later wife) of Carl Kreichelt when she sponsored the 1870 Katherine Carolina baptism.

Researcher view (sources, citations, bottom-line)

Katherine "Catharina" Albrecht (St. Louis-born) was the fiancée — and ten months later wife — of Carl Kreichelt (Auguste's brother) at the time she sponsored the 1870 Katherine Carolina baptism. Daughter of Martin Albrecht and Catharine Wilhelmine Lieber. The Albrecht-Kreichelt marriage on 5 March 1871 — paired with sister Fredericka Albrecht's marriage to Friedrich Pillmann on 5 April 1870, just seven weeks before this baptism — tied the Pillmann / Albrecht / Kreichelt families into a single marriage chain. The infant Katherine Carolina carries Catharina's name as middle namesake.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Catharina is part of the Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann marriage chain — one of the most densely interconnected kin clusters in the Charles & Auguste social world. Her 1870 sponsorship preceded her own marriage by ten months, meaning she stood as godmother to her future niece — a pre-formal-kin courtesy consistent with Lutheran-Evangelical sponsorship customs.

Connection to Charles Gannsen & Auguste Kreichelt

★ Sponsor at the 22 May 1870 baptism of Katherine Carolina

Catharina Albrecht is one of four named sponsors at the 22 May 1870 baptism of Charles & Auguste's third child at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church (entry no. 6644, register page 285). The other three sponsors are Friedrich Pillmann (her newly-married brother-in-law), Carl Kreichelt (her fiancé), and Maria Emma Mincke (recorded as "Amalia Münke" — already married to Auguste's brother August Kreichelt).

The child's full baptismal name Friedrike Catharina Caroline Amalia Gansen mirrors the four sponsors directly: Friedrike for Friedrich Pillmann (or his wife Fredericka, Catharina's sister), Catharina for Catharina Albrecht herself, Caroline for Carl Kreichelt, and Amalia for Maria Emma Mincke (recorded as Amalia Münke). Textbook Hannoverian Lutheran sponsor-namesake structure.

The 1870–1871 Albrecht / Kreichelt / Pillmann marriage cluster

The baptism of Katherine Carolina sits exactly inside a tightly-clustered set of three weddings linking the Albrecht, Kreichelt, and Pillmann families:

5 April 1870Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann × Fredericka Albrecht (Catharina's sister) — St. Louis, Missouri. Just seven weeks before the 22 May 1870 baptism. Friedrich was already a sponsor at the 1866 Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson baptism, but in 1870 he stood as sponsor again now-as-Catharina's-brother-in-law.
22 May 1870Baptism of Friedrike Catharina Caroline Amalia Gansen (Katherine Carolina), St. Marcus, St. Louis — at which Catharina Albrecht stood as sponsor while still fiancée to Carl Kreichelt
5 March 1871Carl Kreichelt × Catharina Albrecht herself — St. Louis. About ten months after the baptism. Standing as a sponsor for one's future sister-in-law's child while still engaged was a recognized form of pre-wedding kinship-formation in the German Evangelical tradition; Catharina was effectively already family in the eyes of the congregation when she stood at the font in May 1870.

After her March 1871 marriage Catharina became sister-in-law to Auguste Kreichelt and aunt-by-marriage to all seven Gannsen children. She and Carl had ten children together over the next sixteen years (1871–1887).

📂 Full research log — Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann marriage chain, broader-investigation significance

The Kreichelt ⟷ Albrecht ⟷ Pillmann marriage chain

The 1870 Katherine Carolina baptism is the key documentary anchor for what Jed and Peter Schräder identified in December 2025 as the "Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann kin network" — three intermarried St. Louis families who furnish almost every named non-Charles-side sponsor across the seven Gannsen baptisms (1866–1880).

  • Catharina Albrecht (1850 St. Louis – 1913) ⟶ marries Carl Kreichelt (Auguste's brother, b. 1846 Clausthal Hannover), 5 March 1871
  • Fredericka Albrecht (1851 – 1915, GSTK-TGT, Catharina's sister) ⟶ marries Friedrich Pillmann (b. 1846 Germany), 5 April 1870 — making Friedrich brother-in-law to Carl Kreichelt
  • Maria Emma Mincke aka "Amalia Münke" (1844 St. Louis – 1905, L52N-1PL) ⟶ marries August Kreichelt (Auguste's other brother and a Battery B veteran)

Once these three marriages settled (by 1872 at the latest) every "stranger-sounding" surname in the Gannsen baptism registers — Albrecht, Pillmann/Billmann, Münke/Mincke — collapsed into one continuous network on Auguste's Kreichelt side. None of the four sponsors at Katherine Carolina's 1870 baptism is from Charles's German side.

Significance to the broader investigation

  1. The "stranger-sounding sponsor" puzzle is solved. Until December 2025 the Albrecht / Billmann / Münke surnames at the 1870 baptism were treated as potential Charles-side leads. The marriage-chain reconstruction shows they are all in fact second-generation St. Louis Germans on Auguste's Kreichelt side — closing this avenue of inquiry into Charles's German origin.
  2. American-born, not immigrant. Catharina was born in St. Louis in 1850 to Albrecht and Lieber parents who had married in St. Louis at Holy Ghost Church (German Evangelical) the year before. Her family had been in the city through the 1850 census and a brief Randolph County, Illinois interval (1860 census), returning to St. Louis by 1880. She is part of the established St. Louis German-American social network into which Auguste's Hannoverian Kreichelts assimilated — not a new arrival from the same region as Charles.
  3. Naming pattern. The middle names of Katherine Carolina (Friedrike Catharina Caroline Amalia) directly mirror her four sponsors. The Gannsen child Katherine Carolina is therefore Catharina Albrecht's namesake; she is one of the rare cases where a Gannsen child's name can be traced directly to a single, individually identifiable sponsor.
