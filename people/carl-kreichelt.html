Researcher view (sources, citations, bottom-line)

Carl Heinrich Kreichelt (1846, Clausthal, Hannover) was Auguste's brother, Charles's brother-in-law, and a St. Louis saloonist & real-estate investor by the late 19th century. He sponsored Katherine Carolina's 1870 baptism while still engaged to Catharina Albrecht — they married 5 March 1871, ten months after this baptism. Part of the Hannoverian Kreichelt-Kreigelt eight-child immigrant family that emigrated to St. Louis in 1853.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Carl is one of three Kreichelt siblings who appear as Gannsen baptism sponsors (alongside August and Bertha Barbara), with the parents Georg and Henriette also sponsoring. His 1870 sponsorship paired with his fiancée Catharina Albrecht is part of the Albrecht-Pillmann-Kreichelt marriage-chain pattern that shapes the inner kin circle of the Charles & Auguste household.

Connection to Charles Gannsen & Auguste Kreichelt

★ Brother-in-law to Charles Gannsen, sponsor at the 1870 baptism of Katherine Carolina

Carl Kreichelt was Charles Gannsen's brother-in-law from the day Charles married Auguste — 7 January 1866 at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church. Carl was 19 at the time of the wedding. Four years later, on 22 May 1870, he stood as one of four named sponsors at the baptism of his niece Friedrike Catharina Caroline Amalia Gansen (Katherine Carolina), the third Gannsen child. The other sponsors were Friedrich Pillmann, Catharina Albrecht (Carl's fiancée at the time), and Maria Emma Mincke (recorded as "Amalia Münke," his sister-in-law via brother August).

The middle name Caroline in Katherine Carolina's full baptismal name is plausibly the namesake echo of Carl Kreichelt himself — Carl/Caroline being the male/female Hannoverian-Lutheran given-name pair. (The standard rule in St. Marcus baptisms was that male sponsors gave their first name in feminine form to female godchildren.)

The 1870–1871 marriage cluster

At the time of Katherine Carolina's May 1870 baptism Carl was still unmarried and engaged to Catharina Albrecht. He married her on 5 March 1871, ten months later — and was the second of his three siblings (after his older brother August Kreichelt who married Maria Emma Mincke earlier, and his sister Auguste who had married Charles Gannsen in 1866) to become a settled head of household in the St. Louis German-Evangelical community.

Carl and Catharina had ten children — the largest of the three Kreichelt-sibling households — and the family persisted through the 1920s in St. Louis. Carl died at age 80 in 1926, having outlived his sister Auguste (d. 1896), his brother August (d. 1877), and Charles Gannsen himself (d. 1903) by decades.

📂 Full research log — Hannoverian Kreichelt family in St. Louis, broader-investigation significance

The Hannoverian Kreichelt family in St. Louis

The Kreichelt family is the Hannoverian half of Charles Gannsen's marital household. Carl's father Georg Andreas Kreigelt was born in 1819 in the same Clausthal-Zellerfeld parish complex on the southwest edge of the Harz Mountains. Georg married Henriette Weiss on 8 April 1844 at Clausthal, and the family emigrated to St. Louis in 1853 — when Carl was 7. The family came as a unit: at least four of Carl's siblings (August, Auguste, Carl Wilhelm Christian Hermann, and Caroline) were born before the migration.

The Kreichelt brothers all served in Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery during the Civil War — the same unit as Charles Gannsen. Auguste's father Georg and her brother August both walked her down the aisle at her January 1866 wedding to Charles. Carl himself was 17 when the war started and 19 by its end; he is not on the documented Battery B roster but moved in the same Soulard / Frenchtown German social circle.

The 1866 marriage register entry for Charles × Auguste reads "Carl Gonnson, aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen, und Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" — the "Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" half of that origin string is the Kreichelt family's place of origin, and Carl is the immediate-family link to that town.

Significance to the broader investigation

  1. The Hannoverian-side family origin is documentary, not inferential. Carl's birth in Clausthal in 1846 and his father Georg's 1844 Clausthal marriage are the documentary anchors for the "Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" reading of Auguste's St. Marcus marriage register entry. There is no remaining ambiguity about the Kreichelt origin.
  2. Surname-drift evidence. The German baptism register spelling Kreigelt (no "ch") in 1846 vs. the American spelling Kreichelt in St. Louis records is a parallel of the surname-drift seen in Charles's own family (Gonnson / Gansen / Konsen / Jansen). Both families illustrate how German-American surnames in mid-19th-c. St. Louis were routinely re-spelled by English-speaking parish clerks, civil clerks, and census enumerators.
  3. Carl is one of two main "non-Charles-side" sponsors at the 1870 baptism. With Catharina Albrecht and the brothers-in-law Pillmann/August Kreichelt, Carl reflects the deeply intra-Kreichelt sponsorship pattern that characterizes the Gannsen baptisms — a pattern that remains the single strongest piece of evidence that Charles had no surviving relatives or close German-side friends in St. Louis to invite as godparents.
