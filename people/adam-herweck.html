The first witness named on Charles and Auguste's 1866 marriage register entry.

Adam Herweck (canonical Johann Adam Herweck · FS K4DW-PXM · b. 16 July 1831 Lampertheim, Hessen · d. 11 May 1897 Edwardsville, Madison Co., IL · age 65) is the first-named witness in the witness column of the St. Marcus marriage register entry for Charles Konzen × Auguste Kreichelt (7 January 1866) and the only Battery B veteran in the wedding-witness column per a May 2026 NPS Soldiers and Sailors System search. Two pages from his Compiled Service Record (NARA M405 Roll 0329) — the Nov–Dec 1862 Detachment Muster Roll and a 9 December 1862 Captain's letter from New Madrid — definitively confirm K4DW-PXM as Charles's Battery B comrade and document Adam's role on recruiting service at St. Louis in late 1862 — making him the project's documented Battery B recruiter.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Adam Herweck is the documentary anchor that ties three of Charles's St. Louis spheres into a single network: (1) Battery B — fellow sergeant, both promoted from Pvt. to Sgt., both at New Madrid in the same months; (2) the wedding party — the named witness on the 1866 marriage; (3) the recruiter pipeline — Adam was on detached recruiting duty at St. Louis in December 1862, the same month Charles enlisted on 2 December 1862. The wedding-party as a whole (Adam from Pfalz-adjacent Hessen-Darmstadt, the Kreichelts from Hannover, the Goetz family from Bavaria, and Charles from Hessen-Cassel) is consistent with a pan-German pension-witness / parish-sponsor / wedding-witness network around St. Marcus that predates and outlasts the war.

Connection to Charles Gannsen

★ Wedding witness · 7 January 1866

Adam Herweck appears as the first-named witness in the witness column of the St. Marcus German Evangelical Church marriage register entry for Charles Gannsen × Auguste Kreichelt, dated 7 January 1866. Other witnesses on the same line: Philipp Weber, Anna Goetz, Sophie Lehm. Officiating pastor: Rev. George W. Wall.

At Charles's wedding, Adam was 34 years old and unmarried — he would not marry Marguerite Wamsganz for another 18 months, on 21 July 1868. Two weeks after Charles's wedding, on 25 January 1866, Adam appears again as witness at the Gels-Morlock marriage — confirming he was a regularly-called St. Marcus witness during this period.

Source: St. Louis Marriage Records 1865–1867, image 72, entry No. 676. FamilySearch image group ↗

★ May 2026 NPS finding · Adam was the ONLY Battery B veteran in the wedding-witness column

A May 2026 search of the NPS Soldiers and Sailors System filtered to Missouri Light Artillery confirms that Philipp Weber, the second-named wedding witness, did not serve in any Civil War unit on either side. The exact-phrase search for "Philipp Weber" returns zero results across the entire 6.2-million-record NPS database. Six other Webers DID serve in Charles's regiment (2nd MO Light Artillery: Benjamin, Carl, Henry, John, Joseph, Peter), but no Philipp anywhere — nor in any other Missouri Light Artillery unit.

This means Adam was the only Battery B veteran in the four-name witness column; Philipp Weber, Anna Goetz, and Sophie Lehm are all civilians from the broader St. Marcus / Soulard German social network. The all-civilian-3-of-4 witness pattern reinforces that the Charles-Adam Battery B comradeship was the singular military thread at the wedding, with the rest of the wedding party drawn from the Kreichelt-side civilian congregation. Anna Goetz is plausibly tied to the 1868 baptism's Maria Agnes Götz sponsor (a still-open identification on the Auguste side); Sophie Lehm and Philipp Weber remain unidentified St. Marcus parishioners.

The Charles C Herweck namesake hypothesis · 1874 · ⚠ inference, not documented

Adam and Marguerite's third child was named Charles C Herweck (b. 18 September 1874 in Missouri; d. 16 August 1957 Los Angeles; FamilySearch LLSN-ZVV). I previously characterized the name as "almost certainly after Charles Gannsen." On reflection, that's an overstated inference. Here's the honest breakdown of what's documented versus what's hypothesized:

What is documented

  • Adam stood as the first-named witness at Charles Gannsen's wedding on 7 January 1866 (St. Marcus marriage register).
  • Adam's son was given the name Charles C Herweck on 18 September 1874 — born in Missouri, almost certainly St. Louis where Adam still resided (his family didn't relocate to Edwardsville, Illinois until between 1874 and 1880).
  • The name "Charles" / "Carl" / "Karl" does not appear in Adam's documented immediate Lampertheim ancestry (father Peter, brothers Peter, Georg, Johannes; no Karl).
  • Both Adam and Charles Gannsen were members of the St. Marcus German Evangelical congregation where Pastor Wall officiated.

What is NOT documented

  • No christening / baptism record is currently attached to Charles C Herweck's FamilySearch profile — so no godparents or sponsors are recorded. This is the single piece of evidence that would settle the question.
  • The middle initial "C" is not expanded — it could stand for Carl (the German form of Charles, in which case the namesake is internal to the German tradition rather than referring to a specific person), Christopher, or any other name beginning with C.
  • No letter, diary, family history, or oral tradition documenting a deliberate Charles-Gannsen-namesake intent has been surfaced.
  • Adam's wider family, godparent network, employment circles, and Lampertheim relatives could have provided alternative "Charles" namesake candidates — none of which have been ruled out.

How the question could be resolved

The most direct path to confirmation or refutation is finding Charles C Herweck's baptism record. If born in St. Louis September 1874, the most likely baptism venue is the St. Marcus German Evangelical Church register (Pastor Geo. W. Wall officiating until at least 1880); other candidates are the Holy Ghost Evangelical Church or another OTR-area congregation. The baptism record would name the godparents:

  • If Charles Gannsen and Auguste Kreichelt Gannsen are listed as godparents — the namesake hypothesis is confirmed beyond reasonable doubt.
  • If a different person named Charles is godfather — the namesake hypothesis is refuted in favour of that person.
  • If no godparent named Charles appears — the name is most likely just a common German given name choice, possibly informed by Adam's broader social orbit but not specifically referring to Charles Gannsen.

What the new evidence means for the Charles-Gannsen-namesake hypothesis

Two of two known Adam-Margaretha baptisms at St. Marcus (1870 Emma, 1873 Margaretha) were sponsored by maternal Wamsgans-side relatives or family friends — not by Charles Gannsen. Charles is conspicuously absent from Adam's documented godparent pool, even though he was the first-named witness at Adam's social-circle peer (Charles's own) wedding four years earlier.

The reciprocity check is equally definitive. Per the previously-completed entry-by-entry sponsor analysis of Charles & Auguste's seven St. Marcus baptisms (Jed × Peter Schräder, December 2025), Adam Herweck never appears as a sponsor at any of Charles's seven baptisms either (1866 Friedrich Wilhelm, 1868 Josephine Maria, 1870 Katherine Carolina, 1872 Auguste, 1873 Louise, 1875 Erwin Carl Konsen, 1880 Bertha). Charles's sponsor pool is dominated by Auguste's Kreichelt–Albrecht–Pillman Hannoverian kin network, with two non-kin exceptions: Erwin Spraul (Baden-born friend who sponsored Erwin Carl Konsen in 1875 — and after whom Charles named Charles Jr.) and the unresolved Jakob & Josephine Reck couple (1868). The 1870 Katherine baptism is particularly telling: it occurred just weeks after Adam's daughter Emma was baptized at the same church (3 April 1870), yet neither family invited the other to participate as godparent.

This is mutual non-sponsorship. The Adam-Charles bond ran through wedding-witness and Battery B comradeship — the public, fraternal, military-civic register — but explicitly not through sacramental kinship (godparenthood). In the 1870s German-Evangelical tradition of St. Louis, choosing a godparent was a deliberate signal of family-tier closeness; both men consistently chose their wives' kin networks instead, and chose them mutually. This pattern would not be present if either family considered the other family-tier-close.

Combined with the negative 1874 findings (no Charles C baptism at St. Marcus, period) and the reciprocity finding (Adam never sponsored a Gannsen child either), the namesake hypothesis is now significantly weakened:

  • Neither family chose the other as godparent across nine combined St. Marcus baptisms (7 Gannsen + 2 Herweck) over fourteen years (1866-1880). This is mutual non-sponsorship, not a one-sided coincidence.
  • Charles named his own son Erwin Carl Konsen after a different godfather (Erwin Spraul, the Baden-born friend) in 1875 — the year after Charles C Herweck was named. Charles followed the standard Lutheran custom of naming a son after the godfather. If Adam had similarly chosen Charles to godparent Charles C, we would expect a Charles-side godparent record to surface; we have only a non-sponsorship pattern instead.
  • The family appears to have left St. Louis between Margaretha's May 1873 baptism and Charles C's September 1874 birth (consistent with the 1880 census placing them in Edwardsville).
  • Charles C's name might just reflect a generic German-immigrant-era preference for Anglicized "Charles," potentially honoring a different Charles in Adam's Edwardsville orbit, the deceased Lampertheim cousin/uncle line, or simply with no specific namesake at all.

Honest verdict — revised again: The Charles-Gannsen-as-namesake claim should now be characterized as "plausible by congregational coincidence but disfavored by the actual godparent record." Both Charles's seven baptism sponsor lists (1866-1880) and Adam's two known sponsor lists (1870, 1873) consistently exclude the other man from sacramental-kin selection, even while both families remained at the same St. Marcus congregation, in the same neighborhood, with overlapping baptism dates. This nine-baptism mutual-exclusion is the strongest documentary evidence we have on the question — and it points away from a namesake-by-deliberate-honoring relationship.

Possibly-related second Herweck couple at St. Marcus · Martin × Elise (Elisabeth) née Eckerle

The 1870, 1872, and 1873 St. Marcus indexes also show baptisms for Martin Herweck und Elisabeth (Elise) geb. Eckerle (register pages 298, 351, and one more). This is a second German-Evangelical Herweck couple in St. Louis during Adam's residence there. They are not in the K4DW-PXM tree as far as we know. Possibilities:

  • Martin could be a Lampertheim Herweck cousin who emigrated separately.
  • Or an unrelated namesake Herweck household — the surname is not so rare in Hessen as to rule this out.

Martin's existence is a future research lead, but is unrelated to the Charles-Gannsen question.

  • Eden Theological Seminary Archives (Webster Groves, MO) — holds many southern Illinois Evangelical Synod congregational registers. Direct researcher inquiry needed.
  • St. Louis County Library, History & Genealogy Department (1640 S Lindbergh Blvd, Frontenac MO) — has microfilm copies of the Eden Archives holdings; in-person browsable.

Given the strength of the new St. Marcus negative finding plus the mutual-non-sponsorship pattern across nine St. Marcus baptisms 1866-1880, the Charles-Gannsen-as-Charles-C-namesake hypothesis is now meaningfully disfavored on the basis of available evidence — and is unlikely to be confirmed even if an Edwardsville baptism record is eventually retrieved off-FamilySearch. The honest stopping point for this question, given digital-source exhaustion, is: "plausibly named after Charles Gannsen, but the documentary record points the other way."

★★★★ DOCUMENTARY CONFIRMATION OF ADAM HERWECK AS BATTERY B RECRUITER

Page 33 is the smoking-gun source for the recruiter role attributed to Adam in family knowledge: the Captain commanding Battery B writes verbatim that "Adam Herweck, of my Company, [is on] recruiting service at St. Louis" as of 9 December 1862. This places Adam at the St. Louis recruiting station precisely when Charles Gannsen enlisted on 2 December 1862. The two events almost certainly intersect: Adam, the Battery B Sergeant on detached recruiting duty in St. Louis, almost certainly enrolled the German-speaking Charles into Co. B that week.

This is the kind of corroboration that turns "family memory of Adam being a recruiter" into documented Civil War institutional history. Adam's recruiting service explains both his Sergeant rank by late 1862 (Battery B recruiters were typically promoted NCOs given the responsibility of enrolling men under federal authority) and the September 11, 1863 special-order transfer recorded on Page 17 (which would be the formal closure of his recruiting detachment and return to the field — at which point his battery had moved south to New Madrid).

Battery B service alongside Charles

Both Adam and Charles enlisted in the 2nd Missouri Light Artillery, mustered into Co. B (Battery B), began as Privates, and mustered out as Sergeants. Charles's enlistment was 2 December 1862 in St. Louis; Adam's enlistment date is not yet pinned down but his Civil War service record (FFY5-MN8) places him on the same regimental roster (NARA M390 master index). Adam may well have been the Battery B recruiter who enrolled Charles at the St. Louis recruiting station that December — a German-speaking sergeant signing up a German-speaking enlistee.

The two men's families remained socially intertwined through at least 1879: the 1868–1875 St. Louis directories list "Adam Herweck" through this period, and Adam's first three children (Emma 1869, Margaretha 1872, Charles C 1874) were all born during the years Charles and Auguste were also having children at St. Marcus.

Why Adam matters to the broader Charles Gannsen investigation

  1. Battery B / St. Marcus pan-German social network confirmed. Adam was Hessen-Darmstadt (Lampertheim, southern Hesse) — different from Charles's Hesse-Cassel but at least the same broader Hesse-region polity. The wedding-party as a whole (Adam Pfalz-adjacent Hesse, the Kreichelts Hannoverian, Charles Hesse-Cassel) confirms that St. Marcus was a genuinely pan-German immigrant congregation, not a Hesse-Cassel enclave.
  2. The Battery B recruiter hypothesis for Charles's 2 December 1862 enlistment is consistent with Adam's Sgt rank and likely recruiting role. A German-speaking recruiter signing up a German-speaking enlistee at the St. Louis recruiting station fits the period.
  3. The 1874 naming of Charles C Herweck is consistent with — but does not prove — a Charles Gannsen namesake. See the dedicated namesake-hypothesis box above for the evidentiary breakdown. The path to confirmation runs through the still-unlocated baptism record (most likely St. Marcus Evangelical Church, St. Louis, autumn 1874).
  4. Adam's Edwardsville (Illinois) move post-1872 ~50 miles up the Mississippi from St. Louis means he physically separated from Charles by the mid-1870s, which is consistent with the absence of his name from Charles's 1890 pension witness affidavits — the pension network was sourced from St. Louis residents only. By 1880 Adam was firmly settled in Edwardsville, and he died there in 1897.
