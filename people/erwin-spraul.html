Erwin Spraul ⚜

Superintendent of the Anheuser-Busch Brewing Association · Baden-born German immigrant friend of Charles · the sole named sponsor — and probable namesake — of Erwin Carl Konsen at the 1875 baptism · died young at 41 in 1889 of post-operative complications

★ At a Glance

✓ ID Confirmed
SPONSOR PROFILE · IDENTIFIED FS KLP1-N62 · obituary surfaced May 2026

The sole named sponsor at the 1875 baptism of Charles's son Erwin Carl — the keystone Konsen-spelling record.

Erwin Spraul (~1848 Baden – 19 June 1889 St. Louis, age 41) was Superintendent of the Anheuser-Busch Brewing Association at his death — the most senior trade title at one of St. Louis's largest German-immigrant employers. The 20 June 1889 St. Louis newspaper obituary establishes his role at A-B, his 1340 South Ninth Street address (in the Soulard / Frenchtown German neighborhood, walking distance from the brewery), Baden birthplace, age 41 at death, "very good circumstances" estate, wife Anna Maria Grassner (m. 1868), and son Frank (b. 1869, d. young 1894). He died of post-operative complications from a shoulder abscess.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Erwin is one of a small handful of non-Kreichelt-side sponsors across the seven Gannsen baptisms (alongside Jacob Reck, Maria Agnes Götz, and Wilhelmine Ebeling) and the sole named sponsor at the 1875 Erwin Carl Konsen baptism, and the child’s probable namesake (the name-sponsor coincidence is suggestive; no record states the naming motive — cf. the parallel Pillmann case). The 1889 obituary sharpens Jed's December 2025 reading of him as a "successful fellow immigrant" to Anheuser-Busch Superintendent — the top tier of St. Louis German-immigrant brewing. The Anheuser-Busch employment chain extends two generations: Charles's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson (1905–1947) later worked as a Bottler at Anheuser-Busch.

📂 Full research log — 1889 Anheuser-Busch obituary, broader-investigation significance

The 1889 obituary · the canonical source

★ "Death of Erwin Spraul" — St. Louis newspaper, 20 June 1889

Erwin Spraul, Superintendent of the Anheuser-Busch Brewing Association, died at 9 o'clock last night, at his home, 1340 South Ninth street, at the age of 41 years. Mr. Spraul had been suffering from an abscess on the left shoulder, and Dr. Mudd performed an operation on it yesterday afternoon. At first he seemed to rally after the operation and his friends were hopeful, but he soon began to sink and slowly failed until death came. He was born at Baden, Germany. He leaves a wife and one son in very good circumstances. His son is in Salt Lake City on a trip and has been recalled.

Source: St. Louis newspaper death notice, 20 June 1889 (image supplied by Jed, May 2026). The obituary establishes — with primary-source authority — Erwin's role at Anheuser-Busch, his exact street address at death, his Baden birthplace, his age (41 → birth year ~1848, contradicting the FS tree's 1841), the "very good circumstances" of his estate, and the wife-and-one-son family composition.

Significance to the broader investigation

  1. The "successful fellow immigrant" reading is now sharpened to "Anheuser-Busch Superintendent." Charles's choice of godfather for Erwin Carl Konsen in 1875 reflects access to the highest tier of the St. Louis German-immigrant brewing trade. In 1875 Erwin would have been 27 and on the rise; by 1889 he held the company's most senior trade title. Charles's selection of him reads as ambitious and forward-looking — Charles wanted his son to be modeled on the most successful peer he could identify.
  2. Anheuser-Busch is now a documented Gannsen-family institution. Frederick Charles Johnson (Charles Sr.'s grandson) worked as a Bottler at Anheuser-Busch — sixty years after Erwin Spraul stood at the 1875 baptismal font. Whether Frederick Charles got the brewery job through any echo of the Spraul godparenting connection is not documented, but the institutional continuity itself is striking. The St. Louis south-side brewing-district neighborhood (Soulard, Frenchtown, Gravois) is the geographic spine connecting all three generations.
  3. Open lead: Erwin's specific Baden parish of origin. The Spraul surname is rare in German records (per Peter, "only post-WWII"). With the obituary's confirmation of the 1848 birth year and Baden birthplace, a focused Baden parish-register search for an "Erwin Spraul" baptism in 1848 is now well-scoped — though still likely to face the rarity-of-surname obstacle. The 1868 St. Louis marriage record (12 November 1868) and a possible 1860s passenger manifest may still surface a witness or familial connection to Charles's own Hesse-Cassel origin.
  4. Birth-year correction needed on the FamilySearch tree. The obituary's "age of 41 years" at death on 19 June 1889 derives a birth year of approximately 1848. The FS tree currently records 1841 — a 7-year discrepancy. The obituary, as a primary source published the day after death and almost certainly informed by either the family or the brewery, should take precedence; the FS tree birth year should be corrected to ~1848.
