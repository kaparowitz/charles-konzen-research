Dr. Charles Hess

Charles Gannsen's first treating physician · post-discharge
Limited record Treating physician · post-1865

★ At a Glance

FIRST TREATING PHYSICIAN · IDENTITY OPEN

The only treating physician Charles ever named — cited by Charles himself in a handwritten letter to the Commissioner of Pensions in 1898.

Dr. Charles Hess is the only treating physician Charles Konzen ever names by name in the entire pension file. The reference appears in Charles's own c. 1898 handwritten letter to the Commissioner of Pensions: "I went to Hospital Surgeon and got medicine, was not in hospitals. Dr. Charles Hess, treated me first…" A multi-database directory search (May 2026) returned five non-physician Charles Hess listings in 1865-1880 St. Louis directories but no physician — leaving the doctor's individual identity open.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: The reference establishes that Charles sought private medical care in St. Louis rather than the federal Soldiers' Home / VA system — this is consistent with his immediate return to civilian work as a railroad conductor on Gates Street and Broadway. Identifying Dr. Hess would re-anchor Charles's earliest post-discharge medical record and possibly back-date his disability narrative by ~25 years before the 1890 pension claim.

What Charles wrote about him

In his c. 1898 handwritten letter to the Commissioner of Pensions, Charles writes:

I went to Hospital Surgeon and got medicine, was not in hospitals. Dr. Charles Hess, treated me first…

Source: Pension File F41-523680269E, Page 29 (Charles's handwritten address-history letter).

Why he matters

Dr. Hess is the only treating physician Charles names directly in the surviving pension correspondence. The reference is brief and occurs in the same letter where Charles explains he "was not in hospitals" — meaning he sought private medical care in St. Louis rather than relying on the federal Soldiers' Home or Marine Hospital system in the immediate post-war years (1865–early 1870s).

Identifying which St. Louis Dr. Charles Hess this is would require consulting 1860s–70s St. Louis medical-society directories. Multiple Hess physicians practiced in St. Louis during this period, several of them German immigrants.

★ Multi-database investigation (3 May 2026)

Negative finding — no St. Louis Dr. Charles Hess in directories

A 3 May 2026 multi-database pass on "Charles Hess" with St. Louis residence 1865-1880 returned the following directory results — none of them physicians:

  • Charles Hess, chairmaker · Heller & Hoffman company · 7th Street between Biddle and Carr · 1865-1866 St. Louis Directory
  • Charles Hess, cigarmaker · 244 South 2nd Street · 1865-1866 St. Louis Directory
  • Charles Hess, steamboatman · east side 6th Street between Gratiot[?] and Rutgers · 1865-1866 St. Louis Directory
  • Charles Hess, laborer · 4246 Hunt Street · 1897 St. Louis Directory
  • Charles Hess, jr., laborer · 513 St. Anthony · 1895 St. Louis Directory

FS Full-Text Search for "Dr. Charles Hess" + St. Louis returned national hits (Bristol Tennessee, Atlantic City, Washington Observer, Rio de Janeiro Probate) but no St. Louis physician matching the period 1865-1880.

Working hypothesis — name variant or non-directory practice

Three plausible explanations for the negative finding:

  1. Name variant. Charles wrote the doctor's name in his own handwriting in a 1898 letter — by which time more than 30 years had passed since the post-1865 treatment. The doctor's actual name may have been Carl Hess, Karl Hess, or Charles Heß — German-language variants Charles wrote phonetically as "Charles Hess." A search for "Carl Hess" + physician + St. Louis would expand the candidate pool.
  2. Outside the directory listing scope. Mid-1860s St. Louis physicians who treated working-class German patients on credit or barter were sometimes uncatalogued by Edwards' / Polk's directories — directory listings were paid placements that not every physician purchased. The doctor may have practiced from his home without a paid listing.
  3. Hospital-attached physician. Charles wrote "I went to Hospital Surgeon and got medicine, was not in hospitals" — possibly meaning he visited a doctor attached to one of the city's German immigrant hospitals (City Hospital, German General Hospital, Lutheran Hospital) without being formally admitted. Such physicians were sometimes listed under the hospital, not their own name.

Open lead — St. Louis Medical Society directory

The Missouri Historical Society holds the St. Louis Medical Directory for 1867, 1869, 1871, and later years (Polk's). A direct page-by-page search on "Hess" entries in those volumes would either locate or definitively rule out a Dr. Charles/Carl/Karl Hess in St. Louis post-1865. This is a holdable archival lead for in-person research at MHS or via interlibrary loan.
