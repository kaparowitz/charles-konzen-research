Christina Hahl (1846–1892)

★ The bridge between the Misch & Gottlob families · mother of Katherine Misch · 2nd wife of Theodore Gottlob Sr.
MISCH ⟷ GOTTLOB BRIDGE b. 1846 d. 1892 (age 46) 8 children · two marriages

★ At a Glance

✓ ID Confirmed
★ MISCH ⟷ GOTTLOB BRIDGE · JED'S 2ND-GREAT-GRANDMOTHER

Charles's son's mother-in-law — Katherine Misch's mother. Her second marriage to Theodore Gottlob is the family-tree link that connects the Charles Konzen line to the Gottlob line. (For the site author Jed Johnson, she is a 2nd-great-grandmother.)

Researcher view (sources, citations, bottom-line)

Christina Hahl (FS KG17-2KY · b. 1846 Germany · d. 1892 St. Louis age 46) is the structural keystone of the Misch ⟷ Gottlob bridge — the documentary chain that ultimately ties Charles Gannsen's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905) to the Theodore Gottlob line and explains why he was "named after his Uncle Frederick Gottlob" per his FamilySearch tree's custom fact. She was first married to Michael Misch (m. 14 April 1864 St. Louis · 5 children including Katherine Misch), widowed at 36 when Michael died in 1882, and remarried Theodore Gottlob Sr. on 29 November 1883 in Madison, Illinois (3 more children including Frederick Gottlob, the half-brother for whom Charles Konzen's grandson would be named).

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Three documentary chains intersect through Christina Hahl. (1) The Misch line: Her first marriage produced Katherine Misch, who in 1895 married Charles's son Erwin Carl Konsen (Charles Johnson Jr.) — making Christina the mother-in-law of Charles's son. (2) The Gottlob line: Her second marriage produced Frederick Gottlob — half-brother to Katherine Misch and namesake of Charles's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson. (3) The DeKalb-Street neighbor relationship (1890): Theodore Gottlob Sr. (Christina's second husband) lived next door to Charles Konzen at 2409 vs. 2408 DeKalb — and witnessed Charles's pension declaration. Christina is therefore the second-generation knot that converts Charles's neighbor (Theodore) into Charles's grandson's uncle by half-blood a generation later.

★ Why she matters

Christina Hahl is the structural bridge between two families that converge on Charles Gannsen's grandson. She was first married to Michael Misch (m. 14 April 1864 St. Louis · 5 children), widowed at 36 when Michael died in 1882, and remarried Theodore Gottlob Sr. on 29 November 1883 in Madison, Illinois (3 more children including Frederick Gottlob). The Misch and Gottlob children were therefore half-siblings via shared mother Christina.

This is the missing piece in the "Uncle Frederick Gottlob" naming chain. Charles Gannsen's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905, son of Erwin Carl Konsen × Katherine Misch) carries the FamilySearch tree custom-fact: "Named after his Uncle Frederick Gottlob." That label is technically a half-uncle — Frederick Gottlob and Katherine Misch shared this same mother, Christina Hahl. The Charles Gannsen ↔ Theodore Gottlob ↔ Misch ↔ Johnson chain is fully reconstructed through her.

The half-sibling bridge. The five Misch children and three Gottlob children share Christina Hahl as mother but have different fathers. So Katherine Misch and Frederick Gottlob are half-siblings — same mother, different fathers (Michael Misch then Theodore Gottlob Sr.). When Katherine's son Frederick Charles Johnson was named "after his Uncle Frederick Gottlob" in 1905, the relationship label is technically half-uncle via the maternal-Misch-half-Gottlob line. The Charles Gannsen ↔ Theodore Gottlob ↔ Misch ↔ Johnson marriage chain is fully reconstructed through Christina Hahl.

Documentary chain  ★ KEY EVIDENCE

Christina Hahl as the Misch ⟷ Gottlob structural keystone

The Misch ⟷ Gottlob bridge that ultimately ties Charles Konzen's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905) to the Theodore Gottlob line rests on three documented marriages and one estate dissolution involving Christina Hahl:

  1. 14 April 1864 · St. Louis — m. 1 to Michael Misch (1842–1882) · 5 children incl. Katherine Misch (b. 1875, the future daughter-in-law of Charles Konzen via Erwin Carl).
  2. 1882 · St. Louis — Michael Misch dies; Christina widowed at 36 with five children.
  3. 29 November 1883 · Madison, Illinois — m. 2 to Theodore Gottlob Sr. (1842–1900, Westphalian Civil War veteran) · 3 more children incl. Frederick Gottlob (the namesake of Charles's grandson).
  4. 1892 · St. Louis — Christina dies at age 46, predeceasing Theodore by 8 years.

Sources: FamilySearch person KG17-2KY (Christina Hahl); KGCS-916 (Michael Misch); KG17-D3H (Theodore Gottlob Sr.); 2B2C-KQQ (Katherine Misch). St. Louis marriage register (1864 entry) and Madison Co., IL marriage register (1883 entry).

Why she matters for the Charles Gannsen investigation

Christina Hahl is the structural keystone in the Misch ⟷ Gottlob bridge that ultimately ties Charles Gannsen's family to Theodore Gottlob Sr.'s. Three documentary chains intersect through her:

  1. The Misch line. Her first marriage produced Katherine Misch — who in 1895 married Charles Gannsen's son Erwin Carl Konsen ("Charles Johnson Jr."). Katherine became the maternal grandmother of every Charles Gannsen descendant carrying the Johnson surname.
  2. The Gottlob line. Her second marriage produced Frederick Gottlob — the literal "Uncle Frederick" referenced in the FamilySearch custom fact for Charles Gannsen's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson. Without Christina's 1883 remarriage, that naming relationship has no genealogical anchor.
  3. The St. Louis neighbor relationship. Theodore Gottlob Sr. lived at 2409 DeKalb Street in 1890, next door to Charles Gannsen at 2408 DeKalb. Theodore co-witnessed Charles's 1890 Pension Declaration. The two families were already in physical contact before the Konsen-Misch 1895 marriage — and the Misch-Gottlob 1883 remarriage means that contact ran through the same household in which Katherine Misch grew up after her father's 1882 death.

Christina's death in 1892 (age 46) preceded the 1895 Konsen-Misch wedding by three years and Theodore's 1900 death by eight years. She did not live to see her daughter Katherine marry Charles Gannsen's son — but the family bridge she built between 1864 and 1883 made that marriage possible inside an already-interconnected St. Louis German-immigrant network.
