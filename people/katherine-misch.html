Charles's daughter-in-law — she married his son Erwin Carl (later Charles Johnson Jr.). Through her mother's second marriage she also became step-related to the Gottlob line, which is how the family-tree branches of this site connect.

Researcher view (sources, citations, bottom-line)

Katherine Misch (FS 2B2C-KQQ · b. 1875 St. Louis · d. 1948 St. Louis age 73) is the second-generation knot that connects the Charles Konzen line to the Theodore Gottlob line. She was the daughter of Michael Misch × Christina Hahl, and via her mother's second marriage (to Theodore Gottlob Sr.) she became half-sister to Frederick Gottlob — the documented namesake of her own son Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905). She married Charles's son Charles Johnson Jr. (formally Erwin Carl Konsen) and was a lifelong St. Louis resident at 168/170/175 Sidney Street through six US censuses (1880–1940).

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Katherine Misch is the documentary linchpin that explains the previously-mysterious "named after his Uncle Frederick Gottlob" custom fact on Frederick Charles Johnson's FS tree. Three chains intersect through her: (1) the DeKalb-Street neighbor relationship — Theodore Gottlob Sr. was Charles's next-door neighbor at 2409 DeKalb in 1890 and pension witness; (2) the Misch ⟷ Gottlob bridge via her mother Christina Hahl's two marriages; (3) the marriage itself — when Erwin Carl Konsen married Katherine Misch in 1895, the daughter of Charles's neighbor's wife became Charles's daughter-in-law, twenty-five years before her son was named for her half-brother. The two ancestral lines that converged on Charles's grandson were already adjacent on DeKalb Street in 1890.

★★★ The Misch ⟷ Gottlob bridge — major finding (3 May 2026)

Katherine Misch's profile resolves the long-standing question of why Charles's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905) was "named after his Uncle Frederick Gottlob" per his FS tree custom fact.

The bridge is documentary: Katherine's mother Christina Hahl (1846-1892) was first married to Michael Misch (1842-1882) on 14 April 1864 in St. Louis. The Misch marriage produced five Misch children, including Katherine. When Michael Misch died in 1882, Christina was widowed at age 36. She remarried Theodore Gottlob Sr. on 29 November 1883 in Madison, Illinois — and the Christina-Theodore marriage produced additional children including Frederick Gottlob.

So Frederick Gottlob is Katherine Misch's half-brother (same mother Christina Hahl, different fathers — Michael Misch then Theodore Gottlob Sr.) — and is therefore Frederick Charles Johnson's literal "Uncle Frederick Gottlob" via the maternal-Misch-half-Gottlob line. The Charles Gannsen ↔ Theodore Gottlob ↔ Misch ↔ Johnson marriage chain is now fully reconstructed across four generations.

This also explains why Theodore Gottlob Sr. (the Civil War Sergeant of 9th Illinois Infantry, who lived next door to Charles at 2409 DeKalb in 1890) became a witness on Charles's pension declaration. He wasn't merely a neighbor — by 1895, when Katherine Misch married Charles's son Erwin Carl Konsen, the Gottlob and Konsen/Johnson families were knitted together by marriage. → Theodore Gottlob Sr. profile

Documentary chain  ★ KEY EVIDENCE

Katherine Misch as the second-generation Konzen / Gottlob knot

Three independent record chains intersect through Katherine Misch's biography and resolve the long-standing question of why Charles's grandson Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905) was "named after his Uncle Frederick Gottlob" per the FS tree custom fact:

  1. The DeKalb Street neighbor relationship (1890): Theodore Gottlob Sr. lived at 2409 DeKalb, next door to Charles Gannsen at 2408 DeKalb, and witnessed Charles's pension declaration. Pension File F41-523680269E, Page 14.
  2. The Misch ⟷ Gottlob bridge via Christina Hahl: Christina Hahl (Katherine's mother) married Michael Misch in 1864 (5 children incl. Katherine), was widowed in 1882, then married Theodore Gottlob Sr. in 1883 (3 more children incl. Frederick Gottlob).
  3. The 1895 marriage: Erwin Carl Konsen (Charles's son · "Charles Johnson Jr.") married Katherine Misch in St. Louis, converting the 1890 DeKalb neighbor relationship into a marital alliance one generation later. Their son Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905) was named after his half-uncle Frederick Gottlob — the only documentary thread that explains the FS custom fact.

Why she matters for the Charles Gannsen investigation

Katherine Misch is the second-generation knot that connects the Charles Gannsen line to the Theodore Gottlob line. Three documentary chains intersect through her:

  1. The DeKalb-Street neighbor relationship (1890): Theodore Gottlob Sr. lived at 2409 DeKalb, next door to Charles Gannsen at 2408 DeKalb. Theodore was a documented Civil War Sergeant of 9th Illinois Infantry. He co-witnessed Charles's 1890 Pension Declaration with "John Heitzenberger" (as written by the affidavit clerk; surname-family identified as Hitzelberger — individual ID open).
  2. The 1883 Christina Hahl remarriage: When Christina (Katherine's mother) was widowed by Michael Misch's 1882 death, she remarried Theodore Gottlob Sr. — bringing the Misch and Gottlob families into shared parentage.
  3. The 1895 Konsen-Misch marriage: Charles Gannsen's son Erwin Carl Konsen (Charles Johnson Jr.) married Katherine Misch on 21 December 1895 in St. Louis. The Konsen / Johnson and Misch / Gottlob lines were now family by marriage, not just neighbors.

Frederick Charles Johnson (b. 1905, Erwin × Katherine's son) was therefore born into a household carrying both Charles Gannsen ancestry (paternal) and Theodore Gottlob Sr. ancestry by re-marriage (maternal-half). His name preserves both connections: "Frederick" honors his maternal-half-uncle Frederick Gottlob; "Charles" honors his paternal grandfather Charles Gannsen and his father Charles Johnson Jr.
