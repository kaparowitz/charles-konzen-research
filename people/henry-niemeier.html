Henry Niemeier

26-year acquaintance · 1891 pension witness · north-side German immigrant (Charles was south-side)
Limited record Pension witness · 1891 Knew Charles since 1865

★ At a Glance

✓ ID Confirmed
CIVILIAN WITNESS · 26-YEAR ACQUAINTANCE

The longest-tenure civilian witness in Charles's pension file — a Westphalia-born acquaintance whose 1890 affidavit attested to 26 years of personal acquaintance.

Henry Niemeier (Herman Heinrich Niemeyer · FS LKY7-31G · b. 11 April 1840 Westphalia — Tecklenburg/Lengerich district per surname cluster · d. 22 January 1908 Old Monroe, Lincoln County, MO) is the longest-tenure civilian acquaintance witness in Charles's pension file. His 15 October 1891 General Affidavit attests 26 unbroken years of intimate acquaintance, beginning 1865 — the year Charles came home from the war. He swears Charles was already hard of hearing at first acquaintance in 1865 and that the deafness has worsened since.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Niemeier's testimony bridges the 1865-1890 gap in Charles's medical history that the pension file otherwise covers only at its endpoints. By placing Charles's hearing loss at 1865 — not 1890 when the pension was filed — Niemeier's account is consistent with the Battery-B comrades' affidavits placing the origin of the deafness at the September 1863 New Madrid cannonading, two years earlier. He is a north-side German immigrant (1300 N. 9th Street) — a different St. Louis sub-community from Charles's south-side Soulard / DeKalb / Sidney neighborhood — making his testimony cross-network rather than coming from Charles's immediate parish circle.

What he swore to

General Affidavit · 15 October 18[91?]

Attests that he has been intimately acquainted with Charles Gannson since 1865 — the year of Charles's Civil War discharge — and through the entire 26 intervening years. Specifically attests:

  • Charles was already hard of hearing at the time of first acquaintance (1865), requiring the affiant to "speak to him very loud" to be understood;
  • The hearing has "grown worse & worse year by year to the present time";
  • Charles has suffered continuously from rheumatism and rupture through the entire 26-year acquaintance.

Source: Pension File F41-523680269E, Page 20 (verso jurat Page 21). Filed by Fred. W. Fout, U.S. Claim Agent.

Why he matters

Niemeier's testimony is the longest-tenure civilian acquaintance affidavit in the pension file — 26 years, beginning the year Charles came home from the war. His statement that Charles was already hard of hearing at first acquaintance in 1865 is independently consistent with the Battery B comrades' testimony that the hearing loss originated from cannonading at New Madrid in September 1863, two years earlier. Civilian and military testimony align on the same disability narrative.

Henry Niemeier's address (1300 N. 9th Street) sits in the German-immigrant district on the city's north side, between downtown and the historic Hyde Park / Bremen neighborhood. Charles, by contrast, lived on the south side at 2408 DeKalb in Kosciusko (between Soulard and the river). The two were therefore not next-door neighbors at the time of the 1891 declaration; the 26-year acquaintance reaches back to 1865, when Charles first lived at 1518 S. 7th in LaSalle Park — also a south-side address. The earliest overlap may instead trace to shared parish / fraternal ties, or to Charles's 1869–1874 railroad-conductor period on Gates Street (modern equivalent unresolved), or to his 1880 residence at 1534 Jackson Street (south-central Ward 9, not the modern north-side Jackson).

★ Multi-database investigation (3 May 2026)

1880 US Federal Census, St. Louis (ED 308) — full household

FS Records returned a strong direct match: Henry Niemeier, 1880 US Census, St. Louis, ED 308. Full household:

  • Henry Niemeier · Self / Head · M · 41 · b. ~1839 Germany · Married · Occupation: Works In Saw Mill · Father's birthplace Germany, Mother's birthplace Germany
  • Caroline Niemeier · wife · F · 40 · b. Germany
  • Henry Niemeier Jr. · son · M · 15 · b. Missouri · ~1865
  • Caroline Niemeier · daughter · F · 13 · b. Missouri · ~1867
  • William Niemeier · son · M · 12 · b. Missouri · ~1868
  • Emma Niemeier · daughter · F · 6 · b. Missouri · ~1874

Critical timeline check: the eldest son Henry Jr. was born ~1865 in Missouri — exactly the year his father Henry Sr. swore he met Charles Gannsen. This independently confirms the affidavit timeline: Henry & Caroline arrived in St. Louis circa 1864–1865 (around the same time Charles came home from the war in late 1865), met Charles immediately, and their friendship spans the next 26 years.

FamilySearch tree person — Herman Heinrich Niemeyer (LKY7-31G)

The 1880 census record is attached to the FS tree person Herman Heinrich Niemeyer (LKY7-31G):

  • Birth: 11 April 1840 (vs. census-implied 1839 — within the typical census imprecision window)
  • Death: 22 January 1908, Old Monroe, Lincoln County, Missouri — ~50 mi north of St. Louis. Henry Sr. relocated out of St. Louis to Lincoln County after Charles's 1903 death; he died there at age 67/68.
  • Wife on FS tree: Cathrina Goldschmidt (1840-1903, MSZB-GL6). The 1880 census recorded her as "Caroline" — likely a multi-part baptismal name (e.g., "Anna Caroline Cathrina") with different documents picking different parts.
  • Children documented on FS tree: Heinrich (1866-Deceased), Caroline (1868-Deceased), and three more (full list visible in the Family Members panel)

St. Marcus Marriage Index 1847–1865 (Buecher) — possible Niemeyer kin network

Two relevant Niemeyer/Niemeier marriages in this 18-year window:

  • Heinrich Niemeyer × Anna Rohmann · both "from St. Louis, Mo." · 1 December 1861 · entry #710. Both St. Louis-born — this is not our Henry (who was Germany-born), but is plausibly an uncle, cousin, or relative whose family emigrated earlier. Establishes a Niemeyer-family network at St. Marcus before our Henry's 1864-65 arrival.
  • Elisabetha Niemeier (Lengerich, Kreis Tecklenburg, Preussen) × Dietrich Cordes (Eystrub, Amt Hoya, Hannover) · 22 May 1851 · entry #137. Lengerich, Tecklenburg in Westphalia is a strong candidate region for our Henry's origin — Niemeier is a documented Westphalian / Lower Saxon surname, and the parish-clerk's "Niemeier" spelling preserves the Westphalian -ei- ending (vs. the Hannoverian -ey- in Niemeyer).

Notably, Buecher's Burial Index 1847–1879 returned 0 Niemeier/Niemeyer entries — Henry was alive past 1879, consistent with his October 1891 affidavit.

Probable region of origin — Westphalia / Lower Saxony, Prussia

Combined evidence: surname pattern (Niemeier/Niemeyer is concentrated in Westphalia and Hannover), the 1851 St. Marcus Niemeier marriage from Lengerich/Tecklenburg-Westphalia, and Henry's 1908 death in Old Monroe, Lincoln County, Missouri — a known German-immigrant farming area. Probable origin: Westphalia or Tecklenburg, Prussia / Lower Saxony — distinct from Charles Gannsen's Hessen-Hofgeismar origin, but closer to it than either Jacob Reck's Pfalz or August Derr's Saxony origins. Confirms Charles-Niemeier connection was formed in St. Louis, not transatlantic.
