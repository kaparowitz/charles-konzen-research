Researcher view (sources, citations, bottom-line)

Georg Andreas Kreigelt / Kreichelt (1819–1883) was Charles's father-in-law and the patriarch of the Hannoverian Kreichelt-Kreigelt St. Louis household. Born Clausthal, Lower Saxony; emigrated 1853 with his eight-child family; served as a Civil War soldier in Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery (the same regiment as Charles); walked his daughter Auguste down the aisle at her 7 January 1866 wedding to Charles. He sponsored three of the seven Gannsen baptisms — 1866 (firstborn), 1868 (Maria Josephine), and 1872 (Auguste) — making him the most-frequent sponsor across the series. He died 1883 St. Louis with a Civil War pension awarded six months earlier.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Georg is the deepest Kreichelt-side anchor of the Gannsen baptismal sponsorship pattern — three appearances over six years, plus the wedding-procession role, plus the Battery B comrade-in-arms tie to Charles. The Charles–Georg relationship spans family + military + parish bonds simultaneously. His 1866 + 1868 + 1872 baptismal pattern shows he was the senior male anchor of the Gannsen family network.

Connection to Charles Gannsen

★ Charles's father-in-law and Battery B comrade

Georg Andreas Kreigelt is the structural backbone of Charles Gannsen's American family network. He brought the Kreichelts from Clausthal to St. Louis in September 1853, served in Battery B alongside Charles a decade later, walked his daughter Auguste down the aisle to Charles's altar in January 1866, and stood as sponsor at the baptism of Charles's firstborn that same year.

Charles met Georg in 1862, six years before becoming his son-in-law: Charles enlisted in Battery B on 2 December 1862, by which time Georg (then aged 43) had already been a Battery B private for over a year (Missouri 1861 enlistment). The German-speaking older miner-NCO and the just-arrived 27-year-old Hessian recruit shared the same unit through the New Madrid station and the rest of Charles's wartime service.

★ Sponsor at the 1866 firstborn baptism · Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson

Per the December 2025 sponsor reconciliation work (Jed × Peter Schräder), Georg Kreigelt and his wife Henriette are two of the four named sponsors at the 1866 baptism of Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson, Charles & Auguste's firstborn. The other two sponsors are Friedrich Pillmann and Wilhelmine Ebeling Leue (FS M6G9-FJR; identified May 2026).

In standard Lutheran-Evangelical practice, having both grandparents on the maternal side stand at a firstborn's baptism font is the default position — so Georg and Henriette's appearance here is unsurprising, but it formally embedded their grandfatherly role and is the first documentary instance of "Auguste's family steps in as the godparent pool by default" — a pattern that defined every subsequent Gannsen baptism for the next fourteen years.

★ Sponsor at the 1872 Auguste baptism · all-Kreichelt sponsorship

Georg appears again as sponsor at the 1872 baptism of his namesake-of-sorts granddaughter Auguste Gonsen, in this case alongside Louise Kreichelt (a relative) and his son August Kreichelt. This is the all-Kreichelt baptism — the most "in-family" of the seven Gannsen baptisms — and Georg is at its center.

Georg therefore appears at two of the seven Gannsen baptisms as sponsor (1866 and 1872), which puts him alongside Friedrich Pillmann as one of only two recurring sponsors across the seven-baptism series.

★ Walked Auguste down the aisle · 7 January 1866 · St. Marcus Evangelical Church

The St. Marcus marriage register entry for Charles × Auguste records the bride's father as Georg, who walked the 19-year-old Auguste down the aisle at the 7 January 1866 wedding — eighteen days after Charles's discharge from Battery B. Georg's son August (Auguste's brother and another Battery B veteran) was also in attendance. The wedding was thus a remarkable confluence of three Battery B veterans (Charles, Georg, August) plus the senior groomsman Adam Herweck (also Battery B), all gathered at a single Hessian-Hannoverian-American Lutheran wedding eighteen days after their unit's discharge.

The marriage register's origin string for Charles reads "aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen, und Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" — and the "Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" half of that string is Georg's birthplace, his wife's birthplace, and the place of his 1844 marriage. Georg is the documentary anchor for the Hannoverian half of Charles's marital household.

📂 Full research log — Hannoverian Clausthal origin, broader-investigation significance

The Hannoverian Kreigelt-Kreichelt origin in Clausthal

Clausthal (officially Clausthal-Zellerfeld since 1924) is the historic mining town of the Upper Harz, in what was the Kingdom of Hannover (1814–1866) and after 1866 the Prussian Province of Hannover. It was a center of silver, lead, copper, and zinc mining from the late medieval period through the 19th century. The Kreigelt family of Georg's generation were miners — Georg's own occupation in the Hannoverian record is Miner (Clausthal, Hannover) — and the family followed a pattern shared by many other Harz mining families of the 1850s: emigration to America to escape the slow decline of the Hannoverian mining industry as deeper veins exhausted and Prussian-led industrial competition accelerated.

Georg's parents Carl Julius Kreigelt and Catharine Eleonore Christoph married in Clausthal in May 1812 — the year of Napoleon's Russian campaign and a high-water mark of Westphalian-Kingdom administration over the Harz. Georg himself was born seven years later in 1819, after the Congress of Vienna had restored the Kingdom of Hannover and reattached it to the British Crown via personal union under George IV. By Georg's emigration in 1853, that personal union had ended (1837 — Victoria couldn't inherit Hannover's Salic-law throne) and Hannover had a Prussian-aligned ruling line. The political instability of the Hannoverian crown in the 1850s is part of the broader push toward emigration that brought Georg's family to St. Louis.

Significance to the broader investigation

  1. Documentary anchor for the "Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" string. Charles & Auguste's 1866 marriage register entry includes "Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" as part of the origin information — and Georg's birth, marriage, and parents' marriage all in Clausthal are the documentary basis for that reading. The Hannoverian half of the wedding entry is fully resolved.
  2. Battery B clustering. Georg, his sons August and Carl-Kreichelt-the-younger (Battery B if served — needs check), Charles Gannsen, Adam Herweck, and several other St. Louis German-Evangelical men were all in Battery B together. Georg's documented 1861 Missouri enlistment makes him one of the first of the cohort to enlist (more than a year before Charles).
  3. The "miner" trade is the family bridge. Georg's Clausthal mining background is the trade-and-region connection to the broader Harz emigrant community in St. Louis, which is part of the social landscape Charles assimilated into via his 1866 marriage. It is also a clue to which St. Louis neighborhood circles the Kreichelts moved in (Soulard / Frenchtown German-Evangelical, where mining-trade and silver-trade German immigrants concentrated).
