Jacob Reck ✓

FamilySearch KLPB-52X · Pfalz-born German Evangelisch-Reformierte (Calvinist) · co-sponsor at the 1 June 1868 St. Marcus baptism of Maria Josephine Gonnson · buried Old St. Marcus Cemetery alongside Charles Gannsen

★ At a Glance

✓ ID Confirmed
SPONSOR PROFILE · IDENTIFIED Latest research pass: 7 May 2026

A Bavarian-born St. Louis Calvinist who co-sponsored the 1868 baptism of Charles and Auguste's second child, and was later buried alongside Charles at Old St. Marcus Cemetery.

Jacob Reck is fully identified as FS KLPB-52X — born 28 January 1837 Freinsheim, Pfalz, Bavaria, christened German Reformed (Calvinist), the son of Georg Conrad Reck × Sophia Schmidt. He arrived NY on 20 November 1865 on the ship Louis Henry from Bremen at age 26, listed as Farmer (Germans to America Index, FS ark:/61903/1:1:KD7J-CTS). In St. Louis he had three successive marriages — Josephine née von Delien (~1866–1867, died ~1868–69), Emma Weingardt (1869–1891), and Frances née Bertke Stanze (1893–1898) — plus four documented children with Emma. He died 29 May 1898 and was buried at Old Saint Marcus Cemetery, the same parish-circle burial ground where Charles Gannsen was interred five years later.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Jacob is one of a small handful of non-Kreichelt-side sponsors across the seven Gannsen baptisms — alongside Erwin Spraul (1875 Erwin Carl baptism), Maria Agnes Götz (also 1868), and Wilhelmine Ebeling (1866 firstborn). His Pfalz birth (~250 km south of Charles's Trendelburg) makes a shared-home-village old-country tie very unlikely — the Charles–Jacob bond was St. Louis-formed through the St. Marcus parish circle. The Old St. Marcus Cemetery alignment with Charles is one of the strongest single pieces of cemetery cross-reference evidence in the project.

Why this matters for the Charles Gannsen investigation

Jacob is one of a small group of non-Kreichelt-side sponsors across the seven Gannsen baptisms (alongside Erwin Spraul at the 1875 Erwin Carl baptism, Maria Agnes Götz at the same 1868 baptism, and Wilhelmine Ebeling at the 1866 firstborn). Where most other sponsors trace to Auguste's Hannoverian / Albrecht / Pillmann / Mincke kin network, Jacob and his first wife Josephine stand outside that family circle — meaning the Reck-Gannsen bond reflects a direct Charles-Reck friendship or church-circle tie, not a marriage-network connection.

Three findings sharpen the picture:

  1. Jacob's Pfalz birth (Freinsheim, 1837) places his German origin ~250 km south of Charles's Trendelburg (Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis). The two men did not share a German home village or even a region. So the bond was formed in St. Louis, almost certainly through the St. Marcus parish circle and/or the broader German-immigrant Union-veteran community. This rules out the previously-floated "Reck couple = pre-emigration old-country tie pointing to Charles's origin" hypothesis.
  2. Jacob arrived 20 Nov 1865, six weeks before Charles & Auguste's 7 January 1866 marriage at St. Marcus. The window for a St. Louis-formed Reck-Gannsen relationship between November 1865 and June 1868 (the Maria Josephine baptism) is exactly ~31 months — long enough for a parish-level friendship to grow but short enough to suggest the bond was forged with intentional speed.
  3. The Old St. Marcus Cemetery alignment is the strongest single piece of cemetery cross-reference evidence in the project. Two parish-circle figures, ~5 years apart, in the same plot of consecrated south-side ground (Jacob 1898, Charles 1903 #3393). Although Jacob was baptized Reformed (Calvinist) at Freinsheim in 1837, by his 1898 death he had been integrated into the Lutheran/Evangelical St. Marcus parish community to which the Gannsens belonged. The Charles–Jacob bond formed in 1866–1868 and persisted to the cemetery thirty years later.

The 1898 Westliche Post obituary  CANONICAL SOURCE

"Reck" · Westliche Post · 30 May 1898

Per the obituary as transcribed via Find a Grave Memorial #249141012: Jacob Reck died 29 May 1898 at age 61. Survived by:

  • Widow Frances née Bertke Stanze (third wife; German Catholic widow with two Stanze stepsons from her prior marriage)
  • Four Reck children (with Emma Weingardt, second wife): Emma W, Rose Otielia, Oscar R, Arthur Jacob
  • Two Stanze stepsons via Frances's prior marriage

Buried Old Saint Marcus Cemetery (the south-side German Lutheran-Evangelical parish-circle burial ground). The Westliche Post obituary is the canonical source confirming his three-marriage life, the children's identities, and the Lutheran-Evangelical funeral arrangements (a German Reformed birth congregation in Freinsheim → St. Louis Lutheran burial 61 years later).

Cross-reference: Charles Gannsen was buried at the same cemetery (St. Marcus Burial #3393) on 19 December 1903 — five years and seven months after Jacob.

The occupation arc — 1865 manifest Farmer; 1880 census G. Agent — is a classic German-immigrant upward-mobility pattern. Fifteen years from peasant-farmer arrival to St. Louis white-collar agent. By 1880 Jacob had ascended into the comfortable middle class. Charles Gannsen in the same year was at 1316 Howard Street working in industrial labor — their economic positions had diverged by 1880, but in 1866–1869 (when they were both newly-immigrant Germans in their early thirties making St. Louis lives) they were peers.

★ Note on a previously-attributed Civil War service — likely an FS-tree merge error  CAVEAT

An earlier framing on this site placed Jacob in the 2nd Missouri US Reserve Corps Infantry, Co. B, spring 1861. That attribution is now in serious doubt. The 90-day Missouri Reserve Corps regiments were raised April–July 1861 in St. Louis from German-immigrant Unionists already resident in the city. Jacob arrived 20 November 1865 — months after Lee's surrender on 9 April 1865. He was not in St. Louis during the war and could not have served in any 1861–1865 Missouri regiment. The 1862-dated VA Master Index source attached to KLPB-52X (a 1917–1940 publication) is most likely erroneously merged from a different Jacob Reck. The site's Pension Mutual-Aid Network theme should not list Jacob as a fellow Civil War veteran.

Adjacent-Battery-C observation kept separate: a NPS Soldiers and Sailors search returned one Reck in Charles's regiment — Frederick Reck, Pvt., Company C, 2nd Regiment Missouri Light Artillery (NARA M390, Roll 39). This is the same regiment as Charles (Company B), one company over. Frederick may be Jacob's brother, cousin, neighbor, or wholly unrelated; nothing yet documents the connection. The earlier collapsing of Jacob onto Frederick (German-script J/F ambiguity) has been dropped.
