Helmarshausen — parish northeast of Deisel

Historical overview

Helmarshausen is a medieval town with deep monastic roots — the Benedictine Abbey of Helmarshausen (founded 944, dissolved during the Reformation in 1538) was one of the most important scriptoria of medieval Germany, producing the famous Gospel of Henry the Lion in 1188. After the abbey's dissolution the town became a small Lutheran market town serving the Diemel-Weser confluence area.

By the 19th century Helmarshausen was a typical Hessian Ackerbürger town of ~700–900 inhabitants, organized around its parish church. The parish records from 1820 onward are well-preserved on Archion as KB volumes covering baptism, marriage, and death entries.

Helmarshausen passed to Prussia along with Kurhessen in September 1866 and became part of Regierungsbezirk Kassel, Provinz Hessen-Nassau. In modern times it is part of Stadt Bad Karlshafen, the spa town that developed at the Diemel-Weser confluence.

Significance to the investigation

Helmarshausen was one of seven parishes searched for a Joh. Heinrich Konze × Wilhelmine Hildebrand marriage — a parent pair drawn from the Entry 195 reading later retracted (corrected 4 May 2026). The marriage register 1820–1860 was fully indexed (Helmarshausen complete index search, 10 April 2026, plus Session 63 follow-up on 25 April 2026). No Konze × Hildebrand marriage record exists in this parish for the relevant date range. The parish is closed as a candidate.

The proximity to Deisel (~3 km) made Helmarshausen a natural candidate for the Konze marriage; the negative finding narrows the search to remaining undigitized parishes (notably Heisebeck) and pre-1822 Schöneberg.
