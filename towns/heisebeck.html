Historical overview

Heisebeck is a small village in the Wesertal in the far north of Hesse — near the Weser and the Lower-Saxony border, northeast of Hofgeismar — with a typical Hessian agricultural population. The village had its own Lutheran parish church serving the immediate community (population ~250–350 in the mid-19th century). Records of the parish go back to the late 17th century but are not digitized on Archion for the pre-1845 window — a critical gap given the investigation's focus on a marriage that would have occurred in the 1820s–1830s.

The village passed to Prussia along with Kurhessen in September 1866 and became part of Stadt Hofgeismar in modern administrative consolidations.

Significance to the investigation

★ Heisebeck is the most important undigitized parish in the entire investigation. Its significance is that its pre-1845 Lutheran parish registers are not digitized on Archion and have never been searched at all — it is the principal unsearched parish remaining in the Hofgeismar-area search radius, while the other candidate parishes have all been worked through. Any Charles-shaped baptism or relevant marriage in this gap would be invisible until the original volumes are examined.

Sessions 66 + 67 (26 April 2026) verified that Heisebeck pre-1845 records are NOT on Archion. The remaining productive paths are:

  • Direct inquiry to Landeskirchliches Archiv Kassel — they hold the original Heisebeck parish books
  • In-person research at the Kassel archive
  • Engagement of a local researcher to scan / photograph the relevant volumes

Heisebeck remains the single highest-priority next step in the German-side search.
