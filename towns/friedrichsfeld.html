Historical overview

Friedrichsfeld is a small Hessian colonial-era settlement founded in 1755 by Landgraf Friedrich II of Hesse-Kassel as a Huguenot-style refugee colony, on land cleared specifically for the purpose. The original 30 settler families were predominantly French Huguenot exiles from the Pfalz and Saarland, but the village quickly absorbed Hessian families through intermarriage. By the 1830s the population was ~250–300 and Friedrichsfeld functioned as a typical small Lutheran agricultural village within Kreis Hofgeismar.

The village's administrative church book is KB 218959 — the principal Friedrichsfeld parish register volume. The Hessian state archive HStAM Marburg holds emigration permits, military conscription rolls, and Volkszählung schedules under Bestand 180 (Landratsamt Hofgeismar).

During the Austro-Prussian War of 1866 Friedrichsfeld passed to Prussia along with the rest of Kurhessen and became part of Provinz Hessen-Nassau. Modern administrative status: Ortsteil of Stadt Trendelburg.

Significance to the investigation

Friedrichsfeld was one of seven parishes searched for a Joh. Heinrich Konze × Wilhelmine Hildebrand marriage — a parent pair drawn from the Entry 195 reading later retracted (corrected 4 May 2026). The KB 218959 marriage section 1820–1840 was searched comprehensively (Session 22, 18 April 2026); no Konze × Hildebrand marriage record found.

The village is also the seat of one of the Quentin candidates from the LAGIS Hessen emigration register: Auguste Quentin (Friedrichsfeld 1853, ledig — i.e., single/unmarried at emigration). The Quentin connection has its own thread in the Schräder Correspondence; Pass 6 (1 May 2026) located a candidate match in the 1860 Cincinnati census.

A complete 1830–1836 baptism search of KB 218959 (Session 9, 9 April 2026) was also negative for any Charles-shaped baptism candidate.
