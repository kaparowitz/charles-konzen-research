Historical overview

Trendelburg sits on the west bank of the Diemel river at the northern edge of historic Kurhessen, dominated by the medieval Burg Trendelburg — a 13th-century hilltop castle that gives the town its name. The town received its Stadtrechte (town rights) in 1305 and developed as a small market town serving the agricultural villages of the surrounding Diemel valley. Through the 18th and 19th centuries Trendelburg was a typical Hessian Ackerbürger town: townspeople who worked their own farms, with a small artisan and council class layered on top.

The Kontze / Konze / Contze family appears in Trendelburg parish records from at least 1773, when Arnold Philipp Kontze is recorded as Rathsverwandter und Ackermann (councilman and farmer). Three generations of the family held the same position into the 1830s. The family did not move from Trendelburg to Deisel as a unit — but individual Konze branches established farms in Deisel (a satellite village about 2 km south) by the early 19th century, which is why the 1835 Deisel baptisms (Entries 194 and 195) appear in the Trendelburg-administered KB 218842.

During the Austro-Prussian War of 1866 (June–August), Trendelburg was occupied by Prussian troops with no major resistance. The town became part of the Prussian Province of Hesse-Nassau on 20 September 1866 and lost its independent administrative status; civil affairs from 1874 onward were handled by the Standesamt Trendelburg under Prussian law.

Religious composition · Peter Schräder's Deisel data (20 Nov 2025)

From a book on Deisel that Peter Schräder studied (cited in his 20 November 2025 email): the religious composition of Deisel — the Trendelburg-parish village where Charles claims to have come from — in roughly the mid-19th century:

Total population1,118 people
Evangelical / Lutheran1,095  (97.94 %)
Catholic5  (0.45 %)
Jewish17  (1.52 %)

Peter's 20 November 2025 email noted: "perhaps we're searching in the wrong community" — the thought being that if Charles wasn't Evangelical, he wouldn't appear in any of the Lutheran parish records that have been searched. With only ~5 Catholics in Deisel and ~17 Jews, the non-Evangelical population is small enough that Peter could exhaustively check both:

  • Catholic check: Peter checked the Catholic records (those few Catholic families would have been registered at the nearest Catholic parish, west of the Weser in Westfalia, e.g. Wehrden). The Catholic record check came back negative — no Charles, no Gonnson, no obvious match. The Catholic-baptism theory for why Charles couldn't be found in the Lutheran records was ruled out.
  • Jewish check: Peter pulled the LAGIS online summary of the Jewish cemetery in Trendelburg (which served Deisel), and the transcribed gravestones contain no Gonson family name. The 1740 Trendelburg "Protected Jews" list and the ~1750 tax registers of Deisel/Trendelburg (covering every citizen, all faiths) were reviewed during the now set-aside Jewish-paternal-line inquiry.

Source: Peter Schräder email "the research" · 20 November 2025 · Stadt Trendelburg historical-statistical book about Deisel.

Significance to the investigation

★ Trendelburg is the earliest documented Konze paper trail. The reconstructed Konze line in the Family Tree section of this site is anchored in Trendelburg burial-register entries 1780–1830. The town parish KB 218842 contains Entry 195, a 23–24 January 1835 emergency baptism that was for several weeks the leading candidate for Charles's birth record — until Peter Schräder's 4 May 2026 re-reading of the original Kurrent script showed it is the child of a Köster day-labourer (no recorded name, born and died within three days, never baptised). Entry 195 is eliminated as a Charles candidate.

Sessions concentrated on Trendelburg uncovered:

  • Arnold Philipp Kontze — d. 3 April 1780, age 71, Rathsverwandter und Ackermann
  • Herr Andreas Contze — d. 7 November 1800, age 78, Rathsverwandter
  • Johann Christoph Contze — Grenadier in Leib-Grenadier-Bataillon, d. 1814
  • Andreas Kontze (b. 1787, d. 1830) — confirmed Easter 1801; two marriages, multiple children
  • Justus Ernst Konze — b. 14 November 1829; last documented at Pfingsten 1843 confirmation in Deisel; emigration unknown

📜 How the spelling evolved — from Konde/Konten and Kontze in the early 1700s, through the feminine Kontzin and the Contze C-spellings, to the stable Konze by Charles’s day (and its fossil Konzen in America): see the dated surname-spelling timeline and the morphology of the ending (with clickable audio & sources).

Peter Schräder's 12 April 2026 verdict, after checking his own Konze database against Deisel-area emigration lists: "Charles is not a legitimate son of the Konze family". The Trendelburg Konze line is preserved in the project as the most thoroughly documented Hessian context to which the surname KONSEN points, but Charles is no longer placed inside it as a biological descendant.

HStAM Marburg · Arcinsys

The Hessian state archive holds Kurhessen administrative files (1815–1866) including emigration permits, military conscription rolls, and tax registers. Search for "Trendelburg" in Arcinsys.

Standesamt Trendelburg
