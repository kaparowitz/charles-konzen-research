# Charles Johnson Jr. / Erwin Carl Konsen (KL18-V2Y) — Life Sketch

Charles Johnson Jr. was born Erwin Carl Konsen on 27 August 1875 in St. Louis, Missouri, the sixth of seven children of Charles Johnson Sr., a Hessian-born immigrant and Civil War artillery sergeant, and Auguste Kreichelt, born in Clausthal, Hanover. He was baptized 3 October 1875 at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church (register p. 453, no. 8854), named for his baptismal sponsor Erwin Spraul — superintendent of the Anheuser-Busch Brewing Association and a fellow German immigrant his father admired — and for his father Carl, who stood as co-sponsor. The parish clerk wrote the family surname as "Konsen," the only Hesse-style spelling of the name in the entire American record trail, and today a key piece of evidence connecting the family to its Hessian origins.

He grew up in the family's German Evangelical world on the south side of St. Louis, appearing as a boy in the 1880 census household. As an adult he took his father's American name and went by Charles Johnson Jr. — fitting, since he was the only son to survive childhood and the one who carried the name into the next generation.

On 21 December 1895, at age twenty, he married Katherine Misch (1875–1948) at St. Marcus. His mother died the following December. Charles and Katherine had three sons — Wilhelm Thomas (1896–1902), Henry Charles (1903–1989), and Frederick Charles (1905–1947) — and his sister Louise stood as baptismal sponsor for all three. He knew the same parental grief his own parents had known: Wilhelm died before his sixth birthday, Fred died at forty-two, and only Henry outlived him.

He was especially close to his father. The two lived together or near each other until Charles Sr.'s death in 1903, when Charles Jr. was twenty-eight, and they were laid in the same family plot. His father's 1898 pension declaration lists him as "Charles Gannson, Jr." — one more spelling in the family's long surname story.

His adult life was rooted on Sidney Street: the censuses of 1900 through 1940 find him at numbers 168, 170, and 175 Sidney Street, St. Louis. He worked as a watchman at a foundry and was a member of Building Service Employees Union Local No. 50. His son Fred later worked as a bottler at Anheuser-Busch — an echo, a generation on, of the brewery superintendent for whom Charles had been named.

Katherine died in 1948. Charles died 13 September 1950, two weeks past his seventy-fifth birthday, of pulmonary tuberculosis after an extended stay at Robert Koch Hospital in St. Louis. He was buried 16 September 1950 at Old St. Marcus Cemetery, in the family plot he shares with his father.
