About Charles & the project

Who was Charles Konzen?

A Hessian-born immigrant who arrived in the United States around 1859, served as a sergeant in Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery during the U.S. Civil War (1862–1865), married Auguste Kreichelt at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church in St. Louis on 7 January 1866, fathered seven children documented in the parish register between 1866 and 1880, was awarded a federal pension for war-onset rheumatism in 1891, and died in St. Louis on 16 December 1903. He was buried three days later, 19 December 1903, at Old Saint Marcus Cemetery (burial #3393).

The first known photograph of him — taken at the Louis Roth studio in St. Louis between 1896 and 1903 — was discovered in September 2025 by a descendant in Independence, Missouri. It is the home-page hero on this site.

Why is this project running? What's the open question?

Charles never named his exact German parish on any U.S. record. The closest he came was "Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" on his 1866 marriage register — phonetically the village of Deisel, in the Kreis Hofgeismar of the former Electorate of Hesse-Kassel. But the specific German parish baptismal entry recording his birth has never been recovered, despite multi-year searches across every digitised parish register in the surrounding 5 km × 5 km area.

The two open questions, in plain English, are: (a) what was his actual German surname? and (b) which parish recorded his baptism on or around 26 January 1835? See the Open Questions page for the precise gaps and what would resolve each one.

What's the project's working answer right now?

As of May 2026, the project's surname conclusion is that Charles's German-side surname was most likely Konzen — the Kontzen-family form documented in the Trendelburg parish complex of Churhessen, ~5 km north of Deisel. The argument is built from an nine-distinct-rendering surname trail across at least five U.S. scribes (Gonnson · Gansen · Gonsen · Konsen · Gonson · Jansen · Konzen · Gannsen · Conson); see Key Evidence Card 1 for the full convergent-evidence reasoning, or the Visualize hub for the diagrammatic version.

This conclusion is held in the project's Working state, not Confirmed, because the specific German baptismal entry has not yet been located.

Why so many surnames?

Why is he sometimes called Gannsen, Konzen, or Johnson?

His German surname was recorded in nine different American spellings by at least five different scribes between 1866 and 1896 — Gonnson, Gansen, Gonsen, Konsen, Gonson, Jansen, Konzen, Gannsen, and Conson. The family ultimately Anglicised to Johnson by the late nineteenth century. The single Konzen (with z) attestation is on the 1890 death record of daughter Auguste, reading "Auguste Somers geb Konzen" — the only American record carrying the full Hessian z-form documented in the Trendelburg parish.

The 1862 Civil War enlistment used the spelling Gannsen, and that's how he appears in his service and pension records. Most of the U.S. records use one or another phonetic capture of his actual German name. The May 2026 project conclusion is that the German form was Konzen.

This site uses the convention: "Charles Konzen" for his German-side identity, "Charles Gannsen" or "Charles Gannsen / Johnson" for U.S.-life references between 1859 and 1903. See the Methodology page for the full convention.

What's the "Konsen" puzzle I keep reading about?

Of the nine American spellings (Gonnson · Gansen · Gonsen · Konsen · Gonson · Jansen · Konzen · Gannsen · Conson, recorded across nine attestations 1866–1896), Konsen and Konzen are the closest to the original German Konzen / Kontzen / Kontze cluster. Konsen (1875) preserves the K and the German -en suffix, with only the tz simplifying to s — American clerks regularly captured the German tz [ts]-affricate as a simple s. Konzen (1890) is the single American attestation of the full Hessian z-form documented in the Trendelburg parish complex. ("Konsen" with an s is the closest American phonetic capture to the Hessian cluster, but is not itself one of the five attested Hesse-parish spellings — see The Konzen Puzzle for the parish-side cluster.)

The 1875 Konsen (Pastor Hoffmann, Swiss-born German speaker) and the 1890 Konzen (his daughter Auguste's death record) bracket the trail: two -en-preserving forms by two different scribes, fifteen years apart. Maiden-name attestations on death records are among the most carefully captured forms in genealogical practice. Together they are the strongest evidence in the project that the family kept the Hessian -en suffix in continuous use through 1890. For the full analysis, see The Konzen Puzzle.

Family, descendants, and "am I related?"

Am I related to Charles?

There are two surviving descendant lines from Charles, both traceable through his son Erwin Carl Konsen (Charles Johnson Jr., 1875–1950) and his daughter Bertha Johnson (1880–1959). Many descendants today carry the Johnson surname; some lines married into other surnames in the early twentieth century.

The Family Tree tab shows the reconstructed multi-generation tree with FamilySearch IDs for each known descendant. The People in the Story tab lists ~80 named individuals connected to Charles's life. If you believe you are related and your line isn't represented, please get in touch.

What were the seven children's names?

Charles and Auguste's seven children, all baptised at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church in St. Louis:

  • Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson · b. 20 June 1866 · d. 26 October 1867 (firstborn, named after Friedrich Wilhelm I, the deposed Elector of Hesse-Kassel)
  • Josephine Maria · 1868–1890 · died at 22
  • Katherine Carolina · 1870–1949 · lived to 79
  • Auguste · 1872–1890 · died at 18
  • Louise · 1873–1947
  • Erwin Carl Konsen (Charles Johnson Jr.) · 1875–1950 — the surname-preservation key in the parish register
  • Bertha · 1880–1959

Each has its own profile page; see The Seven Children index if it exists, or the Family Tree tab.

Who was Auguste Kreichelt?

Auguste Kreichelt (1847–1896) was Charles's wife of thirty years. She was born 12 May 1847 in Clausthal, Kingdom of Hannover, and emigrated with her family in 1853 (age ~6) — six years before Charles. Her parents were Georg Kreichelt and Louise Friederike Henriette née Weiss (who appears across the parish records under both her formal first name "Louise" and her family call name "Henriette"). Auguste had several siblings — Carl, August, Bertha Barbara ("Babette"), Julius — and the Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann-Mincke kin network supplied the named sponsors at all seven of Charles & Auguste's baptisms.

Auguste died in 1896, eight years before Charles. Her death record is the source of the "Conson" spelling — the strongest single Charles-self-attestation of his surname (Charles was the surviving informant; Pastor Eilts wrote what he heard).

Sources & method

What sources does this site use?

Primary sources fall into three classes:

  • U.S. military records — the Compiled Service Record (NARA M405, file G11-422819443E) and the full Pension File (F41-523680269E), obtained as mail-order packets from the National Archives.
  • German parish registers — digitised Kirchenbücher of Trendelburg, Deisel, Friedrichsfeld, Helmarshausen, Hofgeismar, and surrounding Churhessen parishes, accessed via Archion.de. Emigration permits and military registers via Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg through Arcinsys and LAGIS.
  • U.S. vital, immigration, and census records — FamilySearch.org, Ancestry.com, Find a Grave.

For the full bibliography, see the Bibliography page.

What do the colored badges (Confirmed / Working / Open / Eliminated) mean?

Every claim on the site that an independent reader could check is paired with a confidence badge. The four states and their thresholds are codified on the Methodology page:

  • Confirmed — primary source attached, no contradicting evidence outstanding.
  • Working — leading current hypothesis. Convergent indirect evidence supports the claim, but a single dispositive direct record is missing.
  • Open — a live candidate that has not yet been ruled in or out.
  • Eliminated — ruled out by direct evidence, with the contradicting source named.
What's "Churhessen"? Is that the same as "Hesse"?

"Churhessen" (modern German: Kurhessen) is the historical name for the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel — one of several states in the German Confederation before the 1866 Austro-Prussian War. It was annexed by Prussia in September 1866 and absorbed into the Province of Hesse-Nassau. Today the territory is split between the modern German states of Hesse (most of it) and Lower Saxony.

"Hesse" today is a different, larger entity. Charles's 1866 marriage register specifically uses Churhessen — the historical Electorate — which dates the language of the record (and Charles's self-identification) to the pre-annexation period. For more, see the Hesse Political History page.

Contributing & citing

How can I help?

The project welcomes corrections, contradicting evidence, and contributions from descendants, parish historians, and genealogists working on overlapping families. The four highest-priority gaps are:

  • The German baptismal entry for Charles ~1835 (any Trendelburg-area parish)
  • The 1859 trans-Atlantic passenger manifest (NYC, New Orleans, or Baltimore arrivals)
  • The Hessian Auswanderer-Beleg (emigration permit, 1858–1859)
  • The Hessian Stammrolle entry for the 1855 conscription class

If you can help with any of these, please see the contact information on the Methodology page. Substantive contributions are credited inline.

Can I download a photo or document for my own family record?

Yes. Family photographs and primary-source images on this site are reproduced with permission of the holders, where applicable. The Charles Sr. portrait is hosted on his FamilySearch profile and downloadable from there. Primary-source images sourced from public archives (NARA, Archion, Ancestry, Find a Grave) carry the citation of the original source on each evidence card.

Living descendants who appear in correspondence-derived materials are not photographed or named publicly without explicit permission. See the privacy policy.

How do I cite this site?

The recommended citation is given on the Methodology page:

Johnson, Jed, with Peter Schräder and James Gunnis. Charles Research — A Genealogical Investigation of Charles Konzen / Gannsen / Johnson (1835–1903). https://charles-research.netlify.app/, accessed [date].

Most pages display a "Last updated" date in their header; please use that date as the version anchor when citing.

Can I print this site?

Yes — every narrative page on this site has a print stylesheet that strips away navigation chrome, flattens dark hero treatments, and adds the URL of every link in a footnote so the printed document is verifiable offline. The Abstract page in particular is designed for A4 printing.
