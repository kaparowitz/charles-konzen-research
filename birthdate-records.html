Charles's Birth Date Across the U.S. Records

Ten American records, and almost as many different birth dates — from 1833 to 1841. Each one sourced, cross-checked against the original scans, and ranked by genealogical confidence.

A single, framing caveat governs this whole page: no independent birth or baptism record for Charles has ever been found — the German parish baptism remains the project's most-wanted document. Every figure below traces back to Charles's own testimony or, at his death, to his family's. None is a true original, firsthand record of the birth event. They are best read together as a cluster, not as competing facts.

How these are rated — by genealogical standards

The confidence ratings here follow the evidence-analysis model used in professional genealogy — the Source · Information · Evidence framework — together with the Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS), rather than an ad-hoc scale. Three independent classifications are applied to each record, with respect to the birth fact specifically:

Source

Is the document an original (created at the event, or a contemporaneous register) or a derivative (a later copy, abstract, or compiled record)?

Information

Is the birth detail primary (from someone with firsthand knowledge of the birth) or secondary (reported second-hand)?

Evidence

Does it answer the birth-date question directly (states an age or date), indirectly (must be inferred), or as negative evidence (an expected record’s absence)?

The decisive point under these standards: not one of these records carries primary information about Charles's birth. No informant — not even Charles himself — witnessed his own 1835 birth; every figure is second-hand knowledge carried within the family. So all ten are secondary, direct evidence drawn from original or derivative sources. They differ only in source originality, how close the informant stood to a knowledgeable party, precision (a full date vs. a round age), and how well they corroborate one another.

Because the date can be built only from a body of conflicting secondary evidence, the GPS requires the conclusion to rest on correlation and the resolution of those conflicts — and on reasonably exhaustive research. That last test is not yet met: the one record class that would supply primary information, the German baptism register, has never been found. That is exactly why the project holds 26 January 1835 as ★ Working, not Proved. The confidence pills below therefore rank relative reliability within an entirely secondary-information set — not against an absolute standard any of these records could meet on its own.

Standard used

This page applies the two standards in general use in professional U.S. genealogy:

• The Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS) and the source / information / evidence classifications — Board for Certification of Genealogists, Genealogy Standards, 2nd ed. rev. (Nashville: Ancestry, 2021).

• The evidence-analysis model (original vs. derivative source; primary vs. secondary information; direct, indirect, or negative evidence) — Elizabeth Shown Mills, Evidence Explained: Citing History Sources from Artifacts to Cyberspace, 3rd ed. rev. (Baltimore: Genealogical Publishing Co., 2017), ch. 1.

These align with the project's own source-rating taxonomy (Confirmed / Working) and evidence-grade chips (original-firsthand, original-secondhand, derivative, compiled).

Confidence ranking — strongest to weakest, and why

"Confidence" here means how much weight each record deserves as evidence of Charles's true birth date. Two axes drive the ranking, per the project's source-rating taxonomy: originality / informant proximity (was the recorder present, and who supplied the fact) and precision (a full date versus a round age).

  1. Church burial register — 26 Jan 1835 Highest

    The only record carrying a full day–month–year, and internally self-checking: the stated "68 y 10 m 20 d" back-calculates to the same late-January-1835 birth. It is an original parish entry written contemporaneously by the clergyman. Why not higher still: the birth fact is secondhand and post-mortem — supplied by the family after Charles died — not an independent birth record, and its 1835 year runs a year or two older than Charles's own later statements.

  2. 1900 census — Nov 1836 Moderate

    The only other record that fixes a month, and an original federal record taken in Charles's lifetime. Why mid-pack: he was a boarder in the Kuhn household, so the informant may have been the head of household rather than Charles himself; a stated month from a non-self informant is suggestive, not firm.

  3. 9 Feb 1891 pension declaration — age 53 (~1838) Moderate

    Charles's own sworn statement, under oath and penalty of perjury, on an original federal document — high informant proximity. Why not higher: age only, no month or day, and it conflicts with his own 1890 filing by three years, which shows he did not actually know his precise age.

  4. 15 Jan 1890 pension declaration — age ~56 (~1833–34) Moderate

    Also Charles's own sworn original statement. Ranked just below the 1891 one precisely because the two disagree: a man who swears 56 one year and 53 the next is estimating, not reporting a known date.

  5. 1880 census — age 39 (~1841) Low

    Original federal record, but age-only, household-reported (any adult could have answered), a round number, and the youngest outlier of the set.

  6. 2 Dec 1862 enlistment — age 23 (~1839) Low

    Original military record (the Regimental Descriptive Book), but the age was self-reported at enlistment — a context in which men routinely rounded or shaded their age — and it is age-only.

  7. National Homes for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers register (~1900) — birth ~1837 Low

    The Danville Soldiers'-Home admission register indexes Charles's birth as “about 1837.” An original institutional record, but the birth figure is age-derived and almost certainly drawn from the same self-reported pension data — secondary, with no month or day. It re-states the mid-1830s self-report rather than adding an independent one.

  8. 17 Dec 1903 death certificate — age 69 / "born 1834" Low

    Lowest for birth precision. The informant was necessarily not Charles (he was deceased); a survivor gave a round "69," and "1834" is merely that age subtracted from 1903. Authoritative for the death; weak for the birth.

  9. 18 Dec 1903 newspaper obituary — “age 69” (~1834) Low

    The St. Louis Post-Dispatch death notice gives Charles's age as 69 (→ ~1834), agreeing with the death certificate. A contemporaneous newspaper record, but post-mortem, family-supplied, and age-only — it adds a second 1834 attestation, not an independent date. No calendar birth date appears in the clipping itself.

  10. 18 Jan 1890 — age 65 (~1825) Disregard

    An apparent clerical error. Age 65 contradicts every other record by roughly a decade and even contradicts Charles's own declaration filed three days earlier (~56). Listed for completeness; it should not weigh in the birth-date estimate.

Bottom line

The firm, multiply-attested conclusion is a birth in the mid-1830s in Kurhessen. The precise 26 January 1835 rests on a single post-mortem parish entry (Doc N) and is best held as the working anchor until the German baptism is recovered. The only independent month-level support comes from the 1900 census ("Nov 1836"), and Charles's own sworn pension figures cluster around 1833–1838 — consistent with the mid-1830s, but never pinning the day.
