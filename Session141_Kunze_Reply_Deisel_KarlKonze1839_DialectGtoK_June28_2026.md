## The K→G dialect question (verified)

Jed's instinct that the soft-"G" pronunciation would be "a major clue" is **linguistically sound**:

- **North Hessian (Nordhessisch)** is explicitly characterized by the **binnendeutsche
  Konsonantenschwächung** (inner-German consonant weakening): the fortis/lenis contrast is
  neutralized, so voiceless stops lenite toward their voiced partners. Textbook minimal pair:
  **"Keil" and "geil" become homophones** — exactly the K↔G merger.
- ⇒ An initial **K** in *Konze*, spoken in the Deisel/Trendelburg dialect, can land on an
  English-speaking clerk's ear as **"G"**. *Konze → "Gonze" → Gannsen/Gonsen* is phonetically real.
- **Significance for the case:** this **explains the split in the US spellings** — the same emigrant
  recorded as both Kon-/Con- forms (Konsen, Konzen, Conson) *and* G- forms (Gannsen, Gonson, Gansen)
  is one soft-initial Hessian **Konze**, transcribed two ways. It bridges "Konze in the Deisel
  church book" ↔ "Gannsen in US records," and dovetails with the candidate Karl Konze b. 1839.

### Caveats

- The merger is strongest on the **consonant**. The vowel shift (o→a) and the *-en*→*-son* ending are
  looser/clerk-dependent — plausible but weaker; do not over-lean on them.
- The dialect effect explains the **American spelling drift**; it is **not** a claim that Konze and
  Gans were one family in Hesse. Kunze (who knows the dialect) still keeps Konze and Gans separate
  (Session 132). Keep the two questions distinct.
- Karl b. 1839 is the same individual earlier worked as a candidate to *rule out*; Kunze now floats
  him on the theory the US-reported **26 Jan 1835** birth date is off by a few years. **Verify
  against the Archion image before accepting.**
