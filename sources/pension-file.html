About this document

This is the complete U.S. Civil War pension case file for Charles Gannson — depositions, medical examinations, marriage record copy, address-history affidavits, family-roster declarations, and the 1903 death notification. It was filed beginning in 1891 (Charles aged 53) and amended through his death. The original file lives in Record Group 15 (Department of Veterans Affairs) at the National Archives, Washington, D.C.; the digital copy reproduced below was supplied to the project by the National Archives under reference F41-523680269E.

In the 2020 AncestryProGenealogists research journal, this file is indexed as Doc M. It is the single richest U.S.-side primary source on Charles's family, residences, military career, and physical condition.

What the file means in context

The richest primary source for Charles's American life

The pension file is the fullest U.S.-side source for Charles's stated birth date (26 January 1835) — a declared date recorded in the claim rather than a primary attestation — and it likewise documents his service trajectory (Private → Sergeant), the seven children's birth dates, and the address chain that anchors the St. Louis residency map on the main site. Every St. Marcus baptism, every census reading, and every directory entry has been cross-checked against the family-roster declarations and address affidavits in this file.

The file also contains affidavits from Charles's witnesses — most notably August Kreichelt and George Kreichelt (his brother-in-law and father-in-law respectively, both also Battery B Civil War veterans). The witness network here is the key to opening the broader St. Marcus-immigrant social network around Charles.

The most detailed claimant's narrative in the file. Charles was a "drill Sergeant" instructing the 54th Illinois Infantry in heavy-artillery tactics at Rolla, MO, July 1862, and was injured by lifting a heavy gun (right-side rupture); contracted rheumatism at New Madrid, MO, November 1862; lost hearing from cannonading. Pre-service occupation: private watchman (matches the 1895+ St. Louis directory entries). Post-service occupation: saloon-keeper (a detail not previously known on the main site). Personal description on this 1890 declaration: 5'10", light complexion, light hair, blue eyes — note: the 1862 enlistment record (Doc O) records 5'5" and fair complexion, so the height/complexion shift across the 28-year gap reflects either Charles's own recollection drift or a clerical re-read, not a match. Note: Page 36 records that the War Department could not corroborate Charles's claim of detached duty with the Illinois Infantry — his name was not on any Md. or Ill. Inf. Vols. roll. Transcription correction (June 2026): on re-examination of the original scan this declaration is dated 15 January 1890 (not 16), sworn before Circuit Court Clerk Philip H. Zepp (not "Capt. J. J. Setzer"), and Charles's age reads in the mid-50s (~56) — not 53 — implying a birth of ~1833–34. The figure is one of several inconsistent self-reported ages; for the full record-by-record comparison see Charles's birth date across the U.S. records.

The height of 5'6½" here is the third distinct reading across Charles's records: the 1862 enlistment recorded 5'5" (fair complexion), the 1890 Page 14 Declaration recorded 5'10" (light complexion), and this 1890 medical exam records 5'6½" (no complexion notation). The 5'5" and 5'6½" readings are consistent with measurement variance plus 28 years of life; the 5'10" Declaration figure looks like either Charles's own recollection drift or a clerk's re-read in the in-court declaration setting. Pages 14 and this medical exam were filled out the same year (1890) but in different administrative contexts. Logged as a documented variance, not a discrepancy that changes any load-bearing claim.
