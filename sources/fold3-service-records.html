About this document

This is the complete Compiled Service Record for Charles Gannsen as it appears in the National Archives microfilm series NARA M405 ("Compiled service records of volunteer Union soldiers who served in organizations from the State of Missouri"), retrieved via Fold3 from image #228767500 on Roll 0327 (Second Light Artillery, Fo–Gh). It expands on the single-card Compiled Service Record (Doc O) that the 2020 AncestryProGenealogists journal indexed: where Doc O reproduces only the enlistment-summary card, this 25-page Fold3 download reproduces the complete card series the National Archives compiled for Charles between 1890 and 1912 from the original muster rolls.

The "Compiled Service Records" are the result of a federal post-war records-consolidation project: in 1890 Congress directed the War Department to abstract every appearance of every Union volunteer soldier from the original company muster rolls onto individual cards. Each card records one event — an enlistment, a muster-in, a presence-or-absence at each subsequent muster, hospital admissions, a discharge. The cards for a given soldier were filed together as that soldier's Compiled Service Record. Charles's record was compiled between 1890 and 1912 and microfilmed as part of NARA series M405 in the 1960s.

What's inside — the carded-record format

Each card in the M405 record is a small printed form (about 4 × 6 inches) on which a War Department clerk transcribed a single line of information from one company-level muster roll. Charles's 24-card sequence covers the full arc of his service:

Enlistment cardsRecording the original 2 December 1862 enlistment in St. Louis at age 23 (as recorded; calculated 27 per canonical 26 Jan 1835 birth). Birthplace: "Freundburg, Kurhessen." Physical description: 5'5", fair complexion, blue eyes, blond hair (the 1890 pension declaration later records 5'10" and complexion "light"). The "L." initial precedes "Charles" on these cards.
Muster-in cardThe formal swearing-in to U.S. Volunteer service, by company commander.
Bi-monthly muster cards (≈18 cards)One card per company muster — Jan/Feb 1863, Mar/Apr 1863, May/Jun 1863, etc. Each card records "Present" or "Absent" with an explanatory note. Charles's cards are mostly "Present"; a small number record hospitalization for the disabilities later cited in the pension file.
Promotion cardsOne card each for promotion Private → Corporal and Corporal → Sergeant. Charles's promotion to Sergeant occurred in 1864.
Discharge / muster-out cardThe final card in the file: discharged 20 December 1865 at the rank of Private (Charles served as Sergeant from 7 Nov 1863 until his reduction on 15 Mar 1865 per G.O. No. 82).

The cards collectively confirm Charles served continuously through the war with no recorded desertion, no court-martial, and no extended hospitalization — a clean three-year service record that he and his Battery B comrades (Frank Mezger, Henry Derr, Georg and August Kreichelt) would later draw on to support the 1890 pension claim.

What this record adds beyond Doc O

Doc O = the enlistment summary; Fold3 = the entire muster history

The 2020 AncestryProGenealogists journal cited Doc O, which is the single enlistment-summary card found at the top of Charles's Compiled Service Record. That one page tells you when and where Charles enlisted, his stated age, and his recorded birthplace — but not whether he was at his post on any given Tuesday in 1864.

This Fold3 download fills in the rest. Specifically:

  • Continuous-service evidence. The bi-monthly muster cards establish that Charles was actually in his battery throughout 1862–1865, with no AWOL or detached-service notations. This was important for the 1890 pension claim, which required documented honorable service.
  • Promotion timing. The promotion cards date the Private → Corporal and Corporal → Sergeant transitions — letting us read the rank progression in the timeline rather than just the start and end states.
  • Hospitalization notes. Where Doc O is silent on Charles's mid-war health, the muster cards include any "Sick in hospital at [location]" notations that the pension surgeons later treated as supporting evidence for the right hernia, rheumatism, and hearing-loss claims.
  • The "L." initial recurrence. The "L." preceding Charles's first name appears on multiple muster cards across 1862–1865 — not a one-time clerical anomaly on the enlistment card, but a consistent recording that Charles himself used (or accepted being recorded as) throughout his three years of service. This strengthens the working hypothesis that L. Charles compresses a German Ludwig Carl or Lorenz Carl baptismal name.
  • Five distinct surname spellings on the cards themselves. Different Battery B muster-card clerks captured Charles's surname in five distinct phonetic forms across the 25-page sequence: Gunnsen · Gannsen · Gansen · Gransen · Gonson. None of the five renderings preserves the German tz / -en form intact, consistent with English-speaking clerks of the period not knowing how to write the Hessian sound — the same pattern that produced the nine-rendering surname trail in the civilian church records (see Card 1 of Key Evidence). Gansen and Gonson overlap with the civilian-record list; Gunnsen, Gannsen, and Gransen are unique to the military cards. Together with the civilian renderings, the carded record adds three new attestations to the surname-variant cluster, all phonetic descendants of the Trendelburg Kontzen root.

What the record does NOT settle

For all its detail, the Fold3 record does not resolve the two great Civil-War-side mysteries:

  • "Freundburg, Kurhessen." The birthplace string appears verbatim on every card that records it; the cards do not contain any clarification or alternative spelling. The Trendelburg-mishearing reading rests entirely on cross-reference to the 1866 St. Marcus marriage register entry "aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" — see the Civil War tab.
  • The "L." initial. No card spells out the German name behind the L. The hypothesis that L. = Ludwig or Lorenz remains supported by usage pattern only.

★ An original headquarters Special Order relieving Charles from his detached-service duty at Department headquarters and ordering him to report back to his battery for the muster-out process. Based on context (the muster-out card on PG 02 dates to 30 August 1865) this Special Order likely dates from June or July 1865 — the typical 30-to-60-day window before muster-out. The order references Charles being "relieved from duty at these Head Quarters" — confirming the multiple "Detached service · A.A.G. office" cards (PG 03, 04, 05, 06) document a continuous assignment to Departmental headquarters that ended with this special order.
