Hesse during Charles's lifetime — 1835 to 1903

The political map of Charles's homeland was redrawn three times between his birth and his death. He was born a subject of the Kurfürstentum Hessen (Electorate of Hesse), an independent German state at the centre of the loose German Confederation. He emigrated as one — and within seven years his country had ceased to exist as an independent polity, swallowed up by Prussia in the Austro-Prussian War of 1866. By the time he died, the Hesse he had left was a Prussian province inside the German Empire under Wilhelm II.

Kurhessen
(Electorate of Hesse)
1803 – 1866

Kurfürstentum Hessen · Electorate of Hesse-Kassel

Capital: Kassel. Territory included Trendelburg, Deisel, Hofgeismar — Charles's home district. Ruled by Elector Wilhelm II until 1847, then his son Friedrich Wilhelm I (regent from 1831, ruling Elector 1847–1866). A reactionary administration through the 1850s under Minister Hassenpflug; freedom-of-press restrictions and emigration permits were tightly controlled. Currency: Kurhessischer Taler. Member of the German Confederation. Charles is born here in ~Jan 1835 and emigrates from this state in ~1859.

Königreich
Preußen
14 June – 23 August 1866

The Austro-Prussian War · Hesse-Kassel sided with Austria

Friedrich Wilhelm I, Elector of Hesse, sided with Austria against Prussia. Prussian forces occupied Kassel within days. Hesse-Kassel was annexed unilaterally by Prussia under the Annexionsgesetz of 20 September 1866, ending five centuries of Hessian sovereignty. The Elector was deposed and pensioned. This is the moment Charles's homeland legally ceased to exist. Charles, by now five years into his St. Louis life and a Civil-War veteran, would have read about it in the German-language St. Louis press.

A loyalty signal · Charles's first child, born this same year

Three months after Friedrich Wilhelm I lost his throne to Prussia, Charles and Auguste Kreichelt — married 7 January 1866 — had their first child in St. Louis. They named him Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson: the same name as the deposed Elector of Hesse.

Charles had been an adult subject of Friedrich Wilhelm I from the Elector's accession in 1847 (Charles aged 12) until his own emigration in 1859 — the only sovereign whose rule he experienced as a grown man. Naming a son for a now-deposed monarch in the year of his deposition is not an accidental choice. It is among the strongest pieces of identity evidence the investigation has surfaced for Charles's emotional and political tie to the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel specifically — distinct from any other German state. The infant died at 11 months in 1867, but the name choice survives as testimony.

Provinz
Hessen-Nassau
1867 – 1918

Provinz Hessen-Nassau · Prussian Province

In 1867 Prussia consolidated the annexed territories of Hesse-Kassel, Nassau, Frankfurt, and parts of Hesse-Darmstadt into a single new Province of Hesse-Nassau. Provincial capital: Kassel. Charles's home district of Hofgeismar Kreis became Regierungsbezirk Kassel within the new province. The administrative consequence for genealogy is that all civil records from this point forward use Prussian forms (and Prussian Standesämter from 1874 forward).

Deutsches
Reich
18 January 1871 – 1918

The German Empire · Hesse-Nassau under Wilhelm I, Friedrich III, Wilhelm II

After the Franco-Prussian War, the German Empire was proclaimed at Versailles on 18 January 1871. Hesse-Nassau remained a Prussian province within the Reich. Under Wilhelm II from 1888, the Hessian villages from which Charles emigrated were now ordinary German Empire territory — a far cry from the small, independent Electorate Charles was born into. By the time of his death in December 1903, Hessian emigration had slowed dramatically and the German immigrant communities of St. Louis (St. Marcus Church among them) were two generations deep.
