Charles's Surname Across the U.S. Records

Nine different spellings of one man's surname across fourteen records, 1862–1896 — including all seven of his children's baptisms, and two he signed himself — then the adopted “Johnson.” Each entry by date, with the spelling as written, who supplied it, an external verification link, a confidence level, and its evidence classification.

Most of these spellings are a scribe’s phonetic capture of a name Charles spoke — which is exactly why they disagree. Two are different: Charles signed the 1862 military record and his 1890–91 pension declarations himself, so those carry his own hand. Read together, the German-origin spellings cluster on a single sound: an initial G / K, a back vowel, and an n–s–n or n–z–n ending (the s-forms like Konsen and the z-forms like Konzen are the same name). This page is the verification ledger behind the surname search criterion; the deeper phonetic analysis is on The Konzen Puzzle.

How these are rated — by genealogical standards

Each entry is classified with the same evidence-analysis model used on Birth date across the records — the Source · Information · Evidence framework with the Genealogical Proof Standard. Two points are specific to surnames:

All are direct — the axis is the informant

Every record states a spelling, so all are direct evidence of “the surname as recorded.” What separates them is who supplied the name (Charles himself, a parent at a child’s font, a survivor, an unnamed census informant) and how original the source is.

Two records are in his own hand

Charles signed the 1862 enlistment and his 1890–91 pension declarations — those spellings (Gannsen) are holograph, the form he chose for himself in America. Every other entry is a scribe’s rendering of a name he spoke, including each child’s baptism by a German pastor — so confidence tracks how directly he supplied the spelling.

Confidence = fidelity to the true surname

The pills below rank how well each spelling reflects Charles’s actual German birth surname. Spellings he spoke as principal (marriage groom, widower) rank highest; the later “Johnson” is an adopted Americanisation and is marked as such — direct evidence of his U.S. identity, none of his German name.

Standard used

• Genealogical Proof Standard and the source / information / evidence classifications — BCG, Genealogy Standards, 2nd ed. rev. (Nashville: Ancestry, 2021).

• The evidence-analysis model — Elizabeth Shown Mills, Evidence Explained, 3rd ed. rev. (Baltimore: GPC, 2017), ch. 1. See the project’s source-rating taxonomy.

The German-origin cluster — every spelling, with its source

These are the records in which Charles’s original surname was captured — the spellings that matter for matching him to a German parish register. The phonetic trail is tracked in the project’s canonical surname-attestation table; this ledger adds the two records he signed (military, pension), all seven of his children’s baptisms, and — folded in by date — the city-directory listings (marked dir), so the whole chronology sits in one place. The St. Marcus parish registers are browse-only on FamilySearch, so each parish row links the film and cites the page / entry to navigate to.

★ One name was locked — the rest he chose by context

Read down the table and a pattern jumps out. The official, legally-linked records all carry the same n-s-n form “Gannsen”:

1862 enlistment  →  1868 naturalization  →  1890–91 pension  →  1900 Soldiers’ Home

That is not coincidence, it is bureaucratic necessity. Charles enlisted as Gannsen and signed it, so the U.S. record knew him by that name. To naturalize he had to present as the same legal person — so he reused Gannsen. Decades later his pension was paid only because it could be tied back to the “Gannsen” entry in his Battery B muster record — so once again he signed Gannsen. And when he was admitted to the Danville Soldiers’ Home in 1900, that admission was processed through the pension — so the Home’s Historical Register recorded him as “Gannson,” a clerk’s vowel away from Gannsen and the same name, the official surname carried one link further down the chain. On every record that touched the federal government, the name was fixed; using anything else would have broken the chain of identity (and the pension).

Everywhere else he was free — and he used it. At the parish font, in the city directory, on the census, at a funeral, the name was whatever he or the scribe reached for that day, swinging between the German sound (Gonnson, Konsen, Gonson, Konzen, Conson) and the Americanised forms (Johnson, Jansen) as the context invited. The German -en forms were not confined to church use — they also appear on the 1880 census (Gonson) and in the 1874 / 1876 city directories (Konsen, Gonson) — while the Americanised Johnson first appears (on present evidence) about 1871, in the directories of his streetcar-conductor years. The result: a man legally Gannsen, ancestrally Konzen, and casually whatever the moment called for.

A note on the earliest reading. Gannsen is not only the legally-locked form — it is also the earliest surname Charles is recorded under (the 1862 enlistment, in his own hand). A standard genealogical heuristic treats the earliest, first-person attestation as potentially the most faithful, which on that basis would weight Gannsen. The project flags this as a live consideration but holds it to be determined: it sits alongside — not above — the ancestral-Konzen reading argued from the homeland -en forms, since the two answer different questions (his American legal name versus his German birth surname).

The adopted name — “Johnson”

The Americanised Johnson was not only the family's final settled name — Charles used it on and off throughout his life, in the informal records where he was free to (see the note at the top of the page). The city-directory listings (marked dir) show him as “Johnson” during his 1871–73 conductor years and again in the 1887 watchman directory — long before the death records — while in between he was recorded under the German forms (Konsen 1874, Gonson 1876, folded into the cluster table above). These records are direct evidence of his American identity but say nothing about his German birth surname, and are flagged Adopted rather than ranked.

What the spread tells a searcher

Set the nine German-origin spellings side by side — Gannsen, Gonnson, Gansen, Gonsen, Konsen, Gonson, Jansen, Konzen, Conson — and one pattern survives every scribe: an initial G or K (once softened to J), a single back vowel (a / o), and a closing –n–s–n or –n–z–n (the s and z are interchangeable for this sound). That is the evidentiary basis for the surname search criterion on Parish search coverage: look for any surname in the *n-s-n / *n-z-n family (Gansen, Ganssen, Gonsen, Gonson, Konsen, Konzen…), not a single fixed name. The full phonetic reconstruction — vowels, clusters, and the candidate German forms — is on The Konzen Puzzle.
