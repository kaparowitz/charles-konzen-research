# Why Charles Gannsen Resists Identification

### A Taxonomy of Record-Obscuring Mechanisms and the Search Designed to Defeat Them

*Working abstract and methodology · 28 June 2026 · v01*

---

## Abstract

Charles Gannsen (b. 26 January 1835, of German origin; enlisted St. Louis,
2 December 1862, Battery B, 2nd Regiment Missouri Light Artillery; married
St. Louis 1866) has no confirmed birth or baptismal record despite a
multi-year, parish-by-parish search of the Kurhessen church books most likely
to contain it. This study reframes that absence. We argue that the failure to
locate his origin record is not evidence of insufficient searching but the
predictable consequence of a small, enumerable set of mechanisms by which a
mid-nineteenth-century Hessian birth record can evade a modern index search —
and that each such mechanism admits a specific, testable counter-strategy.
We identify seven families of obscuring mechanism: (1) surname instability
across at least nine attested American spellings and a parallel German
variant-pool; (2) given-name displacement, since "Charles" anglicizes a
baptismal Carl/Karl that may sit as a secondary forename or be Latinized;
(3) birthplace error, the U.S.-stated origin being potentially a port, region,
or later residence rather than a true birthplace; (4) birth-date error inherent
to U.S.-derived ages; (5) non-survival or non-existence of the record, through
emergency baptism, confessional mismatch, register gaps, or loss; (6) family
mobility, including the documented possibility — first raised by James Gunnis —
that the family was military and therefore scattered across garrison parishes;
and (7) access and indexing limits that hide even a surviving, correctly-located
record. For each mechanism we specify the compensating search tactic actually
deployed, distinguish negative results that are *bounded* (the record would
have surfaced had it existed in the searched corpus) from territory that remains
*genuinely unsearched*, and state the residual open branches. The result is a
reproducible search architecture in which the absence of a baptism becomes a
bounded finding rather than an open question.

---

## 1. The identification problem

Every fact we hold about Charles Gannsen originates on the American side of his
life: a Civil War enlistment, a pension file, a marriage, censuses, a burial.
These give a birth date (26 January 1835), a German origin (rendered in 1866 as
"Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen"), and a surname recorded in at least nine
distinct spellings. None of them is a German record. The German record — the
baptism that would anchor him to a family, a parish, and a confirmed origin —
has not been found, and the surname as Americans wrote it ("Gannsen") does not
correspond cleanly to any single German parish-book spelling.

A naive search strategy — take the stated name, place, and date, and look them
up — fails here for structural reasons, and it fails *silently*: it returns
nothing whether or not the record exists. The central methodological move of
this project is therefore to stop treating "we didn't find him" as a single
undifferentiated negative, and instead to decompose the ways a true record could
escape each search, build a counter-measure for each, and track precisely which
negatives are conclusive and which merely reflect territory not yet covered.
This aligns the work with the Genealogical Proof Standard's requirement of
reasonably exhaustive research: exhaustiveness is meaningful only when the
*boundaries* of the search are themselves documented.

## 2. Taxonomy of obscuring mechanisms

Each mechanism below is stated as: the failure mode, why it defeats a naive
search, the compensating tactic, and the current evidentiary status.

### 2.1 Surname instability — the record is filed under a spelling we would not guess

**Failure mode.** The family name is unstable on both sides of the Atlantic. In
the United States it appears across nine attested spellings — Gonnson, Gansen,
Gonsen, Konsen, Gonson, Jansen, Konzen, Gannsen, and Conson. In the German
books the confirmed root forms are Konze, Kontze, Konsen, and Konzen, plus the
compound surname Jungkonze and the C-filed variants Conson/Contze; the broad
phonetic field extends to Gans/Ganß. A clerk recording an unfamiliar name by
ear is the single likeliest reason the entry indexes under none of the spellings
a searcher would try first.

**Compensating tactic.** The search is run against the entire surname *pool*,
not a single string — every kon-/kont-/kons-/konz-, gans-/gons-/gann-, jung-,
and C-filed conson-/contze- variant — and the pool is held data-driven from
confirmed attestations rather than from any one American spelling that may
itself be corrupt. Alphabetical name-indexes are read for the whole pool and
each candidate hit is verified against the original entry image.

**Status.** This tactic has produced the project's positive anchors: three
"geb. Konzen" mothers at Deisel (1828–29); the first father-surname Konze
baptisms at Trendelburg; and the Hümme Kontze cluster — a multi-household,
non-extinct population of at least five father-households and ten indexed
baptisms (1830–1863). It has also driven the elimination of sixteen candidate
families where the pool was searched and absent.

### 2.2 Given-name displacement — the entry exists but the forename defeats the search

**Failure mode.** "Charles" is almost certainly an anglicized Carl/Karl, but a
German baptism may record it as a *secondary* given name (e.g. "Johann Carl …"
or "Carl Friedrich Wilhelm …") and be indexed under the first name; it may be
Latinized to *Carolus*; or — less probably — "Charles" was adopted in America
over a German rufname that was something else entirely.

**Compensating tactic.** Candidate leaves are swept by *birth-date and the male
cohort*, not by forename. Any boy born near 26 January 1835 is treated as a
candidate regardless of given name, and every Johann/Carl/Karl/Carolus
permutation is flagged. This converts a forename search into a date-and-sex
search the displacement cannot defeat.

**Status.** Applied in every leaf-by-leaf window sweep. It is what surfaced the
documented "near-miss" Carls — e.g. the illegitimate "Carl August" at Dissen
(1833) and "Johann Carl" at Gottsbüren (15 Feb 1835) — each examined and
excluded on date/surname rather than passed over.

### 2.3 Birthplace error — we may be searching the wrong place

**Failure mode.** The U.S.-stated origin may be a port of departure, a regional
label, a later residence, or simply wrong. "German" can also denote
German-*speaking* origin outside Kurhessen — Hesse-Darmstadt, Hannover,
Westphalia, the Rhineland, or beyond the modern border (Alsace, Luxembourg,
Switzerland, Austria, Bohemia). The 1866 "Deisse, Kreis Cassel" is itself a
phonetic toponym open to several readings.

**Compensating tactic.** The parish search is anchored to the surname *clusters*
actually confirmed in the books (Deisel, Trendelburg, Hümme) rather than to a
single U.S.-stated town, and toponym readings are deliberately stretched — the
"Deissen" reading that opened the Dissen / Grifte / Gudensberg cluster, and the
"Dassen → Fulda Evangelical town church" reading, are both products of treating
the stated place as a phonetic target with multiple admissible expansions. An
explicit "outside-Kurhessen" branch is held open so that a negative sweep of the
core parishes does not foreclose the project.

**Status.** Multiple toponym expansions have been searched to negative,
bounding the core Kurhessen reading; the outside-Kurhessen branch remains a
documented open lane.

### 2.4 Birth-date error — the search window itself may be wrong

**Failure mode.** 26 January 1835 is U.S.-derived (census, military, pension,
gravestone). Such ages are routinely off by one to several years and are
frequently rounded; a point search on a single day fails even when the record
exists.

**Compensating tactic.** Sweeps cover a *widened* window (births c. 1833–1841 on
the confirmation track, not January 1835 alone), and the date is triangulated
against independent age statements — confirmation age (~14), marriage age — so
the search does not rest on one American figure.

**Status.** The widened window is standard in every parish sweep and is what
keeps the near-miss Carls (1833, 1838) inside scope for explicit adjudication
rather than silent exclusion.

### 2.5 Record non-survival or non-existence — no search can find what is not there

**Failure mode.** The record may never have entered the surviving parish book:
an emergency or home baptism (*Nottaufe*) sometimes went unrecorded; the child
may have been baptized in a *different confession* than the one searched —
Kurhessen held Evangelical, Reformed, and Catholic populations (the Fulda lead
turned precisely on a Catholic district whose Protestants used the Evangelical
town church), plus Jewish communities with separate registers; the entry may
fall in a register *gap* (clerical vacancy, missing years, unfilmed leaves, war
or fire loss); or an illegitimate child may appear only as "n.n." / unnamed.

**Compensating tactic.** The project does not rely on baptisms alone. The same
parishes are cross-checked on the *confirmation, marriage, and burial* registers,
so a missing or unrecorded baptism can still surface when the person confirms,
marries, or is buried. Confessional alternatives are pursued where the
geography invites them. Critically, every parish sweep documents *which*
registers and confessions remain unsearched or non-surviving, so that a negative
is explicitly bounded rather than assumed total.

**Status.** Several parishes are now negative on two or three independent
register tracks (e.g. Gudensberg on baptisms, marriages, and burials;
Eberschütz and Gieselwerder on both baptism and confirmation), materially
strengthening those eliminations beyond a single-book negative.

### 2.6 Family mobility, including the military hypothesis — the right family in the wrong parish

**Failure mode.** The confirmed Kontze households are weavers (*Leineweber*),
day-labourers (*Tagelöhner*), and a building-overseer (*Bauaufseher*) — itinerant
trades that move between parishes between children, so a child can be born in a
parish the family only briefly occupied. A sharper variant, first raised by
James Gunnis, is that the family was **military**: a soldier father follows his
regiment between garrison towns, and military families' baptisms were frequently
entered in *separate garrison or military church books* (Militärkirchenbücher)
kept apart from the civil parish register. A complete, correctly-located sweep
of a *town* parish can therefore miss the child entirely, because the record
sits in a different book under a different jurisdiction — and the surname-cluster
logic misleads, because a soldier's child need not be born where the family is
rooted.

**Compensating tactic, and what has actually been tested.** The military
hypothesis was tested two ways, and the boundaries matter:

- *As a demonstrated mechanism.* The Gunnis "Dassen → Fulda" lead produced a
  verified military baptism — Karl Friedrich Gastmann (Fulda Evangelical Taufen,
  3 March 1838), son of *Feldwebel* Michael Gastmann of the 1st Company,
  2. Infanterie-Regiment. The Fulda confirmation classes of 1851–53 contain *no*
  Gastmann, which is exactly what a garrison family predicts: the regiment — and
  the family with it — had moved on. The 2. Infanterie-Regiment is documented to
  have rotated its companies through Kassel, Hanau, Marburg, Hersfeld,
  Ziegenhain, and Fulda. This case is not Charles (surname, Kreis, and year all
  fail), but it *empirically establishes* the scattering mechanism and shows why
  a single-parish negative cannot exclude a military family.
- *As a direct lane for Charles.* A parallel search pursues whether Charles
  himself left a pre-emigration Kurhessen conscription trace. Catalogue research
  has corrected the target fonds to the Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg —
  recruitment muster rolls under Best. 331 *[Amt]*, the central
  Kriegsministerium Best. 12 (1813–1867), and Best. 180 Hofgeismar (which holds
  the Kreis's emigration and Militärersatz files); an inquiry to Marburg
  targeting the 1855 conscription class for Kreis Hofgeismar is outstanding.

**Status — bounded vs. unsearched.** A catalogue survey (filtering
"Rekrutierungsstammrolle" to 1850–1860 across all of Hessen) returned only 21
holdings, just two at HStAM, *none* for Hofgeismar / Trendelburg / Deisel; the
surviving Best. 331 series are largely post-1867. The 1855 local recruitment
roll for Charles's Amt therefore **probably does not survive** at HStAM — a
bounded finding that redirects the lane to the central Best. 12 and to Best. 180
Hofgeismar. The garrison *parish* registers (Kassel, Hanau, Marburg, Hersfeld,
Ziegenhain) are largely un-indexed on Archion and **have not been hand-read** —
this is the principal genuinely-unsearched territory the military hypothesis
opens, and it is logged as such rather than glossed.

### 2.7 Identity error in the source data — the premise itself may be corrupt

**Failure mode.** The American "Charles Gannsen" on whom the whole search keys
may be a *conflation* of two men; the surname may be fully adopted in America
with no German antecedent; or he may have been a stepchild or foster child
raised under a name that is not his birth name.

**Compensating tactic.** Before any single American fact is trusted as a search
key, the U.S. Charles is pinned to *one* documented, internally-consistent
individual — birth date, origin statement, unit, marriage, and pension
correlated to the same man — so that the German search is not chasing an
artefact of two merged records. The surname pool, likewise, is kept anchored to
German attestations rather than to an American spelling that could be an
invention.

**Status.** The American identity is comparatively well-correlated (enlistment,
unit records, pension, marriage, burial), which constrains — though does not
eliminate — the conflation and full-adoption possibilities.

### 2.8 Access and indexing limits — a cross-cutting amplifier

**Failure mode.** Even a surviving, correctly-located, correctly-named record
can be missed because it is behind a subscription login; undigitized or
on-site-only; digitized but unindexed (must be read leaf-by-leaf);
shuffled or duplicated within the digital reel; or defeats machine OCR because
it is written in Kurrent script. This mechanism does not stand alone — it
amplifies every mechanism above by adding a second layer between a true record
and its discovery.

**Compensating tactic.** The project's core protocol is built to neutralize
this: alphabetical indexes are read by eye where OCR fails; each register's
image-to-page calibration is worked out and documented before reading; reels
known to be shuffled or duplicated are de-duplicated by the printed page header
rather than trusted by image number; and every candidate is verified against the
original image to the same evidentiary standard regardless of how it was found.
The OCR-correction in the Gastmann case (machine read "Hartmann"; the hand reads
"Gastmann") is a documented instance of why image verification is non-optional.

**Status.** This protocol is the reason the parish negatives are trustworthy:
they reflect leaves actually read and calibrated, not index queries that may
have silently failed.

## 3. The search architecture as a single net

The seven counter-tactics are not independent patches; they compose into one
search net in which each mechanism's escape route is covered by at least one
other tactic:

- **Full-window, cohort-based leaf reading** defeats both given-name
  displacement (2.2) and birth-date error (2.4) at once.
- **Pool-wide index reading with image verification** defeats surname
  instability (2.1) and the OCR/Kurrent layer of the access problem (2.8).
- **Cross-register triangulation** (baptism ↔ confirmation ↔ marriage ↔ burial)
  defeats baptismal non-survival (2.5) and lets a person be placed by parents'
  identities even when his own entry is missing.
- **Godparent and witness networks**, plus "geb. Konze(n)" maternal entries,
  provide a back-door to records filed under the wrong surname (2.1) or the
  mother's name (illegitimacy, 2.5), and help locate the home parish of a mobile
  family (2.6).
- **Toponym stretching and an open out-of-region branch** guard against
  birthplace error (2.3).
- **The archival / military lane** (HStAM Best. 331, 12, 180; garrison parish
  registers) addresses the military variant of mobility (2.6) and a possible
  conscription-evasion trace tied to emigration.
- **Emigrant tracking** — Konze-pool passengers on Hamburg/Bremen lists — anchors
  the family from the American side, cross-checking identity error (2.7) and
  birthplace error (2.3).

The same evidentiary standard governs the whole net: no candidate is treated as
confirmed until verified against the original image.

## 4. The bounded-negative principle

The methodological payoff is a disciplined treatment of absence. A negative
result is recorded in one of two forms, never conflated:

1. **Bounded negative** — the relevant register and surname pool were read and
   calibrated, so a true record *would have surfaced* had it existed in that
   corpus. The parish is eliminated, and the elimination is as strong as the
   number of independent register tracks it rests on.
2. **Open territory** — the record could exist in a corpus not yet searched
   (un-indexed garrison parishes, a different confession, an out-of-region
   parish, an archival fonds awaiting a reply). This is logged as an active
   branch, not as evidence of absence.

Under this discipline, the project's current state is not "Charles cannot be
found" but a precise map: a large and growing set of *bounded* negatives across
the Kurhessen parish books, a set of confirmed Konze/Kontze anchor populations
the true record most plausibly attaches to, and a small, named set of open
branches — chiefly the un-read garrison parish registers and the central
Kurhessen military fonds — where the record, if it survives, most likely waits.

> **Thesis for the reader.** The absence of a confirmed baptism for Charles
> Gannsen is not evidence of failed searching but a bounded result: each
> mechanism by which the record could evade discovery has been identified and
> paired with a search designed to defeat it, and the territory not yet covered
> is named rather than assumed empty.
