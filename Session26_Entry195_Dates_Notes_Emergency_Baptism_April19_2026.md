## Detailed Readings

### 1. Birth Date Column (Entry 195)

The birth date column contains multi-line text describing the circumstances of birth:

**Line 1**: "Januari den" (January the...)
**Line 2**: "[?] ein eintzg[?]unar" — partially legible; may describe the time or circumstances
**Line 3**: "zwifchen Nacht" (= "zwischen Nacht" = during the night)
**Line 4**: "am 24." (on the 24th)

**Interpretation**: The child was born during the night, on or around January 24, 1835. The notation "zwischen Nacht" indicates a nighttime birth, likely in the late hours of January 23 going into January 24.

### 2. Notes Column (Entry 195) — "Nachträgliche Bemerkungen"

**Line 1**: "Frühgetauft" (= Frühgetauft = emergency baptized / baptized early)
**Line 2**: "am 23,35." (= on [January] 23, [18]35)

**Full reading**: "Frühgetauft am 23,35." = "Emergency baptized on January 23, 1835."

**Critical observations:**
- There is **NO death cross (†)** preceding this notation. Compare with Entry 196 (Sophia Walter), which clearly shows "† vom 29,36." — a death cross followed by a death date.
- "Frühgetauft" (Nottaufe / emergency baptism) indicates the child was in danger — either premature, sickly, or born under difficult circumstances. The midwife or attending person would have performed the emergency baptism immediately.
- The emergency baptism on January 23 aligns with a nighttime birth on January 23-24: the baptism was performed before midnight (still the 23rd), while the birth was recorded as the 24th (after midnight or by the next calendar day's reckoning).

### 3. Birth Date vs. Emergency Baptism — Reconciliation

| Event | Date | Source |

The 2-3 day discrepancy between the register date (Jan 23-24) and Charles Gannsen's stated date (Jan 26) is well within the range of:
- Imprecise family memory after emigration
- Deliberate age adjustment for immigration or military draft purposes
- The difference between actual birth date and official registration date

### 4. Child Name — Analysis

The child's name after "Männl." (male) was examined at maximum native resolution (zoom level 13/14 in the Archion tilezoom viewer). The letters read:

| Position | Letter (literal) | Alternative reading |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| 1 (Capital) | D or J | Kurrent J has similar structure to this scribe's capital |
| 2 | ü (two clear dots) | Could be u with ink marks as "umlaut" |
| 3 | f | ſ (long s) — identical appearance |
| 4 | l | t (without visible crossbar at this resolution) |
| 5 | a | u (common Kurrent confusion) |
| 6 | n | s (round s at word end) |

**Literal reading**: "Jüflan" or "Düflan" — NOT a real German name.

**Most plausible corrected reading**: **"Justus"** (J-u-s-t-u-s), applying standard Kurrent correction patterns:
- J→D (this scribe's known issue, proven with K→D in "Konze")
- u→ü (umlaut dots may be artifacts)
- ſ→f (long s and f are nearly identical)
- t→l (crossbar not visible at this resolution)
- u→a (common confusion in Kurrent)
- s→n (round s vs. n ambiguity)

**Supporting evidence for "Justus":**
- Extremely common male name in Hessen in this period
- The Konze family used this name (Justus Ernst Konze, b. 1829, is another research subject)
- Capital letter structure is consistent with Kurrent J when compared to "Joh." in the father's name

**Against "Justus":**
- Requires 4+ simultaneous letter corrections
- The umlaut dots appear genuine, not artifactual
- The capital letter differs somewhat from the "Joh." J (though scribes vary letter forms by size/context)

**Alternative reading**: "Gustav" is also possible (G-u-s-t-a-v) but less likely given the capital letter shape.

**VERDICT**: The child name **requires the user's Kurrent reading expertise** to confirm. The literal reading is not a real name; "Justus" is the strongest hypothesis but cannot be confirmed from the image alone.
