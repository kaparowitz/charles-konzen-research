1. **No Konze confirmation for a child born ~1835**: The Entry 195 child (born Jan 23-24, 1835, son of Joh. Heinrich Konze, Kaufmann) was NOT confirmed in Deisel. 2. **Joh. Heinrich Konze confirmed as real person**: The confirmation of Ludwig Konze (born 1821) as son of "Joh. Heinrich Konze, Ackermann" independently corroborates the existence of this father and family in Deisel.

3. **Ludwig Konze = older brother**: Born October 1821, Ludwig was ~13 years older than the Entry 195 child. He was confirmed in Deisel around 1835/36 (at age ~14).

4. **Occupation discrepancy**: Joh. Heinrich Konze is called "Ackermann" (farmer) in the 1835 confirmation of his son Ludwig, but "Kaufmann" (merchant) in the 1835 baptism entry (Entry 195). This may reflect an occupation change or dual role.

5. **Multiple Konze branches in Deisel**: At least two Konze families — Joh. Heinrich Konze (Ackermann/Kaufmann) and Jost Konze (Ackermann) — plus Konze women married into Köster and Schildkunpf families.

6. **Emigration hypothesis strengthened**: The absence of both a burial AND a confirmation for the Entry 195 child in Deisel strongly supports the hypothesis that the Konze family emigrated (likely to America) sometime between ~1835 and ~1849. This aligns with the broader pattern of German emigration from Kurhessen in the 1840s.

### Assessment

**Possible Explanations:**

**Scenario A — Same child, date error in confirmation register:**
The confirmation register was written 14 years after the birth. The pastor may have copied the birth date incorrectly from the original baptism record, or the family reported it inaccurately. Date errors of 2-3 weeks were not uncommon in secondary records. The occupation differences (Kaufmann/Ackermann/[?]bäcker) could reflect the father's changing or multiple roles.

**Scenario B — Different Johann H. Konze family:**
There could be TWO Johann Heinrich Konze men in nearby villages (Deisel and Trendelburg/Friedrichsfeld), each with a son born in January 1835. Different mothers, different occupations, different birth dates — but same father's name and surname is coincidental.

**Scenario C — Same father, different mothers:**
If Johann Heinrich Konze had a wife in Trendelburg/Friedrichsfeld (Christina Wilhelmina, geb. [name]) and a different wife in Deisel (geb. Köster?), he could theoretically have children born 15 days apart in January 1835. This is extremely unlikely but not impossible.

**Most likely**: Scenario A (same child, date error) or Scenario B (different family). The 15-day date discrepancy and different occupations make a definitive connection uncertain.

### Key Conclusions (Updated)

1. **Carl Wilhelm Konze confirmed 1849 in Trendelburg**: A male child named Carl Wilhelm Konze, son of Johann H. Konze, born January 8, 1835, was confirmed at Pfingsten 1849 at age 14. "Carl" = "Charles" in German.

2. **Index was incomplete**: This entry was OMITTED from the alphabetical index. Without direct page-by-page searching, it would have been missed entirely.

3. **Connection to Entry 195 is possible but uncertain**: The father's name matches, the birth month/year matches, and "Carl" = "Charles." But the 15-day birth date discrepancy and different occupation/mother details prevent a definitive connection.

4. **Later Konze confirmations exist**: Five additional Konze males were confirmed in Trendelburg in the 1866-1873 period (indexed entries at Seite 159-189). These are a later generation of the Konze family who remained in the area.

5. **Charles L. Gannsen connection**: US records give Charles Gannsen's birth date as January 26, 1835. Entry 195 gives January 23-24, 1835. This Trendelburg entry gives January 8, 1835. The Entry 195 date is closer to the US date (3-day difference vs. 18-day difference), suggesting Entry 195 may be the better match for Charles Gannsen.

6. **"Carl" = "Charles"**: If Carl Wilhelm Konze IS the Entry 195 child, his German name was likely **Carl Wilhelm** and he became "Charles" upon emigrating. The "L" middle initial in "Charles L. Gannsen" remains unexplained by "Wilhelm" (which would give "W").
