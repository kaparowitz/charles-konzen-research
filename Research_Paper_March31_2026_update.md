**ABSTRACT**

*This report summarizes research to date into the German identity of Charles Gannsen (born 26 January 1835, "Freundburg, Kurhessen" = Trendelburg area; died 1903, St. Louis, Missouri), who served in the Union Army as a member of Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery, and later became known as Charles L. Johnson. The central question is: what was his German surname before emigration, and from which village did he come? Two hypotheses have been investigated and neither is currently supported by sufficient evidence: the Herwig/Konze hypothesis (Hypothesis B) is definitively eliminated by destination mismatch; the Heisebeck Gans hypothesis (Hypothesis A) has been dropped for lack of any destination evidence connecting that family to Missouri. The report also presents new findings from the Trendelburg burial and marriage registers — including a previously unknown first wife of Andreas Konze (Wilhelmine née Hofedietz) and her children — and a new candidate for Charles's mother (Charlotte Herwig, b. 1812, Trendelburg). CRITICAL UPDATE (April 3–4, 2026): Peter Schrader has established that (1) there were MULTIPLE men named Andreas Konze in Trendelburg, and the Andreas Konze who married Wilhelmine Hofedietz is not necessarily the same Andreas Konze (b. 9 Mar 1787) who married Anna Christina Heidt — further Archion research is required to disentangle these identities; and (2) the Charlotte Herwig baptism record has been correctly re-read — Charlotte's mother was Anna Christina, widow of Joh. Eckhard Herwig (= Anna Christina Heidt), making Charlotte the illegitimate daughter of Anna Christina Heidt, born after Herwig's death. The identification remains open.*

# **1. The Subject: Charles Gannsen in American Records**

Charles Gannsen is known to American records under several name forms. He enlisted in St. Louis on December 2, 1862 as "L. Charles Gannsen" and later lived under the name Charles L. Johnson. He died of stomach cancer in St. Louis, Missouri in 1903 (FamilySearch Memory 6). The "L." is a middle initial representing a German given name he used in Germany but moved behind "Charles" upon settlement in America, a pattern common among German immigrants of this era.

FamilySearch records for the family note that the surname “Gannsen” derives from the village Hausname (house name) “Gans” — a traditional naming convention in the Trendelburg/Deisel area rather than a hereditary legal surname. This aligns with the documented use of the Deisel hamlet name “Gans” as a household identifier.

Two details in this record are particularly important for German research. First, the birthplace on the enlistment record - "Freundburg, Kurhessen" - is a phonetic rendering by an American clerk of "Trendelburg," the market town in northern Hesse-Kassel of which Deisel is a hamlet. Second, the "L." initial is a German first name. The most common German L-names for this era and region are Ludwig, Lorenz, Leopold, and Leonhard — any of which could be the first given name of a young man born in the Trendelburg area in January 1835.

Charles Gannsen’s home village before emigrating is directly confirmed by his 1866 marriage record at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church (record #676), FamilySearch ARK 61903/1:1:V2DX-4HX (Digital Folder 007513821). That record lists the groom as “Carl Gonnson, aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen.” The place-name “Deisse” is a phonetic rendering of Deisel, the hamlet within the Trendelburg area documented throughout this report. This record directly confirms Deisel (recorded as “Deisse”), Kreis Cassel, Churhessen, as Charles Gannsen’s village of origin.

# **3. Carl Louis Herwig: Definitively Not Charles Gannsen**

The central question raised by Peter's Stammtafel research was whether Carl Louis Herwig - born in Trendelburg in 1830, with the Konze family connection through his great-grandmother Anna Christina - could be the man who arrived in St. Louis as Charles Gannsen.

This question can now be answered definitively: Carl Louis Herwig is not Charles Gannsen.

## **3.1 The Plymouth, Massachusetts Marriage (1855) and Subsequent Fate**

Ancestry collections 2511 and 2495 (Massachusetts vital records) both document the same event: a man named "Lewis Harwig" (spelled "Lewes Herweg" in the original Plymouth town register image) married Catherine Sharda in Plymouth, Massachusetts on October 6, 1855. The groom was age 24, born in Germany, occupation ropemaker. His father is recorded as "John Herweg" (a standard American phonetic rendering of "Johannes Herwig").

The match to Carl Louis Herwig is established by six independent factors: the birth year (~1831, consistent with Carl Louis's 1830 birth); the country of birth (Germany); the father's given name (John = Johannes); the father's surname (Herweg = Herwig); the geographic location (Plymouth, Massachusetts, consistent with the Grandjot report's claim that Carl Louis went to Massachusetts); and the first name (Lewis is an anglicization of Louis, Carl Louis's second given name). Plymouth's location near the Plymouth Cordage Company, one of the largest rope manufacturers in the United States employing German immigrants throughout the 1840s-1870s, provides occupational context.

The conclusive evidence eliminating Carl Louis as Charles Gannsen is the wife. The man in Plymouth, Massachusetts had a wife named Catherine Sharda. Charles Gannsen's wife was Augusta (Auguste) Kreichelt, whom he married in St. Louis around 1866. A married man in Plymouth in 1855 with a living wife could not plausibly be the Charles Gannsen whose only documented wife was Augusta Kreichelt in Missouri.

Additional US records searching found no trace of Carl Louis Herwig (the Plymouth, Massachusetts ropemaker) after the October 1855 marriage to Catherine Sharda. No death record, no 1860 census entry, no children's birth records, and no return passage to Germany were found. He most likely died in Plymouth between 1855 and 1861, before Massachusetts death registration was comprehensive. His wife Catherine Sharda is likewise untraced after 1855. Carl Louis Herwig as a candidate for Charles Gannsen is definitively closed.

Notably, Massachusetts was the destination of the entire Herwig family, not merely Carl Louis. The Grandjot genealogy report, drawing on Hessian emigration records at HStAM Marburg, states explicitly: "The destination for all was Massachusetts." The family group emigrating in 1854 consisted of Johannes Herwig (b. 22 August 1802, Trendelburg, patriarch) and his wife Catharina Elisabeth, née Grandjot (b. 29 July 1802, Schöneberg), traveling with four children under their manifest travel names: "Annette" = Jeanette Elisabeth Herwig (b. 4 July 1833, Trendelburg); "Agnes" = Maria Magdalena Herwig (b. 11 April 1835, Trendelburg); "Carl" = Carl Peter Herwig (b. 24 October 1841, Trendelburg); and "Theodor" = Carl August Theodor Herwig (b. 23 July 1844, Trendelburg). Carl Louis (b. 27 November 1830) had emigrated separately — he likely arrived in New York in September 1851 and made his way to Plymouth, where he is documented by 1855. Massachusetts thus appears as the destination of every member of the Herwig family. The Stammtafel records deaths in the USA for all siblings except Carl Louis, for whom only a departure mark appears — consistent with his early disappearance from the Plymouth record.

## **3.2 The Konze Name Mechanism: Reassessment**

Peter's question "Konze - Gonsson...?" — whether the Konze connection could explain the surname Gannsen — prompted detailed analysis. The question has two distinct components that must be assessed separately: (a) whether Carl Louis Herwig specifically could have adopted a Konze-derived name, and (b) whether the Konze family more broadly could have produced Charles Gannsen through another line.

- Andreas Konze died October 13, 1830 - forty-four days before Carl Louis Herwig was born (November 27, 1830). The proposed mechanism for Carl Louis specifically required that Johannes Herwig or his son adopted a Konze-derived name upon emigration. That mechanism is eliminated by the Plymouth marriage record: Carl Louis Herwig went to Massachusetts as Herwig/Harwig, not as Gannsen or any Konze variant. He never used a Konze-derived name.

- The phonetic pathway from Konze to Gannsen is, however, more plausible than initially assessed. K and G are both velar stops differing only in voicing — a well-attested sound shift in German dialects and in the ears of English-speaking American clerks. "Konze" → "Gonze" → "Gonsen" → "Gannsen" is a credible phonetic chain for either a German speaker in a Hessian dialect or an American clerk rendering a spoken name. The dismissal of any phonetic connection between Konze and Gannsen was premature. If a Konze family member emigrated and gave their name orally to an American official, the resulting spelling could plausibly be Gonson, Gannsen, or a similar form.

- A systematic investigation found no evidence of any Konze-family member known to have emigrated to the United States. The Konze family appears in Deisel civil records through 1883 and 1894. However, this absence of evidence is not conclusive: the pre-1818 Trendelburg church registers — which would document the children of Andreas Konze and Wilhelmine née Hofedietz — remain undigitized and physically held at the Landeskirchliches Archiv Kassel.

- A critical 47-year gap in Trendelburg church registers online (1772-1818) prevents verification of Andreas Konze's parentage or any pre-1818 relationships. This gap is not a data omission but reflects the physical absence of those registers from Archion's digital holdings.

The Carl Louis Herwig → Konze mechanism is definitively closed. The broader Konze thread, however, remains open through a different route: the three daughters of Andreas Konze and Wilhelmine née Hofedietz (Anna Christine, Christine Wilhelmine, and Maria Wilhelmine) are all of the right age to be Charles Gannsen's mother. If any of them married a man named Gans — or emigrated and gave their own surname as Konze, which was then rendered as Gannsen by an American clerk — the Konze family would resurface as directly relevant. A complete survey of all six marriages in the 1827 Trendelburg marriage register (images 1318–1319) found no Konze daughter among the brides; see Section 3.6 for the full inventory.

## **3.3 New Finding: A First Wife — Wilhelmine, née Hofedietz**

On March 26, 2026, the Archion burial register for Trendelburg (register 220114, Image 682, Entry 4) was examined directly. The burial entry for Andreas Konze — confirmed by exact birth date (9 March 1787) and death date (13 October 1830, evening 4:30 PM, buried 16 October) — names his first wife as Wilhelmine. The maiden name in that entry was initially read as "Grosberndt," but a cross-reference search conducted on March 27, 2026 establishes that this reading is almost certainly in error, and the correct name is Hofedietz (see Section 3.3 Note below).

This reveals that Andreas Konze had at least two marriages:

- First marriage: to Wilhelmine née Hofedietz (date unknown, pre-1818). She almost certainly died before 5 June 1818, when Andreas married Anna Christina Heidt. Any children from this first marriage were baptized before 1818 — in the 1772-1818 Archion gap — and cannot be verified from online sources alone.

- Second marriage: to Anna Christina Heidt (5 June 1818), documented by Peter Schrader's Stammtafel. No children from this marriage are documented.

**IMPORTANT UPDATE (April 3, 2026):** Peter Schrader has determined, after examining the Konze/Hofedietz couple records, that the Andreas Konze who married Wilhelmine Hofedietz **cannot be the same man** as the Andreas Konze (b. 9 Mar 1787) who married Anna Christina Heidt in 1818. There were multiple men named Andreas Konze in Trendelburg. Peter requires an additional month of Archion access to disentangle these identities. This finding means that §3.3 and §3.4 (below) may describe a different Andreas Konze than the one in Anna Christina Heidt's Stammtafel. See §13.1 for full details.

Note on the Maiden Name Correction (March 27, 2026): The initial burial register reading produced 'Grosberndt' as Wilhelmine's maiden name. Cross-reference searching on FamilySearch and Ancestry (March 27, 2026) found zero records for 'Grosberndt' in any German or Hessian database — the name is completely unattested in Hesse-Kassel records. By contrast, 'Hofedietz' is a Hesse-Kassel family name attested in the Trendelburg area, the villages immediately adjacent to Trendelburg. The same Image 682 that was examined shows a 'Maria nee Grosberndt' (wife of a Taglohner named Ahrend) buried near the Konze entry; it is probable that 'Grosberndt' was inadvertently read from this adjacent entry and mistakenly assigned to Wilhelmine Konze. The correct maiden name, confirmed by the user (Jed Johnson) against the primary source, is Hofedietz.

## **3.4 Children of Andreas Konze and Wilhelmine Hofedietz: Now Partially Identified**

Following the burial register discovery, a systematic manual search of the Archion Trendelburg Kirchenbuch 1819-1831 (register 220099) was conducted on March 26, 2026. Although the children were born before 1818 (in the undigitized gap), older children would appear in this register at confirmation age (14-16 years) and daughters would appear in the marriage entries. Three children are now documented:

- Anna Christine Konze: appears as a bride in the 1820 marriage entries (Archion register 220099, page 1 of marriages). Described as "legitimate daughter of the local farmer Konze." Born approximately 1798-1803. As the eldest daughter she likely married before her sisters. Father's given name partially legible; the entry is consistent with Andreas Konze, Ackerbuerger (farmer-citizen).

- Maria Wilhelmine Konze: born 22 April 1807. Appears in the confirmation entries (page 7 of confirmations, confirmed 1821). Father described as "hiesiger Ackermann Konze" (local farmer Konze). Middle name Wilhelmine signals naming after her mother, consistent with the Hofedietz marriage.

- Christine Wilhelmine Konze: born approximately 3 October 1805. Appears in the confirmation entries (page 9 of confirmations, confirmed 1822). Father's name in the entry appears to read Andreas Konze. Middle name Wilhelmine again confirms the mother's Hofedietz name.

A fourth Konze, Ludwig Konze, appears as a groom in 1824 and is described as "sole legitimate son of the late Konze, farmer." However, his father is described as already deceased before 1824, whereas Andreas Konze did not die until October 1830. Ludwig therefore belongs to a different Konze branch and is not a son of Andreas and Wilhelmine.

Chronological assessment: Anna Christine (born c. 1798-1803), Christine Wilhelmine (born c. 1805), and Maria Wilhelmine (born 1807) were all approximately 28-37 years old in 1835 when Charles Gannsen was born. All three are old enough to be his mother. For any of them to be Charles's mother, she would need to have married a man named Gans — a Konze daughter marrying a Gans man would produce a son baptized as Gans, later Americanized to Gannsen in Missouri. This remains speculative until a Gans-Konze marriage record is found; the Heisebeck Gans family has not been connected to Missouri and is not being actively pursued, but the underlying question of a Konze-Gans marriage cannot be ruled out from the evidence examined to date.

This is a new research avenue opened by today's burial register examination. The key records needed to take it further are (a) the pre-1818 Trendelburg church register at the Landeskirchliches Archiv Kassel, which would contain the actual baptism entries for these children and confirm the father's name as Andreas Konze; and (b) any marriage record for Anna Christine, Christine Wilhelmine, or Maria Wilhelmine Konze after 1831 — if any married a Gans, that would be significant.

## **3.5 Anna Christine Konze — Marriage to Carl Wilhelm Hofedietz (1820)**

A search of the Archion Trendelburg marriage register (220099) identified the marriage entry for Anna Christine Konze in 1820. She married Carl Wilhelm Hofedietz, a widower and kastenmeister (church treasury administrator) and farmer from Sielen. This is confirmed by FamilySearch Family Tree ID P4TZ-1J2 (Carl Wilhelm Hofedietz, born 24 Aug 1783 Sielen, died 13 Mar 1861 Sielen), whose Archion source note points to the same Trendelburg KB 1819–1831, seite 3/237 entry. His first wife Maria Sophia Hofedietz (1793–1819) had died the previous year, confirming the widower detail. This definitively rules out Anna Christine as a Konze–Gans connection: her husband's surname was Hofedietz, not Gans, and no Gans connection appears anywhere in the entry. Anna Christine died 29 January 1852 in Sielen, with no children documented from the Konze–Hofedietz marriage. She is eliminated from the Konze–Gans marriage hypothesis. Note: the subsequent correction of Andreas Konze's first wife's maiden name to Hofedietz (Section 3.3) adds a new dimension to this marriage — if Anna Christine's mother was Wilhelmine née Hofedietz, then Anna Christine married into her own mother's family (Carl Wilhelm Hofedietz, born 1783 Sielen, was from the Sielen Hofedietz family). Note: Hofedietz and Hofedietz are distinct but phonetically similar names; whether they represent the same family with variant spelling or two different families cannot be determined from current records. If they are the same family, this represents classic rural Hessian endogamy.

# **4. The Deisel Konze Family: Separate Research Thread**

Parallel research into the Konze family of Deisel (a hamlet 3 km from Heisebeck, in the Trendelburg area) documents a separate family network centered on Johann Christoph Konze. **UPDATE (April 4, 2026):** A breakthrough search of the Deisel Kirchenbuch 1736–1796 (register 218821) has now identified the **founding patriarch** of the Deisel Konze family: **Andreas Kontze (I)**, former Küster (sexton), who died March 2, 1791, aged 72 years 6 months (b. ~September 1718). ## **4.1 Two Andreas Konze Men Identified**

Civil records and Peter's Stammtafel together confirm two distinct men named Andreas Konze were active in the Trendelburg area:

- The Stammtafel Andreas Konze (born c. 1787, Trendelburg; died 13 October 1830, Trendelburg), who married Anna Christina Heidt in 1818. His parentage is unknown from accessible records.

- A Deisel Andreas Konze (born c. 1809, likely Deisel area; died 5 May 1883, Deisel), who was a son of Johann Christoph Konze and had a son named Philipp Theodor Konze. This man appears in Hesse civil death records (Ancestry collection 61119).

If the 1787 Trendelburg Andreas Konze was a son of Johann Christoph Konze, the two families would be related. However, this connection cannot be verified from available online records - it would require the pre-1818 Trendelburg church registers described in Section 5.

New FamilySearch research (March 28, 2026) confirms that the Deisel Konze family descends from a Johann Henrich Kontze (born ~1747, FamilySearch PID: G8HG-P2S) who was already resident in Deisel by 1770 — a full generation before the Trendelburg Andreas Konze (born 9 March 1787) was born. This establishes conclusively that the two families are **distinct lines** sharing a common surname and given name, not a father-son relationship. **UPDATE (April 4, 2026):** Johann Henrich Kontze (b. ~March 1747) has now been confirmed as a son of Andreas Kontze (I), the Deisel Küster, through discovery of his burial record in KB 218821 (d. April 17, 1792, Ackermann, age 45 years 25 days). The Deisel family's roots now extend back to ~1718 with the patriarch Andreas. The young “Andreas Konze” who died in Deisel in 1828 (entry §26) was therefore a member of this older Deisel branch, not a son of the Trendelburg Andreas Konze.

Confirmed primary source data — Archion register 218824 (Kirchenbuch Deisel 1796–1831), image 1868, §26: Andreas Konze is recorded as an Einwohner (adult male resident of Deisel) who died on Sunday, 24 February 1828 at 10 o’clock in the morning, and was buried on Monday, 25 February 1828. This definitively establishes that the Deisel §26 Andreas Konze (born ~1799, Einwohner of Deisel, died 24 February 1828) is a completely distinct person from the Trendelburg Andreas Konze (born 9 March 1787, died 13 October 1830) — Anna Christina Heidt’s third husband. The two men differ in birth year (~1799 vs. 1787), in village (Deisel vs. Trendelburg), and in date of death (24 February 1828 vs. 13 October 1830). No conflation of the two is possible.

# **6. Hypothesis A: The Heisebeck Gans Family — Dropped for Insufficient Evidence**

Hypothesis A, which proposed that Charles Gannsen was a child of the Heisebeck Gans family (Henrich Wilhelm Gans, tailor, born 1818, Heisebeck), has been dropped due to the absence of any destination evidence connecting that family to Missouri or St. Louis.

## **6.1 What the Evidence Showed**

- LAGIS HESAUS (the Hessian Emigration and Settlement Index) documents the Heisebeck Gans family in six HStAM files (Best. 180 Hofgeismar Nr. 133). The family emigrated in two waves: around 1840 and around 1846.

- The HESAUS index (Auerbach, Vol. II, p. 137) records a child of the Gans family born approximately 1839 — consistent with Charles Gannsen's stated age of 23 at his December 1862 enlistment.

- Heisebeck is approximately 3 km from Deisel, and the Deisel church would have recorded baptisms for Heisebeck residents. The phonetic rendering "Freundburg, Kurhessen" on the enlistment record is consistent with a Trendelburg-area origin.

- The surname transformation "Gans" → "Gannsen" follows a documented American pattern of adding a suffix to short Germanic surnames.

## **6.2 Why the Hypothesis Was Dropped**

The critical weakness is the absence of any destination evidence. Charles Gannsen is documented in St. Louis, Missouri from at least December 2, 1862 (enlistment date). The HESAUS index and the HStAM emigration file do not specify a US destination for the Heisebeck Gans family, and no record has been found placing any member of that family in Missouri or St. Louis. Without a documented destination connection, the overlap in approximate birth year and Trendelburg-area origin is circumstantial and insufficient to sustain the hypothesis. The coincidence of the surname "Gans" with the Americanized form "Gannsen" is suggestive but not evidentially binding — particularly since the HStAM emigration file has not been examined and may record a destination entirely different from Missouri.

If future research establishes that the Heisebeck Gans family's destination was St. Louis or Missouri — through the HStAM Best. 180 Hofgeismar Nr. 133 file, a US arrival record, or a census entry — the hypothesis could be reconsidered. The physical emigration dossier is held at the Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg, pages 199 and 259, and has not been digitized.

# **9. Charlotte Herwig — Research Results (March 27, 2026)**

On March 27, 2026, Peter Schrader sent an additional email with the subject line "Helene Herwig ?" This email introduced Charlotte Herwig as a candidate for Charles Gannsen's mother. Subsequent research on FamilySearch, Ancestry, and Archion has produced both confirming details and significant complications, summarized below.

## **9.1 Charlotte Herwig: Background and Chronological Problem**

Charlotte Herwig was born 3 April 1812 in Trendelburg. She is connected to the Herwig family network — the same family that produced Johannes Herwig (b. 1802), whose emigration to the United States in 1854 is documented in the Grandjot genealogy report. Charlotte's age is consistent with her being Charles Gannsen's mother: born 1812, she would have been 22-23 years old in January 1835.

A definitive chronological barrier rules out Johann Eckhard Herwig as Charlotte’s father. The baptism record (bap Johann Eckhard Herwig.jpg) confirms his birth as 17 December 1766, baptized 21 December 1766, in Erbsen; the death record confirms his death 2 April 1810 in Trendelburg. Charlotte was born 3 April 1812 — nearly two full years (approximately 23 months) after Johann Eckhard’s death.

**RESOLVED (April 4, 2026):** Peter Schrader has now correctly re-read Charlotte Herwig’s baptism record, which resolves the parentage question definitively. The record shows:

- **Child:** Charlotte
- **Mother:** Anna Christina, **widow** of Joh. Eck[hard] Herwig — this is Anna Christina Heidt (b. 14 Dec 1774), the same woman who later married Andreas Konze in 1818
- **Status:** Uneheliches Kind (illegitimate child) — Charlotte’s biological father is not named
- **Born:** Afternoon, 3rd of April, at 3 PM
- **Grandfather’s occupation:** Hufschmidt (farrier) at the Grafschaft Vorwerk (county estate farm)
- **Godparents:** Catharina Elisabeth, wife of Joh. He[n]mann Heidt. Johann Herrmann Heidt was a "fruit measurer" — Peter notes that "Frucht" (fruit) in this context means grain, so Heidt was a grain measurer

Charlotte was therefore the illegitimate daughter of Anna Christina Heidt, born approximately two years after Anna Christina’s first husband Johann Eckhard Herwig died. The father is unknown. The Heidt family godparents confirm Anna Christina’s maiden name and her continued connection to her birth family. Charlotte carried the surname "Herwig" from her mother’s deceased husband, as was customary for children born to widows in this era and region.

This finding has major genealogical significance: Charlotte Herwig (b. 1812) was Anna Christina Heidt’s daughter, making Charlotte a half-sibling of Johannes Herwig (b. 1802, son of Anna Christina and Johann Eckhard Herwig). Charlotte was also a step-niece of Andreas Konze, whom Anna Christina married in 1818. See §13.2 for full analysis.

## **9.2 Helene Herwig: A Daughter, Not Charlotte Herself**

The Grandjot genealogy report documents "Helene Herwig (single, not married) from Trendelburg emigrated separately in 1855" as an open question — her relationship to Johannes Herwig was unknown. Peter Schrader initially suggested Helene might be Charlotte traveling under a different name.

Research on 27 March 2026 has resolved this question with a definitive finding. FamilySearch (U.S. and Canada Passenger and Immigration Lists, collection 1068) contains a record for Helene Herwig — recorded on the manifest as "Hlem Herwig" — with the following data: age 20, residence Trendelburg, immigrated 25 September 1855, ship Republic departing from Bremen, destination Plymouth, Massachusetts. Her age of 20 means she was born approximately 1835 — not 1812. Helene is therefore almost certainly Charlotte's daughter, not Charlotte herself. This is consistent with the Grandjot report listing Helene as an open question rather than identifying her with the known family members of Johannes Herwig.

Notably, Helene's destination was Plymouth, Massachusetts — the same city where Carl Louis Herwig was documented from 1855 onward, and the same state where Johannes Herwig's family settled in 1854. Helene appears to have emigrated to join the established Herwig cluster in Plymouth, not to go to New York or another city. This destination detail both confirms she is part of the Trendelburg Herwig network and further separates the Herwig family trajectory (Massachusetts) from Charles Gannsen's (Missouri).

### **11.27 Hofedietz-Grossbernd Connection — Analysis (April 3, 2026)**

The question of whether "Hofedietz" was a Hausname (farm name) for the Grossbernd family, or whether the Grandjot Stammtafel's "Marie Wilhelmine Hofeditz" is a different person from Andreas Kontze's wife Wilhelmine née Grossbernd, has been a persistent uncertainty since the correction documented in §11.15.2. This section synthesizes all available evidence and arrives at a conclusion.

#### **11.27.1 The Two Source Conflicts**

Two primary sources give different surnames for Andreas Kontze's wife:

1. **The 1810 baptism record** (register 220096, page 173, image 1070): Clearly reads "Wilhelmine geb. Grossberndin" — the feminine genitive form of Grossbernd. The godfather is "Joh. Friedrich Grossberne, hiesiger Bürger und Ackermann," likely the wife's father or brother.

2. **The Grandjot Stammtafel**: Lists "Marie Wilhelmine Hofeditz" (b. 23 November 1784, Trendelburg; d. 3 June 1850, Schöneberg), annotated as "Hofeditz (married name); born Trendelburg." She appears among the children and in-laws of David Grandjot of Schöneberg.

The 1810 baptism record is a contemporary primary source — a church register entry made at the time of the event. It takes precedence over the Stammtafel, which is a compiled secondary source. The legal surname was Grossbernd.

#### **11.27.2 Evidence for Geographic Overlap**

Both families are documented in the same hamlet:

- **§11.22.3 (image 1085, year 1815):** A baptism entry lists "Maria Elisabeth geb. Hofedietz in Vöhlein" on the same page as "Susanna Margaretha geb. Grossbernd in Vöhlein." Both women are identified as being from Vöhlein, a small settlement near Trendelburg.

- **§11.15.6 (image 1078, year 1813):** Maria Christine, wife of Christian Hofeditz, Ackermann zu Stammen, served as godmother for a Grossbernd child. This confirms social ties between the two families.

- **§11.25a (image 1845, year 1813):** In the Deisel burial register, Hoffeditz family entries appear directly below Ludwig Kontze's burial on page 43 — specifically, Johann Christoph Hoffeditz (stillborn) and Anna Catharina und Johann Hermann Hoffeditz (age 88). This places the Hoffeditz family in Deisel alongside the Kontze family.

#### **11.27.3 Evidence Against a Hausname Relationship**

Several factors indicate the Hofedietz and Grossbernd families are **distinct families**, not alternate names for the same household:

1. **Independent lineages:** The Grossbernd/Grossberndt family has a well-documented separate genealogy tracing to Bartold Grosbert (b. 1595) in Sielen. According to the Grossberndt family genealogy (grossberndt.de), the earliest documentary evidence appears in church registers of Sielen (1595), Trendelburg (1673), Friedrichsfeld (1807), and Deisel (1875). The family were traditionally smiths and wagon builders — a distinct occupational identity.

2. **The Hofedietz family is independently documented:** Christian Hofeditz appears as "Ackermann zu Stammen" (farmer of Stammen) — a separate village from the Grossbernd strongholds of Sielen and Trendelburg. The Hofedietz family also appears in Deisel burial records (1813), establishing their own independent presence across multiple parishes.

3. **Both families appear simultaneously in the same records:** On image 1085 (1815), both a Hofedietz woman and a Grossbernd woman are listed as being "in Vöhlein" on the same page. If one were merely a Hausname for the other, we would not expect both to appear independently in the same register with different family members.

4. **The Grandjot Stammtafel person differs in key details:** Marie Wilhelmine Hofeditz (b. 23 Nov 1784) died 3 June 1850 in Schöneberg — the Grandjot family's home village. If this were the same person as Andreas Kontze's wife (who would have been widowed by 1830), she would have had to remarry into either a Hofeditz or Grandjot family and relocate to Schöneberg. While not impossible, no supporting evidence for such a remarriage exists, and the Stammtafel's annotation that "Hofeditz" is a "married name" suggests she married a Hofeditz man — not that Hofeditz was her own birth surname.

#### **11.27.4 The Hausname Hypothesis — Evaluated**

In the Trendelburg area, the Hausname (farm name) tradition is well-documented. The "Gans" Hausname in Deisel — from which Charles Gannsen derived his American surname — is a prime example. Could "Hofedietz" have been a Hausname for a Grossbernd family farm in Vöhlein?

**Arguments for:**
- Both families lived in Vöhlein, a small settlement where farm names could easily attach to resident families
- The two names appear in overlapping social circles (godparent connections)
- In German Hausname tradition, a family's legal surname (Familienname) could differ from the farm name (Hofname) by which they were locally known

**Arguments against:**
- The Grossbernd family has its own multi-century documented lineage from Sielen (1595) through Trendelburg and beyond — a family with deep roots and a well-established surname
- Hofedietz individuals appear independently with their own family units (Christian Hofeditz, Ackermann zu Stammen; Johann Hermann Hoffeditz, age 88, in Deisel)
- Both names appear as independent surnames on the same register pages, which would be unusual if one were merely an alias for the other
- No record uses connecting language ("vulgo," "genannt," "alias," "modo," "sive") to link the two names

#### **11.27.5 Conclusion**

**The Hofedietz and Grossbernd families are two distinct families** who were geographic neighbors in and around Vöhlein and maintained close social ties (evidenced by godparent relationships). Andreas Kontze's wife was legally named **Wilhelmine née Grossbernd**, as confirmed by the 1810 baptism record. The Grandjot Stammtafel's "Marie Wilhelmine Hofeditz" (b. 1784, d. 1850 Schöneberg) is most likely a **different Wilhelmine** — either a Grandjot daughter who married a Hofeditz man, or a woman from another family who married into the Hofeditz household and eventually relocated to Schöneberg.

The confusion arose from three coincidences: (a) both women were named Wilhelmine, (b) both were from Trendelburg, and (c) the Hofedietz and Grossbernd families were neighbors in Vöhlein with overlapping social networks. The 1807 marriage record at page 322, which was initially read as "Grosbernd" then temporarily "corrected" to "Hofedietz" based on the Stammtafel, should now be read as **Grossbernd** — consistent with the 1810 baptism record.

**The Hofedietz family nonetheless remains genealogically significant** for the Kontze research: their presence in Deisel alongside the Kontze family (image 1845, page 43, year 1813) and their connection to the Grossbernd network suggests they were part of the broader community of families that included both the Kontze and Grossbernd households. The 1827 marriage of Andreas Konze (widower) to Maria Doris/Sophia Hofedietz (§3.6) further confirms that the Kontze and Hofedietz families continued to intermarry in the next generation.

#### **11.29.1 Historical Context: Hessian Military Service**

The Landgraviate of Hesse-Kassel (Kurhessen) was famously involved in renting regiments to foreign powers, most notably to Great Britain during the American Revolutionary War (1776–1783). Hessian soldiers served in North America between 1776 and 1783, and detailed muster rolls and military records survive from this period.

If Johann Christoph Kontze served as a Grenadier in the Hessian army, his service records could include:

- **Muster rolls (Stammrollen):** These typically record the soldier's name, birthplace, age, physical description, unit assignment, and dates of service.
- **Subsidy treaties records:** The British-Hessian subsidy agreements generated lists of troops.
- **Desertion/casualty records:** If he served in America, records of his deployment, injury, or return would exist.

#### **11.29.2 Archival Sources for Hessian Military Records**

The primary repositories for Hessian military records are:

1. **Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg (HStAM):** Holds the main body of Hesse-Kassel military records, including muster rolls (Bestand 4h: Hessische Truppen im Amerikanischen Unabhängigkeitskrieg).
2. **Lidgerwood Collection, Morristown National Historical Park, New Jersey, USA:** Contains captured Hessian military documents relating to the American Revolution.
3. **Hetrina database (University of Kassel):** An online database of Hessian troops in the American Revolution, searchable by name.
4. **Johannes Schwalm Historical Association:** Maintains research resources on Hessian soldiers.

#### **11.29.3 Online Search Feasibility**

The **Hetrina database** (Hessische Truppen im Amerikanischen Unabhängigkeitskrieg) maintained by the University of Kassel is the most accessible online resource. A search for "Kontze," "Contze," or "Konze" in this database could reveal whether Johann Christoph served in the American Revolution. ### **11.31 Task 8: "Freundburg" Interpretation — Reassessment (April 3, 2026)**

The birthplace on Charles Gannsen's 1862 enlistment record — "Freundburg, Kurhessen" — has been interpreted since the outset of this research as a phonetic rendering of "Trendelburg." Peter Schrader originally identified this equivalence, noting that an English-speaking American clerk could easily mishear "Trendel-" as "Freund-" (German for "friend"), yielding "Freundburg" instead of "Trendelburg."

#### **11.31.1 The Deisel Evidence Changes the Frame**

The 1866 St. Marcus marriage record (§1) identifies Charles Gannsen as "Carl Gonnson, aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" — confirming Deisel as his village of origin. The extensive Deisel Kirchenbuch findings (§11.25, §11.25g–11.25k) now document at least 10 Kontze family members in Deisel between 1760 and 1831, firmly establishing the family's multi-generational roots there.

This means "Freundburg" on the enlistment record was likely Charles's way of naming the nearest significant town — Trendelburg — as his birthplace, rather than the small hamlet of Deisel, which would have been unknown to an American clerk. This is a standard pattern among German immigrants: they named the nearest well-known town rather than their actual village when asked for a birthplace in America.

#### **11.31.2 Could "Freundburg" Refer to a Different Place?**

Two alternative interpretations have been considered:

1. **Freudenberg (various):** Multiple German towns are named Freudenberg. However, none in the Kurhessen region has a documented Kontze or Gans connection.

2. **Friedrichsfeld:** The colonial settlement near Trendelburg, where the Kontze family was documented "in Vöhlein" (§11.22.1). "Friedrichs-feld" is phonetically distant from "Freundburg," making this unlikely.

3. **Direct phonetic correspondence:** "Trendelburg" → "Freundburg" remains the most persuasive phonetic match: "Trendel" → "Freund" (common mishearing), "-burg" → "-burg" (preserved).

#### **11.31.3 Revised Interpretation**

The "Freundburg" = Trendelburg interpretation is **confirmed and strengthened** by the Deisel findings. Charles Gannsen was "aus Deisse" (from Deisel), which was a hamlet within the parish area of Trendelburg. When asked for his birthplace at enlistment in 1862, he gave "Trendelburg" — the market town that included Deisel — which the American clerk rendered as "Freundburg." The two records (enlistment: "Freundburg, Kurhessen"; marriage: "aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen") are fully consistent: one names the town, the other names the specific village.

No revision to the Freundburg interpretation is needed.

2. **Incomplete database coverage:** The HETRINA is based on surviving muster rolls and lists, which are not complete for all units and all years. Some soldiers served but left no surviving records.

3. **Different name spelling or Hausname:** The HETRINA indexes names as they appear in original muster rolls. Johann Christoph may have been indexed under a Hausname (e.g., "Altkonze"?) or an unusual spelling not yet searched.

4. **Service without American deployment:** Not all Hessian Grenadiers went to America. Some served in Europe or in garrison duty. Only those who actually deployed appear in HETRINA.

Given that Johann Christoph had a son (Johann Conrad) born ~1777 and another (Carl Wilhelm, illegitimate) born ~May 1776, it is possible that he was already a father at the time of the American deployments and was not called up — his "Grenadier" title referring to earlier or European service.

> **Jan: 4. Johannes, Joh: Christoph Contze, weil[and] Sein[es] Grenadiers auß dem Leib Grenad[ier] Bataillon [?] Söhulein, gestorben am 1te Abends gegen [?] Uhr, alt 3 Jahr 6 Monath einige 2 Wochen.**

**Translation:** Buried January 4, 1808: **Johannes**, [son of] the late **Johann Christoph Contze**, Grenadier from the **Leib Grenadier Battalion**, [child] died on January 1 in the evening, aged 3 years, 6 months, and about 2 weeks.

**Analysis of this entry:**

1. **Direct confirmation of Johann Christoph Contze as a Grenadier:** The entry specifically identifies Johann Christoph Contze as a Grenadier from the "Leib Grenadier Battalion" — a specific unit identification that was not previously known. This is far more detailed than the simple "Grenadier" title from the 1796 Deisel entries.

2. **"Weiland" (the late/deceased):** By January 1808, Johann Christoph Contze was **officially recorded as deceased**. This is critical for the Charles Gannsen research:
   - If Johann Christoph is the father of Charles Gannsen (who emigrated to America), the "weiland" status could mean he died in Germany before 1808, OR he left for America and was presumed dead by his family.
   - The child Johannes was born ~July 1804, meaning Johann Christoph was alive (or at least capable of fathering children) around October 1803–early 1804.

3. **Spelling "Contze" (not Kontze or Kunze):** This is the first documented use of the C-initial spelling in the church registers. It is consistent with the variant spellings found in the HETRINA database (Kontze, Konze, Contze, Cunze, Kuntze, Kunze) and adds another confirmed variant.

4. **"Leib Grenadier Battalion":** This specific unit identification is crucial for military records research. The Hessian Leib-Grenadier-Bataillon was an elite formation. Identifying the specific battalion allows targeted searches in the HStAM military archives (Bestand 4h) for Johann Christoph's service records.

5. **Friedrichsfeld connections on the same page:** The burial entries immediately following the Contze entry reference multiple "Colonist zu Friedrichsfeld" residents — confirming that Friedrichsfeld residents were registered in the Trendelburg Kirchenbuch, as hypothesized in §11.30.3.

6. **Relationship to the Deisel records:** The 1796 Deisel burial entries (§11.25g) describe Johann Christoph as a Grenadier in Deisel. This 1808 Trendelburg entry shows his family also had a presence in Trendelburg. This dual-parish connection is consistent with the family having roots in the Trendelburg area while also being registered in Deisel.

#### **11.36.5 HStAM Military Records Research (Step 5)**

Web research on the Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg (HStAM) and the Leib Grenadier Battalion identified the following:

**Unit identification:**
The "Leib Grenadier Bataillon" referenced in the Trendelburg KB entries corresponds to a battalion of the **Leibgarde-Regiment** of Hessen-Kassel. The regiment's history:
- 1786: The former Hessen-Hanauisches Grenadier Regiment was renamed "Leib-Grenadier-Regiment"
- 1788: The "2. Regiment Garde-Grenadier" became "Garde-Grenadier-Regiment"
- 1789: Both were consolidated, with the Leib-Grenadier-Regiment as the 1st Battalion
- 1795: Reorganized into three battalions including a "Flügel-Grenadier-Bataillon"
- **Garrison: Kassel** (the former Hessen-Hanauian regiment moved to Kassel after 1785)
- **Dissolved: November 1, 1806** when Napoleon occupied Hessen-Kassel and created the Kingdom of Westphalia

**Relevant archive holdings at HStAM:**
- **Best. 10a**: Anciennitätslisten (seniority lists) — lists of officers by seniority
- **Best. 10c**: Maß- und Rangierbücher (measurement and ranking books) — physical descriptions and rankings of soldiers by regiment
- **Best. 10d**: Militärgrundbücher (military basic records) — fundamental military service records
- **Best. 4h Nr. 4159**: Records of soldiers who died in America and their estates (HETRINA database) — relevant for the broader Contze/Kunze connection
- **Best. 15 Nr. 51**: Monthly lists of the Leibinfanterieregiment from June 1783

The **HETRINA** database (lagis-hessen.de) provides searchable records for ~30,000 Hessian soldiers who served in America, organized by regiment.

## **12.2 MAJOR DISCOVERY: Andreas Kontze (I) — Burial Record**

**Location:** Image 1558, right page, entry 223
**Month header:** "Martij" (March)

**Transcription:**

> *223t Andreas Kontze, gewesenen Küster, † d. 2ten dieses Abends 9 Uhr, alt 72 Jahr 6 Monat. Nr. 1*

**Translation:** Entry 223: Andreas Kontze, former sexton (Küster), died the 2nd of this [month] in the evening at 9 o'clock, aged 72 years 6 months. House Nr. 1.

**Derived facts:**
- **Death date:** March 2, 1791
- **Birth date (calculated):** ~September 1718
- **Age at death:** 72 years, 6 months
- **Occupation:** Gewesener Küster — "former sexton." The Küster was the church custodian/sexton who assisted the pastor, maintained the church building, rang the bells, and often served as schoolmaster. The position required literacy and was a mark of respectability in the village.
- **Residence:** House Nr. 1, Deisel — the first house in the village numbering system, suggesting long-established family presence.

**Context on the page:** The entry appears under the month header "Martij" on a right-hand page whose preceding entries include January/February 1791 entries. The left page of the same spread contains November–December 1790 entries, confirming the chronological sequence.

**Significance for the research:**

This is the **single most important primary source discovery** in the entire Charles Gannsen research project to date. Andreas Kontze (I) is documented as the father of Johann Friedrich Kontze (from a February 1795 marriage record in the same register) and is now confirmed as:

1. **The earliest identified Kontze patriarch in Deisel** — born ~1718, predating all other known Deisel Kontze records by decades.

2. **A man of standing in the community** — the Küster position was semi-official, salaried, and required literacy. This explains how the Kontze family was established and prominent in Deisel.

3. **The father of at least four sons** (see §12.4 below) — establishing a large family network in Deisel that persisted through the 19th century.

4. **The likely grandfather or great-grandfather of Charles Gannsen** — through one of his four known sons, all of whom were established in Deisel during the period when Charles's family originated.

## **13.2 Charlotte Herwig Baptism Record — Parentage Resolved**

Peter Schrader's April 4 email corrects the reading of Charlotte Herwig's baptism record (born 3 April 1812, Trendelburg). Peter describes his correction as "an important error" — meaning the record had previously been misread. The corrected transcription is:

- **Child:** Charlotte
- **Mother:** Anna Christina, **widow** of Joh. Eck[hard] Herwig
- **Grandfather's occupation:** Hufschmidt (farrier) at the Grafschaft Vorwerk (county estate farm)
- **Status:** Uneheliches Kind (illegitimate child)
- **Born:** Afternoon, 3rd of April, at 3 PM
- **Godparents:** Catharina Elisabeth, wife of Joh. He[n]mann Heidt. Peter notes: "Johann Herrmann Heidt was fruit measurer. Fruit means here grain."

**Analysis:**

1. **Charlotte's mother is now positively identified** as Anna Christina (née Heidt), who was the widow of Johann Eckhard Herwig at the time of Charlotte's birth. Johann Eckhard died 2 April 1810; Charlotte was born 3 April 1812, approximately two years later. Anna Christina was still using the Herwig surname as a widow.

2. **Charlotte was illegitimate.** Her biological father is not named in the baptism record. This was previously speculated (§9.1) but is now confirmed from the record itself.

3. **The Heidt family connection is confirmed** through the godparents. Catharina Elisabeth, wife of Johann Herrmann Heidt (grain measurer), served as godmother. The Heidt family was Anna Christina's birth family, and their involvement as godparents confirms the identification of Charlotte's mother as Anna Christina Heidt.

4. **The grandfather's occupation as Hufschmidt (farrier)** at the Grafschaft Vorwerk (county estate farm) provides a new occupational detail for the Herwig or Heidt family. This likely refers to a paternal or maternal grandfather — either Johann Eckhard Herwig's father or Anna Christina Heidt's father.

5. **Genealogical significance:** Charlotte Herwig (b. 1812) was a half-sibling of Johannes Herwig (b. 1802), who was the legitimate son of Anna Christina Heidt and Johann Eckhard Herwig. Charlotte and Johannes shared the same mother. Charlotte was also, from 1818 onward, a step-daughter of Andreas Konze (whichever Andreas Konze married Anna Christina Heidt — see §13.1). Charlotte's daughter Helene Herwig (b. ~1835) emigrated to Plymouth, Massachusetts in 1855 to join the Herwig family cluster there (§9.2), further confirming the family connection.

6. **Impact on the Charlotte Herwig hypothesis (§9):** Charlotte being Anna Christina Heidt's daughter strengthens her connection to both the Herwig and Konze family networks. If Charlotte had a relationship with a man named Gans — producing a son baptized Gans in January 1835 — Charles Gannsen would be a grandson of Anna Christina Heidt and a nephew of Johannes Herwig. However, no baptism record for such a child has been found in either Trendelburg or Deisel (§9.3, §9.4), and the Heisebeck Kirchenbuch remains unsearched.
