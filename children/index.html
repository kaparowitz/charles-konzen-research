The Seven Children of Charles Konzen & Auguste Kreichelt

Each baptized at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church, St. Louis · the surname spelling drifted across five distinct baptism-register renderings — the children's-baptism subset of the nine-attestation surname trail.

Charles and Auguste's seven children

Charles and Auguste married at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church on 7 January 1866. Over the next fourteen years they had seven children — four daughters and three sons. The eldest, Friedrich Wilhelm, died as an infant. Two daughters — Maria Josephine and Auguste — died young, in their early twenties, both in the same year (1890). The other four children — Katherine Carolina, Louise, Erwin Carl, and Bertha — lived into the twentieth century; three of them lived past seventy. Each child is profiled below in birth order.

A note on the surnames you'll see across these pages: six of the seven baptisms used a different surname spelling for the family — only the 1875 baptism of Erwin Carl Konsen preserved the Hessian K-form (the spelling closest to the original German). The variety is itself the evidence: it tells us the spelling came from Charles's spoken voice rather than from any one pastor's pen. See Key Evidence, Card 1 for the full surname-trail argument.

★ The surname-preserving baptism

1875 · Erwin Carl Konsen

Of the seven children, only one was baptized under the Hessian spelling. On 27 August 1875, the St. Marcus parish clerk recorded Charles and Auguste's sixth child as Erwin Carl Konsen — the "K" + "-en" form that matches the Konze / Konsen / Konzen surname pool of the Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis parish registers (1773 onward). Across all seven baptisms and the 1896 funeral entry, this is the single spelling that points home.

Erwin lived to 74 (he would later Anglicise to Charles Johnson Jr.) and married Katherine Misch. → Open his profile for the full baptism record, sponsors, and Generation 3 line.

A 15-year arc

The seven children were born between June 1866 and 1880 — a fifteen-year span anchored by Charles's 1865 discharge from Battery B and Auguste's 1880 forty-year-old final pregnancy. Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson, the firstborn, arrived on 20 June 1866 — three months after the Austro-Prussian War broke out, three months before Charles's homeland Elector lost his throne to Prussia. His name — Friedrich Wilhelm I — was an act of memorial naming. He died at sixteen months.

Every one of the seven was baptized at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church on St. Louis's south side. The same parish that married Charles and Auguste on 7 January 1866 baptized their first child five months later and their last fourteen years after that — one congregation, one font, one cluster of sponsors and witnesses pulled from Charles's Battery B comrades and the Kreichelt in-law network.

The St. Marcus parish clerk wrote the family's German surname five different ways across the seven baptisms — Gonnson in 1866 and 1868, Gansen in 1870, Gonsen in 1872, Jansen at the 1874 and 1881 baptisms (Louise and Bertha, born 1873 and 1880), and only once — the sixth baptism, on 27 August 1875 — the Hessian-style Konsen. That single 1875 spelling is the strongest piece of homeland-pointing evidence we have from the American side. Twenty-one years later, when Auguste was buried by the same parish in 1896, the surname was once more written as Konzen — the two self-attested forms bracket the question.

Of the seven, three died young: Friedrich Wilhelm as an infant, then Maria Josephine and Auguste both in 1890 at twenty-two and eighteen, dying within months of each other. The four who survived — Friedrike Catharina (Kate Ruddy), Louise (Foster), Erwin Carl Konsen (Charles Johnson Jr.), and Bertha — all lived into the 20th century. Bertha, the youngest, lived until 1959, ninety-three years after her firstborn brother died as a baby.

The surname trail across the family records · 1866 → 1896

One family, five distinct baptism-clerk spellings (plus the self-attested marriage and funeral renderings)

A different surname on almost every visit to St. Marcus. The 1875 Konsen rendering preserves the homeland-style "K" + "-en" suffix and is the strongest single piece of surname-preservation evidence. The 1896 Conson at Auguste's funeral closes the bookend "Charles-as-informant" span: in both 1875 and 1896, Charles himself supplied the surname.
