1860 U.S. Federal Census — Research Log

Charles Gannsen's first U.S. enumeration · expected but never located · all searches negative
EVIDENCE NOT FOUND Charles arrived 1859 — should appear in this census

★ What this record would tell us if found

Charles's first U.S. residence and household composition within months of his 1859 arrival. Specifically:

  • Where he was living — boarding alone, lodging with a Hessian family, or already settled with people he later named as wedding witnesses (Adam Herweck, Philipp Weber).
  • Who his neighbours were — neighbouring households on the same census page would identify his chain-migration cohort and reveal whether he travelled with anyone he later co-existed with in St. Louis.
  • His occupation in 1860 — still farmer (per the 1862 enlistment)? laboring? already blacksmithing? — establishing the trade he brought from Germany.
  • His surname spelling at arrival — the indexed name in 1860 would be the closest U.S. record to his German-side surname before American clerks introduced their own variants.
  • Whether he was already naturalised or alien — the 1860 census recorded foreign-born status but not naturalisation timing; its absence vs. presence would frame the 1865+ naturalisation question.

What level of detail to expect on origin

The 1860 schedule's "Place of birth" column was free-text and the granularity depended entirely on the enumerator. Three plausible outcomes:

  • Just "Germany" — most common outcome for German-born adults in 1860 St. Louis. Adds nothing the project doesn't already know.
  • State-level — "Prussia," "Bavaria," "Hannover," "Hesse," "Hesse-Cassel," "Hesse-Darmstadt," "Württemberg," "Baden." For Charles, "Hesse" or "Hesse-Cassel" / "Kurhessen" would corroborate the 1862 enlistment-record's "Freundburg Kurhessen" reading.
  • Region or city level — usually only when the household head spoke clearly with the enumerator. Could include "Cassel" (the Kurhessen capital) or a specific village. For Charles in 1860, just one year after arrival and presumably still answering in German, this is the least likely but most transformative outcome.

Why this record should exist

Per the 1900 U.S. census, Charles answered "1859" as his year of immigration. The 1860 federal census was enumerated 1 June – mid-October 1860, so anyone in residence in the United States as of June 1860 should be recorded. Charles enlisted in the Union Army at St. Louis on 2 December 1862 (per the Civil War enlistment record), giving "St. Louis" as his location and "Farmer" as his occupation, which means he had already established residence in Missouri by mid-1862. He was almost certainly somewhere in the U.S. on 1 June 1860 — but no enumeration entry has been positively identified.

Search log — passes attempted

negative Surname-variant exact-match passes

FamilySearch and Ancestry 1860 census collections searched with each of the following surname variants combined with birthplace = Germany / Hesse / Hannover / Kurhessen / Hesse-Cassel and birth year ~1835 ± 5:

  • Gannsen, Gansen, Gansin, Gansing — zero matches with Hesse-born + St. Louis residence
  • Gonson, Gonnson, Gonsson, Gonsen — zero matches with Hesse-born + St. Louis residence
  • Konsen, Konze, Kontze, Contze — zero matches with Hesse-born + St. Louis residence
  • Jansen, Janson, Janssen, Jensen — multiple results, none with Charles + St. Louis + Hesse profile
  • Hansen, Hanson — multiple results, none matching profile
  • Johnson with Germany birthplace — too broad to reliably surface a match; no St. Louis-area Charles Johnson b. ~1835 Hesse identified

negative First-name-only sweep

Searches restricted to "Charles" or "Carl" or "Karl" with birthplace Hesse / Hesse-Cassel / Kurhessen and lived-in St. Louis Missouri 1860, surname blank — produced no candidate matching age 25 ± 3 with no wife and no children (Charles was unmarried in 1860, married Auguste Kreichelt in Jan 1866).

negative Address-anchor approach

Charles's earliest documented St. Louis address (1518 South 7th Street, 1865-1869 per pension address history) was a 1860s residence — by 1860 he was likely at a different address. No 1860 St. Louis directory entry under any surname variant has been located. The 1860 census images for St. Louis Wards 1-12 have not been walked page-by-page for this record (the 1870 walk around the Kreichelts established the methodology but was not extended back to 1860).

collateral Outside St. Louis

Possibility considered: Charles may not have been in St. Louis on 1 June 1860. He could have been in transit (rural Missouri, Illinois, etc.) or working seasonally. A geographic-broad first-name-only sweep across all of Missouri / Illinois has not been completed. Given the Battery B unit-mate Adam Herweck's 1860 location is unclear, the possibility that Charles was elsewhere in the same year is open.

Next paths to chase

  • Page-by-page walk of 1860 St. Louis Wards 4 and 5 — the wards most likely to have covered Soulard / South Broadway / South 7th Street area. Mirroring the 1870 Kreichelt-side walk methodology.
  • Cross-state sweep — test the hypothesis that Charles was outside St. Louis on 1 June 1860. Search Illinois, Indiana, Ohio for Hesse-born unmarried male age 25 ± 3.
  • 1860 St. Louis city directory — physical / paper directory hold by Missouri Historical Society and St. Louis Public Library. Not yet ordered.
