"Freundburg, Kurhessen" — Research Log

The Civil War enlistment record's place-of-birth · 2020 Freudenberg-on-Rhine eliminated · 2026 Trendelburg phonetic reading is the project's working interpretation
2020 → 2026 search arc Source: 1862 Compiled Service Record (Doc O)

★ What this tells us — why Freundburg → most likely Trendelburg

"Freundburg, Kurhessen" is the project's foundational geographic anchor — and it most likely points to Trendelburg, Kreis Hofgeismar. This is a phonetic inference — Peter Schräder's proposed re-reading of a German place-name misheard by an English-speaking Civil War clerk in 1862 — not a documentary fact. It is a strong inference because a second, independent record (the 1866 marriage register's "Deisel") corroborates it from a different direction, but it remains an inference until a parish record positively identifies Charles's home village.

Step 1 — the phonetic substitution (Peter Schräder, 26 Feb & 6 May 2026):

Charles, a native German speaker one year out of basic English, gives his birthplace orally to a recruiting clerk on 2 December 1862. The clerk hears the spoken German place-name Trendelburg and writes the English-shaped Freundburg. The mishearing is regular, not random:

Tr-endel-burg→Fr-eund-burg"Tr-" cluster flattens to "Fr-" in an English ear
Tr-endel-burg→Fr-eund-burgmeaningless "endel" → familiar German Freund ("friend")
Trendel-burg→Freund-burgGerman place-suffix -burg survives intact (clerk recognises it)

The clerk substitutes a meaningful German word he knows (Freund) for a meaningless place-name fragment (Trendel) — exactly the substitution mechanism documented in the Toeppe → Deppe naming-variant methodology. "Freundburg" is therefore not a real place; it's a phonetic fingerprint of "Trendelburg" passed through one Civil-War-clerk's ear.

Step 2 — the triangulation that makes it stick:

Phonetic decoding alone would be speculative. What turns the decoding from a guess into a working identification is that a second, independent record names the village right next door:

YearSourceRecorded asDecodes to
1862Civil War enlistment (English clerk)"Freundburg, Kurhessen"Trendelburg
1866St. Marcus marriage register (German Lutheran pastor)"aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen"Deisel

Trendelburg and Deisel are ~2 km apart in the same Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis parish complex. Two scribes, two languages, four years apart, no contact between them — and they each capture a place-name from the same answer Charles gave, both pointing at the same small stretch of the Diemel valley. Either record on its own is ambiguous; together they bound the answer.

"Kurhessen" (1862) and "Churhessen" (1866) are spelling variants of the same political unit — the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel — which Prussia would dissolve in summer 1866.

Why this matters for the search:

Once "Freundburg" → Trendelburg and "Deisse" → Deisel, the German-side search radius collapses from all of Hesse to a specific six-parish complex inside the Kreis Hofgeismar. Every candidate-family search the project has run since February 2026 — Deisel, Trendelburg, Hümme, Hofgeismar Altstadt & Neustadt, Helmarshausen, Schöneberg, Heisebeck — operates inside that radius. The 2020 alternative reading (Freudenberg-on-the-Rhine, 160 miles south) was eliminated by negative Schierstein-parish results — see below.

The original record

From the 1862 Compiled Service Record (Doc O), NARA M405 — the Regimental Descriptive Book entry recorded at Charles's enlistment on 2 December 1862, Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery, St. Louis. The "Where born" field reads:

"Freundburg Kurhessen"

Other fields recorded at the same enlistment: name as "L. Charles Gannsen," age 23, height 5'5", fair complexion, blue eyes, blond hair, occupation Farmer, enlisted by Lt. Hinrich at St. Louis. The enlistment was four days after Charles's 27th birthday.

"Freundburg" appears only once in Charles's entire documentary record. Every other reference to his origin uses a different spelling — "Deisse" (1866 marriage), "Germany" (later censuses), or no specific place at all. The Civil War clerk was the first person to write down a real Hessian place-name and a real Hessian state-name for Charles, and he got the place-name wrong.

2020 foundation research — AncestryProGenealogists

The 2020 engagement with AncestryProGenealogists (Paul A. Schmidt & Brian A. Podoll, Feb-Jun 2020) listed "Identify Freundburg, and look for alternative places in Hessen-Nassau" as one of the seven research strategies in their initial plan. Their search returned:

eliminated Hansen, Map Guide to German Parish Registers, Hessen-Nassau II

Searched for any place name resembling "Freundburg." The closest plausible match the gazetteer surfaced was Freudenberg, a small place near Schierstein on the Rhine — about 160 miles south of the Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis. The 2020 working hypothesis read "Freundburg" as a clerk's misreading of Freudenberg.

eliminated Holzappel · Freiendiez · Schierstein parish baptisms

Three Hessen-Nassau Lutheran parishes searched for any 1834–1836 baptism matching Charles's profile (male, born Jan 1835, German Lutheran). The Schierstein parish was the primary target since it was geographically closest to the proposed Freudenberg-on-Rhine reading. Holzappel and Freiendiez were checked as alternative readings of "Deisse" (Dies / Diez) and "Freundburg" (Freudenberg). Result: no candidate matching Charles in any of the three parishes. The 2020 Freudenberg-on-Rhine reading was effectively closed off here.

found Deisel anchor (independently)

While "Freundburg" returned negatives, the parallel search for "Deisse" from the 1866 St. Marcus marriage register did surface a real place: Deisel, Kreis Hofgeismar, Hessen-Nassau, Lutheran parish. Two Konze candidates surfaced in Deisel's 1830s baptism register — Ludwig Konze (b. 25 Oct 1836) and Karl Konze (b. 7 Jun 1839). Ludwig was eliminated by his 1864 Frankfurt marriage; Karl was structurally eliminated in 2026. The Deisel anchor held even though the Freundburg anchor did not.

2026 Peter Schräder breakthrough — the Trendelburg phonetic decoding

The Peter Schräder correspondence reopened the Freundburg question by approaching it phonetically rather than orthographically. The decoding emerged across multiple emails:

26 Feb 2026
Peter's first phonetic-constraint email. Peter — a native German speaker working from Hessen — proposed that the spoken-German fragment Charles gave the recruiting clerk in 1862 was misheard at the level of the first syllable. The German "Tr-" cluster, said quickly, can flatten toward "Fr-" in the ear of an English-speaking clerk. The middle compresses: "-endel" → "-eund." The final "-burg" suffix survives intact (a German place-name suffix the clerk would recognise). Result: the clerk hears "Trendelburg" and writes "Freundburg" — substituting the familiar German word Freund (friend) for the meaningless-to-him fragment "Trendel."
27 Feb 2026
Pivot to Trendelburg. Peter proposed refocusing the German-side search from Deisel onto Trendelburg proper. Jed agreed; the search center-of-gravity moved up the Diemel valley to Trendelburg.
6 Dec 2025
"Trendelburg / Freudenberg" formal email — the Freundburg breakthrough. Peter's framing email that becomes the project's standing methodology paper. He explicitly walked through the substitution: when Charles said Trendel-burg in spoken German, the English-speaking clerk heard "Trendel" as "Friend" (because the first syllable sounds like that), translated mentally to the German Freund, and wrote down "Freundburg." The mishearing is a substitution of a meaningful German word for a meaningless-to-the-clerk place-name fragment. This is one of Peter's most important contributions to the project — it converted a long-puzzling document into a positive identifier.

Triangulation — two independent records, same place

The Peter Schräder reading is supported by independent corroboration from a second source written four years after the enlistment:

  • 1862 Civil War enlistment (English-speaking clerk): "Freundburg, Kurhessen" → decoded as Trendelburg (clerk substituted Freund for Trendel).
  • 1866 St. Marcus marriage register (German-speaking Lutheran pastor, G.W. Wall): "aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" → decoded as Deisel (Lutheran-pastor's fricative-sigma rendering of "Deisel" → "Deisse").

Trendelburg and Deisel are ~2 km apart in the same Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis parish complex. The two clerks differed in which adjacent place-name they captured and in the spelling-fidelity they brought to the task — but the underlying answer Charles gave was internally consistent. "Kurhessen" (1862) and "Churhessen" (1866) are spelling variants of the same political unit: the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel, which Prussia would dissolve in summer 1866. (See the "What this tells us" callout at the top of this page for the full logic.)

Subsequent search radius — what the decoding unlocked

Once "Freundburg" was read as Trendelburg and "Deisse" was read as Deisel, the German-side search radius collapsed from "all of Hesse" to a specific six-parish complex inside the Kreis Hofgeismar. Every candidate-family search in the project since February 2026 has operated within this radius:

  • Deisel (Archion KB 218842 / 218824) — exhaustively re-walked across ~30 sessions.
  • Trendelburg (Archion KB 220108 / 220099 / 220096) — including the 11 April 2026 K-section index breakthrough confirming three Kontze burials.
  • Hümme (Archion · 1815-1840) — searched.
  • Hofgeismar Altstadt · Hofgeismar Neustadt · Helmarshausen · Schöneberg (post-1822) · Friedrichsfeld — all searched.
  • Heisebeck — not on Archion; on-site research at Landeskirchliches Archiv Kassel pending.

The "fourteen negative marriage searches" Key Evidence card documents this radius collapse from the marriage-register angle.

What the reading is not

The Trendelburg reading is the working interpretation, not a proven identification. Three things would still falsify it:

  • A 1834-1836 Trendelburg or Deisel baptism positively identifying Charles's home parish would confirm the reading.
  • A surviving 1859 passenger manifest naming a different home village would refine the reading (e.g. Charles emigrated from a Trendelburg-adjacent village rather than Trendelburg itself).
  • A Hessian Stammrolle entry naming Charles's parish-of-registration would localise the reading definitively.

None of those three records has yet been found — see the Evidence not found subsection for each.
