1859 Passenger Manifest — Research Log

Charles's Atlantic crossing · the largest single documentary gap · all searches negative
EVIDENCE NOT FOUND Stated immigration year: 1859 (per 1900 census)

⚠ Correction — May 2026 FamilySearch sweep rediscovered candidates the project already eliminated

A May 2026 session ran a FamilySearch Family Tree sweep on Konze + Deisel and surfaced the family of Johann Christoph Konze (1802–1845, Ackermann, Deisel) × Maria Drönner (1816–1840) with documented son Ludwig Konze (b. 25 October 1836, Deisel). The session initially framed this as a major breakthrough identifying Charles's parents. This was an error. Both the Konze-family hypothesis and the Justus-Köster-Entry-195 hypothesis were already definitively eliminated by the project — the most recent elimination as recently as 4 May 2026.

What the project has actually established (existing record):

  • Ludwig Konze (b. 25 Oct 1836) — son of Joh. Christoph Konze (Bürgermeister of Deisel) × Maria Drönner — was eliminated in 2020 itself by the AncestryProGenealogists journal: his Frankfurt civil-marriage record (11 November 1864, to Wilhelmine Rufine Eva Schleifer) is incompatible with Charles's documented St. Louis marriage to Auguste Kreichelt on 7 January 1866. See AncestryProGenealogists Journal — June 2020.
  • Karl Konze (b. 7 Jun 1839) — son of the same Joh. Christoph Konze line — was eliminated by wrong birth year.
  • Ludwig Friedrich Konze (b. early Jan 1835, Andreas-Konze-Ackermann line, Entry 194) — also worked through the broader Konze disentanglement and not Charles. See Andreas Konze Identity Disentanglement.
  • Entry 195, KB 218842, baptism dated 23 Jan 1835 (the Justus-Köster hypothesis) was eliminated 4 May 2026 by Peter Schräder's Kurrent re-reading of the original parish-register image. Peter's email opens "No Jed, you're completely wrong" and corrects three points:
    • The child received no first name — the field previously transcribed as "Justus" actually reads "männlich" (male), the Lutheran-register convention when an infant dies without baptism;
    • The child was born and died three days later, without baptism — cannot be Charles, who lived to ~age 60 in St. Louis;
    • The father's family-name was Küster / Köster, profession Tagelöhner (day-labourer), not Konze and not Kaufmann. The Entry 195 mother is therefore not the same Wilhelmine geb. Hildebrand who appears in 1849 Confirmation Entry 261 — those two entries belong to different Deisel Köster households.
    See the canonical Entry 195 source page (with eliminated banner) and the Peter Schräder correspondence timeline.
  • There is no remaining Konze-surname descendant candidate. The 2020 AncestryPro investigation, the Stammtafel-disentanglement work (Sessions 40–47), the 2025 Peter Schräder Stammtafel correspondence, the LAGIS/St. Marcus pass, and the 4 May 2026 Entry 195 elimination together close every Konze candidate the project has identified. There is also no Archion baptism record placing Charles in any Konze family.
  • The "Konze / Konzen / Kontze / Gannsen" surname-cluster question is itself unresolved at the church-register level — the surname-as-Hausname interpretation (GANS-house at Gänsewinkel pointing toward the official surname Köster) was the working hypothesis behind the Entry 195 reading; with Entry 195 eliminated, that mapping is no longer load-bearing for any specific candidate either.

What was actually new from the May 2026 sweep (kept as collateral, not breakthrough — does not connect to Charles by any traced lineage):

  • Willhel(m) Konze, age 38, b. 1815 in KURHESSEN, immigrated 1853 to New Orleans on the ship Medora. NARA M259 Film 038, FamilySearch ark:/61903/1:1:QKNP-DX84. Useful only as collateral context for the Kurhessen → Mississippi route's plausibility.
  • Frederick Konze (b. 1887, Deisel) + Heinrich Philipp Konze (b. 1876, Deisel) — naturalised NY 1925/1906. Late-19th-century Deisel Konze descendants, consistent with the project's existing record of "at least 7 distinct Konze patriarchs documented in Deisel by ~1880."

The remainder of this research log — the manifest sweep findings, the 1860 census negative, the narrowed 1860–1862 arrival window — is unaffected by this correction. The German-side identity question is back to fully open with no current leading candidate. The Hausname → official-surname disentanglement and the documented Open Questions are captured in Kontze Family Tree & Research Paths and the Peter Schräder correspondence timeline.

★ What this record would tell us if found

The single most diagnostic document the project does not have. A 1859 passenger manifest entry for Charles would resolve more open questions than any other potential discovery in the project. Specifically:

  • His actual surname spelling at emigration — resolving the Konsen / Konze / Gannsen / Gonnson / Gansen disambiguation. The German-side or port-of-departure spelling is what should appear on the manifest.
  • His exact age and stated birthplace at the moment of departure, recorded by the shipping line or port authority — fresh data not yet filtered through American clerks' approximations.
  • His occupation — Farmer (per 1862 enlistment) vs. Blacksmith vs. Laborer vs. unspecified — establishing his economic self-description in 1859.
  • Any fellow travellers under the same surname — siblings, parents, cousins, or aunts/uncles who travelled with him would unlock his entire German-side nuclear family in one stroke. This is the single highest-impact field.
  • Port of departure — Bremen / Bremerhaven (most likely for a Hesse-Kassel emigrant via the Weser route through Karlshafen), Hamburg (less likely, eastern route), or Le Havre (very unlikely). Pinning the departure port narrows the German pre-emigration archives to search.
  • Port of arrival — New York / Castle Garden, New Orleans, Baltimore, or Boston — narrowing the U.S.-side trail between arrival and the 1862 St. Louis enlistment.
  • Ship name and date — anchoring his ~5-week Atlantic transit in a single line of the historical record.

What level of detail to expect on origin

Granularity varies dramatically by manifest type — the same crossing typically generates two or three records, each with different fields:

  • U.S. arrival manifest (NY / New Orleans / Baltimore / Boston): typically captured only country-level data — usually just "Germany" or sometimes a state ("Prussia" / "Hessen" / "Hanover"). Useful but not narrowing.
  • Bremen departure manifest: recorded the emigrant's last residence — typically a specific village or city. A Bremen entry could yield "Trendelburg" or "Deisel" by name. Most pre-1907 Bremen lists are destroyed (WWII), but surviving fragments published by Die Maus carry this granularity.
  • Hamburg departure manifest (Auswanderungslisten): the most field-rich German source. Records last residence (village-level), plus a separate province / state of birth column. Fully digitised 1850-1934.
  • Liverpool transit listing: common for German emigrants taking cheaper trans-Atlantic packets. Captures last residence at the German level + the U.K. embarkation date. Granularity matches Bremen.

Best case: A Bremen or Hamburg departure entry naming Charles's home village explicitly — would close the entire German-side question in a single line. Worst case: A U.S. arrival entry saying just "Germany" — still narrower than nothing, and would at least confirm the surname spelling at emigration. Either outcome would carry the field that the U.S.-side records do not capture: fellow travellers under the same surname.

Why this record should exist

Per the 1900 U.S. census, Charles answered "1859" as his year of immigration. By 1859 transatlantic passenger lists were standard practice: U.S. ports required incoming-passenger lists under federal law (since 1820), and German shipping lines kept departure manifests. The Bremen state archive (Die Maus / Staatsarchiv Bremen) also kept independent emigration registers. Charles's manifest entry should exist in at least one of these collections — the question is which surname spelling and which port.

negative ★★ Zenodo CHUP Bremen dataset (735,545 records, 1830–1939) — May 2026

The most comprehensive single corpus of surviving Bremen-port records ever published: the December 2025 Zenodo release "A Dataset of 735,000 Migration Records (Bremen, 1830-1939)" (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.17977460). Compiled by the CHUP project from the Bremen Society for Family Research (Die Maus) and the Bremen Chamber of Commerce, with normalization, occupation-to-HISCO coding, port-of-arrival georeferencing, and structural cleaning. The full 260 MB CSV (735,545 rows × 62 columns) was downloaded and queried directly.

Year-by-year coverage in the 1855–1862 window (the entire window of Charles's potential sailing):

  • 1855: 1 record — the Brig Julius to Baltimore, single passenger Joseph Lurz (born Merkershausen, Bayern). Wrong destination, wrong origin.
  • 1856: 0 records.
  • 1857: 61 records — entirely the Fanny Kirchner sailing from Bremerhaven to Sydney, Australia (Oct 25, 1857). Wrong destination.
  • 1858: 0 records.
  • 1859: 0 records.
  • 1860: 9 records — entirely the crew manifest of the Ship Globus's 23 Nov 1860 Bremen → New York sailing (officers, sailors, ship's carpenter), not passengers.
  • 1861: 0 records.
  • 1862: 0 records.

Surname-cluster sweep against all 735,545 records (Konze · Konzen · Konsen · Kontze · Gannsen · Gonnson · Gansen · Gonsen · Gunnsen · Hansen): zero direct hits at the relevant date. One unrelated 1923 hit on "Konzer" (Katharina, age 24, born ~1899). One 1860 "Kunze" hit (Fritz, age 24, sailor/Matrose on the Globus crew manifest — not a passenger, not Charles). Multiple 1920s "Hansen" hits, all unrelated by date.

Bottom line: the Zenodo dataset is the largest single body of post-WWII-recovered Bremen records that has been published, and it independently confirms what the ISTG Vol. 16 sweep showed earlier in May 2026: the corpus that would have documented Charles's 1859 sailing simply does not survive. 1858, 1859, 1861, and 1862 are completely empty in the dataset. Bremen-side reconstruction has reached its evidentiary floor for this question.

What this leaves as next-step paths: (1) U.S.-side NARA M237 microfilm reels for the eight ISTG candidate ships, line-by-line under every surname variant (the U.S. arrival manifests survive even where the German departure manifests don't); (2) the Hamburg Auswanderungslisten under further surname variants and a wider age window; (3) the Baltimore / New Orleans passenger-list collections; (4) the Antwerp and Le Havre records (much less complete survival).

Citation: CHUP project, "A Dataset of 735,000 Migration Records (Bremen, 1830-1939)," Zenodo (Dec 2025), DOI 10.5281/zenodo.17977460. CC BY 4.0. See also the related preprint: "A Century of Migration (1830–1939): 735,000 Enriched Records from Bremen's Ship Passenger Lists".

collateral ★★★★ Carl-variant sweep + closest near-namesake found (May 2026)

A targeted follow-up sweep recognised that German-language departure manifests (Hamburg, Bremen) would have recorded Charles as "Carl" rather than the anglicised "Charles." Each surname variant was re-queried with first-name = Carl across Hamburg 1068, NY 7488, and NOLA 7484 — and additionally with K-cluster wildcard K*nz* and G-cluster wildcard G*ns* to catch any clerk distortion of the medial consonants. This is the most significant single finding of the entire May 2026 sweep: the 1860 U.S. Federal Census, queried under every viable name spelling and birth year, does not contain a German-born Charles / Carl Konze / Konzen / Gansen / Gannsen of birth year 1835. If Charles had arrived in 1859 as he later told the 1900 census enumerator, he should appear here. He does not.

Implication for the search window: Charles likely arrived in the U.S. after Census Day (1 June 1860) and before his Aug 1862 St. Louis enlistment. The 1900-census "1859" answer is approximately 1–2 years too early, consistent with the well-documented tendency of late-life census respondents to round arrival dates. This narrows the search window to summer 1860 – summer 1862, eliminating the seven candidate ships before May 1860 and shifting attention to:

  • Bark Fella, 14 Apr 1860 — borderline; Charles would have been on the 1860 census if so. Less likely.
  • Bremen-to-NY sailings summer 1860 – summer 1862 not in the original ISTG Vol. 16 transcription (the volume covers 1858–1868 but the project's earlier focus was on 1858–1860). The 1860–1862 sailings now become first-priority candidates.
  • Christoph Konze's ship — Elise & Mathilde, 9 June 1862 — the same ship that brought the well-documented "wrong Konze" candidate. Worth re-examining whether Charles was on a sister sailing or earlier voyage of the same vessel.
  • Hamburg or other-port departures 1860–1862 — the same surname-variant + Carl-variant sweeps should be re-run with the new arrival window.

  • Carl Konze + departure 1860–1862: 3 results — Carl Kunz (1857) to Brazil; C O Kunz (b. 1845, age 17, too young); C L Kunz (already eliminated).
  • Carl K*nz* + b.1835 + departure 1860–1862: 1 result, C L Kunz (already eliminated).
  • Carl G*ns* + b.1835 + departure 1860–1862: 1 result — Carl Gensch, age 27, residence Gartz an der Oder, Preußen (Pomerania, far northeastern Prussia near today's Polish border), occupation Seiler (rope-maker), departure 1 Jul 1862 Hamburg → Quebec on the ship Washington. Eliminated by region (Pomerania ≠ Hessen-Kassel), destination (Quebec; Quebec→St. Louis transit by 5 Aug enlistment is implausible for a sailing ship), and occupation (rope-maker ≠ Farmer).

NY 7488 — narrowed window

  • Carl K* + b.1835 + arrival 1860–1862: 58 results — too many to fully transcribe but the top 30 were reviewed for K-N-Z soundex matches. Closest by ship-and-date timing: Carl Koch, age 28 (b. 1834), departure Bremen and Bremerhaven, arrival NY 25 Jun 1862 on the ship E F Gabain. Bremerhaven is the correct port for Hessen emigrants and 25 Jun 1862 → 5 Aug 1862 enlistment timeline is workable. Opened individually: Place of Origin "Wishben, Prussia" — Wischbeń is in West Prussia / today's Poland, far from Hessen-Kassel. Eliminated. Other K* arrivals in window (Klug, Klebsattel, Karig, König, Pretzer, Schade, Hiller, Heim) are post-census but none have K-N-Z soundex matching Konze, and Le Havre and Liverpool ones are filtered out as wrong departure ports.
  • Christoph Konze's ship — Elise & Mathilde 9 Jun 1862, Bremen → NY: 6-page manifest image inspected directly. Christoph Konze appears on page 2. Page 2 also shows "Hermann Riemer, age 76, male, Farmer, Prussia → St. Charles" — St. Charles MO is on the Mississippi just north of St. Louis. Other K-cluster surnames not visible in the inspected pages, but full image-by-image transcription would require manual OCR re-read of all six pages.
  • Christine Kunze (NY 30 Aug 1862, Bremen, ship Herzogin Von Brabant): arrives after Charles's 5 Aug 1862 enlistment date — cannot be his ship.

What this leaves

The narrowed-window sweep produced no new strong candidate. Charles is not findable in the surviving Hamburg departure index, the NY arrival index, or the NOLA arrival index under any tested name × birth year × window combination. The candidate-pool of potential ships in the narrowed Jun 1860 – Aug 1862 window has been systematically swept — every surfaced individual has been ruled out by region, destination, occupation, household structure, or timeline.

The remaining live hypotheses are now:

  • Charles arrived as a passenger whose place-of-origin field was left blank on a Bremen-departed ship 1860–1862 (and is therefore not surfaceable by any place-of-origin filter).
  • Charles's surname was distorted on the manifest beyond any tested wildcard or soundex bridge (e.g. "Konze" → "Hansen" → ?).
  • Charles arrived under a completely different given name on the manifest (e.g. his actual baptismal name, then changed in the U.S.).
  • Charles arrived via Antwerp, Le Havre, or another non-Hamburg/Bremen port whose 1860–1862 records are partially indexed.
  • Charles's actual arrival was earlier than 1859, before our previously-tested window — possible but unlikely given the 1860 census negative.

The next-step paths for breaking the impasse remain: (1) FHL Salt Lake City alphabetical 15-year Hamburg index (definitive), (2) Marburg HStAM Hessian-side emigration registers under any village-level pre-emigration paper trail, (3) post-1862 records that might reference Charles's actual arrival ship (e.g. naturalisation papers, witness statements, pension records).

negative Hamburg departure manifests

Hamburg State Archive's Auswanderungslisten — the most complete surviving German departure dataset, fully digitized 1850-1934 (the 1859 records sit in the third volume, covering February 1859 through November 1861). Unlike Bremen, the Hamburg lists were not destroyed — they survive in full at the Staatsarchiv Hamburg (Bestand 373-7 I, VIII Auswanderungsamt I, microfilm rolls K 1701 – K 2008). Searches conducted via:

  • Ancestry · Hamburg Passenger Lists 1850-1934 — surname variants tried under birth year 1835 ± 5; no Charles match
  • FamilySearch · Hamburg Passenger Lists 1850-1934 — same negative result
  • Deutsche Auswanderer-Datenbank (DAD) at the Historisches Museum Bremerhaven, which aggregates Hamburg AND Bremerhaven manifest data 1820-1897 (~5 million records, covering Charles's window) — November 2025 query for "GANSEN, CARL" returned a doctor (not Charles); subsequent multi-spelling sweep also negative.
  • Zenodo open-data check (May 2026): No Hamburg-equivalent of the Bremen CHUP dataset (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.17977460) has been released. The Hamburg Auswanderungslisten remain gated behind Ancestry, FamilySearch, the Hamburg State Archive's fee-paid search (ABC-Straße 19, Hamburg), and the FHL Salt Lake City microfilm collection — there is no openly-downloadable machine-readable Hamburg corpus for direct local sweep at this time.

Focused next-step path for Hamburg: the 15-year alphabetical index 1850–1871 exists on 46 microfilm reels at the Family History Library in Salt Lake City — sorted alphabetically by passenger surname rather than chronologically by departure date. A targeted FHL visit (or a Family History Center microfilm rental, where still available) under each of the ten surname variants would give a definitive yes/no across the entire 1850-1871 Hamburg corpus in a single sitting. The Hamburg State Archive's fee-paid surname search is a paid alternative (ABC-Straße 19, D-20354 Hamburg).

negative U.S. arrival manifests

Major U.S. ports of entry searched for 1859 arrivals matching profile (German-born male, ~24 years old, surname variant):

  • New York Castle Garden / NY Passenger Lists 1820-1957 — primary search target. Multiple surname variants. No match.
  • New Orleans Passenger Lists 1820-1945 — secondary candidate (lower-cost passage for German emigrants who could not afford NYC route). No match.
  • Baltimore Passenger Lists 1820-1872 — no match.
  • Boston / New England port lists — no match.

negative Liverpool transit listings (Session 78)

Many German emigrants in 1859 transited via Liverpool to take advantage of cheaper trans-Atlantic packets. Session 78 investigated a specific candidate — Carl Kinzer / Carl Krieger on a 1859 Liverpool ship — but ruled it out as not Charles. No other Liverpool listing has surfaced a candidate match.

collateral Adjacent emigrants found

The same searches that returned negatives for Charles surfaced documented emigrants in his geographic and surname space — useful context but not Charles himself:

  • Johannes Grandjot (Schöneberg → NYC, 25 Sep 1855) — Bremen → NYC arrival as "Joseph Grandjos" with co-traveler Lisette Grandjot. Demonstrates the Bremen-route record-survival pattern but is four years too early for Charles.
  • Meier Meierhof (Deisel → NYC, 1854) — surfaced in the Marburg state archive emigrant register; demonstrates the Hessian-side record-survival pattern.
  • 1853 Friedrichsfeld emigrant cohort (Quentin / Frickhöfer / Adolph Quentin) — surfaced in HStAM Best. 16 Nr. 3237 p. 110. Demonstrates that the Hessian emigration registers DO carry detailed records when an emigrant is registered.

The fact that contemporaneous Hessian emigrants surface cleanly while Charles does not suggests his emigration was either un-registered (illegal, see Stammrolle entry) or recorded under a surname so different from any tried variant that it remains unrecognised.

  • Direct paid lookup at Bremen state archive — Staatsarchiv Bremen accepts genealogical queries for a fee. The Bremerhaven 1859 emigrant lists, where they survive, have not been fully searched manually.
