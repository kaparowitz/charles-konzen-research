"Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" — Research Log

The 1866 St. Marcus marriage register's place-of-origin · 2020 toponym anchor · still the project's strongest village-level identifier
2020 → 2026 search arc Source: 1866 St. Marcus marriage register · entry 676

★ What this tells us — why Deisse = Deisel

"aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" is the project's most explicit village-level identifier — and it decodes to Deisel, Kreis Hofgeismar, Hessen-Nassau (Lutheran parish: Deisel). Unlike the Civil War clerk's "Freundburg," this reading is supported by the German-Lutheran pastor's own pen, a published gazetteer match, and direct geographic continuity with the Trendelburg anchor 5 km away.

Step 1 — the pastor's transcription (St. Marcus, 7 January 1866):

When Charles married Auguste Kreichelt at St. Marcus, the German-Lutheran pastor G.W. Wall took down Charles's home origin in his own German hand. Pastor Wall was a native German speaker writing in German for a German-speaking congregation — exactly the conditions under which a Hessian villager's place-name would be captured most faithfully. The entry reads:

"aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen"

The mishearing here is much smaller than the Civil War clerk's. Pastor Wall hears Deisel and writes Deisse — collapsing the final unstressed "-l" into a fricative "-sse" ending, which is a regular feature of German handwritten 19th-century parish registers when capturing rural place-names. Every other element of the place-string is correctly captured:

Deisel→Deissefinal "-el" → fricative "-sse" (regular pastor's transcription)
Kreis Hofgeismar→Kreis Casselsubstitution of capital-city locator for the precise Kreis-level subdivision (see explanation below)
Churhessen→Kurhessenarchaic "Chur-" spelling = "Kur-" (same political unit)
Why "Kreis Cassel" instead of "Kreis Hofgeismar": Deisel sat administratively inside Kreis Hofgeismar, not Kreis Cassel. Pastor Wall's substitution is explicable on three converging grounds, none of which undermines the Deisel reading:
  1. Charles likely said "Cassel," not "Hofgeismar." A peasant from a small Hessian village, asked where he came from, would naturally name the regional capital (Cassel) rather than the bureaucratic Kreis-level subdivision. Cassel was the seat of the Electorate, the major nearby city, and the natural orienting reference for a rural Hessian speaking to an outsider. "Hofgeismar" is administratively precise but vernacularly invisible — comparable to a Cotswolds villager telling an American clerk "I'm from near London" rather than "I'm from West Oxfordshire District."
  2. Pastor Wall was a Württemberger working from spoken German in St. Louis, with no atlas of Hesse. Georg Wendelin Wall was born in Owen near Kirchheim, Württemberg (south Germany — not Hesse), emigrated to St. Louis as a child with his parents, and had been an active St. Louis Evangelical pastor since at least 1836 (St. Marcus tenure July 1850 – November 1866). His pastoral training was via the Basel mission society. He had no Hessian-administrative background of his own, so he took down Charles's vernacular "Cassel" and formalised it as "Kreis Cassel" — meaning "the Cassel administrative orbit" — without distinguishing the small post-1821 Kreis Cassel proper from the broader regional sense. This rules out the alternative hypothesis that the Hessian content of the entry came from Wall's own emigration year/origin: he had no default Hesse-Kassel spelling reflex. The Kurhessen identification came from Charles, captured by a non-Hessian pastor.
  3. It is geographically still correct one level up. Deisel sat inside the Provinz Niederhessen of Kurhessen, whose seat was Cassel; after the August 1866 Prussian annexation, Kreis Hofgeismar fell formally within the Regierungsbezirk Cassel. So at the province-or-region level, "Kreis Cassel" was an honest paraphrase, not a contradictory misreading. The marriage occurred 7 January 1866 — seven months before the Prussian reorganisation — so the pastor was using the older Electoral-period vernacular framing where "Cassel" stood in for the whole Niederhessen administrative orbit.

The result is the same kind of regular, explicable transcription pattern as "Deisse" for "Deisel": Pastor Wall captured what he heard and formalised it into administrative-sounding German, substituting the orienting capital (Cassel) for the precise Kreis-level subdivision (Hofgeismar). Cross-checked against the gazetteer match, the Lutheran-parish denomination match, and the 1862 Trendelburg triangulation, "Kreis Cassel" is not a counter-argument to the Deisel reading — it is a vernacular paraphrase consistent with it.

Step 2 — why Deisel is the right answer:

Multiple independent constraints all converge on Deisel as the unique fit:

  • Gazetteer match — Hansen's Map Guide to German Parish Registers, Hessen-Nassau II (the standard reference) returned Deisel, Kreis Hofgeismar as the unique German place-name matching "Deisse" inside the Kreis Cassel administrative orbit. No other candidate cleared the same combined name + Kreis filter.
  • Lutheran parish, matching Charles's denomination — Deisel is a Lutheran (evangelical) parish, consistent with Charles's later church affiliation at St. Marcus (also evangelical-Lutheran).
  • Inside Kurhessen / Churhessen — Deisel sat within the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel until Prussia's 1866 annexation, exactly matching the political unit Pastor Wall captured.
  • Inside Kreis Cassel — Deisel was administered from Kassel as part of the Kassel district, exactly matching "Kreis Cassel" in the entry.
  • Surrounding evidence — Deisel's baptism register surfaced two contemporary Konze baptisms (Ludwig 1836, Karl 1839; both eliminated as Charles, but the surname presence in Deisel is itself a positive signal). Deisel's 1850 emigrant cohort included Hildebrands going to Rockford, Illinois — matching Charles's working maternal-line surname.
  • Negative evidence on alternatives — the 2020 engagement also tested Holzappel and Freiendiez (alternative readings of "Deisse" as "Dies / Diez") and Schierstein (alternative reading of Freundburg). All three returned no candidate matching Charles. Deisel was the only place-name that survived all gazetteer + parish-record passes.

Step 3 — the Trendelburg triangulation (mirror of the Freundburg page):

The reading sticks because a second independent record — the 1862 Civil War enlistment ("Freundburg, Kurhessen") — separately decodes to Trendelburg, the parish town immediately north of Deisel. Two scribes, two languages, four years apart, no contact between them — and they each capture a place-name from the same answer Charles gave, both pointing at the same small stretch of the Diemel valley inside the Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis:

YearSourceRecorded asDecodes to
1862Civil War enlistment (English clerk)"Freundburg, Kurhessen"Trendelburg
1866St. Marcus marriage register (German-Lutheran pastor)"aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen"Deisel

The two readings cross-validate: each constrains the other into the same six-parish complex (Trendelburg / Deisel / Hümme / Hofgeismar / Helmarshausen / Schöneberg / Heisebeck) inside the Kreis Hofgeismar.

Why this matters for the search:

"Deisse" is the project's one explicit village-level identifier in any Charles record. Every German-side search since 2020 has used Deisel as the parish anchor, and every neighbour-parish (Trendelburg, Hümme, Hofgeismar Altstadt & Neustadt, Helmarshausen, Schöneberg, Heisebeck) has been searched outward from Deisel. The two original Konze candidates from Deisel (Ludwig 1836, Karl 1839) have both been eliminated as Charles — but the parish anchor itself has held across the entire 2026 investigation.

The original record

From the St. Marcus Lutheran Church marriage register, St. Louis, Missouri — entry №676 (running №4 for 1866). Pastor: G.W. Wall.

Carl Gonnson und Auguste Kreichelt
Heimath: aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen / aus Clausthal, Königr. Hannover
Trautag: 7 Jan 1866
Zeugen: Adam Herweck, Philipp Weber, Anna Goetz, Sophie Lehm
Cop: G.W. Wall, Pfr.

"Deisse" appears only once in Charles's entire documentary record. The 1859 passenger manifest, 1860 census, and 1862 Civil War enlistment all use a different identifier ("Freundburg, Kurhessen" in 1862; just "Germany" in later censuses). The 1866 St. Marcus register is the single document where a German-speaking clerk took down Charles's home village in German script. That singularity is exactly what makes it a high-leverage record.

The companion entry — Auguste Kreichelt's home as "aus Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" — is corroborated independently (Auguste's pre-marriage records survive in Clausthal). It serves as a control: Pastor Wall correctly captured Auguste's Hessian-adjacent place-name in detail, so his rendering of Charles's "Deisse" should be treated with comparable seriousness.

2020 foundation research — AncestryProGenealogists

The 2020 engagement with AncestryProGenealogists (Paul A. Schmidt & Brian A. Podoll, February – June 2020) listed "Look for possible alternatives to Deisse in gazetteers" as the first of seven research strategies. Their search returned:

held Hansen, Map Guide to German Parish Registers, Hessen-Nassau II

The standard reference for German parish-register research. Searched for any place name resembling "Deisse" inside the Kreis Cassel orbit. Returned a single match: Deisel, Kreis Hofgeismar, Hessen-Nassau, Lutheran parish. The toponym anchor was established here. This match has held up across every subsequent search the project has run — six years and ~30 sessions later, no alternative reading of "Deisse" has displaced Deisel.

eliminated Holzappel · Freiendiez parish baptisms

Two Hessen-Nassau parishes searched as alternative readings of "Deisse" (treating it as a possible miscapture of "Dies" or "Diez"). Both returned no candidate matching Charles. The alternative readings were closed off here, and Deisel was confirmed as the only surviving interpretation.

eliminated Deisel baptism register, 1830-1861 — two Konze candidates surfaced & ruled out

The Deisel parish baptism register for 1830-1861 was searched for any plausible match to Charles. Two candidates surfaced:

  • Ludwig Konze (b. 25 Oct 1836, Deisel) — eliminated 2020. Ludwig's 11 Nov 1864 marriage to Wilhelmine Schleifer in Frankfurt civil records places him in Frankfurt 14 months before Charles's 7 Jan 1866 St. Louis marriage to Auguste Kreichelt. Ludwig is a different person.
  • Karl Konze (b. 7 Jun 1839, Deisel) — structurally eliminated 2026. The 2026 work in Deisel turned up no Karl Konze marriage or death record in Deisel after 1839, but also no positive evidence linking Karl to St. Louis. The age gap (Karl 1839 vs. Charles 1835) and absence of corroborating records have ruled Karl out as the working candidate.

Critically, no Gonnson / Johnson surname was present in the Deisel parish at all. Konze was the closest surname in the parish, and that closeness — combined with the 1875 St. Marcus baptism's KONSEN spelling — is what made the Konze line the project's leading hypothesis from 2020 to early 2026.

eliminated Deisel marriage register 1830-1901 · death register 1830-1956

Searched for any Karl Konze marriage or death entry that would resolve the second 2020 candidate. Negative — no Karl Konze marriage in Deisel, no Karl Konze death in Deisel through 1956. Karl either left the parish or was buried elsewhere; either way, his Deisel paper trail ends at his 1839 baptism.

framing Surname distribution across Germany

Konze concentrates in the NRW Rhine area (176 households; 18 in Hessen). Gonnsen is North Frisian (25 households). Gansen appears in NRW / Rhineland-Palatinate (266 households). The phonetic-drift framing — J / G / K interchangeable across regional dialects and between English-speaking American clerks and German-speaking parents — was set up here. This framing supports reading the various St. Marcus baptism spellings (Gonnson, Gannson, Konsen) as renderings of the same underlying surname.

2026 work — Deisel parish thoroughly re-walked

The 2026 investigation has re-walked every digitised Deisel-side Kirchenbuch (KB) on Archion. Each volume returned negative for a Charles match, but each also produced collateral information on the broader Konze and Hildebrand families:

KB 218821
Deisel marriage register, c. 1820-1850. Searched for Joh. Heinrich Konze × Wilhelmine Hildebrand marriage (the working hypothesis for Charles's parents). Negative. The parental-marriage absence is one of the project's "fourteen negative marriage searches" — collectively narrowing the marriage to either pre-1822 or to non-digitised Heisebeck records.
KB 218824
Deisel separate baptism volume. Re-walked entry by entry for the 1834-1836 window. No candidate matching Charles surfaced.
KB 218836
Deisel baptism volume, c. 1820-1840. Re-walked for any Konze, Konsen, or surname-variant candidate matching Charles's birth window. Negative.
KB 218842
The contested Entry 195. The April 2026 leading hypothesis: KB 218842 Entry 195, 23 Jan 1835, Deisel — read with HTR assistance as a baptism for "Justus Konze" with parents Joh. Heinrich Konze × Wilhelmine Hildebrand. Held as the project's leading candidate for ~3 weeks. Eliminated 4 May 2026 when Peter Schräder's careful re-reading showed Entry 195 was actually for a Köster Tagelöhner child (no first name; just "männlich"; died at 3 days). Not Charles.
KB 218851 / 218860
Later Deisel volumes, 1840s-1860s. Re-walked for any later Konze emigration annotation, marginal note, or mention of Charles's possible family. Collateral info found on other Deisel Konze branches; nothing tying back to Charles.
KB 220093 / 220096 / 220099 / 220108
Trendelburg parish complex covering Deisel. Walked for K-section entries. The 11 April 2026 K-section index breakthrough confirmed three Kontze burials (Arnold Philipp 1780, Andreas 1800, child Johannes 1808), establishing the Konze family's continuous multi-generational presence in the Trendelburg-Deisel parish complex from the mid-18th century. Geographic anchor confirmed; specific Charles match still pending.

The collective result of the 2026 Deisel work: the parish anchor has held with every additional pass, but no positive Charles baptism, marriage, or emigration entry has yet been recovered from Deisel itself. The investigation has therefore expanded outward to the surrounding Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis parishes — see the German baptism research log for the full radius search.

Triangulation — the same answer captured by two scribes

The Deisel reading is the partner of the Trendelburg reading from the Civil War enlistment. The pair operates as a triangulation:

  • 1862 Civil War enlistment (English-speaking clerk): "Freundburg, Kurhessen" → decoded as Trendelburg (clerk substituted German Freund for the meaningless-to-him fragment Trendel). See Freundburg research log.
  • 1866 St. Marcus marriage register (German-speaking Lutheran pastor): "aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" → decoded as Deisel (pastor's regular fricative-sigma rendering of village-final "-el" → "-sse").

Trendelburg and Deisel are ~2 km apart in the same Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis parish complex. Two scribes, two languages, four years apart, no contact between them — and they each capture a place-name from the same answer Charles gave, both pointing at the same small stretch of valley. The pair is what makes either reading defensible: each constrains the other into the same six-parish complex.

"Kurhessen" (1862) and "Churhessen" (1866) are spelling variants of the same political unit — the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel — which Prussia would dissolve in summer 1866.

What the Deisel anchor unlocked

Once "Deisse" was read as Deisel, the German-side search radius collapsed from "all of Hesse" to a specific six-parish complex inside the Kreis Hofgeismar. Every candidate-family search the project has run — both before and after Peter Schräder's Trendelburg phonetic decoding in 2026 — has operated within this radius:

  • Deisel (KB 218821 / 218824 / 218836 / 218842 / 218851 / 218860 / 220093 / 220096 / 220099 / 220108) — the original anchor; exhaustively re-walked.
  • Trendelburg (KB 220108 / 220099 / 220096) — adjacent parish town; including the 11 April 2026 K-section index breakthrough confirming three Kontze burials.
  • Hümme (Archion · 1815-1840) — searched.
  • Hofgeismar Altstadt · Hofgeismar Neustadt · Helmarshausen · Schöneberg (post-1822) · Friedrichsfeld — all searched.
  • Heisebeck — not on Archion; on-site research at Landeskirchliches Archiv Kassel pending. The single remaining digital gap inside the radius.

The Deisel anchor also drove the Hildebrand-line investigation: 1850 emigrants from Deisel to Rockford, Illinois (the Hildebrand cohort) became a candidate maternal-line connection for Charles, given Wilhelmine Hildebrand was the working hypothesis for his mother.

What the reading is not

The Deisel reading is a parish-level identification, not a confirmed birth record. Three things would still refine or falsify it:

  • A 1834-1836 Deisel or Trendelburg baptism positively identifying Charles's home parish would confirm the reading at the entry level.
  • A surviving 1859 passenger manifest naming a different home village inside the same Kreis Hofgeismar would refine the reading (e.g. Charles emigrated from a Deisel-adjacent village rather than Deisel itself — Trendelburg, Hümme, Heisebeck, Sielen).
  • A Hessian Stammrolle entry naming Charles's parish-of-registration would localise the reading definitively.

Crucially: elimination of the two Deisel Konze candidates does not eliminate Deisel itself. The toponym anchor is independent of any specific Konze candidate-family. Charles could still be from Deisel under a different surname (Gansen, Kontze, Konsen, or another spelling), or from a Deisel-adjacent village in the same six-parish complex. The K-section Trendelburg burials (Kontze 1780/1800/1808) keep that possibility open.

None of the three falsifying / refining records has yet been found — see the Evidence not found subsection for each.
