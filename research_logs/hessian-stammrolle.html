Hessian Military Draft / Stammrolle — Research Log

Kurhessen recruitment register · 1855-1859 conscription window (20th-birthday cohort + flight period) · all searches negative · likely most informative single record if recovered
EVIDENCE NOT FOUND Marburg HStAM Bestand 4h

★ What this record would tell us if found

The Stammrolle would carry, in a single state-recorded entry made when Charles turned 20 (~1855):

  • Full birth name — including any second / middle name dropped in U.S. records. Resolves the "L. Charles Gannsen" L-prefix question (Ludwig? Louis?).
  • Exact birth date — locks the 25 Jan / 26 Jan ambiguity.
  • Parish of registration — the same field every other German-side record needs but does not yet provide. Localises Charles's home village within the Kreis Hofgeismar.
  • Parents' names — usually the father's name and occupation, sometimes the mother. Equivalent to the baptism record's parental field but recorded by the state.
  • Height, eye colour, hair colour, distinguishing marks — usable for cross-reference with the 1862 U.S. enlistment record (which captured: 5'5", fair complexion, blue eyes, blond hair).
  • Trade / occupation at age 20 — would establish his pre-emigration profession in 1855, four years before he left Germany.
  • Conscription disposition — Drafted? Excused (medical / family)? Fled / desertiert?
Why the Stammrolle is potentially the highest-value record in the entire project: the conscription disposition field would directly explain why the project finds no Hessian Auswanderer-Beleg for Charles. A "desertiert" or "nicht erschienen" annotation would establish that Charles emigrated illegally to evade conscription — a documented 1850s pattern in Hesse-Kassel. That would close the question of why he is missing from every Hessian state-emigration record, and would also explain the surname-spelling drift after emigration: someone who left Germany to evade the draft had every reason to anglicise their name aggressively on arrival.

Why this record should exist

The Electorate of Hesse maintained Stammrollen (recruitment rolls / military registers) for every male citizen who reached age 20 (the conscription threshold). The system functioned without interruption from the late Napoleonic era through the 1866 Prussian annexation. Every village in the Kreis Hofgeismar reported its draft-age men annually to the regional military office. The records are archived at Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg, Bestand 4h.

Charles turned 20 in 1855 (born 26 Jan 1835 per pension affidavit). He should appear on the Stammrolle for whichever home village the Hessian state assigned him to — the same village that should appear on a German baptism record (which the project also has not yet located). The Stammrolle and the baptism record are mutually-reinforcing: locating either one would localise Charles's home parish, which would unlock the other.

Search log — passes attempted

negative HETRINA database (Universität Kassel)

HETRINA indexes Hessian troops in North America (1776-1783) and a parallel post-Napoleonic militia collection. Surname variants searched:

  • Konze, Konsen, Kontze, Contze — multiple unrelated 18th-century soldiers (Joh. Christoph Contze, etc.); zero 1855-1859 conscription-age matches
  • Gansen, Gannsen, Gansin — zero matches
  • Gonson, Gonnson — zero matches
  • Stern — multiple Stern entries, none linkable to an 1835-born Hofgeismar Stern

HETRINA is primarily an 18th-century database; its 1850s coverage is partial.

unsearched Marburg HStAM Bestand 4h directly

The full Stammrollen are held at the Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg under Bestand 4h. Most of this Bestand is partially indexed in Arcinsys-Hessen's catalog at the file/volume level, but the individual-record level is not online — it requires either on-site research at Marburg or a paid lookup request. This research has not yet been attempted. It is the most concrete unexplored path remaining for German-side identification.

negative LAGIS Hesse military pages

LAGIS Hessen publishes some military-related lists (officer rosters, Reservist registers). No Charles match in the available indexed pages under any spelling.

The illegal-emigration hypothesis

The 1850s saw a documented surge of illegal emigration from Hesse-Kassel driven by conscription evasion. Quoting the project's April 5 2026 Research Paper: "emigration scholars have noted that 1850s departures from Hesse-Kassel were frequently illegal — undertaken specifically to evade conscription." The mechanism was straightforward: a draft-age man would skip his conscription notice, cross to Bremen via the Weser, embark for America, and never appear on the Hessian state's emigration roster because the state never authorised his departure.

This hypothesis is consistent with three observations the project has made independently:

  • Charles is missing from the Hessian Auswanderer-Beleg series (no legal-emigrant registration)
  • Charles is missing from every U.S.-side passenger manifest under any tested surname (consistent with travelling under an assumed name to evade extradition)
  • Charles's surname spelling drifted aggressively after emigration (Konsen → Gonnson → Gannsen → Johnson) — a pattern observed in conscription-evasion cases

If the Stammrolle entry is recovered and shows a "desertiert" / "fled" annotation, all three of these gaps become explicable simultaneously.

Next paths to chase

  • Direct paid query to Marburg HStAM Bestand 4h — a targeted lookup for "Konze / Konsen / Gansen / Stern 1855 Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis" (Charles's 20th-birthday cohort) should be feasible for a modest fee. This is the single highest-leverage remaining German-side action.
  • On-site research at Marburg — if a paid lookup proves insufficient, on-site research at the archive can scan the full Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis Stammrollen for the 1855 conscription cohort.
  • Cross-reference with parish baptism entries — the Stammrolle's parish-of-registration field will localise the home parish, which will then unlock the German baptism research. Mutually-reinforcing search.
  • Hesse-Cassel illegal-emigration ledger — the project's Open Question (Section VI) asks: "Does any Hesse-Cassel illegal-emigration ledger (conscription-age young men, fled c. 1855–1859) preserve a Konze / Konsen / Stern match?" — this is the same hypothesis approached from the other direction.
