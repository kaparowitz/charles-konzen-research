Charles Research  /  DNA Evidence

DNA Evidence

What genetic testing adds to a documentary investigation — a FamilyTreeDNA Big Y-700 on Charles's direct paternal line and autosomal estimates from two vendors (AncestryDNA and MyHeritage) on a descendant — and, just as important, the limits of what each can show.

Genealogy and genetics answer different questions. The records reconstruct who Charles was; DNA tests whether the biological line agrees. This page reports both tests in full, including the matches they did not produce, so a reader can weigh the genetic evidence against the documentary conclusions on the claims dashboard.

Bottom line. The DNA to date is consistent with Charles's documented German origin but does not confirm either the parish (Hesse / Trendelburg) or the surname (Konzen). The Y-DNA places his strict paternal line in a young (~1450 CE) subclade whose nearest relatives are — so far without exception — Ashkenazi-Jewish lineages — pointing to a possible Ashkenazi-Jewish origin in the deep male line. On the strength of only two-to-three tested members this is held as a working line of inquiry, not a conclusion; it concerns deep ancestry that sits beneath, and does not contradict, the documented Hessian-Evangelical family. The autosomal estimates — from two independent vendors, AncestryDNA and MyHeritage, in agreement — are heavily Germanic but, taken from a descendant, average every ancestral line and cannot single out Charles's. No match yet ties the line to a documented Konze family, though a German-resident paternal cluster at 3rd-cousin depth (MyHeritage, June 2026) is under active correspondence — not yet assigned between Charles's line and Auguste Kreichelt's. The origin and surname conclusions therefore remain Working — see the confidence taxonomy — and the single highest-yield next step is to recruit a documented Konze male from the Trendelburg–Deisel area to Y-test.

Y-DNA — the direct paternal line

FamilyTreeDNA · Big Y-700

Terminal subclade: I-Y38863  ·  Haplogroup: I2, the I-M223 branch.

I-M170 › I-M223 › I-P78 › I-S9302 › … › I-Y6401 › I-Y6406 › I-Y11261 › I-Y50708 › I-Y38863

The Y chromosome passes essentially unchanged from father to son, so it tracks one line — Charles's patriline — back through the centuries, independent of every other ancestor. A Big Y-700 reads that line at high resolution and places it on the human paternal tree by its terminal SNP.

YFull · independent analysis · sample YF147577

The same Big-Y data was independently interpreted by YFull (sample YF147577), which places the line on the identical terminal subclade I-Y38863 (YTree v14.03, formed ~750 ybp, TMRCA ~700 ybp ≈ 1300 CE). On YFull this subclade has exactly three members: YF147577 (Charles's tested descendant), YF078713 — flagged Poland [PL-14] with YFull's “yid” Ashkenazi origin tag — and YF002081. So the two men sharing Charles's exact YFull branch are an Ashkenazi Pole and one further sample — the same Poland/east-European signal FamilyTreeDNA shows, reached on a separate tree. The full Y-STR panel (~780 markers, reviewed 6 June 2026) is an unremarkable I2-M223 modal haplotype (DYS393=13, DYS390=22, DYS19=16, DYS391=10, DYS385=16-17) with no rare or null markers — consistent with the placement and adding no conflicting signal.

What it shows

I-M223 is an old, broadly European paternal haplogroup. But the relevant signal is Charles's specific terminal subclade, I-Y38863, which on both the FamilyTreeDNA and YFull trees sits inside an Ashkenazi-Jewish, east-central-European cluster (Poland, Ukraine, Belarus, Romania, Russia, with some German presence) — not the northwest-European distribution of the parent haplogroup as a whole. So while the deep haplogroup is unremarkably European, the subclade that actually defines Charles's male line points toward a possible Ashkenazi origin — on the present two-to-three-tester evidence (developed in the next sections).

How recent is the shared ancestor?

This is the part that makes the Y-DNA genealogically useful rather than merely deep-historical, and two labs, analysing the same single Big-Y file, agree on a medieval window (an independent re-interpretation of one kit, not a second sample). FamilyTreeDNA's Discover estimates place the I-Y38863 branch's formation near 1400 CE and its common ancestor near 1450 CE; YFull's independent tree (v14.03) dates the subclade's formation to ~750 years before present and its most recent common ancestor to ~700 ybp — roughly 1300 CE. Either way the shared ancestor is medieval — a few centuries before Charles's 1835 birth — so I-Y38863 is a comparatively young subclade: a Konze man who tested into it would share a paternal ancestor with Charles within a genealogically meaningful window, a far more probative test than a deep-haplogroup match.

On both trees the branch's members cluster in east-central Europe — Poland, Ukraine, Belarus, Romania, Russia, and Germany — the Ashkenazi heartland, not a specifically Hessian or German-Christian distribution. Several of the YFull samples on and just above this branch carry YFull's “yid” origin tag (its marker for Ashkenazi-Jewish submitters), which is what makes the next section's reading independent of FamilyTreeDNA.

What it does not show

  • No Konze surname match. Across both the two close Big Y matches and the wider set of Y-STR (111-marker) matches, none carries a Konze / Kontzen / Contze surname. In keeping with the project's living-relatives policy, living testers are not named on this page. What the match list does reveal is discussed in the next section.
  • Subclade geography includes but does not pinpoint Hesse. The branch's German / Polish / Russian spread is consistent with a German origin, but a three-tester branch with a ~1450 CE common ancestor cannot resolve to a region within Germany, let alone to a parish.
  • A recent-ish common ancestor cuts both ways — and still isn't evidence against Konzen. Because the branch's ancestor sits near 1450 CE, a Konze match would carry real genealogical weight. But the absence of one among only three testers — whose surnames are not Konze and whose lines fixed surnames at different times across this range — is most simply explained by no Konze male-line descendant having Big-Y tested yet, not by Charles's line being non-Konze.

The match cluster — an unexpected Ashkenazi paternal line

The most striking result is not in the haplotree but in who else sits on it. Reading the Y-STR match list (eight matches, tested to 111 markers or Big Y) by their position on the tree, a clear pattern emerges: Charles's closest paternal-line matches are predominantly Ashkenazi-Jewish lineages from the Ashkenazi heartland of east-central Europe.

FamilyTreeDNA's Match Time Tree shows the same structure: both men who share Charles's exact terminal subclade (I-Y38863), and the closest match one branch up (I-BY2640), are Ashkenazi Jewish — three independent lines, with documented earliest-known ancestors born in Poland and Russia in the mid-1800s, all converging on Charles's line around 1400–1450 CE. This is the most striking pattern in the genetic evidence, though it rests on only three testers.

What it most likely means

Charles's strict paternal line — his father's father's father, and so on — descends from a lineage that is, on the present evidence, Ashkenazi-Jewish in origin. Three independent Ashkenazi lines coalescing with Charles's around 1400–1450 CE are most simply read as a common paternal ancestor of that period who was himself part of this Jewish lineage — though with only two-to-three testers this is a working inference, not a demonstrated fact. That reading of the deep male line does not, at least, rest on a single company's analysis: YFull's independent tree reaches the same result, placing the line in the same I-Y38863 subclade, with the same medieval coalescence, among samples it flags as Ashkenazi — though this is an independent re-analysis of the same Big-Y file, not a second tested sample. What remains genuinely open is when and how that line entered the Christian, Hessian, Konze-surnamed milieu in which Charles was born and baptised — by an early assimilation centuries before his birth, or by a later undocumented-paternity event closer to it.

What it does not mean

  • It does not make Charles or his documented family Jewish. The Konze family appears in Hessian Evangelical parish registers; Charles married at a German-Evangelical church and his children were baptised there. The Y-DNA sits beneath that documented Christian identity — it speaks to ancestral origin, not to the religion the family practised in the 1800s.
  • It does not date the Jewish ancestor. The signal could reflect an ancestor many generations before Charles. Neither the AncestryDNA nor the MyHeritage ethnicity estimate flags a European-Jewish region, yet the FamilyTreeDNA list carries several small autosomal matches to Ashkenazi-surname individuals (see the match list below) — together pointing to a Jewish contribution that is real but limited and/or deep, and diluted across the generations down to the tested descendant.
  • The matches are not close cousins. Genetic distances are moderate (GD 7–10 at 111 markers; 8–10 differing Big Y STRs), consistent with the ~1450 CE common-ancestor estimate rather than a recent shared grandfather.

Why it matters to this investigation

The finding intersects two unresolved threads, and is logged as a new line of inquiry on Open Questions:

  • The never-found Konze baptism. One explanation for years of negative parish searching is that Charles's biological paternal line is not, in fact, the documented Konze family — i.e., an undocumented-paternity event somewhere up the line. The Ashkenazi Y-signature is consistent with (though does not prove) such a scenario.
  • The January-1835 illegitimacy hypothesis. The project has long entertained the possibility that Charles was born illegitimate (the basis of the multi-parish 1835 illegitimacy scans). An unknown biological father would carry whatever paternal line he carried — and could be the source of this Y-result.

Both remain hypotheses. The Y-DNA neither confirms nor refutes them; it adds a concrete, independent constraint that future record work can be tested against.

This is not a new direction for the project — the Y-DNA independently supports an old one. From August 2025, the investigation pursued a Hessian-Jewish paternal-origin inquiry with Peter Schräder, working from documented local Jewish families: the 1740 Trendelburg “Protected Jews” (Seligmann Ganß and neighbours), the Meimbressen Gans / Segelbaum family, the Christian Gans of Lippoldsberg, and the Stern / Kleeburg line of Hofgeismar. That inquiry was set aside in May 2026 because its basis was a weak surname-resemblance argument (Gans → Gannsen) and because the specific candidates were eliminated or never tied to Charles by a record. The Y-DNA result arrives at the same destination by a completely different road — biology rather than name-similarity — and so independently supports the direction while leaving those specific eliminations standing. It does not revive the surname-Gans theory; it does make the documented Jewish presence in Charles's own locality worth a fresh, evidence-led look. The reactivated thread is tracked at Open Questions · deep paternal origin.

Re-checked 7 June 2026 (public FTDNA Discover data): the subclade roster is unchanged — still three tested men (earliest-known paternal origins Germany, Poland, Russian Federation; TMRCA ~1450 CE, 95% CI 1102–1673). Their public group-project memberships add quiet corroboration: of the three, two belong to the Avotaynu Jewish Census project, with single memberships across the German-Jewish (GerSIG), Sephardi-Ashkenazi-Connections, JewishGen-Scandinavia, and Jewish-DNA projects, one in a Jewish surname project, and one each in the Germany and Poland geographic projects — i.e., the subclade's other members publicly research their lines as Ashkenazi lines.

Match individuals are not named here, per the project's living-relatives policy; the pattern is described from surnames, recorded earliest-known ancestors, and paternal countries of origin in the FamilyTreeDNA match list (retrieved 30 May 2026; Discover re-checked 7 June 2026). Kit identifiers can be supplied to bona-fide researchers on request.

Autosomal DNA — the whole-ancestry estimate

AncestryDNA & MyHeritage · descendant test

Autosomal DNA recombines each generation and reflects all of a person's ancestry, not one line. The test here is from a descendant of Charles, so Charles is only one of many ancestors contributing to the estimate — and a smaller share with each generation of distance. The kit has been run through two independent vendor reference panels — AncestryDNA and MyHeritage — which segment Europe differently but agree on the essentials.

The Germanic component (AncestryDNA)

Among the descendant's fifteen reported regions, the Germanic cluster dominates the Western-European portion:

The two vendors draw their region boundaries differently — MyHeritage's single “Germanic” region spans roughly what Ancestry splits into “Southern Germanic Europe” and “Northwestern Germany” — but they converge on the same picture: a dominant Germanic / Dutch block of roughly half the genome (~48% MyHeritage, ~54% Ancestry), a large British-Isles / Celtic remainder (English, Scottish and Welsh, Irish, and Breton total ≈40% at MyHeritage), and small trace regions. Notably, neither vendor reports a European-Jewish region — the same below-threshold silence discussed under the match list below. Agreement between two independently built reference panels strengthens confidence in the broad admixture picture, but it does not relax either of the limits that follow.

What it shows — and its two limits

A Germanic block of roughly half the genome — reported by both vendors — is consistent with Charles's documented German origin and with the broader family reconstruction. But two limits keep it from confirming anything specific:

  • It is a descendant's admixture. By the time the line reaches the tester, Charles contributes only a fraction of the genome; the 54% Germanic signal aggregates Charles's line with every other German ancestor and cannot isolate his.
  • The regions are broad. Ancestry's "Southern Germanic Europe" and "Northwestern Germany" — and MyHeritage's single "Germanic" region even more so — each span large territories. Hesse falls within them but is not resolved by them — the estimates cannot distinguish a Hessian ancestor from a Bavarian, Rhinelander, or Saxon one.

The FTDNA match list — what ~14,300 autosomal matches add

Beyond the ethnicity estimate, FamilyTreeDNA's Family Finder returns about 14,300 autosomal matches for the tested descendant. Their structure is itself informative:

  • One immediate-family match — a close relative who has also tested, whose tree carries the Gannsen / Konze surnames. It confirms the kit sits where expected in the family but adds no independent evidence.
  • No close cousins on the German side. Beyond that relative, the nearest match shares only ~119 cM (a 2nd-to-4th-cousin range), and the mid-range matches are predominantly British-Isles / American surnames — the autosomal signal is dominated by the tester's other lines, not Charles's.
  • No match to the documented Hessian families. Searching every match's surname and ancestral-surname list returns no Konze / Kontzen, Kreichelt, Grandjot, or Herwig connection at a meaningful segment size. The Hessian reconstruction remains unconfirmed autosomally — the same gap as on the parish side.
  • A faint Ashkenazi echo of the Y-line. The list does contain several small matches (≈9–12 cM, single segments) to individuals whose ancestral-surname lists are distinctly Ashkenazi — including, notably, the same paternal surname that heads Charles's closest Y-subclade match (a different individual). On their own these are weak: single small segments are exactly what Ashkenazi endogamy produces in abundance and can be coincidental. But arriving alongside the Y-DNA result, they are consistent with genuine Ashkenazi ancestry in the paternal line. They are not, however, independent confirmation of the Y reading: the same endogamy that makes the Y-subclade Ashkenazi-clustered is also what scatters these small segments, so the two are largely one signal seen twice rather than two. They do not date it.
  • The echo localises to a specific segment (7 June 2026). A full segment export (15,610 segments) shows the Ashkenazi-surname matches are not scattered: 35 matches carry ≥5 cM segments overlapping one block on chromosome 17 (≈54–64 Mb), many with near-identical breakpoints — suggestive of a shared founder haplotype rather than random noise (and distinct from the known chr-17 pile-up region at 43–45 Mb). This is the same block on which the tester, his father, and the MyHeritage German paternal cluster triangulate. If these segments sit on the paternal copy of chromosome 17, the German cluster and the Y-line's Ashkenazi signature would converge on a single inherited segment — a testable claim: FTDNA's paternal/maternal bucketing (pending an account-linking step) will assign them definitively. Until then this is an unverified but striking alignment.
  • X-DNA: structurally uninformative. The X chromosome never passes father-to-son, and Charles sits on the tester's strict paternal line — so X-matching can never bear on the Charles question from a male-line descendant's kit. This avenue is closed by inheritance logic, not by absence of data.
  • mtDNA: not pursued (and not informative for Charles's line). No mitochondrial test has been run. mtDNA traces the strict maternal line, which from the tested descendant leads away from Charles entirely, so it could not speak to his paternal-origin or surname question. It would, however, be the correct tool for a different open thread — testing the maternal side of the unwed-mother / illegitimacy hypothesis raised below — and is noted here for completeness of the test plan rather than as a gap in the Charles inquiry.

The apparent tension with the ethnicity estimates — neither of which flagged a European-Jewish region — is not a real contradiction: small Ashkenazi segments can fall below an estimate's reporting threshold, especially once diluted across the generations down to a descendant. Read together, the two suggest an Ashkenazi paternal contribution that is real but limited and/or deep.

The MyHeritage match list — a German-resident paternal cluster (June 2026)

The same descendant's kit returns 6,896 matches at MyHeritage, whose tester base is far more continental-European than FTDNA's or Ancestry's. Crucially, both of the tester's parents and a sibling have also tested there, so every match can be assigned to the paternal or maternal side by shared-match triangulation — a discriminating power the FTDNA list lacks. Reviewed 7 June 2026:

  • The documented-surname gap persists. Searching all matches' names and ancestral-surname lists returns no Konze / Kontzen, Gannsen, Kreichelt, Grandjot, or Herwig — the same negative as FTDNA, in a second database.
  • 327 matches reside in Germany. The largest (≈50–80 cM) resolve to the maternal side and carry Swabian / Württemberg surnames — a reminder that not every German signal belongs to Charles.
  • A paternal German cluster — the most promising autosomal lead to date. Two Germany-resident testers and U.S.-resident children of one of them match the tester at ≈52–60 cM, match the tester's father and not the mother, and share ≈52 cM with each other — one ancestral couple plausibly links the cluster to the father's German ancestry. The vendor's estimate places both German testers at 3rd-cousin depth to the father: a most-recent-common-ancestor couple born roughly 1800–1840 — Charles's own generation or one above it. The cluster's (shallow) trees carry ancestral surnames pointing to two regions — Lower Saxony / Hannover on one side and Westphalia on the other (the specific surnames belong to living matches' family lines and are withheld here per the privacy policy; available to bona-fide researchers on request).
  • The cluster is not yet assigned to a line. The father descends equally from Charles and from Auguste Kreichelt, whose family was Hannoverian (Clausthal, Oberharz; emigrated 1853). The cluster could therefore descend from a sibling of Charles who stayed in Germany (→ the long-sought German anchor for the Konze conclusion) or from Auguste's collateral kin (→ the first autosomal corroboration of the Kreichelt reconstruction) — the Hannover-region surnames fit the Kreichelt geography at least as well as the Hessian one. Either resolution advances the project. Correspondence with members of the cluster opened June 2026; one member had independently contacted the project years earlier, naming one of these ancestral lines. The discriminating question — Oberharz or Hofgeismar district — is now with them.
  • The cluster triangulates. A one-to-many chromosome-browser run (tester × the German tester's family × the tester's father) confirms one triangulated segment on chromosome 17 (≈54.2–64.3 Mb, 12.4 cM, 4,608 SNPs) shared by all four kits — a true identical-by-descent segment from a single common ancestor, inherited through the father. One cluster member's kit has shared-segment viewing disabled, so whether their 55.1 cM segment sits on the same block awaits their reply.
  • AutoClustering isolates the same group — and nothing else. MyHeritage's AutoClusters analysis (run 7 June 2026; 100 matches ≥~30 cM in 17 clusters) places the three cluster members in one cluster of exactly three, the only Germany-resident paternal cluster in the entire match list. Shared-match clustering, the members' ≈52 cM overlap with each other, and the chr 17 triangulation now agree independently: one ancestral couple likely stands behind the group. The same run also resolved the remaining large German matches into two maternal clusters (the Swabian block and a second family group), clearing them from the Konzen-side candidate list.
  • The faint Ashkenazi echo replicates. Small matches (≈9–20 cM) to individuals with distinctly Ashkenazi ancestral surnames recur inside the paternal cluster's shared-match lists, and only four matches reside in Israel, all ≤20 cM — the same weak-but-consistent pattern as the FTDNA list, now visible in a second database, and still undated.

The kit was attached to the project's family tree on MyHeritage on 7 June 2026, activating automated tree-matching (Smart Matches™ live immediately; Theories of Family Relativity™ pending the next recalculation). Granular workings — side assignments, segment figures, the surname-sweep table, and the AutoClusters parse — are in the session log (Session 96, 7 June 2026); living matches are not named here per the privacy policy.

What the DNA changes — and what it doesn't

Nothing in the genetic results promotes a claim to Confirmed, and nothing refutes one. The Y-DNA and autosomal evidence both corroborate a German / Germanic origin in general while remaining silent on the two questions the project most wants answered: was the parish Hesse / Trendelburg, and was the surname Konzen? Those conclusions continue to rest on the documentary record (the surname trail and the “Freundburg” / “Deisse” origin decodings) and are held Working accordingly. See the claims dashboard for how this feeds the load-bearing claims, the methodology · genetic evidence section for the source-rating treatment, and open questions · DNA for the live thread.

The highest-yield next step

Because the Y-DNA line is the one piece of evidence that could directly test the surname conclusion, the most valuable single action is targeted recruitment:

  • Y-test a documented Konze / Kontzen male from the Trendelburg–Deisel area (ideally Big Y-700). If his line lands on or near I-Y38863, the surname conclusion is strongly confirmed; if it lands elsewhere on the tree, the conclusion is challenged. Either outcome is decisive in a way the documents alone are not.
  • Widen the autosomal net by inviting known descendants of the candidate Hessian families to test or to share kits, and by uploading existing kits to additional matching databases where permitted.
  • Fish for autosomal matches against the documented Kreichelt, Grandjot, and Herwig descendants to confirm the U.S.-side reconstruction on its own genetic footing.
  • Resolve the MyHeritage paternal cluster (above): correspondence is open with its German members; their answer to the Oberharz-vs-Hofgeismar question assigns the cluster to the Kreichelt or the Konze line. Attaching the kit to a family tree on MyHeritage (to unlock automated tree-matching against the cluster) is the highest-leverage free action.
  • Activate FTDNA Family Matching: with the father's kit linked as a parent, FTDNA buckets all ~14,300 matches paternal/maternal — immediately testing whether the chromosome-17 Ashkenazi founder block sits on the paternal copy (the convergence claim above) and giving the FTDNA list the same side-resolution the MyHeritage analysis already enjoys.

If you descend from the Trendelburg–Deisel Konze / Kontzen family, or from any of these lines, the project would welcome contact — see Contribute.
