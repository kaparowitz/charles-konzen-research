The Pension Mutual-Aid Network

How Charles and his St. Louis circle worked the 1890 Dependent and Disability Pension Act together
THEMATIC 1889 – 1891 7 affidavits · 5 witnesses · 1 claim agent

The pattern

Charles Gannsen's 1891 pension award was not the work of one man working through paperwork alone. It was the product of a cross-city mutual-aid network of St. Louis Civil War veterans and civilian neighbors who knew each other through Battery B service, post-war workplaces, and the German Lutheran / Evangelical parish circle — spanning both halves of the city. Charles himself lived on the south side at 2408 DeKalb (Kosciusko) by 1890; only Theodore Gottlob (next door at 2409 DeKalb) was a literal neighbor. The other 1891 declaration witnesses — Hebbeler at 916 N. 6th, Thurbecker at 638 N. 7th, Niemeier at 1300 N. 9th — were all on the north side in the historic Hyde Park / Bremen / Old North St. Louis German corridor. The network was held together by long-term Battery B and parish ties, not address. Five separate witnesses swore seven affidavits across an eighteen-month window in 1890-91, all funneled through a single U.S. Claim Agent — Fred. W. Fout — and notarized by the same handful of St. Louis notaries.

The names recur on each other's pension files in this same period. Several of the witnesses filed their own pensions in the same 1889-1891 window in which they testified for Charles. The pattern is not coincidence; it is the fingerprint of a working network.

Why 1889–1891? The legal trigger

The Dependent and Disability Pension Act of 27 June 1890 changed federal Civil War pension eligibility in a way that suddenly made it worth filing. Before 1890, a Union veteran had to prove that his disability was service-connected — that the rheumatism, hernia, or hearing loss originated in his time in uniform. That was a high evidentiary bar requiring contemporaneous medical records the typical Missouri private had never possessed.

The 1890 Act swept that aside. Any permanent disability that prevented a veteran from earning a support by manual labor now qualified, regardless of when or how it was contracted. A veteran's word, supported by neighbor and comrade affidavits, was enough.

The result was a national surge in pension applications — by some estimates, the number of pensioners on the federal rolls roughly doubled in the five years after passage — and a small army of claim agents, notaries, and circuits of mutually-witnessing veterans appeared in every major Union city to handle the load. St. Louis, with its dense Union veteran population from the early-war Missouri regiments, was a particular hot spot.

Charles's pension file is a snapshot of this surge. Every affidavit in it is dated after 27 June 1890. The earliest filing — May 1891 — is just inside the window in which most St. Louis Battery B alumni were quietly putting their own pension paperwork together.

The actors

Fred. W. Fout
U.S. Claim Agent · St. Louis
Filed Charles's claim. Likely identified the available Battery B comrades in the city, drafted the affidavits in the right legal language ("heavy cannonading at New Madrid," "rupture from heavy lifting"), and shepherded the package through the Pension Bureau. The connective tissue.
August Derr
1st Sgt., Co. B, 2nd MO Light Artillery
Charles's own NCO at New Madrid. Filed his own pension 24 May 1889 — well ahead of the 1890 Act, but updated under it. Swore two affidavits for Charles: 29 Sept and 14 May 1891 — one on rupture, one on hearing loss + rheumatism.
Christian Meyer
Pvt., Co. B, 2nd MO Light Artillery
Battery B private, fellow gun-crew member. Reconnected with Charles at his residence "nearly three years ago" — i.e. ~1888, exactly when the pension organizing began. Swore 3 May 1891; corroborated Derr on cannonading and exposure narrative. Teamster in the 1880 census.
Henry Stoll
Pvt., Co. L, 2nd MO Light Artillery + 1st U.S.R.C. Cav.
Sister-battery comrade in the same regiment as Charles. Swiss-born; long-time St. Louis neighbor. Witness to Charles's post-war disability progression as a regimental peer.
Michael Nolan
Pvt., Co. F, 1st MO Light Artillery
Adjacent-regiment veteran. Enlisted April 1861 (Sumter month). Co-worker of Charles since 1867 — likely on the St. Louis railroad. Swore in October 1891. The civilian-style affidavit describes time lost from work — exactly what the 1890 Act required.
Henry Niemeier
Civilian neighbor · 26-year acquaintance
Westphalian-born St. Louis sawmill worker. Met Charles in 1865, the year of his Civil War discharge. Swore 15 October 1891 — the longest-tenure civilian acquaintance affidavit in the file. Crucial because his testimony placed the hearing loss already at first acquaintance.
Theodore Gottlob Sr. ⚜
Sgt., Co. A, 9th Illinois Infantry · 1861-1864
★ Identified as Civil War veteran 3 May 2026 late evening. Westphalian-born; enlisted Alton, IL on 19 April 1861 — 4 days after Lincoln's call for volunteers, 90-day militia. Re-enlisted 25 July 1861, Cairo, IL, as Sergeant for 3-year term; saw Vicksburg; discharged 20 Aug 1864, Springfield, IL. Pension $15/month for rheumatism "since the war" — the same primary disability Charles claimed. NHDVS Leavenworth admission 1893. Co-witness with Hitzelberger on Charles's 1890 Pension Declaration; the declaration-witness pair was both Union-veteran-credentialed, not just neighbor-credentialed. Also Jed's 2nd great-grandfather — see profile.
John Heitzenberger = a Hitzelberger
Pension Declaration co-witness · 1890 (surname-family ID)
Pennsylvania-Maryland-German Hitzelberger (clerk's "Heitzenberger" was a misreading). Co-witnessed Charles's 1890 Pension Declaration alongside Theodore Gottlob. Lived at 2412 Arsenal Street (south side). Specific individual identity still open between A. J. Hitzelberger and a brother / brother-in-law. Civil War service status unknown — none of the documented Hitzelberger Civil War records (Joseph K. of 13th MD Inf., d. Ft. McHenry 1865; Capt. George of 1797 Lancaster PA militia) match.

Now seven personal witnesses identified, of whom five (potentially six) are confirmed Civil War veterans: August Derr (Battery B 1st Sgt.), Christian Meyer (Battery B Pvt.), Henry Stoll (Battery L of 2nd MO Light Artillery), Michael Nolan (Co. F 1st MO Light Artillery), and now Theodore Gottlob (Co. A 9th Illinois Infantry Sgt.) — plus the still-open Hitzelberger. Only Henry Niemeier is firmly civilian.

The timeline

27 June 1890 Dependent and Disability Pension Act signed by President Benjamin Harrison. Eligibility no longer requires service-connected origin of disability.
24 May 1889 August Derr files his own pension claim in Missouri (under the still-restrictive 1879 Arrears Act regime). Establishes the playbook within the Battery B circle.
~1888 Christian Meyer reconnects with Charles at Charles's residence — "nearly three years ago" before his May 1891 affidavit. Witness preparation begins informally.
3 May 1891 Christian Meyer swears General Affidavit (Page 22) — heavy cannonading at New Madrid, rheumatism from exposure. Notary: Theo. W. Boutje Jr.
14 May 1891 August Derr swears second affidavit (Page 26) — corroborates Meyer on cannonading + exposure; identifies himself as "1st Sergt. of same Co." Notary: Theo. W. Boutje Jr.
29 Sept 1891 August Derr swears first affidavit (Page 24) — rupture from heavy lifting at New Madrid.
15 Oct 1891 Henry Niemeier swears 26-year civilian acquaintance affidavit (Page 20). Filed by Fred. W. Fout, U.S. Claim Agent.
October 1891 Michael Nolan swears co-worker affidavit (Page 18) — time lost from work. Filed by Fred. W. Fout. Notary: Fred. W. Fritz Jr.
November 1891 Charles awarded $12.00 / month pension by the Pension Bureau on the strength of the assembled affidavits — about $425–435 / month in 2026 dollars (≈$5,100/year, roughly 30% of a common laborer's annual income at the time). Drawn for ~12 years until his death in December 1903; total drawn ≈$1,728 in 1891–1903 dollars (≈$62,000 in 2026 equivalent).

The mechanics: how a claim agent ran the operation

Fred. W. Fout — the U.S. Claim Agent listed on Charles's filings — operated in St. Louis as one of dozens of similar agents nationally. The agent's role was three-part:

  1. Identify the witnesses. Locate surviving Battery B and adjacent-regiment comrades still living in St. Louis. Cross-reference local directory listings, GAR post rolls, and word-of-mouth in the German parishes. The fact that Charles's Battery B 1st Sergeant (Derr), a fellow Battery B private (Meyer), and a Battery L regimental comrade (Stoll) all happened to live within a few miles of each other in St. Louis was not luck — it was a community-wide post-war settlement pattern of which the agent was professionally aware.
  2. Frame the affidavits. The Pension Bureau wanted very specific phrasings. "Heavy cannonading at New Madrid in September 1863." "Rupture from heavy lifting." "Exposure resulting in rheumatism." The actor-card affidavits in Charles's file all use this exact language because the agent drafted it; the witnesses signed off and added a few personal details. Compare Meyer's wording (Page 22) and Derr's wording (Page 26): the structural similarity is unmistakable.
  3. Notarize and submit. St. Louis notaries Theo. W. Boutje Jr. and Fred. W. Fritz Jr. recur across the file. They are part of the operation. The agent files the package with the Bureau, follows up on Bureau correspondence, takes a contingency fee on the awarded pension.

This mode of operation was so widespread by 1891 that it generated a backlash: the 1893 Pension Bureau under Hoke Smith began aggressively challenging "claim agent" cases as fraudulent, and several thousand pensions were briefly suspended pending re-examination. Charles's pension survived this scrutiny intact, suggesting the underlying disability evidence was sound.

What this changes about reading the pension file

Reading Charles's pension file as a collection of seven independent depositions — the way a casual reader might — overstates the testimonial diversity. Reading it as a coordinated mutual-aid filing — which is what it was — sharpens the reader's understanding in three ways:

  1. The shared language is not corroboration; it's coordination. Identical phrasings between Meyer and Derr should not be read as two independent witnesses converging on the same facts — they reflect the agent drafting both affidavits.
  2. The strongest evidence is in what's not shared. Niemeier's "already hard of hearing in 1865" — a date before any of the soldier witnesses had reconnected with Charles in St. Louis — is the single strongest unprompted corroboration of the New Madrid cannonading hypothesis, because it's the one detail that doesn't fit a rehearsed narrative.
  3. The witness selection itself reveals Charles's social network. Five Civil War veterans (four 2nd MO Light Artillery, one 1st MO Light Artillery), one civilian neighbor of 26 years' standing, and a workplace co-worker of 24 years' standing — that is the structure of Charles's mature St. Louis life as understood by the man who knew it best, his claim agent.

The bigger picture: artillery alumni in post-war St. Louis

Four of the six witnesses are Missouri Light Artillery veterans. This is not an accident of friendship; it reflects a specific feature of how the artillery arm of the Union service worked.

Light Artillery batteries in the Civil War were small (typically 80-150 men), tight-knit units in which a private served at close quarters with the same gun crew for years. Survival required cooperation in a way infantry company life did not. Artillery batteries also recruited from urban populations more than rural ones — drawing the literate, mechanically-skilled tradesmen and immigrants who could handle ordnance. After the war, those men tended to settle back into the same urban districts from which they had been recruited. St. Louis Battery B's post-war census of survivors, in the city's German immigrant neighborhoods, is the natural sediment of that pattern.

By 1891, the city's Light Artillery alumni community was thick enough that a claim agent could assemble the right witnesses for a Battery B pension claim within an afternoon's work — and the witnesses themselves had been quietly looking out for each other for the previous twenty-five years.
