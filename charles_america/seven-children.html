The Seven Children — Gannsen-Kreichelt Generation 2

Born 1866–1880 · all baptized at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church · 4 surviving past childhood
CHARLES IN AMERICASurnames recorded as Gonnson · Johnson · Konsen

★ At a Glance

All seven children of Charles Gannsen × Auguste Kreichelt were baptized Lutheran at St. Marcus Church, with surnames recorded variously as Gonnson, Johnson, and (critically, in 1875) Konsen. Two of the seven names are themselves evidence: the firstborn Friedrich Wilhelm (named for the deposed Elector of Hesse the same year he lost his throne) and the sixth child Erwin Carl Konsen (whose 1875 baptism preserves the German Hesse spelling of the surname). Three children — Friedrich Wilhelm (16 months), Maria Josephine (22), and Auguste (18) — died young; four survived into the 20th century.

⚜ A name that points home — Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson · 20 June 1866 → 2 September 1866

Charles and Auguste Kreichelt married at St. Marcus Church on 7 January 1866. Their first child was born 20 June 1866 and christened 2 September 1866 as Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson. Per the FamilySearch tree custom-fact attribution (Jed S Johnson, Aug 2021): "Named after Friederich Wilhelm Pillmann, one of the baptism sponsors." The same summer of 1866 saw the political destruction of Charles's homeland.

The day-by-day timeline is striking:

  • 17 June 1866: Prussia declares war on Hesse-Kassel — start of the Austro-Prussian War
  • ~19 June 1866: Prussian troops occupy Kassel, the Electoral capital
  • ★ 20 June 1866: Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson born in St. Louis — three days after Prussia's declaration of war, ~one day after Kassel falls. Trans-Atlantic telegraph not yet operational; steamship news ~10–14 days behind. Charles in St. Louis almost certainly did not yet know about the war.
  • 23 June 1866: Elector Friedrich Wilhelm I is transferred as a prisoner to Stettin — the operative moment of his deposition, three days after the baby was born.
  • ~late July 1866: News of Kassel's occupation and the Elector's arrest reaches St. Louis
  • 23 August 1866: Peace of Prague ends the war
  • ★ 2 September 1866: Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson christened at St. Marcus — Charles has known about the Elector's deposition for ~6 weeks. He proceeds with the original sponsor plan: Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann (godfather) + Wilhelmine Ebling Leue (godmother, with the female form of Wilhelm) + Georg & Henriette Kreichelt.
  • 20 September 1866: Hesse-Kassel formally annexed by Prussia — the Electorate ceases to exist.

Two readings of this naming.

Reading A · Coincidental name, deliberate sponsor follow-through. The name Friedrich Wilhelm was chosen weeks earlier in spring 1866 because Charles & Auguste's first-choice godfather was Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann, a friend whose name happened to match the Elector's. The match was coincidental — both Pillmann and the Elector were named Friedrich Wilhelm, a common Hessian name. Charles did not yet know about the deposition when the baby was born. But by 2 September Charles knew, and he did not change sponsor plans or rename the baby — proceeding with a baptism that registered the Elector's name in the German Evangelical record at the precise moment the Electorate ceased to exist.

Reading B · Intentional sponsor selection. Among the godfather candidates in his St. Louis circle (Adam Herweck, August Kreichelt, Georg Kreichelt himself, other Battery B veterans), Charles deliberately selected Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann — and the godmother Wilhelmina Ebeling, with the female form of Wilhelm. The matched pair structurally guaranteed the baptism record would carry the Elector's name. Pillmann was unmarried and not yet kin to the Kreichelts in 1866 (he would only marry into the Albrecht family in 1870), which makes his selection as firstborn-godfather more pointed: Charles singled him out because of his name, not family-tier closeness.

Verdict. Both readings end at the same place. By 2 September 1866, when the parish clerk wrote Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson, Charles knew the Elector had been deposed. Whether he engineered the moment from spring 1866 (Reading B) or accepted it as a meaningful coincidence (Reading A), the result is identical: the firstborn is named for the Elector at the precise moment the Electorate ceased to exist. This places Hesse-Kassel specifically at the emotional center of Charles's American identity in his first year of fatherhood. The infant died at 16 months old and was buried at Old St. Marcus Cemetery, but the name survives as the strongest single piece of identity evidence the investigation has produced.
