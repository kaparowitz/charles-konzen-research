Sponsor reconciliation

A baptism-by-baptism audit of every named sponsor across the seven Gannsen children's baptisms at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church, St. Louis, 1866–1881 — plus the four 1866 wedding witnesses — with each person's documented kin relationship to Auguste Kreichelt, and the explicit absence of any documented Charles-side blood relative.

The "immigrated alone" hypothesis on this site rests on a structural claim: across seven baptisms over fifteen years, not a single named sponsor is a documented blood relative of Charles. This page turns that narrative into an auditable artifact — one row per baptism × sponsor, with the kin link to Auguste named explicitly and the absence of a Charles-side link logged in every row.

Thesis Across the seven Gannsen baptisms at St. Marcus (1866–1881), 17 distinct sponsorship slots were filled by 15 named individuals — two people came back to stand up at a later baptism, most prominently Auguste's father Georg Kreichelt, with the affinal in-law Friedrich Pillmann also recurring, which is why the 17 slots map to 15 distinct named individuals. Of those 15 individuals, every one is documented as a Kreichelt-side relative, in-law, or St. Louis parish-circle associate of Auguste's family. Zero are documented blood relatives of Charles. German-Lutheran baptism sponsorship in this period was structurally a kin-default convention; the asymmetry across all seven baptisms over fifteen years is consistent with Charles having no German blood family accessible by mail or in person between 1866 and 1880 — corroborating the working hypothesis that he immigrated alone in 1859 and never re-established contact close enough to bring kin to the baptismal font.

Wedding witnesses — the 1866 marriage

The same pattern holds at the wedding. The St. Marcus marriage register (7 January 1866, entry no. 676; Pastor G. W. Wall) names four witnesses (Zeugen): Adam Herweck, Philipp Weber, Anna Goetz, and Sophie Lehm. As at the baptisms, the only documented blood kin in the room are Auguste's — her father Georg and brother August Kreichelt. Charles's side is represented not by a relative but by Adam Herweck, his Battery B comrade and likely recruiter (b. 16 July 1831, Lampertheim, Hessen; FS K4DW-PXM) — a fellow soldier, not kin. Weber, Goetz, and Lehm are St. Louis parish-circle names with no documented blood tie to Charles. So across the wedding and the seven baptisms, not one of the ~two dozen named sponsors and witnesses is a documented Charles-side relative.

AncestryProGenealogists (2020) This thread was opened by the original 2020 research. The AncestryProGenealogists journal's research plan named it explicitly — “identify baptism sponsors for Charles's children not obviously related to his wife” — and left it as follow-up work. The 2026 sponsor reconciliation above, and the witness review of the non-Kreichelt sponsors (e.g. Erwin Spraul on Doc K), executed that plan; the result is a documented absence of any Charles-side blood relative among sponsors or witnesses.

Counterarguments considered

Counterargument 1 "Maybe a Charles-side relative was present but recorded under an unfamiliar surname Charles didn't recognise." The seven St. Marcus baptismal registers identify each sponsor by name and, where applicable, by relationship; the project has cross-walked every named sponsor to a FamilySearch profile or to a documented Auguste-side kin link. No sponsor on any of the seven baptisms has been left without an identification that places them on Auguste's side or in the St. Louis parish circle. The hypothesis of a hidden Charles-side relative under a non-obvious surname is not supported by any documentary trace and would require positing an individual who left no other footprint in the project's 78+ research sessions across Trendelburg, Deisel, and St. Louis records.
Counterargument 2 "Maybe Charles had Hessian relatives in St. Louis whom Auguste's clerks routinely passed over." Two of the seven baptisms (Erwin Carl 1875; Maria Josephine 1868) explicitly involve a non-Kreichelt sponsor — Erwin Spraul, Jacob & Josephine Reck. These slots demonstrate that the family did reach outside the Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann-Mincke kin network when it chose to. The absence of a single Charles-side person in any of those reaches — over fifteen years and across five pastors — is itself the structural evidence.
Counterargument 3 "Maybe German baptismal sponsorship in 1860s–1870s St. Louis was no longer kin-default, in which case the absence of Charles-side kin is uninformative." Comparative work on the parallel Kreichelt-side baptisms is instructive: across the ten St. Marcus baptisms of Carl Kreichelt's children (Auguste's brother), the sponsor pool is similarly kin-default — Kreichelt parents, siblings, in-laws. The 1866–1880 St. Marcus parish was operating with the kin-default convention intact in the same congregation; the asymmetry across the seven Gannsen baptisms therefore reads as informative, not as a period-and-place artifact. The comparative analysis is on the Charles in America tab.

Confidence status

The structural "immigrated alone" reading is held in the project's Working state per the source-rating taxonomy: the sponsorship pattern is convergent indirect evidence rather than a single dispositive record. Promotion to Confirmed would require a positive identification of Charles's German baptismal entry, his 1859 passenger manifest, or a Hessian Auswanderer-Beleg / Stammrolle entry — any of which would directly establish whether Charles had surviving kin in Hesse and whether any of them ever joined him in America. Those four primary-source gaps are catalogued on Open Questions.

Confidence tag — how sure are we they aren't family?

That no named sponsor or wedding witness is a documented Charles-side blood relative: High — a documented absence across ~24 sponsor/witness slots, seven baptisms plus the 1866 marriage, 1866–1881, under five officiating pastors, every identified name tracing to Auguste's kin or the St. Louis / Battery B circle.

That this proves Charles had no kin who ever joined him in America: Moderate — Working — convergent indirect / negative evidence, not a single dispositive record; a German baptism, the 1859 manifest, or a Hessian Beleg could still surface a relative.

How this table was built

The sponsor identifications come from the seven children's individual profile pages (children/), which in turn cite the St. Marcus baptism register entries by page and entry number plus the FamilySearch profile for each sponsor. Each kin-relationship-to-Auguste claim traces to a documented FamilySearch tree link or to a St. Louis civil-record citation; each absence-of-Charles-side-link claim is the absence of any such trace across the project's research sessions. Counterargument framing follows Mills, Evidence Explained, §1.31 on the structure of negative-evidence arguments.
