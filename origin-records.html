Where Charles Said He Was From — the U.S. Records

In forty years of American records, Charles named a specific German place in just three of them — two distinct statements, “Freundburg” and “Deisse” (the latter recorded twice in 1866), both decoding to the same corner of Kurhessen. Every other record places him only at region or country level. Each statement by date, sourced, linked, and ranked by genealogical confidence.

This is the companion to Birth date across the records and Surname across the records, applying the same evidence model to the question that drives the whole search: where in Germany was Charles born? He never named his parish. The closest he ever came are the two town-level statements at the top of this page — both phonetic, both pointing to the Trendelburg / Deisel area — which is exactly why the German search is concentrated there (Parish search coverage).

How these are rated — by genealogical standards

Each statement is classified with the same Source · Information · Evidence framework and Genealogical Proof Standard used on the birth-date and surname pages. For origin, two things drive confidence:

Specificity

Does the record name a town / Kreis, a region (Kurhessen / Hesse-Cassel), or only a country (“Germany”)? A town-level statement does far more work than “Germany.”

Informant & directness

Did Charles himself give it (enlistment, marriage, naturalization, 1900 census) or a survivor / household member? And is it direct (a stated place) or indirect (inferred from an act, like whom he named a son after or which sovereign he renounced)?

The town is an interpretation

What the records actually contain is the recorded spelling — “Freundburg,” “Deisse.” The modern villages (Trendelburg, Deisel) are Peter Schräder’s phonetic interpretation — a strong reading, but not a recorded fact, and one a researcher could read differently. The confidence below is High* for specificity; the asterisk marks exactly this decode dependency.

How “Freundburg” and “Deisse” have been read

Because neither spelling matches a real place, the town behind each is an interpretation. Three researchers have proposed readings; here they are with the reasoning, ranked under the project’s Working / Open / Eliminated taxonomy. “Working” is the current best reading driving the search — not a proven identification.

Sources: James Gunnis’s readings are from his Ancestry correspondence (June 2026); Peter Schräder’s from his December 2025 emails; the AncestryProGenealogists readings (Freudenberg for “Freundburg”; Dies and Diez weighed, but Deisel concluded, for “Deisse”) from their 2020 journal. The two leading readings (Trendelburg, Deisel) are adjacent villages, so both “Working” statements point to the same corner of Kurhessen; James’s “Open” alternatives (Dissen, Grifte, Gudensberg) sit a little south, toward Kassel, and are folded into the widened search.

“From” is not “born in”

A caution AncestryProGenealogists raised and James Gunnis echoed: a man saying he was from Deisse / Deisel tells us where he considered home or last lived — not necessarily where he was baptized. He could have been born in one parish and raised in another (James: “if Charles was born somewhere else but lived there a long time…”). It also means the Rhine reading isn’t fully dead as biography even though it’s dead as origin. This gap is exactly why the search leans on confirmation registers, which record each child’s birth date and place regardless of where the family later moved.

A telling silence — the pension file

Charles’s 40-page pension file (1890–1903) is the single richest body of his own sworn testimony — and a page-by-page audit found no self-stated birthplace anywhere in it. Even where he had every reason to give it, he never named his German home again after 1868. That silence is itself part of the record.

What the statements point to

Strip out the country-level “Germany” entries and only the town statements carry real geographic information — and they converge: “Freundburg” (1862) decodes to Trendelburg, and “Deisse, Kreis Cassel” (1866, recorded in both the church and the civil marriage registers) decodes to Deisel — two adjacent villages in the same Kreis Hofgeismar corner of the former Electorate of Hesse-Cassel. Every other record is consistent with that and contradicts none of it. That convergence is the working geographic anchor of the whole investigation and the reason the parish search is centred on Trendelburg and Deisel. The decoding itself — how Trendelburg becomes Freundburg to an English-speaking clerk — is laid out on The Konzen Puzzle.
