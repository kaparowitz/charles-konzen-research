Story 06

The pastor who wrote two different vowels

1870 and 1872 · St. Louis

In 1870 Pastor Braschler of St. Marcus baptised one of Charles & Auguste's daughters and recorded the family surname as Gansen. Two years later he baptised another daughter and recorded it as Gonsen. Same pastor, same family, two different vowels, two years apart.

If the spelling reflected the pastor's preference, both entries would say the same thing. They don't. Braschler wrote down what he heard. The vowel he heard varied. Which means the spelling reflected Charles's voice rather than the pastor's pen.

This is what genealogists sometimes call a "smoking gun" of vocal-versus-scribal capture. It is the project's strongest single piece of evidence that the nine distinct American renderings of Charles's surname are not many independent scribes' guesses at an unknown name — they are at least five different scribes' transcriptions of one spoken Hessian original.

The original is the Hessian Konzen, with the silent t in the tz-affricate that English-speaking ears reduced to a simple s, and the unstressed -en ending that an American clerk could just as easily have written -on. The cluster of phonetic captures — Gonnson, Gansen, Gonsen, Konsen, Conson — all sit close to a single spoken target [ˈkɔn.sən]. It just happened to land in the middle of Pastor Braschler's vocabulary, twice, with two different vowels.

Sources: St. Marcus baptisms 1870 (Katherine Carolina) and 1872 (Auguste); Pastor Braschler's hand. Key Evidence Card 1 for the full surname-trail argument; Visualize for the diagrammatic version.
