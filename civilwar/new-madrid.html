New Madrid, Missouri

Field posting (twice) · Sept – Nov 1863 with battery + 24 April 1864 – 27 March 1865 detached · ~14 months total
CIVIL WARSecond tour newly resolved May 2026 by NARA mail-order packet

★ At a Glance

Charles served two distinct tours at New Madrid, Missouri — the strategic Mississippi River fort captured by Pope's army in March 1862. Tour 1 (Sept – Nov 1863) with Battery B is the source of the hearing-loss and rheumatism disabilities he later claimed (sworn to in 1891 by 1st Sgt. August Derr and Pvt. Christian Meyer, both fellow Battery B veterans). The tour ended with Charles's promotion to Sergeant on 7 November 1863. Tour 2 (24 April 1864 – 27 March 1865, ~11 months) was a long detached service at the same Mississippi River garrison — newly resolved by the May 2026 NARA mail-order packet (PG 09 Card B), correcting an illegible Fold3 reading. After Tour 2 ended on 27 March 1865, Charles transferred to Rolla / Fort Wyman for ~3 months under G.O. No. 82.

What New Madrid looked like — period engravings, maps, and Pope's headquarters

The Battle of New Madrid (March 3 – 13, 1862) and the linked Siege of Island No. 10 (Feb 28 – April 8, 1862) produced the first Union victory on the Mississippi and were heavily covered by the war's illustrated newspapers. Period engravings, official War Department maps, and the surviving headquarters mansion in town give a remarkably full visual record of the post Charles would garrison about a year later.

★ Reading correction (May 2026): The Fold3 PG 06 reading of the March–April 1864 detached destination was illegible and had been provisionally interpreted as A.A.G. office detached service in St. Louis. The NARA mail-order packet's clearer scan resolves the destination as New Madrid, Mo. Charles was therefore at New Madrid in two distinct tours, not just the fall 1863 tour previously documented — and Tour 2 ran ~11 months, ending with the 27 March 1865 transfer to Rolla.

Historical context

The strategic geography of New Madrid

New Madrid sits on a sharp bend of the Mississippi River — the so-called "New Madrid Bend" — in the southeastern bootheel of Missouri, opposite Tennessee and just upstream of the river's joining with the Ohio at Cairo, Illinois. The town's position made it a strategic chokepoint for river traffic; in Confederate hands (1861–March 1862) it threatened all Union shipping descending the Mississippi from St. Louis. Pope's Union army captured New Madrid and the adjacent Island No. 10 between 28 February and 8 April 1862, opening the river to Union steamboats southward toward Memphis. Battery B garrisoned the fort from spring 1863 through April 1864 — Charles's two New Madrid tours fall inside this Union-garrison period.

Why a light artillery battery at a river fort

By 1863 New Madrid had become a "static" Union position — the front had moved south to Vicksburg — but the post was kept manned to deny the river to Confederate raiders and to support Union counter-guerrilla operations in southeast Missouri. Battery B's 12-pounder Napoleon guns and 3-inch ordnance rifles were configured both for direct fire (against river targets and approaching cavalry) and for siege / fortress duty. The "heavy cannonading" Charles's Battery B comrades later attested to in their 1891 affidavits is consistent with garrison-artillery practice firings — daily training rounds, response-fire to Confederate guerrilla activity, and warning shots on river craft of uncertain affiliation.

The November weather and the rheumatism narrative

The Mississippi River bottomlands at New Madrid are notoriously damp and cold in late autumn — the river itself moderating frost but the persistent humidity and wind off the water producing the kind of chronic-cold exposure that 1860s medicine recognized as a rheumatism risk. Both Derr and Meyer's 1891 affidavits specifically locate Charles's "rheumatism contracted by exposure" in November 1863 at New Madrid — fitting the seasonal-weather profile of the post.

Visit today — museums, markers, and historic sites

New Madrid preserves an unusually rich Civil War footprint. The town is a stop on the Missouri Civil War Passport Program, and three core sites bracket the experience: the historical museum on the riverfront, Pope's actual headquarters from the 1862 campaign, and the marker tracing the Siege. The New Madrid Chamber of Commerce publishes a Civil War Driving Tour brochure covering 16 historic sites in town — embedded below.

★ Driving Tour of Civil War Sites of New Madrid (brochure detail)

The official New Madrid Chamber of Commerce brochure, "Driving Tour of Civil War Sites of New Madrid, Missouri." The brochure walks visitors through sixteen numbered stops in town, including:

  • 9. Evergreen Cemetery — granite monument and burial site for seven Confederate veterans (Allen, Fontaine, Lee, Lewis, Barnes, Dawson, Digges).
  • 10. Hunter-Dawson Cemetery — family burial ground including George Washington Dawson (1832–1862) and William Colson Hunter (1841–1902), both veterans of the 1st Regiment, Missouri (Confederate) Infantry.
  • 11. Hunter-Dawson State Historic Site — the Hunters' 15-room cypress mansion, used as a hospital during the war and as Pope's headquarters during the Siege of New Madrid.
  • 12. Canal / Fort Bankhead — Pope's 12-mile bypass canal cut through swampy lowland in 19 days to outflank the Confederate batteries at Island No. 10. Route WW closely parallels the canal today. Fort Bankhead, the small companion redoubt at the mouth of St. Thomas Bayou, was washed away by the Mississippi.
  • 13. East Side Cemetery — unmarked Civil War graves, including the only surviving stone (Pvt. Henry Riddle, drummer, Co. C, 4th Regiment U.S. Colored Heavy Artillery — died at age 14 at Columbus, KY, on 21 June 1863).
  • 14. New Madrid County Library — a 13-inch mortar shell from the Battle of Island No. 10 sits at the base of the flagpole, recovered from Rucker's Battery on the Tennessee shore.
  • 15. Masonic Lodge — Union forces burned the original lodge in 1862.
  • 16. Immaculate Conception Catholic Church — interior painting depicts the burning of St. John Baptist Catholic Church by Union forces, who suspected the priest of Confederate sympathies (specifically with Brig. Gen. M. Jeff Thompson, "Missouri's Swamp Fox").

The brochure also summarizes the town's pivotal place in the war's western theater: "It was North against South, blue against gray, neighbor against neighbor. The year was 1862…" Featured facts include New Madrid as the August 1861 site where expelled MO Governor Claiborne F. Jackson signed the secession proclamation; the home of Co. I, 1st MO Infantry ("the shock troops" — the first MO unit to enter Confederate service); the appearance of "Old Abe," the eagle mascot of the 8th Wisconsin, at New Madrid in March–April 1862; and the only U.S. gas balloon reconnaissance operation in the entire Western theater. Capturing New Madrid took 20,000 Union troops and four 128-pounder siege guns.

★ Hunter-Dawson State Historic Site — Pope's headquarters (1862)

The 15-room mansion Maj. Gen. John Pope used as his command post during the Siege of New Madrid and the operations against Island No. 10. Built 1859–1860 for William Washington and Amanda Hunter; about 80% of the original 1860 furnishings are still in the house. Operated by the Missouri Department of Natural Resources as a state historic site, with guided tours.

Address: 310 Dawson Road, New Madrid, MO 63869.

Links: Missouri State Parks page · visitor information & hours · American Battlefield Trust profile · Wikipedia · Missouri Civil War Passport entry

★ New Madrid Historical Museum — Civil War Room

A riverfront museum in the former Kendall Saloon at the foot of Main Street. The Civil War Room displays letters, uniforms, equipment, and weapons from the Battle of New Madrid and the Siege of Island No. 10 — "much of it donated by local residents with family connections to the pieces." The Battle of Island No. 10 is interpreted from a local-historian perspective. The museum sells reference books on the campaign in its gift shop. Joint admission ticket available with the Higgerson School Historic Site.

Address: 1 Main Street, New Madrid, MO 63869 · phone 573-748-5944.

Hours: open 7 days a week, 358 days a year. April–November: Mon–Sat 9 AM – 5 PM, Sun noon – 5 PM. December–March: Mon–Sat 10 AM – 4 PM, Sun noon – 4 PM.

Links: newmadridmuseum.com · Official New Madrid city page · Facebook

Higgerson School Historic Site

A restored one-room schoolhouse at Higgerson Landing (just outside New Madrid), interpreting rural Bootheel education from the early 19th century forward. Joint-ticket eligible with the New Madrid Historical Museum.

Links: TripAdvisor reviews

What the comrades' pension affidavits say

Charles's 1890–1891 Civil War pension claim rested in part on the sworn affidavits of two Battery B veterans, both placing the disability cluster at New Madrid in fall 1863:

★ "Hearing loss originated from heavy cannonading at New Madrid in September 1863." Rheumatism "originated from exposure to cold and hardship at New Madrid in November 1863." The hernia / rupture came from "heavy lifting" at the same station. — Substance of affidavits sworn in 1891 by August Derr (1st Sergeant, Battery B) and Christian Meyer (Pvt., Battery B). Pension File pages 22, 24, 26.

These comrade affidavits are the primary documentary support for the pension award. They are independent of Charles's own 1890 Declaration for Invalid Pension, which locates the heavy-lifting injury at Fort Wyman, Rolla (the late-war 1865 detachment, not at New Madrid). The two narratives — Charles's own declaration vs. the Derr / Meyer affidavits — are partly inconsistent. See the Research log below.

★ Research notes — New Madrid vs. Fort Wyman, the disability sources

The pension file contains two complementary accounts of where Charles's disabilities originated:

  1. Charles's own 1890 Declaration for Invalid Pension (Pension File Page 14) locates the heavy-lifting / right-hernia incident at Fort Wyman, Rolla, with a date that is internally garbled — consistent with his ~3-month Rolla detachment (27 March – 1 July 1865) on the corrected reading of the muster cards.
  2. The 1891 affidavits of August Derr and Christian Meyer (Pension Pages 22, 24, 26) locate the hearing loss (heavy cannonading) and rheumatism (cold exposure) at New Madrid in fall 1863, when all three men were together with Battery B at the Mississippi River fort. The Derr/Meyer affidavits also bundle the heavy-lifting / rupture into the New Madrid period — likely reflecting what they could personally witness; both Tours 1 (fall 1863) and Tour 2 (1864–65) were at New Madrid, so the heavy-lifting incident could have originated there as well.

Working synthesis. The cleanest reading: hearing loss + rheumatism originated at New Madrid in fall 1863 (Tour 1, with battery, per Derr/Meyer); the right-hernia / heavy-lifting may have originated at New Madrid Tour 2 (April 1864 – March 1865) per Derr/Meyer's bundling, OR at Fort Wyman / Rolla (March – July 1865) per Charles's own declaration. The pension award stands on either reading.
