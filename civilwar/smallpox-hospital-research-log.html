Smallpox Hospital — Research Log

Investigating two open questions: did Charles serve as a nurse after a smallpox sickness, and where was the original mainland facility?
CIVIL WARLast updated 6 May 2026

★ At a Glance

Charles's hospital sequence has two distinct events in winter 1862–1863, both documented on the carded record and corroborated by the pension file: (1) a sickness admission to the Regimental Hospital from 22 December 1862 — only 20 days after his enlistment — and (2) a detached service assignment to the Smallpox Hospital from 20 February 1863 through ~early May 1863 under personal Special Order of Maj. Gen. Samuel R. Curtis. Question 1 — was the Smallpox-Hospital detail a recovered-patient-as-nurse role? The sickness-then-staff sequence is now established (correcting an earlier reading on this site that missed the Dec 22 sickness annotation). What is NOT established is whether the original Dec 1862 illness was specifically smallpox — the muster card says only "Sick in Regt Hospital," not naming the disease. The patient-first-nurse-after hypothesis is therefore PARTIALLY SUPPORTED (sequence yes; specific smallpox-immunity rationale still unconfirmed). Question 2 — which facility for the detached service? Most-likely the St. Louis city Smallpox Hospital on Quarantine Island (renamed Arsenal Island in 1866), in the Mississippi River opposite the U.S. Arsenal at Second & Arsenal Streets — not the mainland City Hospital at Lafayette & 14th, and not Sunflower Island near Alton (a separate Alton-prison facility upstream).

Status as of 6 May 2026 (revised)

Q1 — patient-first-nurse-after hypothesis: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED. User correction (6 May 2026 evening): the Nov–Dec 1862 muster card (NARA PG 6 right card / Fold3 PG 21) explicitly records "Sick in Regt Hospital since Dec 22, 1862" under remarks — an entry the earlier transcription on this site had missed. The pension file page 8 (the War Department's 1890s summary reply to the Pension Bureau) independently corroborates the chain: "He was present except as follows: Dec 31, 62 Sick in Regt Hospt since Dec 22, 62 (Present) Feb 28/63 Det service in small Pox Hosptl. Other records furnish nothing additional bearing upon this." The earlier conclusion that the carded record showed "no sickness preceding the Smallpox Hospital detail" was wrong. What remains open is whether the original Dec 1862 sickness was specifically smallpox (which would make the Smallpox Hospital staff detail a recovered-immune-nurse assignment, the original hypothesis) or a different illness (in which case the staff detail is administratively coincidental).

Q2 — facility identification: working answer remains Quarantine Island / Arsenal Island. Strong period-source convergence; not yet directly verified by a primary record naming Charles at that address.

Combined hospital timeline (revised):

  • 22 Dec 1862: Admitted to Regimental Hospital, sick (illness not specified). Only 20 days post-enlistment.
  • ~late January 1863: Recovered (implied — the next muster shows him "Present" status before the Smallpox Hospital detail begins).
  • 20 Feb – ~9 May 1863: Detached to Smallpox Hospital under Curtis Special Order. ~10–11 weeks.
  • May 1863 onward: Returned to battery at the St. Louis garrison forts.

Question 1 — Did Charles serve as a nurse after his own smallpox sickness?

The hypothesis is reasonable on its face: smallpox survivors had partial immunity, and recovered patients were a preferred labor pool for smallpox-ward nursing throughout the Civil War. So if Charles had contracted smallpox himself in late 1862 / early 1863 and then been retained as a nursing orderly during convalescence, the muster cards might plausibly carry that arc.

The carded record contains two distinct hospital events in this window — a sickness preceding the Smallpox Hospital detail, and the Smallpox Hospital detail itself. Sourced verbatim:

Event 1 — Sickness admission to the Regimental Hospital, 22 December 1862

"Sick in Regt Hospital since Dec 22, 1862. Enrolled Dec 2, 1862, St. Louis Mo. for 3 yrs. Mustered in Dec 2, 1862, St. Louis Mo." — NARA PG 06 Card B (right card, Nov–Dec 1862 Battery Muster Roll). The "Sick in Regt Hospital since Dec 22, 1862" line is part of the Remarks block and was MISSING from the earlier transcription on this site — surfaced 6 May 2026 by re-reading the original card. The same notation appears on the parallel Fold3 PG 21 transcription.
"[Charles Gannsen] was present except as follows: Dec 31, 62 Sick in Regt Hospt since Dec 22, 62 (Present) Feb 28/63 Det service in small Pox Hosptl. Other records furnish nothing additional bearing upon this." — Pension File F41-523680269E, page 8 (the War Department's 1890s summary reply to the Pension Bureau, abstracting Charles's service from the original muster rolls). This independently corroborates the muster-card sequence, with the War Dept clerk explicitly chaining the two events.

Event 2 — Detached service at the Smallpox Hospital, 20 February 1863

"On det. service at Small Pox Hospital since Feb. 20, 1863 by order of Maj. Genl. Curtis" — NARA PG 06 Card A (left card, Jan–Feb 1863 Battery Muster Roll). Black, Copyist.
"On det service at Small Pox Hospital under Maj Genl. Curtis" — Fold3 PG 20 (the same Jan–Feb 1863 muster, transcribed by a different clerk). Black, Copyist.
"Attached to Small Pox Hospital by Special Order of Maj. Genl. Curtis" — Fold3 PG 19 + NARA PG 07 Card A (Special Master Roll, 18 April 1863). Black, Copyist.

What the two events together support — and what they don't

The two events establish a clear sequence: Charles was sick first (Regt Hospital admission, 22 Dec 1862), recovered, and was then detached to the Smallpox Hospital nine weeks later (20 Feb 1863). This is the "sickness-then-staff" pattern the user recalled. The earlier conclusion on this log — that "the carded record uniformly characterizes Charles as STAFF at the Smallpox Hospital" with no preceding sickness — was based on an incomplete reading of NARA PG 06 Card B that missed the Dec 22 sickness annotation. The corrected reading restores the sequence.

What remains open is the specific identification of the Dec 1862 illness. The card says only "Sick in Regt Hospital" — it names no disease. The Regimental Hospital was Battery B's own unit medical facility, not a smallpox-quarantine ward, so the original illness was probably not initially diagnosed as smallpox (smallpox cases were typically transferred out to the Quarantine Island complex). The most plausible scenarios:

  1. Smallpox at Regt Hospital → recovered → kept on as immune nurse at Smallpox Hospital. Possible but not directly supported. Smallpox cases SHOULD have been transferred out, but in a December 1862 outbreak surge they may have been initially held at the Regt Hospital before transfer. The 9-week gap between admission and Smallpox-Hospital staff detail (Dec 22 – Feb 20) matches the typical 4–8 week recovery time for smallpox plus a return-to-duty buffer.
  2. Different illness (typhoid, dysentery, pneumonia, generic camp illness) → recovered → detached to Smallpox Hospital for administrative reasons unrelated to immunity. The administrative-convenience hypothesis (literacy, available recovered soldier, departmental need) doesn't require smallpox to have been the original disease. This is the conservative reading.
  3. Two unrelated events. Sickness and detail-assignment happen in sequence purely because Charles was a recently-recovered enlisted man in St. Louis when the Department needed to staff the smallpox cordon. The "patient-then-nurse" reading would be a later 1890s pension-narrative gloss, not a documented institutional logic.

The carded record as currently understood doesn't distinguish between these scenarios. Resolving the question definitively would require the Regimental Hospital's admission ledger for December 1862 (which would name the diagnosis) — typically held in NARA RG 94 or RG 393. The Special Order for the Smallpox Hospital detail itself, if it exists in NARA RG 393, may also name the assignment role explicitly (orderly, nurse, hospital corps clerk, guard, etc.).

Why the verbatim card phrasing matters

Civil War muster-card clerks distinguished consistently between personnel-assignment idioms and hospital-admission idioms:

  • Personnel-assignment ("staff"): "On detached service at __", "Attached to __", "On duty at __", "Detailed to __", "Assigned to __ as orderly / nurse / cook / guard". The issuing authority is typically a departmental commander or post commander.
  • Hospital admission ("patient"): "Sick in hospital at __", "In hospital at __", "Sick, absent in U.S.A. General Hospital at __", "Reported sick to hospital __", "Furloughed sick from __". The issuing authority is typically a surgeon, not a commander.

The Dec 22, 1862 entry uses the patient idiom ("Sick in Regt Hospital") — Charles was admitted as a patient. The Feb 20, 1863 entry uses the staff idiom ("On det. service at" / "Attached to") with Curtis personally as the issuing authority — Charles was assigned as staff, by a departmental commander. So the cards distinguish two different roles in the two different events. This is informative: at the Smallpox Hospital itself, Charles was staff, not a patient. But the question of whether the original Dec 1862 sickness was the smallpox he was later assigned to nurse remains undetermined by the cards alone.

The CSR enclosures index — corroborates staff role at the Smallpox Hospital

The CSR file's back-of-jacket "Enclosures" form (NARA PG 03 / Fold3 PG 22) tracks which auxiliary documents the file originally contained. The ticked enclosures for Charles are:

  • "Med. Examination" (1 tick) — refers to the medical-disability paper trail (the late-war 1864 hospitalization and the pension medical exams), not a Dec 1862 admission.
  • "Misc. Information" (1 tick) — the Special Order of Curtis on PG 24.

The categories "Admission to Hosp'l", "Discharge from Hosp'l", "Transfer to Hosp'l", and "Bed Cards" are all unticked. The Dec 22, 1862 Regt Hospital admission was therefore handled at the regimental level (battery sick-list / regimental hospital ledger) rather than escalated to a separate carded admission record at the CSR level. This is consistent with a routine illness handled in-unit rather than a major hospital admission requiring separate paperwork. (The diagnosis itself is not preserved in the CSR.)

Question 2 — Where was the original mainland facility?

The muster cards say "Small Pox Hospital" without naming the specific building. Identifying the facility requires period St. Louis sources.

The strongest period source

The 1875 Pictorial St. Louis (a city-history compendium published a decade after Charles's detail) describes a city institution called the Quarantine Hospital:

"[T]he Quarantine Hospital is situated in the southern portion of the city … of the same character as the City Hospital, supported by, and under the direction of, the city, through the board of health. This hospital contains a separate 'lying-in ward,' and a department for the exclusive treatment of small-pox patients." — Pictorial St. Louis (1875), via the Lossos compilation "Early St. Louis Hospitals, Homes, and Asylums" (stlouis.genealogyvillage.com/hospitals.htm).

The "Quarantine Hospital" of the Pictorial St. Louis description was located on what was then called Quarantine Island, in the Mississippi River, ~4 miles south of downtown St. Louis, mid-channel directly opposite the U.S. Arsenal at Second & Arsenal Streets. The city took ownership of the island on 4 May 1849 and operated the institution there through the 1860s and 70s. The island was renamed Arsenal Island in 1866 (per Lafayette Square Archives historical research), but during Charles's window (early 1863) the institution was still under its Quarantine Island name.

The federal-military-hospital co-tenancy

What ties the Quarantine Island identification specifically to Charles's detail is the wartime co-tenancy:

"Two large buildings constructed on the island held federal military hospitals. Two other long buildings comprised the city smallpox hospital." — Lafayette Square Archives, "1866: The Tale of Quarantine Island" (lafayettesquarearchives.com).

The federal military hospital buildings and the city smallpox hospital occupied the same physical island, in the same complex. That co-tenancy gave the Department of the Missouri's commander (Curtis) a single physical site to which a battery soldier could be assigned by departmental Special Order — covering either the federal military hospital staffing OR the smallpox quarantine cordon, on the same site, with one administrative gesture. This institutional arrangement is the most coherent single explanation for why a battery sergeant was being detached to a "Small Pox Hospital" by a departmental commander rather than by a hospital surgeon.

Why NOT Sunflower Island ("Smallpox Island")

The Sunflower Island story (also called "Smallpox Island") is well-known but is a different facility from the one Charles served at. Sunflower Island is approximately 25 miles upstream of St. Louis, near Alton, Illinois. It was operated by the Alton Federal Military Prison, not by the St. Louis department, and only began receiving smallpox patients in August 1863 — five months after Charles's detail had ended. The previous version of this site referenced a "move" of patients to Sunflower Island; that reference was incorrect (the Sunflower transfer was for Alton prison patients, not for St. Louis civilian/military patients). The Quarantine Island institution in St. Louis continued operating in place throughout this period.

Why NOT the City Hospital at Lafayette & 14th

St. Louis City Hospital (founded 1845) at Lafayette Avenue and 14th Street was the main municipal mainland hospital and did receive smallpox cases — but its standing wartime policy was to transfer such cases out to the Quarantine Island complex. So while City Hospital may have been the initial admission point for some smallpox patients, the dedicated Smallpox Hospital ward was on the island, and that's where staffing details would have been deployed. (Confirming this conclusively requires the city Board of Health annual report for fiscal 1862–63, held at Missouri History Museum.)

Two distinct hospital periods:

  • Patient at Regt Hospital: 22 December 1862 through approximately late January 1863 (~5–6 weeks of recovery, illness not specified)
  • Staff at Smallpox Hospital: 20 February through ~9 May 1863 (~10–11 weeks of detached duty under Curtis's Special Order)

The two periods together account for almost all of December 1862 through early May 1863. Charles's first five months of Civil War service were dominated by hospital settings — first as a patient, then as staff at a different (smallpox-quarantine) hospital. Whether the two events are causally connected (smallpox in Dec 1862 → recovered, then immune-nurse at the Smallpox Hospital from Feb 1863) or merely sequential remains undetermined by the carded record alone.

Round 3 6 May 2026 (evening), user correction

The earlier rounds of this log concluded that "the patient-first-nurse-after hypothesis is NOT SUPPORTED by the carded record" because every Feb–Apr 1863 muster card uses staff-assignment idioms ("On det. service at" / "Attached to") and never patient idioms. That conclusion was based on a transcription that missed the Dec 22, 1862 sickness annotation on NARA PG 06 Card B (the right-side Nov–Dec 1862 muster card).

The user pointed this out: the right card under remarks reads "Sick in Regt Hospital since Dec 22, 1862" followed by the enrollment/muster-in line — and pension file page 8 independently confirms this with the War Department's 1890s summary "He was present except as follows: Dec 31, 62 Sick in Regt Hospt since Dec 22, 62 (Present) Feb 28/63 Det service in small Pox Hosptl. Other records furnish nothing additional bearing upon this."

Revised conclusion. The sickness-then-staff sequence is documented by both the muster card and the pension file — the user's original recollection was correct on this point. What's still not directly documented is whether the original Dec 1862 illness was specifically smallpox. The "patient-first-nurse-after" hypothesis goes from REJECTED to PARTIALLY SUPPORTED: sequence yes, smallpox-immunity rationale not yet confirmed. Resolving the disease question requires the Regimental Hospital admission ledger for December 1862 (NARA RG 94 / RG 393, paid order).

Open questions and where to resolve them

  1. The Regimental Hospital admission ledger for December 1862. Would name Charles's specific Dec 22 diagnosis. If the entry reads "Variola" or "Smallpox", the patient-then-immune-nurse hypothesis is essentially confirmed. If it reads anything else (typhoid, pneumonia, dysentery, "general debility"), the Smallpox Hospital staff detail in February becomes administratively coincidental rather than driven by smallpox immunity. Likely held in NARA RG 94 (Adjutant General's Office Hospital Records) or RG 393 (Continental Commands). Paid order via NATF Form 86.
  2. The Special Order itself. Neither carded packet preserves the actual order text. NARA RG 393, Department of the Missouri, Special Orders volume for January–May 1863, would carry the verbatim order naming Charles. Paid order via NATF Form 86 / 81 (~$30). The order text would name the specific role (orderly, nurse, hospital-corps clerk, guard, etc.) and the specific facility (Quarantine Hospital, City Hospital, etc.), settling both Q1's role question and Q2's facility question.
  3. Hospital-attendant carded records. NARA RG 94 includes a "Carded Records of Hospital Attendants, Matrons, and Nurses" series. If Charles served as hospital staff, a card may exist for him there. Free indexing via the NARA online catalog at archives.gov/research/military/civil-war/resources. Absence would be additional negative evidence on the nurse hypothesis; presence would resolve it positively.
  4. St. Louis Board of Health annual reports for fiscal 1862–63 and 1863–64. Held at the Missouri Historical Society / Missouri History Museum, Forest Park, St. Louis. These would name the specific facility (Quarantine Hospital, City Hospital, or other) and may list military detail-staff. Free in person; paid copy by mail.
  5. Western Sanitary Commission records. The WSC ran the Federal hospitals at St. Louis during this window. Its papers are at the Missouri Historical Society and (Western Department papers) at the New York Public Library Manuscripts Division. Free in person.
  6. Surgeon General's correspondence (Department of the Missouri). NARA RG 112, Letters Received, 1863. May contain returns / requisitions naming the smallpox-hospital staff complement. Paid.
  7. Period St. Louis maps. 1860s Compton/Dry "Pictorial St. Louis" plates and U.S. Coast Survey charts of the Mississippi at St. Louis would show Quarantine Island in situ during the war. Available at Missouri History Museum and via Library of Congress online (free).
