Battery B, 2nd Regiment, Missouri Light Artillery 🇺🇸 Union Army

Charles Gannsen's unit · 1862–1865 · U.S. Volunteer artillery, Department of the Missouri
CIVIL WARCharles served 2 Dec 1862 – 20 Dec 1865

★ At a Glance

Battery B, 2nd Regiment, Missouri Light Artillery was a Union Army U.S. Volunteer light-artillery battery raised in St. Louis at the start of the Civil War. The 2nd Missouri Light Artillery as a regiment was organized between September 1861 and February 1862; Battery B was one of its twelve lettered batteries. Charles Gannsen enlisted in Battery B on 2 December 1862 and served with the unit through 20 December 1865 — a clean three-year enlistment covering the entire arc from Charles's first muster-in to his honorable discharge. The battery was attached to the Department of the Missouri (headquartered in St. Louis), with most of its 1862–1865 service concentrated within Missouri: garrison duty at the St. Louis forts, two tours at the strategic Mississippi River post of New Madrid (Sept–Nov 1863 with battery; April 1864 – March 1865 with Charles detached), and a late-war re-equipping as cavalry that took the unit to Omaha and the Powder River Expedition in summer 1865 — without Charles, who in spring 1865 was on detached duty at Rolla / Fort Wyman and then at Department HQ St. Louis through November 1865.

The unit's flag and its guns

Two visual artifacts ground what Charles's three years of service looked like: the regimental guidon (a battery-level flag) and the M1857 12-pounder Napoleon, the standard light-artillery field piece of the 2nd Missouri Light Artillery and the most common cannon in the Union army.

Regimental history

The 2nd Missouri Light Artillery — formation and structure

The 2nd Regiment, Missouri Light Artillery was a U.S. Volunteer regiment organized at St. Louis in late 1861 and early 1862. The regiment was made up of twelve lettered light-artillery batteries (A through M, skipping J) — each battery operating semi-independently under its own captain, but all administratively part of the regiment. Charles served in Battery B, which was raised in fall 1861 and mustered in by early 1862. Charles's future father-in-law, Georg Kreichelt, had served an early-war 3-month enlistment in Battery B before Charles arrived (the standard 3-month volunteer term Lincoln called for in 1861). When Charles himself enlisted on 2 December 1862, he was joining the same battery his future father-in-law had served in — and his future brother-in-law August Kreichelt served Battery B as Charles's contemporary comrade. The Kreichelt-Battery B link is therefore two-generational (father then son, with Charles bridging into the family by marriage), and is one of the cleanest single Civil War threads in Charles's life — see The Kreichelt & Herweck Connection for the full analysis.

Each light-artillery battery operated a section of six guns — typically a mix of 12-pounder Napoleon smoothbores and 3-inch ordnance rifles — manned by ~150 enlisted men plus officers. Charles's enlistment card specifically notes he "Joined the right section," meaning the right half of the battery's six-gun complement. Light artillery in the Civil War was distinguished from heavy artillery by its mobility: the guns were horse-drawn and intended for field deployment, as opposed to the static fortress / coastal-defense guns of heavy-artillery regiments.

Battery B's 1862–1865 service trajectory

Per the National Park Service Soldiers and Sailors System battle-units page (UMO0002RAL) and the regiment's Wikipedia / Dyer's Compendium entries, Battery B's documented service trajectory ran:

  • Late 1861 – 1862: Organized at St. Louis. Initial garrison and training in the District of St. Louis.
  • 1862: Service with the Army of the Mississippi during John Pope's New Madrid / Island No. 10 campaign (the Union capture of New Madrid, March 1862, before Charles enlisted).
  • Spring 1863 onward: Garrisoned at New Madrid, Missouri on the Mississippi River — the strategic Mississippi River fortification opposite the Tennessee shore. Charles served two distinct tours here: Sept – Nov 1863 with the battery (source of hearing loss + rheumatism per Derr/Meyer), and 24 April 1864 – 27 March 1865 detached (~11 months).
  • April 1864 – 19 November 1864: Battery rotated through Cape Girardeau → Springfield → Sedalia → Rolla, Mo. — the southwest Missouri demobilization circuit — while Charles served his later tours individually under departmental special orders.
  • 19 November 1864 – 1 June 1865: Garrison duty at Franklin, Missouri (per NPS verbatim). Approximately 60 miles east of Rolla. Charles was NOT with the battery during most of this period — he was on detached service at Rolla / Fort Wyman from 27 March 1865 (G.O. No. 82) and then at Department HQ St. Louis from 1 July 1865 (S.O. No. 159).
  • 1 June 1865 onward: Battery ordered to St. Louis and re-equipped as cavalry (an unusual repurposing for a light-artillery unit), then moved to Omaha, Nebraska (11–20 June 1865) and marched on the Powder River Expedition (Wyoming / Montana / Dakota Territory) from 11 July to 20 September 1865 — Indian-war operations against Lakota and Cheyenne forces. Charles did not go on the Powder River Expedition; his individual special order (G.O. No. 82) had detached him to Rolla months before the battery was re-equipped as cavalry, and he served his last six months at Dept HQ St. Louis.
  • August – November 1865: Battery muster-out paperwork closed at St. Louis on 30 August 1865, with individual discharges through December. Charles received his honorable discharge on 20 December 1865.

Why a "Light" Artillery battery was at fortress / garrison duty

Despite the "light" designation (which referred to the gun caliber and the horse-drawn mobility), Battery B spent much of its 1863–1865 service in garrison and fortress roles — at New Madrid (river fortification), the St. Louis garrison forts, Franklin, and (briefly) Rolla. This was a common pattern for Missouri-based light-artillery units in the trans-Mississippi: with the main field armies operating east of the Mississippi (Sherman's Atlanta and Carolinas campaigns, Grant's Petersburg / Appomattox campaign), light-artillery batteries in the western theater were often diverted to defend Union supply depots and counter Confederate guerrilla / cavalry incursions. Battery B's two New Madrid tours, the Franklin garrison, and the late-1864 Price's Raid administrative role at the Department of the Missouri all fit this pattern.

People Charles served with in Battery B

Charles's documented Battery B comrades — the men who appear as wedding witnesses, baptism sponsors, and pension affidavit affiants in his post-war records — were the human network the carded service record produced. The most consequential:

  • Georg Kreichelt — Charles's future father-in-law. Served an early-war 3-month enlistment in Battery B in 1861, before Charles enlisted in December 1862. When Charles eventually married Georg's daughter Auguste Kreichelt on 7 January 1866, he became the son-in-law of his own battery's earlier veteran — the deepest single Battery B–marriage thread in Charles's life. → The Kreichelt & Herweck Connection
  • August Kreichelt — Charles's future brother-in-law. Auguste's eldest brother (b. 1844 Clausthal, Hannover). Served Battery B as Charles's contemporary comrade-in-arms. Sponsored the 1872 baptism of his namesake niece Auguste Gonsen.
  • 1st Sgt. August Derr — pension witness; sworn affidavit (1891) attesting to Charles's hearing loss from cannonading at New Madrid.
  • Pvt. Christian Meyer — pension witness; sworn affidavit (1891) attesting to Charles's rheumatism from cold exposure at New Madrid.
  • Sgt. Adam Herweck — wedding witness at Charles's 1866 St. Marcus wedding; lifelong post-war friend.
  • Henry Stoll, Michael Nolan, and others — additional Battery B pension affiants and baptism sponsors.

The full roster of identified Battery B comrades, with cross-links to their individual people-profile pages, is documented at the People Charles Served With page.
