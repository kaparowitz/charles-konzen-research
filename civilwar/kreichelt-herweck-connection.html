⚜ The Kreichelt & Herweck Connection

Battery B at the wedding · 7 January 1866 · St. Marcus Evangelical Church, St. Louis
CIVIL WAR · KEY STORYThe single most consequential fact about Charles's service

★ At a Glance

The single most consequential fact about Charles's Civil War service is that he served alongside the family he would later marry into, and that one of his Battery B comrades stood as a formal witness at the wedding. The 7 January 1866 St. Marcus marriage register entry names four wedding witnesses — and Battery B accounts for at least three people in the wedding party. The civilian-life social network that sustained Charles for the rest of his life was forged in a Missouri artillery battery during the war.

Date7 January 1866 · 18 days after Charles's discharge
Battery B at the wedding3+ veterans

The wedding-party roll call

  • Charles Gannsen himself — enlisted 2 Dec 1862; served as Sergeant 1863–65, discharged 20 Dec 1865 as a Private.
  • Adam Herweck — fellow Battery B veteran. Born 16 July 1831 in Lampertheim, Hessen, Germany (parents Peter Herweck × Catharina Margaretha Schollmeier). His NARA M405 Compiled Service Record places him as a Sergeant on detached recruiting service at St. Louis in November–December 1862 — the exact time and place of Charles Gannsen's 2 December 1862 enlistment. The Captain commanding Battery B wrote in a 9 December 1862 letter from New Madrid: "Adam Herweck, of my Company, [is on] recruiting service at St. Louis" — documentary confirmation that Adam recruited Charles into Co. B. Named first in the witness column of the 7 Jan 1866 St. Marcus register entry — the wedding's senior groomsman. Single at Charles's wedding (age 34); married Marguerite Wamsganz 21 July 1868 in St. Louis; relocated to Edwardsville, Illinois between May 1873 and 1880 and died there 11 May 1897.
  • Christian Meyer — fellow Battery B private. Not at the wedding but provided the 1891 pension-claim affidavit attesting Charles's loss of hearing and rheumatism from cannonading and exposure at New Madrid in fall 1863. Lived at 1803 Allen Avenue, St. Louis at swearing. → See Pension Page 22.
  • August Derr — 1st Sergeant of Battery B — Charles's direct NCO during the 1863 New Madrid station. Provided two separate 1891 affidavits corroborating Charles's hernia, hearing loss, and rheumatism. → See Pension Pages 24 + 26.
  • Georg Kreichelt — Charles's future father-in-law. Walks his daughter Auguste down the aisle on 7 January 1866 — eighteen days after Charles's discharge.
  • August Kreichelt — Auguste's brother. Decades later (1875) sponsors at the baptism of Charles's son Erwin Carl Konsen, the surviving Hesse-spelling of the family name.

The register entry itself

The St. Marcus marriage register entry of 7 January 1866 reads "Carl Gonnson, aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen, und Clausthal, Königr. Hannover" — and the witness column directly to its right reads Adam Herweck · Philipp Weber · Anna Goetz · Sophie Lehm · Cop: G.W. Wall. The civilian-life social network that sustains Charles for the next thirty-eight years was forged in a Missouri artillery battery during the war.

Why this matters

Three things at once. Adam Herweck's role at the wedding documents three things simultaneously: (1) the Battery-B-as-marriage-network thesis is real — Charles, Georg Kreichelt, August Kreichelt, and Adam Herweck were all in Co. B and all at the wedding; (2) Charles's social bonds reached beyond the Hesse-Kassel community to embrace other Hessians and Hannoverians (the bride's family) — Adam from Hesse-Darmstadt, Georg/Auguste/August from Hannover, Charles from Hesse-Kassel — making St. Marcus a genuinely pan-German-immigrant congregation rather than a Hessian enclave; and (3) the recruiter role Adam played in St. Louis in November–December 1862 plausibly provided the actual mechanism by which the German-speaking Charles entered Battery B in the first place.

The bond between Adam and Charles is best characterized as deep military-civic friendship — wedding-witness intimate but explicitly stopping short of sacramental-godparent kinship.
