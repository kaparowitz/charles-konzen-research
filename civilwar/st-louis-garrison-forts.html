St. Louis Garrison Forts

Primary station · 2 Dec 1862 – 20 Dec 1865 · the bulk of Charles's three-year enlistment
CIVIL WARMultiple sub-postings inside St. Louis

★ At a Glance

St. Louis was Charles's primary station throughout his three-year Civil War enlistment. He enlisted at St. Louis on 2 December 1862 (age recorded as 23 — about four years younger than the canonical 26 January 1835 birth implies, a common self-reported enrolment-age discrepancy) and was discharged here on 20 December 1865. Most of his service ran inside the St. Louis garrison-forts system that ringed the city, with the battery rotating between specific forts and providing artillery support to the Department of the Missouri. Punctuated by three out-of-city detached postings (Smallpox Hospital briefly in early 1863; New Madrid in fall 1863 and again in spring 1864; Fort Wyman / Rolla March – November 1865), but the through-line was St. Louis.

The garrison forts and the Union's St. Louis military complex

The "St. Louis garrison" wasn't a single fort but a system: ten numbered earthwork forts (Fort No. 1 through No. 10) ringing the western approaches to the city, plus the federal St. Louis Arsenal on the riverfront and the post at Jefferson Barracks seven miles south. Battery B rotated through this network on garrison duty. No photographs of Fort No. 1–10 are known to survive — the period visual record is dominated by Frank Leslie's and other illustrated newspapers, plus the surviving arsenal and barracks structures.

Historical context

St. Louis as the Union's "second logistical capital"

St. Louis in 1862-1865 was the second-largest Union supply depot after Washington, with a federal arsenal, ironclad shipyards on the Mississippi (James B. Eads's City-class gunboats were built upriver at Carondelet), and major hospital infrastructure. The city was under semi-martial law from August 1861 through the war's end, with provost marshal patrols, loyalty oaths, and German-immigrant Home Guard units forming the backbone of the Unionist majority. Charles's Battery B was one of three Missouri Light Artillery regiments responsible for fortress and garrison defense of this critical infrastructure.

The forts ringing the city

By 1864 St. Louis was protected by a ring of ten earthwork fortifications — Fort No. 1 through Fort No. 10 — built between 1861 and 1862 to defend the city against the cavalry raids and irregular incursions that were the dominant Confederate threat to Missouri throughout the war. The forts ringed the city's western approaches in a rough arc; the largest were Forts No. 4 (north of the city) and No. 7 (west). Battery B rotated through the forts on garrison duty; specific assignments for individual months are not preserved in Charles's carded record. The earthworks at Bellefontaine north of the city, the riverfront artillery batteries downtown, and the post at Jefferson Barracks south of the city all operated alongside the numbered fort system.

Three commanders of the Department

During Charles's enlistment three different major generals commanded the Department of the Missouri from St. Louis: Samuel R. Curtis (Sept 1862 – May 1863), who personally signed the Special Order detaching Charles to the Smallpox Hospital in February 1863; John M. Schofield (May 1863 – Jan 1864); and William S. Rosecrans (Jan 1864 – Dec 1864), under whom the Department absorbed Price's Raid. Grenville M. Dodge took over December 1864 – February 1866, with John Pope personally signing Charles's final Special Order (S.O. No. 92, 4 November 1865) that relieved him from the Wyman / Rolla detachment. The Department's headquarters building stood at the corner of 9th and Olive Streets in downtown St. Louis — about 1.5 miles from the South 7th Street tenement Charles would move into after his discharge.

Why St. Louis kept Charles when other Battery B men went west

The carded record's pattern — Charles repeatedly recalled to St. Louis, repeatedly detached on individual special orders, never re-equipped as cavalry for the battery's June 1865 westward deployment to the Powder River Expedition — suggests Charles was on a "Department-utility" track rather than a "field-battery" track from early 1863 onward. The detached-instructor / hospital / clerical assignments may have reflected his German-English bilingual literacy (a rare combination among artillery enlisted men), the accumulated disabilities (right-hernia / rheumatism / hearing-loss cluster traced by the pension comrade affidavits to the New Madrid period) that became his pension claim, or simply administrative convenience — a steady detached-duty soldier in the District is more useful than one who has to be re-equipped and moved with his unit.

What the muster cards say — verbatim

"Joined the right section. Enrolled Dec 2, 1862, St. Louis Mo. for 3 yrs. Mustered in Dec 2, 1862 St. Louis Mo." — NARA PG 06 Card B (Nov–Dec 1862 Battery Muster Roll, the initial muster-in)
"Battery Muster-out Roll, dated St. Louis Mo., Aug. 30, 1865. Mustered out to date Dec. 20, 1865." — Fold3 PG 02 / NARA PG 12 Card C (the muster-out roll). The "Dec. 20, 1865" reading is visible identically on both packets; an earlier transcription that read this as "Dec. 7" was a transcription error and has been corrected.

Research notes

The St. Louis garrison record is the most thoroughly documented portion of Charles's service — both the Fold3 and NARA carded packets cover it densely, and the pension file confirms the enlistment and discharge dates independently. Two open questions remain:

  1. Which specific fort (or forts) in the No. 1 – No. 10 ring did Battery B garrison? The carded muster cards record Battery B at "the forts about St. Louis" without specifying which numbered fort by month. The 1864 ordnance-stoppage debits on Charles's pay (NARA PG 10 Cards A–C) might indicate which ordnance was being drawn at which fort — but reconstructing that mapping requires the post returns of each fort, available via NARA RG 393 (paid order).
  2. What were the late-1864 ordnance-stoppage debits for? The small-dollar ordnance-stoppage debits visible on NARA PG 10 Cards A–C across July–December 1864 (the largest is $2.52) deduct pay for ordnance items Charles had drawn but not returned. Whether these correspond to specific equipment lost or damaged at a specific fort is not preserved on the cards. (Earlier site documentation framed this period as a disputed hospitalization; that framing has been retracted — Charles was NOT hospitalized in late 1864, and the canonical reading of NARA PG 10 Card C is PRESENT.)
