Where Charles Served — Places, Postings & Historical Context

Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery · 2 December 1862 – 20 December 1865
CIVIL WAR · 3 YEARS~100 miles of the St. Louis levee

★ At a Glance

Charles spent his entire three-year enlistment within roughly 100 miles of the St. Louis levee. He never marched south to Vicksburg, never went east to fight at Chattanooga, and was already discharged before his battery rode west to the Powder River Expedition. The carded muster rolls (Fold3 / NARA M405, 25 pages) put him on detached service for most of 1863-1864 — at the St. Louis smallpox quarantine, at the Department of the Missouri's adjutant office, and inside St. Louis-area garrison forts. The hypothesis: Charles's literacy and German-English bilingual capability made him too valuable in the city to send to the field.

Total stations3 St. Louis postings + New Madrid (1 sept-nov 1863)
Field actionNew Madrid garrison only · no campaign battles

Individual location pages

Each major posting in Charles's three-year enlistment has its own dedicated page with overview, detailed service notes, historical context, verbatim muster-card quotes, and (where applicable) a research log:

Primary station · 1862–1865

St. Louis Garrison Forts

Charles's enlistment, the bulk of his service, and his discharge — 2 Dec 1862 through 20 Dec 1865, with the battery rotating through the ten earthwork forts ringing the city.
Detached · 20 Feb – Apr 1863

St. Louis Smallpox Hospital

First detached posting after enlistment. ~10 weeks of hazardous nursing / quarantine duty by Special Order of Maj. Gen. Samuel R. Curtis.
Field posting (twice)

New Madrid, Missouri

Two tours at the strategic Mississippi River fort: Sept–Nov 1863 (with battery) and April–~July 1864 (detached). The source of all three pension disabilities he later claimed.
Detached drill instructor · 1865

Fort Wyman, Rolla

Late-war detached service, 27 Mar – 1 Jul 1865 (~3 months). The fort named in Charles's pension claim's heavy-lifting / right-side injury narrative — corroborated May 2026 by the NARA mail-order packet.

Historical context

The Department of the Missouri

The Department of the Missouri was a Union Army administrative jurisdiction headquartered at St. Louis. It encompassed Missouri, Arkansas, Kansas, the Indian Territory, and parts of Iowa and Illinois. During Charles's enlistment three different major generals commanded it from St. Louis: Samuel R. Curtis (Sept 1862 – May 1863), who issued the smallpox-hospital order that detached Charles in early 1863; John M. Schofield (May 1863 – Jan 1864); and William S. Rosecrans (Jan 1864 – Dec 1864). After the Civil War wound down, John Pope commanded the Department by November 1865 and personally signed the Special Order (S.O. No. 92) that relieved Charles from his Rolla detachment. The Department's headquarters building was on the corner of 9th and Olive streets in downtown St. Louis — about 1.5 miles from the South 7th Street tenement Charles would move into after his discharge.

St. Louis as a Union "logistical capital"

St. Louis in 1862-1865 was the second-largest Union supply depot after Washington, with a federal arsenal, ironclad shipyards on the Mississippi (Eads's City-class gunboats were built upriver at Carondelet), and major hospital infrastructure. It was a city under semi-martial law from August 1861 through the war's end, with provost marshal patrols, loyalty oaths, and German-immigrant Home Guard units forming the backbone of the Unionist majority. Charles's Battery B was one of three Missouri Light Artillery regiments responsible for fortress and garrison defense of this critical infrastructure. The forts ringing the city — Fort No. 1 through Fort No. 10 — included earthworks at Bellefontaine, Jefferson Barracks south of the city, and the riverfront artillery positions Charles's battery rotated through.

Why Charles stayed inside Missouri

Charles's three years of service were entirely within Missouri — the St. Louis garrison, two tours at New Madrid (Sept–Nov 1863 with battery + 24 April 1864 – 27 March 1865 detached, ~11 months), the late-war detachment to Fort Wyman, Rolla (27 March – 1 July 1865, ~3 months), and a final ~4-month detail at Department HQ St. Louis (July – November 1865). He never marched east to Vicksburg or Chattanooga, never went south to Mobile or to the Carolinas with Sherman, and was already discharged before his battery rode west to the Powder River Expedition. Two working hypotheses explain the in-Missouri pattern:

  1. The detached-instructor track. Once Charles was detached to the smallpox hospital by Special Order in February 1863, he stayed on the "detached" track for much of his service. Detached personnel were rotated through specific in-jurisdiction assignments by departmental Special Order rather than moving with their parent battery — explaining the second New Madrid tour (April 1864 – March 1865), the late-war Rolla / Fort Wyman drill-instructor detail (March – July 1865), the Department HQ St. Louis role (July – November 1865), and his absence from the battery's June 1865 westward deployment to the Powder River Expedition.
  2. Accumulated disabilities. The right-hernia / rheumatism / hearing-loss conditions documented in the pension file all originated during Charles's service. The comrade affidavits trace the hearing loss and rheumatism to the fall 1863 New Madrid tour (heavy cannonading, cold exposure); Charles's own pension declaration locates the heavy-lifting / right-hernia injury at Fort Wyman, Rolla — consistent with the spring 1865 Rolla detachment. By 1865 these accumulated disabilities could have made him unfit for re-equipping with the battery as cavalry, ensuring he stayed on detached duty in Missouri rather than being sent west on the Powder River Expedition.
