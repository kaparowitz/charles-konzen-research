The "Freundburg, Kurhessen" & "L." Mysteries

Two clerk's-pen oddities on the 1862 enlistment card that anchor the entire German-side search
CIVIL WAR · NAMINGBoth run back to a German-language original

★ At a Glance

The 1862 Civil War enlistment card records Charles's birthplace as "Freundburg, Kurhessen" and his name as "L. Charles Gannsen". Neither piece of text appears anywhere else in his life. The current working interpretation is that both are clerk's-pen distortions of a German-language original: "Freundburg" = phonetic guess at Trendelburg · "L." = first letter of his German baptismal name (likely Ludwig or Lorenz) later abandoned in favor of the more American "Charles." Together they concentrate the entire German-side candidate-family search inside the Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis, looking for a boy whose first name begins with L.

The "Freundburg, Kurhessen" mystery

The 1862 enlistment card records Charles's birthplace as "Freundburg, Kurhessen." No place by that name has ever existed in 19th-century Hesse. The 2020 AncestryProGenealogists journal initially proposed reading it as Freudenberg, a small place near Schierstein on the Rhine — but the Schierstein parish baptism index for the 1830s contained no candidate matching Charles, and that lead was eliminated.

The current working interpretation reads "Freundburg" as a Civil-War clerk's phonetic mishearing of Trendelburg, the parish town immediately north of Deisel inside the Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis.

★ Peter Schräder's "Trendel → Freund" reading (Feb 2026 emails)

The leading interpretation comes from a sequence of Peter Schräder's emails in February 2026. Peter — a native German speaker working from Hessen — proposed that the spoken-German fragment Charles gave the recruiting clerk was misheard at the level of the first syllable:

  • "Trendel" — the first half of Trendelburg — sounds in spoken German very close to "Freund" (German for friend). The initial Tr- consonant cluster, said quickly, can flatten toward Fr- in the ear of an English-speaking clerk who has no map of Hessian place-names; the -endel middle compresses toward -eund; and the same -burg suffix (common in German place-names) survives intact.
  • So the clerk hears something like "Frrrrndelburg" from Charles's mouth and transcribes it as a familiar German word he knows — Freund — plus the standard German place-suffix -burg. Result: Freundburg. The mishearing is a substitution of a meaningful German word for a meaningless-to-the-clerk place-name fragment.
  • Peter's 26 Feb 2026 email formalized this as the project's working "phonetic constraint": Charles was answering an English-speaking clerk in German, and any clerk transcription of a German place-name needs to be read as a phonetic guess, not a spelling.

Two independent records that triangulate to the same place

  • The 1866 St. Marcus marriage register — written four years later by a German-speaking Lutheran pastor — records Charles's origin as "aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen". Deisse = Deisel, the village immediately south of Trendelburg.
  • "Kurhessen" is consistent across both records (the 1866 register spells it "Churhessen") — pointing at the same political unit, the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel, which Prussia would dissolve in summer 1866.
  • Triangulation. The English-clerk hears Trendel-burg → writes "Freundburg." The German-pastor hears Deisel → writes "Deisse." Both are mishearings of distinct adjacent place-names in the same parish complex. Charles answered consistently across the two interviews; only the clerks differed in spelling-fidelity.

Why this matters

The Trendelburg / Deisel reading concentrates the entire downstream candidate-family search inside the Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis. From an initial pool of roughly 21 surface-trawl leads, the 13 formally documented candidate families investigated through the Schräder Correspondence all live within a 10-mile radius of Trendelburg. Without Peter's Feb-2026 "Trendel→Freund" reading, "Freundburg, Kurhessen" remains a dead end. With it, the geographic search radius collapses from "all of Hesse" to a specific half-dozen parishes around the Diemel valley — and the Charles ↔ Trendelburg parish anchor becomes the single most productive clue in the German-side investigation.

The "L." initial mystery

The enlistment card records Charles as "L. Charles Gannsen" — with an initial L. that does not appear in any other record of his life. The 1866 marriage register has him as Carl Gonnson; the 1875 baptism of his son Erwin Carl as Konsen; the 1880 census, 1890 Veterans Schedule, 1898 pension declaration, 1900 census, and 1903 death certificate all use just Charles or Carl.

The leading reading of the "L." is that it stands for Charles's actual German first name — most likely Ludwig or Lorenz — which he abandoned in favour of the more American "Charles." German emigrants of the 1850s commonly compressed compound names: Ludwig Karl → L. Charles; Lorenz Carl → L. Carl. The choice to recover the suppressed first letter only on the formal enlistment paperwork — never afterwards — is itself a fingerprint of the original German baptismal name.

This makes "Ludwig Carl Konsen" or "Lorenz Carl Konsen" the working hypothesis for Charles's German baptismal name. The candidate search began as an initial pool of ~21 leads — surface-trawled from Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis baptisms 1834-36 for any boy with first names beginning L — narrowed to the 13 formally documented candidate families eliminated one by one. → See the candidate-family table

Other naming peculiarities

  • Gannsen / Gonnson / Konsen. The surname mutates across records: enlistment 1862 = Gannsen; marriage 1866 = Gonnson; son's baptism 1875 = Konsen. Peter's Toeppe → Deppe methodology (worked example, 30 Dec 2025) treats these as scribe-and-dialect mutations of the same surname. → See the full methodology
  • "Deisse" for Deisel. The pastor at St. Marcus wrote the village name as Deisse — a fricative-sigma rendering of Deisel under spoken-German pronunciation by a German-Evangelical clergyman.
  • "Carl" vs "Charles." Charles signed his 1898 pension letter "Charles Gannson," but his children's baptism records have him as Carl. He used both names interchangeably for the rest of his life.

Why these mysteries matter. Both the "Freundburg" and "L." entries are clerk-mediated translations of an oral German-language original. They are bad data on the surface — neither name appears anywhere else — but excellent data once you accept that the clerk was hearing German and writing English. On the project's working decodings, they are the strongest documentary pointers to: (1) a German baptismal name beginning with L; (2) a birthplace most likely in the Trendelburg / Deisel parish complex, Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis — a phonetic inference, not a record-confirmed origin; and (3) that he answered in German at every formal moment of his civilian life. These are the constraints the entire German-side investigation operates under.
