Negative-results audit

Every Hessian parish register the project has searched and every candidate family the project has eliminated — with date of search, scope, what was looked for, and the specific evidence that closed the lead. The auditable backbone of the Genealogical-Proof-Standard "reasonably exhaustive research" claim.

A finding is only as strong as the searches that did not produce it. This page lists each negative result the project has logged: which parish or candidate family was investigated, when, what scope was covered, what was searched for, and what evidence eliminated the candidate. Where a result is partial (some volumes searched, others outstanding) the gap is named. Where a result is open, it is logged on Open Questions with a "what would resolve this" pointer.

Standing statement on the proof standard. The central parentage question — who were Charles's parents, and where exactly was he baptised? — does not yet meet the Genealogical Proof Standard. The research below is extensive but not yet exhaustive: principal registers remain unsearched (the rows marked open below — e.g. pre-1822 Schöneberg, not yet digitised — are unsearched ground, not eliminations; Heisebeck post-1831 baptisms have since been digitised and read, surfacing an unconfirmed candidate), the Marburg emigration and conscription records have not been pulled, and DNA testing — though completed (Y-DNA and autosomal) — has not yet produced a confirming match to a documented Konze line. The parish-origin and surname conclusions are therefore held Working, and the baptism itself Open, rather than Confirmed. A negative row means a register was searched and Charles was not in it; a partial row means some volumes were covered and others remain; an open row means the register has not yet been accessible at all.

How "negative" is established

A negative result on this site does not mean "we couldn't find it" — it means the project completed a documented, scoped search and the candidate was not present. Per the project's source-rating taxonomy, each negative is recorded with:

  • Date of search — anchors the result to the state of the source at that time (digitisation status changes; archive holdings expand)
  • Scope — exactly which volumes, years, surname spellings, and records were checked
  • Tools used — index search, image-by-image walk, HTR, Kurrent transcription, Peter Schräder's catalogue, etc.
  • Closing evidence — for an elimination, the specific record that contradicts the candidate (wrong birth year, wrong parents, wrong destination); for a negative, the absence of any match across the scoped corpus
  • What would reopen it — newly digitised material, a new candidate surfacing, an HTR re-read that changes a previous reading

Where a negative is partial (some volumes covered, others outstanding), the gap is named explicitly so a future researcher knows what work remains. The four primary-source gaps still open at the time of writing — Charles's German baptismal entry, the 1859 trans-Atlantic passenger manifest, the Hessian Auswanderer-Beleg, the 1855 Stammrolle entry — are catalogued on Open Questions with priority rankings.
