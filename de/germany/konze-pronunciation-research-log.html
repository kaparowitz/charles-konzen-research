Konze in the Mouth — Dialect & Pronunciation Research Log

How a North Hessian [ˈkɔ̞ntsn̩] from Deisel became nine American spellings · compiled 28 June 2026 · phonetics-focused
METHOD · PHONETIC RECONSTRUCTION

This page asks one narrow, testable question: if Charles’s German surname really was Konze (the form documented in the Deisel and Trendelburg church books), and he spoke it in the North Hessian (Nordhessisch) dialect of Deisel im Amt Trendelburg before emigrating in the 1850s — what did the name sound like, and does that sound predict the spellings American clerks actually wrote down? It is a reconstruction from documented dialect sound-laws, not a recovered recording; no nineteenth-century Deiseler can be heard today. The value of the exercise is that the prediction can be checked against the nine independently-attested American renderings on the Konzen puzzle — and it matches.

Headline. A single Deisel-dialect pronunciation of Konzen — roughly [ˈɡ̊ɔ̞nsn̩], with a half-voiced initial, an open back vowel, the German z-affricate already half-collapsed to [s], and a syllabic -n ending — generates the entire attested spread (Gonnson, Gansen, Gonsen, Konsen, Gonson, Jansen, Konzen, Gannsen, Conson) by turning only three or four phonetic dials. The strongest, best-evidenced effects are the K ↔ G initial and the z → s collapse; the vowel and the exact suffix-spelling are looser and clerk-dependent.

1 · Where Deisel sits on the dialect map

Deisel (51.568 N, 9.418 E; Kreis Hofgeismar / Landkreis Kassel, in the Diemel valley just above Trendelburg) lies on the northern frontier of Nordhessisch, a West Central German (westmitteldeutsch) dialect centred on Kassel. This placement is not incidental to the surname problem — it is the whole key to it. Per the standard description, the dialect boundary between Nordhessisch and Low German (Niederdeutsch) runs “west and north along a line Göttingen – Hofgeismar – Waldeck.” Deisel sits essentially on that frontier, north of the Kreis town Hofgeismar, in the transition zone where High German speech shades into Low German.

Two consequences follow, and they pull in opposite directions — which is exactly why the name was so unstable in writing:

Feature of Deisel’s positionWhat it does to Konze
South of the Benrath line (the maken/machen isogloss) — i.e. genuinely High German, having undergone the Second Sound Shift.The <z> in Konze is a real affricate /ts/ (the High German reflex of Germanic *t), not the Low German plain /t/ that would give a Kont(e)-type name. So the name genuinely carried a ts-sound to be misheard.
On the very edge of the Low German zone, with a documented Low German lexical substrate (e.g. hä ‘he’, cf. English he, Danish han).The northern dialect carries the heaviest consonant lenition (next section) and the retained final -n — the two features that most deform the name toward the American forms.

In short: Deisel speech is High German enough to have given Konze a true ts-affricate, but northern enough to soften the initial consonant and to keep a syllabic -n. The American spellings sit precisely in the gap this opens.

2 · Baseline — how “Konze” sounds before the dialect touches it

Standard German Konze is [ˈkɔntsə]. Segment by segment, this is the raw material every later distortion works on:

LetterStandard valueDescriptionWhere it goes

o/ɔ/open-mid back rounded vowel→ wanders between English o and a

Notice already that only the medial n is phonetically “safe.” That is why the consonantal skeleton …n·s·n is the one constant across all nine American spellings, while the initial, the vowel, and the affricate are all in play.

3 · The dialect overlay — four sound-laws that reshape the name

Four well-documented features of North Hessian speech act on the baseline. The badges mark how much evidentiary weight each carries.

(a) Initial K → G — binnendeutsche Konsonantenschwächungstrong

North Hessian is characterised by the inner-German consonant weakening: the fortis/lenis (“hard/soft”) contrast is neutralised, so voiceless stops lenite toward their voiced partners. The textbook minimal pair is Keil and geil becoming homophones — precisely the K↔G merger. Spoken in Deisel, the initial of Konze is therefore not a crisp English-style K but a half-voiced [ɡ̊] sitting on the boundary between K and G. The name was ambiguous on its first sound in the dialect itself, before any American ever heard it. → This is the single fact that explains why the same man is recorded under both Kon-/Con- and Gon-/Gan- spellings.

Hear the merger — one synthesised minimal pair, identical but for the initial: [ˈkɔntsə]  vs  [ˈɡɔntsə]

(b) The English ear doubles the effect — aspiration / VOTstrong

A second, independent force pushes the same direction. English distinguishes word-initial k from g mainly by aspiration (voice-onset time): English k comes with a puff of air, English g does not. Continental German fortis stops in this dialect are un-aspirated. An un-aspirated, half-voiced [ɡ̊] falls on the English side of the g boundary — the same reason German Tante can sound like “Dante” to an English speaker. So even a clerk hearing a perfectly standard initial would tend to write G. Dialect lenition (a) and English re-perception (b) are two separate mechanisms both delivering K → G — which is why the G-forms (Gonnson, Gansen, Gonsen, Gonson, Gannsen) are, if anything, more numerous than the K-forms.

(c) The affricate z /ts/ → [s]strong

German <z> is a voiceless alveolar affricate [ts] — the cluster at the end of English cats. English has this sound word-finally but not after n inside a name, and English listeners reliably reduce the t-onset, capturing only the [s]. After the nasal, [nts] simplifies to [ns]. So Konze [ˈkɔntsə] is heard and spelled …nsen — the s in Konsen / Gonsen / Gansen / Conson is the English capture of the Hessian tz. The only American record to preserve the full affricate in writing is the 1890 St. Marcus death entry, Konzen with a z — the dialect’s own historical spelling, surfacing once.

Hear the affricate thin to s — the same name, tz then s: [ˈkɔntsə]  vs  [ˈkɔnsə] — an English ear keeps the second. (Cf. German Katze vs Kasse.)

(d) The ending — the North Hessian retained -n and the “Konzen” citation formmoderate–strong

Why does every American spelling end in -en / -on, never bare -e? Two converging facts. First, an immediately audible hallmark of Nordhessisch is the consistent retention of final -n where southern Hessian and the standard drop it (North Hessian Meh ginn vs. South Hessian miä gehe, standard wir gehen). The northern dialect simply likes a final -n. Second, the everyday spoken citation form of the surname is the inflected Konzen — the form the church books themselves record (the three Deisel mothers entered as “geb. Konzen” = née Konze; cf. the “house-name” oblique bei Konzen, ‘at the Konzes’’). What a Deiseler would actually say is therefore [ˈkɔntsn̩] — Konzen, with a syllabic -n — not a clean Konze. The American -en is not an English addition; it is the German spoken form, faithfully captured. But that -en has a deeper history than the dialect alone — it is the fossil of an older surname-inflection system (feminine -in and weak oblique -en) that had largely collapsed by Charles’s lifetime: see the excursus below.

(e) The vowel — /ɔ/ ↔ [a]weaker / clerk-dependent

The open-mid back rounded /ɔ/ is the one nucleus an English transcriber can place several ways. Written o it gives Gonnson / Gonsen / Gonson / Konsen / Konzen; lowered and unrounded toward [a] it gives a — Gansen / Gannsen / Jansen. This swing is real but soft: it depends on the individual scribe’s ear and orthographic habit more than on a single regular sound-law, so it should not be over-interpreted. It is, however, enough to account for the o/a split with no further assumptions.

3½ · Excursus — Konze, Konzen, Kontzin: the morphology behind the -en

Law (d) treats the -en as the dialect’s spoken ending. That is true at Charles’s date, but it under-tells the story: in the older records the ending is not decorative — it is grammatical. German surnames, especially in the 16th–18th centuries, were inflected, and Konze sat inside three overlapping systems that all generate an -(e)n:

SystemWhat it did to Konze / KontzStatus by c.1835
Feminine marking (Movierung)A woman’s surname took a feminine suffix — standardly -in (Kontz → Kontzin / Contzin), and in the northern/Saxon-leaning belt the reduced -en (a regional form of the same -in). So a Kontze daughter or wife is written Kontzin, Contzen, Konzen. The three Deisel mothers recorded “geb. Konzen” (1828–29) carry exactly this feminine/oblique marking — “née Konze.”Receding; survives mainly in set genealogical phrases (“geb. …”).
Weak / oblique inflectionKonze is a weak masculine (ends in schwa), so its accusative, dative and possessive (“house-name”) forms take the superstable -en: bei Konzen, des Konzen Sohn. The everyday spoken citation form was thus Konzen, not the bare nominative.Still audible in speech (feeds law d).
Male -en / patronymic-clan formsIn the 17th–18th c. the -en also appears on men, as a weak/clan form — the Deisel combined register has a male “Conrad Andreas Kontzen” baptised c.1730/31 (218821 img 1440). The base name itself goes back to Kon(rad)/Kunz — the same root as the surname Kunze.Largely gone for men by 1800; the 19th-c. Deisel registers write the stable Konze (e.g. Joh. Heinrich Kontze, Philipp Konze).

The thread tying these together is standardization: as official, invariant legal-name registration spread through the German states in the late 18th and 19th centuries, the gender- and case-inflected variants (Kontzin, Kontzen) were abolished in favour of one fixed nominative. By the time Charles was born (1835), the Deisel/Trendelburg books had settled on the bare base Konze, with the old -en surviving chiefly fossilized in the feminine “geb. Konzen.” This is precisely the chronology you would reconstruct from the registers themselves — male -en/-en-clan forms in the 1700s, a stable Konze by the 1830s.

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR THE AMERICAN TRAIL

It means the American -en (Konsen, Konzen, Gansen, Gonnson…) is not a quirk an English clerk invented, nor purely the dialect’s phonetic habit — it is the last living trace of the German surname-inflection system, carried abroad in the form the family actually spoke (the oblique/feminine Konzen). The root name in 1835 was the stable nominative Konze; the spoken form that crossed the Atlantic was its inflected Konzen. Both readings — “the name was Konze” and “the family said Konzen” — are correct, and they are not in conflict.

4 · Putting it together — one pronunciation, nine spellings

Treat the four reshaping effects as independent dials. A single Deisel-dialect utterance of Konzen — somewhere in the range [ˈkɔ̞ntsn̩] – [ˈɡ̊an­sn̩] — is enough to generate every documented American spelling by setting:

  • Initial: K/C ↔ G  (law a + b)
  • Vowel: o ↔ a  (law e)
  • Medial: s ↔ z/tz  (law c — nearly always lands on s)
  • Ending: -en ↔ -on  (law d; -on = an English spelling of unstressed [ən], often nudged toward the familiar -son)

5 · The outliers — where the regular laws run out

WEAKEST LINK

Jansen (1881, Pastor Johannes Nollau). No regular sound-law turns an initial /k/ into /j/. The best account is scribal substitution / folk-etymology: a German-speaking Lutheran pastor, hearing a lenited initial and the skeleton …ansen, reached for the familiar Low German patronymic Jansen (‘John’s son’) — an everyday name in his world — rather than the rare Hessian Konzen. It is a recognition error at the parish font, not a phonetic derivation. (There is a quiet irony in it: the family would later settle on exactly the English equivalent, Johnson.) Treat Jansen as corroborating the …ansen body of the name while saying nothing reliable about its initial.

The -on spellings (Gonnson 1866, Gonson 1880, Conson 1896). These are not a different vowel in the final syllable — unstressed [ən] is simply spellable as either -en or -on in English, and -son is the magnetic, familiar pattern (Anderson, Johnson) that an English-trained hand drifts toward. They belong with the -en forms phonetically. Notably, the two records where Charles himself was the informant — his 1866 marriage (Gonnson) and his 1896 widower’s entry at his wife’s funeral (Conson) — both take -on, which may reflect how he himself anglicised the ending aloud.

6 · What this does — and does not — establish

SCOPE OF THE CLAIM

It establishes: the nine American spellings are not nine guesses at an unknown name — they are the predicted phonetic spread of one North Hessian pronunciation of Konzen, filtered through documented dialect lenition, the German z-affricate, the retained final -n, and ordinary English mis-hearing. The fit is close enough that the variation itself becomes evidence the underlying name was a Hessian Konze/Konzen-type form, spoken with a soft initial.

It does not establish: (1) that the root was Konze rather than a different Hessian surname — Gans / Gansen / Ganson yields the same n·s·n skeleton and an initial already on the K/G boundary, and remains a live alternative pool (kept formally separate from Konze, per Klaus Kunze, 28 Jun 2026). (2) The dialect argument explains the American spelling drift; it is not a claim that Konze and Gans were one family in Hesse. (3) The strong load is borne by the consonants (initial K↔G; z→s); the vowel o/a swing and the -en/-on spelling are looser and should not be leaned on as independent proof. These are the same caveats logged when the dialect mechanism was first verified with Kunze.
