Charles's Occupation Across the Records

From a Hessian farmer's son to a St. Louis watchman — every record that names a trade, 1862 to 1903, sourced and ranked by genealogical confidence, with the records that left the occupation field blank noted alongside.

Unlike Charles's birth date — which no living informant ever witnessed — his occupation is something he and his household knew firsthand. These records therefore carry primary information about the trade, and read as a coherent working life rather than a set of conflicts: a brief agricultural self-description on enlistment, then two decades of St. Louis wage work, ending as a watchman.

How these are rated

The confidence ratings follow the same evidence-analysis model used elsewhere on the site — the Source · Information · Evidence framework with the Genealogical Proof Standard (see methodology). One point distinguishes this page from the birth-date page: because Charles (or his household) reported these trades from firsthand knowledge, every entry below is primary, direct information about the occupation. They differ mainly in source type (a contemporaneous directory or census vs. a benefit-claim declaration) and in how well they corroborate one another.

Source

Is it an original contemporaneous record (directory, census, certificate) or a derivative (a later abstract or compiled card)?

Information

Before 1862 — the German trade, still unknown

No record yet states Charles's pre-emigration occupation. The two that would — the Hessian Stammrolle (military levy roll, which records a man's trade at age 20, ~1855) and the 1859 emigration manifest — have not been found. The 1862 "Farmer" is therefore the earliest occupation of record, and the only one pointing to the land; every later record describes urban wage work.

Reading the arc

Taken together the records trace a recognisable immigrant working life: a young man who called himself a farmer at enlistment, then — after the war and marriage — strings of St. Louis wage jobs, first on the street railway (conductor, then driver, 1871–76), then in a cotton press (1880), and finally the steady, lower-exertion role of watchman, which is the most consistent thread of all: it appears in the 1887–88 directory and again on the 1903 death certificate, sixteen years apart.

Two tensions worth flagging

Farmer vs. the rest. The 1862 "Farmer" is the lone agricultural descriptor and the term underlying the Freundburg / Deisel origin discussion; it may be a genuine pre-emigration trade or a nominal default on an enlistment card. The pre-1862 records that would settle it remain unfound.

Saloon-keeper vs. watchman. The pension's one-off "saloon-keeper" sits oddly against the watchman record on either side of it. It may reflect a short-lived venture, an interim trade, or the notary's gloss; held Moderate pending corroboration.
