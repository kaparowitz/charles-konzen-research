Theodore Gottlob ⚜

★ Jed Johnson's direct ancestor · Charles's next-door neighbor on DeKalb Street · 1890 pension declaration witness
JED'S 2ND GREAT-GRANDFATHER (or step) ★ CIVIL WAR VETERAN · 9TH ILLINOIS INFANTRY · SGT 2409 DeKalb · next door to Charles Westphalia → St. Louis

★ At a Glance

✓ ID Confirmed
PENSION DECLARATION CO-WITNESS · JED'S 2ND-GREAT-GRANDFATHER (OR STEP, VIA SECOND MARRIAGE)

Charles's next-door neighbor on DeKalb Street in St. Louis, co-witness on Charles's 1890 pension declaration, and — via second marriages — a direct ancestor of the site author.

Theodore Gottlob Sr. (FS KG17-D3H · b. 17 March 1842 Essentho, Kreis Büren, Westphalia, Prussia · d. 1900) is the two-ancestor convergence point of the entire Charles Research project: he was simultaneously (a) Charles's next-door neighbor on DeKalb Street (2409 vs. Charles's 2408) and the co-witness on Charles's 1890 Detailed Pension Declaration alongside John Heitzenberger, and (b) Jed Johnson's own 2nd great-grandfather via the Misch ⟷ Gottlob bridge. ★ Civil War service confirmed (3 May 2026): NHDVS Leavenworth admission record (1893) plus Veterans Record document two enlistments — Pvt., Co. K, 9th Illinois Infantry (Apr–Jul 1861, 90-day militia) and a 3-year re-enlistment as Sgt., Co. A, 9th Illinois Infantry (1861–1864, Vicksburg). Both terms come from the veteran-reported NHDVS admission summary; because admission records are self-reported, the second-term (Co. A, Sgt.) attribution is held ★ Working pending a regimental-roster cross-reference rather than asserted as settled.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Two implications, of different kinds. Documentary: Theodore's own Civil War service (now confirmed) means both witnesses on the 1890 Detailed Declaration were veterans — Heitzenberger's family also having Civil War-adjacent records. The pattern repeats across the pension file: most of Charles's identification witnesses are fellow veterans rather than civilian neighbors. Personal: Charles Gannsen and Theodore Gottlob were both direct ancestors of Jed Johnson, the project's compiler — meaning the 1890 DeKalb Street neighborhood was simultaneously the documentary site of Charles's pension claim and the geographic point of convergence for two of Jed's separate ancestral lines, fifteen years before they would intermarry through the next generation (Erwin Carl Konsen × Katherine Misch, 1895).

★ Two-ancestor convergence on DeKalb Street, 1890

Theodore Gottlob is not just a neighbor witness in Charles's pension file. Per the FamilySearch tree, he is also Jed Johnson's 2nd great-grandfather (or step-2nd-great-grandfather) — the husband of Jed's 2nd great-grandmother. This means Charles Gannsen and Theodore Gottlob were both direct ancestors of Jed, and they were literally next-door neighbors on DeKalb Street in 1890 when Theodore co-witnessed Charles's pension declaration. Two ancestral lines converging in the same St. Louis German-immigrant neighborhood, then merging four generations later in Jed.

The historical figure documented in Charles's 1890 pension file is Theodore Gottlob Sr. (1842–1900, FamilySearch KG17-D3H).

★ Civil War service confirmed (3 May 2026 late evening)

Theodore Gottlob Sr.'s FS tree (KG17-D3H) carries two attached sources that together establish his full Civil War record — previously cataloged only as the cryptic "Military Service 1893 Leavenworth Kansas" event:

Source 1 — National Homes for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers, 1866-1938

Admission record dated 1893, age 51, Western Branch NHDVS at Leavenworth, Kansas. Page 7047, Register no. 6000-7499, NARA M1749 (Historical Registers of National Homes for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers). The NHDVS network was the federal home-and-pension system established for Union veterans of the Civil War; admission required documented Union service.

Source 2 — Veterans Record, Leavenworth Muster Rolls 1885-1934

The original Leavenworth NHDVS admission record (FS image group 33S7-9PD6-P5Y, Image 549 of 788) records his complete military history in two enlistments:

1st Enlistment 19 April 1861, Alton, Illinois
Pvt., Co. K, 9th Illinois Infantry
90-day militia term · discharged 26 July 1861, Alton, IL · Cause: Expiration of Service
Note: Lincoln called for 75,000 volunteers on 15 April 1861. Theodore enlisted just FOUR DAYS later. He was 19 years old, brand-new to America (eight years post-immigration), and one of the first wave of Sumter-month volunteers. His unit, the 9th Illinois Infantry, was raised in Alton-Carlinville-Springfield to defend the Mississippi River corridor.
2nd Enlistment 25 July 1861, Cairo, Illinois (the day before his 90-day discharge)
Sergeant, Co. A, 9th Illinois Infantry
Full 3-year term · discharged 20 August 1864, Springfield, Illinois · also referenced: Vicksburg
Note: he reenlisted IMMEDIATELY at the end of his 90-day term, this time for 3 years, with a promotion from Private to Sergeant. The 9th Illinois Infantry served at the Battle of Fort Donelson (Feb 1862), the Battle of Shiloh (April 1862), the Vicksburg Campaign (May-July 1863), and the Atlanta Campaign (May-September 1864). Theodore served the entire 3-year term and was honorably discharged in summer 1864 at the expiration of his enlistment, weeks before the fall of Atlanta.
Pension $15.00/month — for rheumatism "since the war" (same chronic-rheumatism disability that Charles Gannsen claimed; Charles received $12/month in 1891)

★ Why this matters for the broader Charles Gannsen investigation

This finding adds Theodore Gottlob to the documented roster of Civil War veterans in Charles's St. Louis circle, and it changes how the 1890 Pension Declaration witness pairing should be read:

  1. Charles's pension declaration witnesses were both veterans. Theodore Gottlob (9th Illinois Infantry, Sgt., 1861-1864) co-witnessed alongside the still-uncertain Hitzelberger. The choice of Gottlob as a witness was not just about geographic proximity (next-door neighbor at 2409 DeKalb) — it was about shared Union veteran credibility. The Pension Bureau gave more weight to declarations witnessed by fellow Union veterans.
  2. The "shared rheumatism" detail is striking. Both Charles (artillery cannonading + exposure → rheumatism) and Theodore (3 years' infantry campaign service → rheumatism "since the war") had the same primary disability. The two men commiserating about chronic joint pain on adjacent DeKalb porches in the 1880s-90s is a documentary-warranted scene.
  3. Theodore is the SIXTH documented Civil War veteran in Charles's circle. He joins Adam Herweck (Battery B), August Derr (Battery B 1st Sgt.), Christian Meyer (Battery B), Henry Stoll (Battery L of 2nd MO Light Artillery), Michael Nolan (Co. F, 1st MO Light Artillery), and now Theodore Gottlob (Co. A, 9th Illinois Infantry). Of the six, Theodore is the only Illinois-regiment veteran — the others are all Missouri.
  4. The Pension Mutual-Aid Network theme should be updated to include Theodore as a documented veteran witness.

Per Jed's note: Theodore "may have lived close to a Hitzelberger (the clerk-recorded 'John Heitzenberger'; specific individual ID open) and Charles Jr." This dovetails with the south-side cluster of his grandson Frederick Charles Johnson (Bottler at Anheuser-Busch, 1905-1947, buried Mount Hope Cemetery Lemay) and the 2412 Arsenal Street Hitzelberger residence. Theodore's later residence (post-1893 Leavenworth NHDVS, pre-1900 St. Louis death) and his burial at St. Matthew Cemetery (south St. Louis County) place him in the south-side German-veteran corridor in his final years — geographically converging with the Anheuser-Busch / Gravois / Lemay cluster the next-generation Johnson family inhabited.

What Theodore Gottlob Sr. witnessed

Witness signature · Detailed Original Invalid Pension Declaration · 1890

Co-witnessed (with John Heitzenberger of 2412 Arsenal Street) Charles Gannsen's signature on the detailed Original Invalid Pension Declaration filed in 1890. This is the long-form claim document that includes Charles's physical description, narrative of injury, and the company-officer declaration that grounds the entire pension case. Theodore lived directly next door to Charles, at 2409 DeKalb Street. Charles himself was at 2408 DeKalb.

Source: Pension File F41-523680269E, Page 14 (declaration) + Page 15 (verso witness affidavit and filing label). Court Clerk: Philip H. Zeppen[felder?], Clerk of the Circuit Court, City of St. Louis.

Why Theodore Gottlob matters — twice

  1. To the Charles Gannsen pension claim (1890): Theodore Sr. was a documented identity-verification witness on Charles's 1890 pension declaration. Living one house number off (2409 DeKalb vs. Charles's 2408 DeKalb), Theodore was a credible attestor that the man signing the document was the same Charles Gannsen the neighborhood knew.
  2. To the Jed Johnson family tree: Theodore Sr. is the husband of Jed's 2nd great-grandmother — i.e., Jed's 2nd great-grandfather (or step-2nd-great-grandfather). This means Jed descends from both Charles Gannsen and Theodore Gottlob Sr. through different ancestral lines, and those two lines were physical neighbors in 1890 St. Louis. Four generations later they merged in Jed.

This two-fold significance is unique among the people in Charles's pension file. The other civilian witnesses (Henry Stoll, Michael Nolan, Henry Niemeier, John Hebbeler, John P. Thurbecker, John Heitzenberger) and even the Battery B affiants (Christian Meyer, August Derr) are not (so far as we know) direct ancestors of Jed. Theodore Gottlob Sr. is.

Open questions

  • Which Gottlob marriage carries Jed's descent line? Theodore Sr. had two wives: Pauline Franceska Pfeiffer (1848–1930, married 1867 Iowa, 2 children) and Christina Hahl (1846–1892, married 1883 Illinois, 3 children). Determining which wife is Jed's 2nd great-grandmother — and which child of theirs is Jed's great-grandparent — would identify the exact descent path. The relationship label "husband of my 2nd great-grandmother" applies to either wife configuration.
  • Is there a documented social or kinship link between Theodore Gottlob Sr. and Charles Gannsen, beyond physical proximity? Both were German immigrants, both Lutheran/Evangelical, and both lived on the same south-side DeKalb Street block in modern Kosciusko (zip 63104, between Soulard and the river). Theodore was Westphalian (Essentho, Prussia); Charles was Hessian (Hofgeismar Kirchenkreis). No documented blood / marriage tie between them is known, but the dual-ancestor convergence at adjacent house numbers is striking.
  • Was Frederick Charles Johnson (1905–1947, KLBX-VR1, Jed's grandfather) named for Charles Gannsen? His middle name "Charles" combined with his Johnson surname (the American name Charles Gannsen used) suggests he might have been a deliberate namesake. This would be a parallel question to the open Charles C Herweck namesake hypothesis.
