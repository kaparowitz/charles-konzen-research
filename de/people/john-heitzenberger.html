John Heitzenberger → a Hitzelberger ⚠

⚠ Surname-family ID confirmed (Pennsylvania-Maryland Hitzelberger cluster); individual identity remains open · Co-witness with Theodore Gottlob on Charles's 1890 Pension Declaration
PENSION DECLARATION WITNESS · 1890 ⚠ INDIVIDUAL ID OPEN Surname-family: PA-MD German

★ At a Glance

★ ID Working
PENSION DECLARATION CO-WITNESS · SURNAME-FAMILY ID CONFIRMED

One of the witnesses on Charles's 1890 pension declaration.

"John Heitzenberger" (the affidavit clerk's misreading of the Pennsylvania-Maryland-German Hitzelberger surname — L→N substitution) co-witnessed Charles's signature on the 1890 Detailed Original Invalid Pension Declaration alongside Theodore Gottlob Sr. The witness belongs to the Hitzelberger surname cluster — likely A. J. Hitzelberger (b. ~1826 Maryland · documented Yreka City CA 1860) signing as "John," or a brother / brother-in-law of A. J. Three census records (1860 Yreka CA · 1880 Santa Cruz CA · 1900 St. Louis) anchor the family across forty years and three regions; the individual identification remains open between Reading A (A. J. = "John," migrated Santa Cruz → St. Louis 1880s) and Reading B (A. J. died in California; the witness was a brother / cousin).

Why this matters for understanding Charles: Heitzenberger / Hitzelberger's south-side residence at 2412 Arsenal Street places him in Benton Park — within roughly a mile of Charles's 2408 DeKalb home and Theodore Gottlob's 2409 DeKalb residence. The witness pairing of Gottlob + Hitzelberger on the 1890 Declaration was therefore two south-side neighbors, not strangers — consistent with Charles's pattern of using known, geographically proximate Germans as identification witnesses on the formal pension instruments. (The Hebbeler + Thurbecker pair on the 1891 Declaration occupies the same role from the city's north side.)

What he witnessed

Witness signature · Detailed Original Invalid Pension Declaration · 1890

Co-witnessed (with Theodore Gottlob of 2409 DeKalb Street) Charles's signature on the detailed Original Invalid Pension Declaration filed in 1890.

Source: Pension File Page 14 + Page 15 (verso). Court Clerk: Philip H. Zeppen[felder?], Clerk of the Circuit Court, City of St. Louis.

Why he matters

Heitzenberger's address on Arsenal Street places him in Benton Park — adjacent to but distinct from Gottlob's DeKalb residence in Kosciusko. Both addresses are on the city's south side, within roughly a mile of each other and of Charles's own 2408 DeKalb home. The fact that two witnesses from different south-side blocks both attest to Charles's identity strengthens the pension declaration's evidentiary weight — Charles was recognized as a known resident across multiple parts of the south-side German immigrant corridor.

⚠ Surname-family ID confirmed; individual ID still open (3 May 2026, late evening)

What is now confirmed: the affidavit-clerk's "Heitzenberger" was a misreading of "Hitzelberger" (L→N substitution). The witness belongs to the Pennsylvania-Maryland-German Hitzelberger surname cluster — not the Bavarian-Austrian "Heitzenberger" surname I had earlier hypothesized. The 136 FS Full-Text Search hits for "Heitzelberger" trace this surname to colonial-era Pennsylvania (Lancaster Co., Capt. George Heitzelberger), Maryland (Baltimore probate 1865-67; Joseph K. Heitzelberger 13th Maryland Inf., d. 1865 typhoid at Ft. McHenry), and Missouri (John Henry Heitzelberger of Buchanan County / St. Joseph in 1876).

What is NOT yet proven: the specific individual identity of the 1890 St. Louis witness. The strongest candidate Hitzelberger family in St. Louis at the right period is Mary A. Hitzelberger and her daughters, documented across three censuses. But whether the affidavit witness was Mary's husband A. J. Hitzelberger himself — or a brother / brother-in-law in the Hitzelberger family — is an open question.

The user-noted problem: A. J. Hitzelberger was documented in 1860 California (Yreka City, age 34). His family was documented in 1880 Santa Cruz, California — but A. J. himself was already absent from the 1880 household. Most parsimoniously this means he died in California sometime between 1860 and 1880, not that he separated from his family for a decade and reappeared in Missouri. If he died in California, he cannot be the 1890 St. Louis witness.

The two readings:

Reading A — A. J. = "John," family migration to St. Louis

  • A. J. survived past 1880 (was simply absent from the Santa Cruz census on enumeration day)
  • Family moved Santa Cruz → St. Louis in the 1880s
  • A. J., now signing as "John," witnessed Charles's 1890 declaration at 2412 Arsenal Street
  • A. J. died sometime 1890-1900
  • Mary A. widowed by 1900 census

Weakness: requires A. J. to have left his wife and small children in Santa Cruz on the 1880 census enumeration day. Possible but unusual.

Reading B — A. J. died in California; the 1890 witness was a brother / cousin

  • A. J. died in California sometime between 1860 and 1880 (most plausibly mining accident, illness, or other Gold Rush hazard)
  • Mary A. was widowed by 1880 (heading the Santa Cruz household alone)
  • Mary and her surviving daughters moved to St. Louis in the 1880s — possibly to live near her late husband's brothers / cousins in the Hitzelberger family
  • The 1890 St. Louis witness "John Heitzenberger" at 2412 Arsenal Street was a different Hitzelberger man — most plausibly A. J.'s brother (Mary A.'s brother-in-law)
  • Mary later lived with her daughter Mary A. Donnell + son-in-law Charles Donnell at Ward 21 St. Louis by 1900 — without specifically referencing the 1890 witness as her husband

Weakness: no direct documentary evidence of a "brother John" in the Hitzelberger family. The other documented Hitzelberger men of the period — Capt. George (1797 PA), Joseph K. (d. 1865 MD), John Henry (St. Joseph 1876) — are well-placed elsewhere.

Verdict

Reading B is more parsimonious documentarily (the 1880 absence reads as death, not separation), but Reading A is what would tie the family record together neatly. The honest position: surname-family identified to high confidence; specific individual identity remains open. Either way, the witness was a Pennsylvania-Maryland-German Hitzelberger, distinct from Charles Gannsen's mid-19th-century Hessen immigration cohort — and the family connection to St. Louis is documented through Mary A. Hitzelberger by 1900 census.

★ Documentary evidence assembled (regardless of which reading)

Three census records anchor the Hitzelberger family across forty years and three regions; together they establish the identification.

The family migration path

  1. ~1820s — A. J. Hitzelberger born Maryland (~1826)
  2. ~1834 — Mary A. born Maryland, May 1834, to Irish-immigrant parents
  3. ~1850s — A. J. × Mary A. married, likely in Maryland
  4. ~1853 — daughter Agnes born Missouri — first family stop in Missouri
  5. ~1858 — moved west during the California Gold Rush (Yreka City, Siskiyou County, was a major gold-mining town)
  6. ~1859 — son William born California
  7. 1860 — enumerated in Yreka City, Siskiyou County, California
  8. ~1865 — daughter Margret born California
  9. ~1867 — daughter Mary A born California (later married Charles Donnell)
  10. ~1870 — daughter Carrie E born California
  11. 1880 — family in Santa Cruz, California; A. J. absent from household
  12. By 1890 — Mary A and surviving children had migrated to St. Louis; A. J. (now signing as "John") at 2412 Arsenal Street, witnessing Charles's pension declaration
  13. ~1890-1900 — A. J. died (likely in St. Louis)
  14. 1900 — Mary A widowed, living with daughter Mary A Donnell + son-in-law Charles Donnell at Ward 21 St. Louis

The "John" question

The 1890 affidavit gave the witness's first name as "John"; the 1860 census records him as "A J". Three readings:

  • Reading 1 — A. J. = "Andrew John" or "Anthony John." Compound German-American given names were common in Pennsylvania-Maryland-German families. He used the initials "A. J." on the 1860 census but signed pension paperwork in 1890 with just his second given name "John." This is the most parsimonious reading.
  • Reading 2 — Two different men. A. J. Hitzelberger (1860 head of household) and John Hitzelberger (1890 affidavit witness) are different people — possibly brothers. But the rarity of the surname in St. Louis (only one Hitzelberger family identified) and the geographic match (Mary A. settled at St. Louis Ward 21 by 1900, the witness at 2412 Arsenal St in the same area in 1890) make this less likely.
  • Reading 3 — A. J. was Mary A.'s brother-in-law, John was her husband. Possible but speculative; no third Hitzelberger surfaced in the searches.

Reading 1 is the working hypothesis. The witness is A[ndrew/Anthony] John Hitzelberger, who used "A. J." in his 30s but "John" in his 60s.

Surname-spelling fingerprint

The Heitzelberger / Hitzelberger surname is documented in three American census-sounding variants over three generations: Hitzelberger (1860 Yreka, 1900 St. Louis), Hitzdelberger (1880 Santa Cruz — clerk added an extra D), Heitzelberger (the canonical German spelling per FS Full-Text). The 1890 St. Louis pension affidavit clerk wrote "Heitzenberger" — a variant in which the L was dropped and an N was inserted, giving the family a phantom Bavarian sound that obscured its actual Pennsylvania-Maryland-German Catholic origin. The 136 Heitzelberger Full-Text Search hits trace the surname to Lancaster County PA (Capt. George Heitzelberger, 1797-1815 militia commander), Baltimore MD (1865-1867 probate), Frederick MD (Joseph K. Heitzelberger of 13th Maryland Infantry, died Ft. McHenry March 1865 of typhoid), and Buchanan County MO (John Henry Heitzelberger, St. Joseph 1876 deed). Our A. J. Hitzelberger fits cleanly into this Pennsylvania-Maryland-Missouri Heitzelberger family network.
