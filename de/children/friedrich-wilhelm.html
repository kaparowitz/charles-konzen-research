Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson ⚜

First-born child of Charles Gannsen & Auguste Kreichelt · died as an infant
Born 20 June 1866 · Died 26 October 1867 · ~16 months

★ At a Glance

FIRST-BORN · CHILD 1 of 7 · DIED AS INFANT FS LB5X-RYC

Charles and Auguste's firstborn son. He arrived in June 1866 — just months after their wedding — and died as an infant 16 months later. He never knew his six younger siblings.

Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson (b. 20 June 1866, d. 26 October 1867 at age 16 months) was the first-born child of Charles & Auguste, born three days after Prussia declared war on Hesse-Kassel and christened on 2 September 1866 — about six weeks after news of the deposition of Elector Friedrich Wilhelm I would have reached St. Louis. The baby's documented namesake is his godfather Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann (per the standard Lutheran firstborn-naming convention); the name happens also to be that of the deposed Elector, an echo discussed below. Sponsors: F.W. Pillmann, Wilhelmine Ebeling Leue, and the maternal grandparents Georg & Henriette Kreichelt. Surname rendered Gonnson — same spelling as Charles's January 1866 marriage record.

Why this matters for understanding Charles: The 1866 timing alignment with the Austro-Prussian War and the Hesse-Kassel deposition is a suggestive cultural detail consistent with a Hesse-Kassel origin — but it is not, on its own, diagnostic of it: Friedrich Wilhelm was a very common German given-name pair, and the documented namesake here is the godfather, Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann. The two readings below (deliberate invocation vs. meaningful coincidence) are both interpretations of Charles's intent, which the record does not settle. The geographic identification of Charles's origin rests on the 1862 enlistment and 1866 marriage records (Key Evidence Cards 2–3), not on this naming.

Baptism record & sponsors

Friedrich Wilhelm was baptized at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church, St. Louis, on 2 September 1866. The 2020 AncestryProGenealogists journal indexed Doc G–Doc L of the St. Marcus baptism register (children 2 through 7) and the firstborn's entry was added in subsequent reconstructions. He was baptized as "Gonnson" — the same surname Charles used at his marriage in January 1866 — fitting the post-marriage pattern that ran until summer 1870.

St. Marcus Baptism Register · A.D. 1866 · pages 164-165 · Entry №5201 / running 372 · FHL Microfilm 1503034

The 1866 sponsor pool sits on the standard Lutheran-Evangelical default for a firstborn: both maternal grandparents stand at the font (Georg and Henriette), plus an additional male and female sponsor drawn from the wider parish-circle. Friedrich Pillmann's appearance four years before he formally married into the Albrecht/Kreichelt family is the strongest piece of evidence that the Kreichelts and the Pillmann/Albrecht families were already a single tightly-bonded social network in 1866 — even before the formal marriages of 1870 and 1871 created kinship ties on paper. The Wilhelmine Ebeling Leue identification (FamilySearch M6G9-FJR) was confirmed May 2026 via exact death-date match and matched daughter-death-years cross-check; she was a senior Soulard German Lutheran neighbor, not a relative — the only non-Kreichelt-side sponsor at the firstborn baptism. Originally documented in the 2020 AncestryProGenealogists journal, Doc G; sponsor identities resolved by Jed × Peter Schräder, 16–18 December 2025.

⚜ A name that points home — the precise 1866 timeline

Charles and Auguste married at St. Marcus Church on 7 January 1866. Their first child was born 20 June 1866 and christened 2 September 1866 as Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson. The same summer of 1866 saw the political destruction of Charles's homeland, the Electorate of Hesse-Kassel. The exact day-by-day timeline is striking:

17 June 1866Prussia declares war on Hesse-Kassel, Hanover, and Saxony — start of the Austro-Prussian War
~19 June 1866Prussian troops occupy Kassel, the Electoral capital
20 June 1866★ Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson born in St. Louis — three days after Prussia's declaration of war, ~one day after the occupation of Kassel
23 June 1866Elector Friedrich Wilhelm I refuses to negotiate with Prussia and is transferred as a prisoner to Stettin (per Wikipedia, citing the New International Encyclopedia 1905). This is the operative moment of his political deposition — three days after the baby was born.
~late July 1866Trans-Atlantic news of Kassel's occupation and the Elector's arrest reaches St. Louis (10–14-day steamship lag, before the second transatlantic telegraph cable was operational on 27 July 1866)
23 August 1866Peace of Prague ends the war
2 September 1866★ Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson christened at St. Marcus — by this date Charles had known about the Elector's deposition for ~6 weeks; the war was over but formal annexation had not yet occurred
20 September 1866Hesse-Kassel formally annexed by Prussia (Annexionsgesetz) — the Electorate ceases to exist as a polity

★★★ Two competing readings of "Friedrich Wilhelm"

The exact dates above allow us to test two interpretations of the firstborn's name. The choice between them turns on what Charles knew when, and what he chose to do with it.

Reading A · Coincidental name, deliberate sponsor choice

The baby was born 20 June 1866, the very day Prussian troops occupied Kassel. Charles in St. Louis on that day would not yet have known about the war's outbreak (declared three days earlier on 17 June, with no transatlantic telegraph yet operational and steamship news ~10 days behind). The name Friedrich Wilhelm was very likely chosen weeks earlier in spring 1866, when the parents arranged for the godfather: Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann was their first choice for godfather, and per the standard German Lutheran convention, the firstborn was named after his godfather. The match with the Elector's name was, on this reading, a coincidence — both Pillmann and the Elector happened to share the common Hessian name Friedrich Wilhelm.

However: by the 2 September 1866 baptism, Charles knew his sovereign had been deposed. He could have changed sponsor plans — invited a different godfather, given the baby a different name, or distanced himself from the now-politically-charged "Friedrich Wilhelm" associations. He did not. He proceeded with the original sponsor pool: Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann (godfather), Wilhelmine Ebeling Leue (godmother — note the female form of Wilhelm), Georg & Henriette Kreichelt (maternal grandparents). The September baptism formally registered the baby's name in the German Evangelical record as Friedrich Wilhelm — exactly the name of the now-imprisoned Elector — at a moment when Charles knew exactly what that name meant politically.

Reading B · Intentional sponsor choice to invoke the Elector's name

An alternative reading credits Charles with deliberate sponsor selection. Among the available godfather candidates in his St. Louis circle (Adam Herweck, August Kreichelt, Georg Kreichelt himself, fellow Battery B veterans), Charles selected Friedrich Wilhelm Pillmann — and the godmother Wilhelmina Ebeling with the female form of Wilhelm. The combination of these two sponsors structurally guaranteed that the baptism record would textually carry the Elector's name. The fact that Pillmann was unmarried and not yet kin to the Kreichelts in 1866 (he would only marry into the Albrecht family in 1870) makes his selection as firstborn-godfather more pointed — Charles was singling out a friend specifically because of his name, not because of family-tier closeness.

If Reading B is correct, the firstborn's name is Charles's first deliberate political act in America regarding his lost homeland: a documented loyalty gesture at the precise moment the Electorate ceased to exist. The choice of Wilhelmina Ebeling as the female sponsor, with the matching Wilhelm root, is the strongest support for this reading — a single sponsor with a "Friedrich" name could be coincidence, but the matched pair is harder to explain as coincidence.

Verdict

Both readings end in the same place: by 2 September 1866, when Charles stood at the baptismal font and the parish clerk wrote Friedrich Wilhelm Gonnson, Charles knew the Elector had been deposed. Which reading is correct cannot be settled from the record. Reading A — the more parsimonious one — is that the firstborn was named for his godfather per ordinary Lutheran convention, and the overlap with the Elector's name is a coincidence (a common name, a charged year). Reading B credits Charles with a deliberate gesture. The naming is consistent with a Hesse-Kassel origin and is a genuinely evocative detail, but it is an indirect, interpretive piece of context — not independent proof of origin. The geographic identification of Charles's homeland rests on the 1862 enlistment and 1866 marriage records (Key Evidence Cards 2–3).
