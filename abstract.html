Abstract

A formal one-page summary of the investigation — question, method, evidence, working conclusion, and acknowledged gaps. Print-ready; designed for citation in academic and genealogical work.

Subject
Charles Konzen (also recorded in U.S. records as Gannsen, Gonsen, Konsen, Jansen, Conson, Johnson) — 26 January 1835 – 16 December 1903†.

Jed Johnson, with Peter Schräder of Trendelburg as German genealogist.

Last updated

Citation

Question

Two questions structure this investigation: what was Charles's German surname before its multiple American renderings, and which Hessian parish recorded his baptism? Everything that follows is in service of those two questions.

The subject is Charles — the man recorded across his U.S. records under nine surname spellings (Gonnson, Gansen, Gonsen, Konsen, Gonson, Jansen, Konzen, Gannsen, and Conson — the Gannsen his own holograph, on the 1862 enlistment and again in the 1895 city directory), a Hessian-born sergeant of Battery B, 2nd Missouri Light Artillery. He emigrated to the United States in 1859, served in the Union Army from his enlistment at St. Louis on 2 December 1862 to December 1865, married Auguste Kreichelt at St. Marcus German Evangelical Church in St. Louis on 7 January 1866, fathered seven children documented in the parish register between 1866 and 1880, was awarded a federal pension for war-onset rheumatism in 1891, and died in St. Louis on 16 December 1903, buried three days later at Old Saint Marcus Cemetery.1

Method

The investigation is conducted in alignment with the Board for Certification of Genealogists' Genealogical Proof Standard — reasonably exhaustive research; complete and accurate citations; analysis and correlation of the collected information; resolution of conflicting evidence; soundly reasoned written conclusion — with one documented residual gap (the German baptismal entry remains unrecovered), so the surname conclusion is held in the project's Working state rather than promoted to Confirmed.2 Source-rating taxonomy, transcription conventions, and naming conventions are codified on the project's Methodology page.

Primary research draws on three archival classes. U.S. military and pension records: the Compiled Service Record (NARA M405, file G11-422819443E) and full Pension File (F41-523680269E), obtained as mail-order packets from the National Archives (full citation block: Bibliography §1).3 German parish records: digitised Kirchenbücher of Trendelburg, Deisel, Friedrichsfeld, Helmarshausen, Hofgeismar, and the surrounding Churhessen parishes, accessed via Archion.de (per-volume listing in Bibliography §2); emigration permits (Auswanderer-Belege) and Stammrollen via Hessisches Staatsarchiv Marburg through Arcinsys and LAGIS.4 U.S. vital, immigration, and census records: FamilySearch.org, Ancestry.com, and Find a Grave. American records of every class were mined for the leads and place-name attestations that point back to Germany — church (the St. Marcus baptism, marriage, and burial registers), city (the St. Louis directories), state and county (the civil marriage license, the 1868 naturalization, the death certificate), and federal (census, the Compiled Service Record, and the pension file). Field research and Stammtafel construction in the Trendelburg-area parish complex was undertaken by Peter Schräder.

Transcription provenance: German-language quotations in this abstract are working transcriptions by Peter Schräder from the Archion-imaged registers, cross-checked against Archion's HTR output by the author. Per the transcription conventions on the Methodology page, original-language quotations are italicised inside double quotes; bracketed editorial insertions denote uncertain readings. A full Archion citation audit on 21 June 2026 re-opened every cited register in the Archion viewer and confirmed each Seite/image locator against the original manuscript page — all 19 registers with a checkable anchor (≈25 anchor reads across baptism, marriage, and burial sections) validated, with zero genuine citation errors; the keystone Card 1 Kontze/Contze entries additionally carry one-click Archion image permalinks (image links inline below).

Evidence — surname trail

Charles's surname was recorded in nine distinct spellings across his American records (1862–1896): his own holograph form, Gannsen, he signed on the 1862 enlistment and his 1890–91 pension declarations, and it recurs in the 1895 Gould’s St. Louis city directory (“Gannsen Charles, watchmaker, r. rear 2407 DeKalb”). The other eight forms are independent scribal phonetic captures by at least five different scribes between 1866 and 1896: Gonnson (1866 St. Marcus marriage register, Pastor Wall), Gansen (1870 St. Marcus baptism of Katherine, Pastor Braschler), Gonsen (1872 St. Marcus baptism of Auguste, Pastor Braschler), Konsen (1875 St. Marcus baptism of Erwin Carl, Pastor Hoffmann), Gonson (1880 U.S. Federal Census, 1534 Jackson St., enumerator unknown), Jansen (1881 St. Marcus baptism of Bertha, Pastor Nollau), Konzen (1890 death record of daughter Auguste, reading "Auguste Somers geb Konzen" — the single American attestation of the full Hessian z-form documented in the Trendelburg parish; the 17 May 2026 re-reading that reversed an earlier Konzen→Konsen pass is logged in errata · 17 May 2026 · 1890 Konzen reading), and Conson (1896 St. Marcus death record of wife Auguste, Pastor Eilt Heeren Eilts, with Charles himself as the surviving informant).5

Five of the nine distinct spellings (Gonnson, Gonsen, Konsen, Gonson, Conson) collapse to one spoken root, differing only in the voicing of the initial consonant — the [ˈkɔn.sən] forms (Konsen, Conson) and their voiced [ˈɡɔn.sən] variants (Gonnson, Gonsen, Gonson). The two -en-preserving Hessian-form attestations — Konsen 1875 (Pastor Hoffmann's phonetic rendering) and Konzen 1890 (the Hessian z-form) — recur at a fifteen-year interval by two different scribes, establishing the Hessian -en suffix as continuously in family use through 1890. The remaining variants (Gansen 1870, Jansen 1881) are best explained as scribal mishearings — Pastor Braschler's recording of the same family in 1870 and 1872 with two different vowels (Gansen / Gonsen) provides direct evidence that the spelling reflected Charles's own pronunciation, not the pastor's preference. The same forms recur independently in the contemporaneous St. Louis city directories, where the surname was supplied by Charles or his household to the canvasser rather than a pastor: the 1874 directory's Konsen ("Konsen Charles, driver") matches Pastor Hoffmann's 1875 baptism rendering a year before it, and the 1876 directory's Gonson matches the 1880 census form four years before it — two non-ecclesiastical scribes capturing the same spellings the church registers did, which strengthens the inference that the variation tracks Charles's own pronunciation across independent record streams (he also appears as his signed legal Gannsen in the 1895 directory, and under the Anglicised Johnson in his 1871–73 and 1887 directory years). The full directory-by-directory ledger is on Surname across the records.

The spelling tracked the context. The earliest recorded form is his own signed Gannsen on the 1862 enlistment, and it is the form that recurs on every record that touched the federal government — the 21 October 1868 St. Louis naturalization, the 1890–91 pension declarations, and the 1900 admission to the Danville National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers (entered as “Gannson,” a clerk's vowel away) — because each was tied by law back to the “Gannsen” of his Battery B muster record. A methodological view that weights the earliest attestation as potentially the most faithful reading would, on that basis, favour Gannsen; the project notes this but treats it as to be determined, not settled. The homeland -en forms (the [ˈkɔn.sən] / [ˈɡɔn.sən] cluster) appear most often across the St. Marcus parish registers but are not confined to church use — they also surface in the 1880 census (Gonson) and the 1874 / 1876 city directories (Konsen, Gonson). The Americanised Johnson, the family's eventual settled surname, first appears (on present evidence) around 1871 in the city directories of his streetcar-conductor years, decades before the death records. Charles, in short, used whichever rendering the setting called for — the locked legal Gannsen on government paperwork, the homeland -en forms at the font and in everyday records, and Johnson where assimilation invited it — so the American surname history is a record of context as much as of sound.

Surname conclusion (May 2026)  ★ Working: Charles's German-side surname is most likely Konzen — the Kontzen-family form documented in the Trendelburg parish complex of Churhessen, and the form that appears directly on the 1890 American death record of Charles's daughter Auguste ("Auguste Somers geb Konzen"). Eight of the nine American spellings reach this form via regular German-American transcription shifts (initial K voicing to G; tz-affricate softening to s; terminal -en Anglicising to -on only in the 1866 and 1896 records); the 1890 Konzen attestation alone carries it directly. The conclusion is held in the project's Working state per the source-rating taxonomy — promotion to Confirmed requires recovery of the specific German baptismal entry (see Open Questions, Priority 1; the surname's promotion history, including the 4 May 2026 first-promotion entry, is logged in errata · 4 May 2026).

Evidence — geographic placement

Two records, written by different scribes, in different languages, four years apart, anchor the geographic search. The 1862 Civil War enlistment register gives the place of birth as "Freundburg, Kurhessen" — a place that does not exist; phonetic analysis points to Trendelburg as the most probable reading — the German Tr- cluster heard as English Fr- and the meaningless syllable -endel- normalised to the familiar German Freund- ("friend"). This decoding is a proposed reading, not a confirmed identification: “Freundburg” could be Trendelburg, but no record states the equivalence outright, and competing decodings remain on the table (below).6 The 1866 St. Marcus marriage register reads "aus Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen" — the village of Deisel, ~5 km south of Trendelburg in the same Kirchenkreis Hofgeismar. The same "Deisse" origin is captured a second time by an independent, non-ecclesiastical hand: the St. Louis County state marriage license for the same 7 January 1866 marriage — the civil counterpart to the church register — records the identical place-name, so two scribes in two record streams agree on it. Either origin record alone would be ambiguous; together they bound the answer to a 5 km × 5 km square inside the Hofgeismar district. Both town-readings are phonetic interpretations, not recorded facts; the competing readings — AncestryProGenealogists's eliminated Freudenberg/Schierstein and Dies/Diez, and James Gunnis's still-open Dissen / Grifte alternative — are weighed and ranked on Origin across the records.

The Trendelburg parish registers document the surname pool from which Charles is most likely to derive. The name stands there from 1773: the 26 November 1773 marriage (Kirchenbuch 220096, Seite 288) records Johann Henrich Kontzen, eldest son of Philipp Kontzen (Rathsverwandter); and a 1795 baptism in Johann Friedrich Kontzen's family names his late father Andreas Kontzen (I) (220096, Seiten 90–91, image 1029). The 220096 burial register (1773–1818) then records three Kontze / Contze entries read firsthand at the manuscript page — Arnold Philipp Kontze, buried 1780 (Seite 361, image 1178); Herr Andreas Contze, buried 1800 (Seite 396, image 1196); and the infant Johannes, son of Johann Christoph Contze, buried 1808 (Seite 414, image 1205). A parish-internal cross-reference note "siehe auch K. Contze, Cortze, Cordes, Contz" establishes that the parish itself treated the K-, C-, and -en variants as orthographically interchangeable.7 Register 220108 (Trendelburg Taufen 1830–1863) preserves an alphabetical index entry "Konze, Johann Friedrich Wilhelm — Seite 243" (the cited page is image 1623) — the first Konze father-surname baptism confirmed in post-1831 Trendelburg.

Eliminations

Sixteen candidate Hessian families were investigated — the Konze / Kontzen pool, the Quentin, Herwig, Grandjot, Branson/Frisse, Person, Lucas, Janson, Köster, Hund, Steitz and Bonhagen lines, and (prompted by a Jewish Y-DNA signal) the Gans families of Meimbressen and Deisel and the Stern / Kleeberg line. Two analytic filters — birth-year window (b. 26 January 1835 ± a few weeks) and surname / parish congruence with the U.S. surname trail — eliminated all but the Trendelburg / Friedrichsfeld Konze pool. Eliminated parishes include Deisel itself (no Konze fathers in the right window; three "geb. Konzen" mothers attested 1828–1829 but no candidate male b. 1835), Helmarshausen (full-index search returned no Konze-pool male b. ~1835), Sielen, Schöneberg, Hümme, both Hofgeismar parishes, and the Mainz Andreas Konze line. Two undigitised Lutheran registers — Heisebeck (pre-1845) and Schöneberg (pre-1822) — at the Landeskirchliches Archiv Kassel remain the project's substantive open candidates. A multi-register illegitimacy scan of January 1835 across Trendelburg, Friedrichsfeld, Helmarshausen, and Hofgeismar surfaced no candidate child.8 Per-parish search scope, dates walked, and the specific contradicting record that closed each lead are catalogued on the negative-results audit · Hessian parish registers searched; the U.S.-side searches that ruled out competing immigrant Konzes appear at negative-results · U.S.-side negative searches.

In June 2026 the search was widened beyond the Konze surname pool. The Hofgeismar-area confirmation registers — which list every confirmand's birth date and father — were read for any boy named Carl / Karl born 1833–1841 under any surname and either confession. The classes read (Helmarshausen, Sielen, Hümme; Deisel and Trendelburg queued for AI transcription) yielded several local boys named Carl, but none with a Konze-, *n-s-n-, or *n-z-n-type surname — strengthening the inference that Charles's birth parish is not among the already-digitised Hofgeismar registers, and pointing the remaining work to the not-yet-digitised on-site tier (Heisebeck, pre-1822 Schöneberg, Liebenau) and the Tier-3 parishes (Dissen, Grifte, Gudensberg). Full coverage, with the key search terms, is on Parish search coverage.

A June 2026 reconciliation against the collaborative FamilySearch shared Family Tree — which holds an independently register-sourced reconstruction of the entire Trendelburg / Deisel Konze clan — corroborated the project's hand-built family framework (the Andreas Kontze "Küster" line, the 1795 Andreas × Hofeditz household, and the Schildknecht branch all confirmed and dated) and sharpened the candidate picture by negative evidence. Running every documented Konze male of Charles's own generation through the searchable German death and emigration indexes, all but one are fully accounted for in Germany — they stayed, married, and died in the parish complex. The one apparent exception with a fitting given name, Carl Wilhelm Konze (b. 15 September 1831, Trendelburg), was surfaced and then eliminated: he is documented dying at Trendelburg on 30 October 1869, having married locally. The single Konze male of the cohort who leaves no adult German footprint at all — no death or marriage record under any indexed variant — is Justus Ernst Konze (b. 14 November 1829, Friedrichsfeld; son of Andreas Kontze 1795 × Maria Sophia Hofeditz), untraced after his 1843 Trendelburg confirmation. Two cautions weigh firmly against reading this as Charles, however. First, Justus Ernst was born in November 1829 — roughly five to six years before Charles's stated 26 January 1835, placing him outside the birth window the U.S. records describe; on age alone the likelihood that he is Charles is low. Second, an exhaustive search for an emigration record — U.S. passenger lists, the Mainz emigration register, and the LAGIS Kurhessen Auswanderer index — turned up nothing for him, so the positive evidence one would want for the emigration hypothesis is also absent. His standing is therefore only that of the one cohort member not yet affirmatively excluded — not a strong match — and the conclusion remains an inference to the Konze surname pool rather than to a confirmed individual. The two decisive, still-open tests are both offline: a page-by-page read of the un-indexed Stammen burial register (Archion 220072, where two of his siblings died) and an individual emigration / release file in HStAM Bestand 180 Hofgeismar. This negative-evidence finding is logged in full on the negative-results audit · candidate families.

Sponsorship pattern — corroborating "immigrated alone"

Across seven baptisms at St. Marcus over the 1866–1880 window — and the four witnesses at the 1866 marriage itself — not a single named sponsor or witness is a documented blood relative of Charles (his wedding witnesses were his Battery B comrade Adam Herweck and St. Louis friends; high confidence none is his blood kin — see Sponsor reconciliation). Every kin sponsor traces to Auguste's Kreichelt-Albrecht-Pillmann-Mincke side. German-Lutheran baptism sponsorship in this period was structurally a kin-default convention; the asymmetry across Charles's seven baptisms is consistent with Charles having no German blood family accessible by mail or in person between 1866 and 1880, corroborating the working hypothesis that he immigrated alone in 1859 and never re-established contact close enough to bring kin to the baptismal font.9 A complementary line of corroboration runs through the 1891 pension award: the seven affidavits collected by Claim Agent Fred. W. Fout in 1889–1891 form a coordinated mutual-aid filing run through one Light Artillery alumni network; the narrative phrasings recur word-for-word across affidavits, identifying the agent's drafting hand.10

Consolidated proof argument

No single record names Charles's parents or his parish, so the origin conclusion cannot be a proof statement resting on direct evidence. It is offered instead as a proof argument in the sense set out in the Methodology: a chain of independent evidence items that, taken together, point to one answer — with the competing explanation (coincidence) weighed explicitly rather than assumed away.

Link 1 — the surname (independent of geography). Charles's nine American spellings collapse phonetically to a single root: an initial G / K, a back vowel, and an –n–s–n ending (see Surname across the records). The Hessian surname family that satisfies every one of those phonetic constraints and is documented in the candidate region is Konze / Kontzen / Contzen. This link is derived purely from the sound of his name, with no reference to where he said he was born.

Link 2 — the place, by two independent attestations. Two different U.S. records, written by two different scribes years apart, each record Charles's stated origin: the 1862 enlistment (information he supplied himself) reads “Freundburg, Kurhessen,” and the 1866 marriage register reads “Deisse, Kreis Cassel, Churhessen.” Decoded phonetically, “Freundburg” resolves to Trendelburg and “Deisse” to Deisel — neighbouring villages within the same Kreis Hofgeismar of the former Hesse-Kassel, roughly five kilometres apart. Two independent place-attestations converging on one small district is itself a non-trivial result, since they share no scribe, no document, and no date.

Link 3 — the convergence of two independent streams. The Konze surname pool of Link 1 is documented to be concentrated in precisely the Trendelburg / Friedrichsfeld / Deisel parish complex that the place-name decodings of Link 2 independently point to. The surname evidence (from the sound of his name) and the geographic evidence (from his stated birthplace) are methodologically independent, yet they meet in the same handful of parishes.

Weighing the alternative — coincidence. The load-bearing question is whether an –n–s–n-surnamed man from a place that decodes to Trendelburg / Deisel could be unrelated to the Konze pool there purely by chance. Three considerations argue against that: the Konze surname is regionally concentrated rather than ubiquitous, so its co-location with the decoded place is not what one would expect at random; the two place-attestations are themselves independent; and a systematic sweep of the surrounding digitised registers (see Negative results and Parish search coverage) has surfaced no competing surname-and-place fit. The convergence is therefore most simply explained by Charles belonging to the Trendelburg-area Konze pool. The residual chance that an independent surname match and an independent place decoding coincide is real but small — and it is exactly the reason the conclusion is held at Working rather than promoted to Confirmed.

What the eliminations and the DNA do and do not add. Every specific Konze family tested as Charles's parents has been eliminated or left inconclusive (Peter Schräder's verdict, 12 April 2026), so the argument supports an inference to the surname pool, not to a named couple. The Y-DNA is consistent with a German paternal origin but confirms neither the parish nor the surname, and it opens a separate deep-paternal question rather than closing the documentary one (see DNA evidence). DNA thus neither completes the chain nor breaks it.

Working conclusion

On the strength of the argument above, Charles Konzen — recorded across his American records under nine distinct spellings — most likely descends from the Trendelburg / Friedrichsfeld Konze branch of the Hofgeismar parish complex, in the former Electorate of Hesse-Kassel (Churhessen). The conclusion is held in the project's Working state pending recovery of the specific German baptismal entry, which has not yet been located in any digitised parish register. Every specific Konze family examined as Charles's parents has been eliminated or left inconclusive (Peter Schräder's verdict, 12 April 2026); the conclusion is therefore an inference to the Trendelburg-area Konze surname pool, not to a confirmed individual family. A tie to that branch would not, by itself, settle where Charles was born: he could have been born within the Trendelburg / Friedrichsfeld complex, or born elsewhere and connected to the branch later in life, or originate from somewhere else entirely — the surname pool and the birth parish remain logically distinct, and the latter is exactly what the unrecovered baptismal entry would fix. The convergent evidence supporting this conclusion is documented in the project's Key Evidence Card 1 and visualised in the Visualize hub.

Open questions

The conclusion is not promoted to Confirmed because four primary sources remain unrecovered: (i) Charles's specific German baptismal entry; (ii) his 1859 trans-Atlantic passenger manifest; (iii) the Hessian Auswanderer-Beleg or unfiled-departure record; and (iv) the Stammrolle entry for the 1855 conscription class. Each is documented with its last-attempted date and the conditions under which it would resolve on the project's Open Questions page. Following the June 2026 candidate reconciliation, the two highest-value remaining tests are both offline: a page-by-page read of the un-indexed Stammen burial register (Archion 220072, ~1843–1875) and an individual emigration / release-from-citizenship file in HStAM Bestand 180 Hofgeismar — together they would either record Justus Ernst Konze's German death (closing the leading line of the hypothesis) or harden the "emigrated under a new surname" reading.
